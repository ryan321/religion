# Scriptorian

A future site for LDS adults who want to become deeply knowledgeable in the scriptures.

## The Problem

There used to be a tradition of thorough, systematic scripture study in LDS culture -- Cleon Skousen's *Thousand Years* series (First, Third, Fourth, Fifth), Joseph Fielding Smith's doctrinal works -- that walked you through the scriptures themselves in depth, from an LDS understanding, without looking beyond the mark. That tradition has largely died out. Nothing has replaced it.

What exists now tends to be:

- **Topical** -- gospel topics with scripture support, rather than the scriptures themselves
- **Speculative** -- novel interpretations, particular views on the last days, new ways to view a topic
- **Devotional** -- conference talks, Come Follow Me, short-form content

None of those are the same as actually knowing the scriptures deeply. The Skousen and Joseph Fielding Smith books are out of print, two generations old, and there is really nothing like them now.

## The Vision

Help LDS adults who want to become scriptorians -- people with deep, thorough knowledge of the actual text of the scriptures.

- **The scriptures themselves**, not gospel topics, not commentary-heavy interpretation
- **Face value** -- what the scriptures actually say, not esoteric or novel readings
- **LDS understanding** -- Book of Mormon, D&C, Pearl of Great Price, and the Bible read together as a unified witness
- **Thorough and in-depth** -- not devotional, not surface-level
- **Series of books** that systematically cover the scriptures

## The Conviction

While pure religion is visiting the sick and afflicted, and it is more important to live a good Christian life than to study the scriptures, there is a place and a need for some people to become very knowledgeable about the scriptures. From great knowledge of the scriptures should come increased understanding of truth and the gospel -- not just of what the scriptures say, but of the gospel itself. But it is very hard to have a great understanding of the gospel without having a great knowledge of the scriptures.

The goal is not to replace living the gospel. The goal is to serve those who want depth.

## The Name

**Scriptorian** -- an LDS-familiar term for someone deeply knowledgeable in the scriptures.

## Inspiration

- Cleon Skousen: *The First 2000 Years*, *The Third Thousand Years*, *The Fourth Thousand Years*, *The Fifth Thousand Years*
- Joseph Fielding Smith: *Doctrines of Salvation*, *Answers to Gospel Questions*, etc.

These authors gave thorough, faithful, scripture-grounded treatments without looking beyond the mark. The knowledge gained from them was excellent. That is the standard.

## Structure: Tiered Progression

The site is organized as a tiered progression, not a buffet of disconnected books. Learners move through tiers, with freedom to choose their order within each tier.

### Tier 1 — Foundations

The first tier. Covers the factual and narrative landscape of the scriptures: people, places, times, events, and key doctrines across all four standard works.

- **Factual and informational** -- who, what, when, where. Building the mental map that everything else sits on.
- **Comprehensive, not selective** -- covers all of it, including the parts most people skip. A scriptorian needs to know the minor prophets, the later D&C sections, the war chapters, Jacob's allegory.
- **Not shallow, but not interpretive** -- facts are connected and contextualized, but the goal is knowledge of what's in the scriptures, not novel readings or deep doctrinal analysis. That comes in later tiers.
- **The foundation for everything that follows** -- later tiers assume this knowledge. Without it, deeper study falls flat.
- **Common scriptural and Christian terminology** -- the learner should come out knowing the basic terms used in Christian parlance beyond LDS culture. For example, "Pentateuch," "synoptic Gospels," "Pauline epistles," "Septuagint," "apocrypha," "eschatology," etc. LDS members often don't encounter these terms at church but a scriptorian should know them. Not everything the outside world uses, but at least the basics.

- **Heavy on names, facts, and details** -- the learner needs to know who Raphael was, even if there's not much on him. Who was Rahab? Who was Abish? Who were the men named Moroni? How many Nephis are there? Foundations requires learning a large volume of names, places, and facts. This is the Grammar stage of the Trivium -- it may be the least "fun" tier, but you just have to learn it. The reward comes in later tiers when all of this knowledge makes deeper study possible.
- **Strong sense of place and time** -- just like with history, if you don't know when and where the Romans and Greeks lived you are going to be severely hampered. The learner needs to know when the Kingdom of Israel split, when the Babylonian captivity happened, where Babylon was, which prophetic books were written before, during, and after the exile. They need to know where Palmyra is and when Joseph Smith was there, where Kirtland and Far West and Nauvoo are. They need to know where Zarahemla was relative to the land of Nephi, where King Noah was, when Alma lived relative to Captain Moroni. Time and place are the scaffolding that holds everything else together -- without them, people and events float unanchored.
- **Structure and organization of the scriptures** -- how many books are in the Old Testament, that Psalms is really five collections, that the Book of Mormon is mostly Mormon's abridgment but some books (Nephi's, Moroni's) are original, that D&C isn't chronologically ordered throughout. The learner should know how the scriptures are put together -- what's in each volume and how it's organized.
- **Basic authorship** -- who wrote each book and who is speaking. Moses wrote the Pentateuch. Mormon abridged most of the Book of Mormon but Moroni finished it and added his own material plus the Jaredite record. Paul wrote Romans. Some D&C sections are Joseph, some are the Lord speaking directly. Knowing who is talking in any given passage is foundational.
- **The narrative arc** -- beyond isolated facts, the learner should be able to tell the story from beginning to end. The grand throughline from the pre-earth council through the Second Coming as told across all four standard works. Not just "I know who Gideon is" but "I know where Gideon fits in the story."
- **Key passages and where to find them** -- a scriptorian should know where things are. Where is the Sermon on the Mount? Where is Alma's discourse on faith? Where is the vision of the degrees of glory? Where is Lehi's teaching on the Fall? Not memorized word for word, but "I know where to find it and what's in it."
- **How to use the study tools** -- the LDS scriptures come with a system: footnotes, cross-references, the Topical Guide, Bible Dictionary, JST appendix, chapter headings, the index to the triple combination. A scriptorian uses these constantly. Most members barely touch them. This is a practical skill that belongs in Foundations.

The person who finishes Foundations knows what's in the scriptures -- all the people, places, events, timelines, and key doctrines -- even if they haven't yet gone deep on any one part.

**Potential books in Foundations:**

Old Testament:
- The Pentateuch -- Creation, Fall, Flood, patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph), Exodus, Sinai, the Law, the wilderness
- Historical books -- Joshua and the conquest, the judges, the united monarchy (Saul, David, Solomon), the divided kingdom, exile to Babylon, return under Ezra/Nehemiah
- The prophets -- who each one was, when they prophesied, what kingdom they were in, what their core message was. At this level it's "know who Hosea is and when he fits" not "deeply understand Hosea"
- Wisdom literature -- Job, Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes. What's in them, basic themes

New Testament:
- The Gospels -- life of Christ from birth through resurrection, harmonized across all four accounts
- Acts -- the apostolic church, Paul's conversion and missions
- The Epistles -- who Paul was writing to and why, the general epistles, basic content of each letter
- Revelation -- overview of structure and major visions, not deep interpretation

Book of Mormon:
- Lehi through Mosiah -- the departure, the split, the kings, Benjamin, Abinadi, Alma's conversion
- Alma through Helaman -- the judges, the wars, the missionary accounts, the Gadianton robbers
- 3 Nephi through Moroni -- Christ's visit, the peace, the collapse, Mormon, Moroni, the Jaredites

D&C / Pearl of Great Price:
- Pearl of Great Price -- Moses, Abraham, Joseph Smith-History, Joseph Smith-Matthew, Articles of Faith. What's in each one
- D&C by period -- the New York revelations, Kirtland, Missouri, Nauvoo. Key sections and what they address. The learner should be able to place major sections in time and context at least roughly
- Official Declarations

### Tier 2 — Context

Where Foundations asks "what's in the scriptures?", Context asks "what was the situation?" The learner moves from knowing the parts to understanding the conditions that produced them and the distinctions that get lost when you study casually. Assumes Foundations.

Context has several distinct aspects:

- **Historical setting** -- what was happening in the world when this was written. The surrounding peoples, empires, cultures. Not just the group at hand but the full setting they were in
- **Resolving the blur** -- making distinctions where most learners have flattened different things into one fuzzy category. Assyria and Babylon were different empires with different methods and different consequences. The Pharisees and Sadducees opposed each other as much as they opposed Jesus. The apostasy wasn't one event but a process that unfolded over centuries in specific ways. "The scribes added rules to the Law" -- but what were their additions versus what was actually in the Law of Moses? Context is where the learner stops saying "those are basically the same thing" and starts seeing the real differences that matter
- **The thing as a system** -- understanding what something actually was as a defined, bounded whole. Not just "the Law of Moses included sacrifices" but what defines the Law, what are its boundaries, how did it function as a coherent system. Same for the priesthood, the Abrahamic covenant, the Nephite political system
- **Literary and editorial context** -- why a text was written the way it was, who wrote it, for whom, and what they were trying to accomplish
- **The situation behind the text** -- what specific problem or question prompted a revelation, a letter, a prophecy
- **Geography as a shaping force** -- why location mattered, not just where things were. Why Israel sat on a land bridge between empires, why the narrow neck of land was strategically critical

**Potential books in Context:**

Old Testament in context:
- The ancient Near East -- what the world looked like when Abraham lived, when Moses led the Exodus, when Israel entered Canaan
- The surrounding peoples and empires -- not just Israel in isolation, but the full setting. The Philistines, the Hittites, Egypt's control of Canaan, the rise of Assyria, the rise of Babylon, Persia. You can't understand David without knowing who the Philistines actually were. You can't understand why the prophets were so alarmed without knowing Assyria was systematically destroying nations around Israel. Studying only the group at hand without the setting they're in leaves the context incomplete
- The Assyrian and Babylonian crises -- why the prophets were saying what they were saying. And how they were different: Assyria scattered the ten tribes and repopulated the land with foreigners (creating the Samaritans). Babylon took Judah captive but kept them together and eventually let them return. Those aren't the same event
- The Law of Moses as a system -- the sacrificial system, the feasts (Passover, Tabernacles, Day of Atonement, Pentecost), the Levitical priesthood organization, the tabernacle. Not just what was in it, but what defines it, where its boundaries are, and how it functioned as a coherent whole. What was actually the Law versus what was added later by tradition
- The temple system -- Solomon's temple, its destruction, the Second Temple, Herod's temple. What happened there, how it functioned, what it meant
- The intertestamental period -- Greeks, Maccabees, Romans. The 400 years between Malachi and Matthew that most LDS members know almost nothing about
- Jewish traditions and practices that illuminate the text -- Sabbath observance, synagogue worship, rabbinic teaching methods, wedding customs, purity laws. Jesus's interactions with the Pharisees make much more sense when you understand what they were actually teaching and practicing

New Testament in context:
- The Jewish world Jesus was born into -- Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, scribes, the Sanhedrin, the temple system under Herod, Roman occupation, messianic expectations. Not lumping them all together as "the bad guys" but understanding how they differed from each other
- Each Gospel's audience and emphasis -- why Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John tell the story differently
- Paul's letters as responses to specific situations -- not abstract theology but "Corinth had this problem, so Paul said this." The letters change completely when you know the situation
- Literary forms and genres -- Hebrew poetry works through parallelism, apocalyptic literature has its own conventions, chiasmus is a deliberate structure. Knowing the form of what you're reading changes how you read it

Book of Mormon in context:
- What was happening in Jerusalem when Lehi left (connects to OT)
- The political and cultural structure of Nephite/Lamanite civilization -- the transition from kings to judges, the complexity of the Lamanite/Nephite relationship (not just "enemies" but conversions, dissidents, cultural mixing)
- Geographic relationships -- the land of Nephi, Zarahemla, Bountiful, the narrow neck. How geography shaped the narrative
- Mormon as editor -- what he chose to include, what he skipped, his editorial interjections and why they matter. Moroni's additions and what he chose to add after Mormon died
- The timeline relative to the Old World -- what was happening in Israel/Babylon/Rome at the same time as Book of Mormon events

D&C in context:
- Each revelation tied to what was happening in the Church at the time -- Section 76 in the context of the Sidney Rigdon partnership, Section 121 from Liberty Jail, Section 132 in Nauvoo
- The historical sequence of the Restoration -- what came when and why
- Key relationships -- Joseph and Sidney Rigdon, Joseph and Oliver Cowdery, Joseph and Emma. The human dynamics behind the revelations

Pearl of Great Price in context:
- The Book of Moses as part of Joseph's Bible translation project -- when he did it, what he was learning, how it shaped early Church doctrine
- The Book of Abraham -- when it was produced, the circumstances, what it contains that goes beyond Genesis

The translation context:
- What the KJV is -- a 1611 English translation with specific characteristics. What the JST is and isn't. Not to undermine the Bible but to understand the lens you're reading through
- How the Bible was compiled -- how the OT and NT canons were assembled, when, and by what process. A scriptorian should know what they're holding in their hands and how it got there

### Tier 3 — Meaning

With Foundations and Context in place, the learner is now ready to see what the scriptures actually mean. This tier is interpretive, but not in the sense of editorializing or speculating. When a learner has the foundation and the context, the meaning often becomes obvious -- it was always there, they just didn't have enough to see it yet. The best reaction is "oh, that's clearly what it's saying" not "huh, that's an interesting take."

The role of the content is not to say "here's our interpretation." It is to walk the learner through the text carefully enough, with enough foundation and context already in place, that they see it themselves. We are not adding meaning. We are removing the barriers that kept the learner from seeing the meaning that was always in the text. This keeps us aligned with the original vision: face value, not looking beyond the mark.

This is probably one of the biggest tiers. There is a lot of meaning in the scriptures that most members have never slowed down enough to really see. But the actual instruction should go relatively quickly and easily, because the first two tiers have done the heavy lifting. We don't have to explain who someone is, where they are, what was happening, or why it matters -- the learner already knows. The lessons can go straight to the text and its meaning without preamble. This is the payoff for the work of Foundations and Context.

Meaning stays within the text -- "what does this passage, this event, this teaching mean on its own terms." The next tier (Connections) is where you start pulling across standard works. You need Meaning first, because you can't connect things you don't yet understand individually. Assumes Context.

**Categories of meaning in this tier:**

- **What events teach** -- not just what happened, but what the event is designed to teach. The Exodus isn't just history -- it teaches about deliverance, about God's power, about what it costs to leave bondage. Abinadi before King Noah isn't just drama -- it teaches about prophetic duty and the cost of testimony. Many members know the stories but have never asked "what is this teaching me?"
- **What teachings teach** -- the scriptures are full of direct instruction (sermons, discourses, letters) that members have read but not really worked through. What is Jesus actually saying in the Sermon on the Mount, line by line? What is Paul actually arguing in Romans 8? Most members have a general sense ("faith is like a seed") but haven't traced the argument carefully enough to really understand it
- **What prophecies mean** -- not speculative "what will happen in the last days" but what did the prophet actually say, and what does the text itself tell us it means? Many prophecies have their own built-in interpretation (Daniel interprets his own visions, Nephi interprets Lehi's dream, the Lord explains parables). The meaning is often right there if you read carefully
- **What ordinances and rituals mean** -- the sacrament prayers, baptismal covenants, priesthood ordinances have specific words that carry specific meaning. What does each word and phrase in the sacrament prayer actually mean? Most members have never slowed down enough to work through it word by word
- **What patterns mean** -- the scriptures are full of recurring patterns: pride cycles, covenant-breaking-and-renewal, prophetic calls that follow a pattern, apostasy and restoration. At the Meaning level, you understand what these patterns teach within a single book or era. (At the Connections level, you trace them across standard works)
- **What names and symbols mean** -- scripture is dense with symbolic language that has specific meaning. Names that carry meaning (Israel, Melchizedek, Moroni), numbers that recur (7, 12, 40), images that reappear (water, fire, mountains, wilderness). Not speculative symbol-hunting, but the meanings the text itself establishes
- **What the hard sayings mean** -- every standard work has passages that members read and skip past. "The natural man is an enemy to God." "By grace ye are saved, after all we can do." "The letter killeth, but the spirit giveth life." These get quoted constantly but rarely understood carefully. Meaning is where you actually work through what they're saying
- **What the small details mean** -- the scriptures reward careful reading. Why does Mormon mention that Moroni "did not delight in bloodshed"? Why does John record that "it was night" when Judas left the Last Supper? These aren't accidents -- the authors chose their words. Meaning is where you start noticing

**What does NOT belong in this tier:**

- Cross-standard-work analysis -- that's Connections
- Highly disputed or speculative interpretations -- that violates the "face value" principle
- Gospel topics organized thematically -- this tier should follow the text, not reorganize it by topic

**Potential books in Meaning:**

Old Testament:
- What Abraham and Isaac actually teaches about God, obedience, and sacrifice -- not just "it happened" but what it means
- The prophets' teachings -- not just who they were and when they lived (Foundations) or what crisis they were responding to (Context) but what they were actually saying and what it means. What is Hosea teaching about God's relationship with Israel? What is Amos saying about justice?
- The Psalms as theology -- what they teach about God's nature, about suffering, about praise, about the Messiah
- The meaning embedded in the Law of Moses -- not just what the feasts and sacrifices were (Context) but what they were designed to teach

New Testament:
- The Sermon on the Mount worked through carefully -- what the Beatitudes mean, what "fulfilling the law" means, what Jesus is actually asking
- What Paul is actually arguing in Romans, Galatians, Hebrews -- not just who he was writing to (Context) but what his arguments mean
- The parables -- not just "Jesus told stories" but what each one teaches and why he chose that form
- The "I Am" statements in John -- what Jesus is claiming and why it mattered
- What "endure to the end" actually means in context

Book of Mormon:
- Lehi's dream as more than a flannel board story -- what it actually teaches
- King Benjamin's address -- what he's teaching about the natural man, the Atonement, the covenant he's making
- Alma on faith (Alma 32) -- what the analogy of the seed really means, step by step
- Alma on justice and mercy (Alma 42) -- the doctrine embedded in the text
- 2 Nephi 2 -- what Lehi is teaching about the Fall, opposition, and agency
- What Mormon wants you to learn -- his editorial comments aren't random; they carry meaning

D&C:
- The doctrine embedded in the sacrament prayers, word by word
- What D&C 93 is actually teaching about intelligence, light, truth, and progression
- The oath and covenant of the priesthood (D&C 84) -- what it means and what it requires

Pearl of Great Price:
- Moses 1 -- what Moses's vision of God teaches about man's relationship to God
- The Book of Abraham's teachings on the pre-earth council and the nature of intelligences

### Tier 4 — Connections

Cross-referencing across all four standard works. How does the Abrahamic covenant thread from Genesis through the Book of Mormon through D&C? How do temple themes appear everywhere? How does Alma 42 illuminate Romans? This is where the LDS perspective really shines -- four witnesses that illuminate each other. Typology lives here too (events that foreshadow Christ). Assumes Meaning.

**Potential books in Connections:**

Covenant threads:
- The Abrahamic covenant from Genesis through the Book of Mormon through D&C -- one continuous thread
- The covenant pattern: God offers, people accept, blessings/cursings follow. How it repeats everywhere

Typology:
- How OT events prefigure Christ -- the Passover, the brazen serpent, Abraham and Isaac, the Day of Atonement
- How Book of Mormon prophets explicitly taught typology (Nephi, Abinadi, Alma)

Parallel passages:
- Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7) vs. 3 Nephi 12-14 -- what's the same, what's different, what the differences tell you
- Isaiah chapters that appear in both the OT and the Book of Mormon
- Malachi in the OT, in 3 Nephi, and in D&C

Doctrinal threads across standard works:
- What the scriptures teach about the nature of God -- pulling from all four standard works together
- The doctrine of the Fall across Genesis, Moses, 2 Nephi, Alma
- The Atonement as taught across all four standard works -- not a gospel-topics treatment but "what does each scriptural author actually say about it?"

Temple in scripture:
- Temple themes, imagery, and language across all four standard works

### Tier 5 — Mastery

The hardest material and the integration of everything. Isaiah in the Book of Mormon cross-referenced with the OT originals. Revelation. The JST and what it changes. The most complex doctrinal passages. The ability to see the scriptures as a unified whole -- not just knowing the parts, but understanding the whole. Assumes everything below it.

**Potential books in Mastery:**

- Isaiah -- full, deep treatment chapter by chapter. The poetry, the layers, the messianic passages, the historical passages, and why Nephi and Christ both said to study Isaiah
- Revelation -- working through the actual text with all the OT and D&C cross-references needed to make sense of it
- The JST -- systematic study of what Joseph Smith changed and what it reveals. Not cherry-picked highlights but a thorough treatment
- The most complex doctrinal passages -- D&C 76, 84, 88, 93; D&C 121, 130, 131, 132; Jacob 5 (allegory of the olive tree); Alma 12-13 (Melchizedek, foreordination, the mysteries); Alma 40-42 (justice, mercy, resurrection, restoration); 2 Nephi 2 (the Fall and opposition)
- Ezekiel and Daniel -- prophetic visions that require OT context, NT awareness, and D&C connections to fully appreciate
- The scriptures as a unified whole -- the capstone. Seeing the entire scriptural canon as one continuous revelation from the pre-earth council through the Millennium

### Notes on tiers

- The progression follows the pattern found in every serious religious education system (Yeshiva, Catholic seminary, MDiv programs) and maps to the classical Trivium (Grammar → Logic → Rhetoric) and Bloom's Taxonomy: absorb the material, then reason about it, then synthesize.
- The LDS educational system (Seminary/Institute) is a flat rotation with no progression. Scriptorian fills that gap.
- 5 tiers is a starting point, not a hard limit. More tiers may emerge as the content takes shape -- don't be afraid to add them if the material calls for it.
- Within each tier, learners have freedom to choose which books to study and in what order. The tier structure provides progression; the books within each tier provide choice.
