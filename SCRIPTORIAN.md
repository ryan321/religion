# Scriptorian

A future site for LDS adults who want to become deeply knowledgeable in the scriptures.

## The Problem

There used to be a tradition of thorough, systematic scripture study in LDS culture -- Cleon Skousen's *Thousand Years* series (First, Third, Fourth, Fifth), Joseph Fielding Smith's doctrinal works -- that walked you through the scriptures themselves in depth, from an LDS understanding, without looking beyond the mark. That tradition has largely died out. Nothing has replaced it.

What exists now tends to be:

- **Topical** -- gospel topics with scripture support, rather than the scriptures themselves
- **Speculative** -- novel interpretations, particular views on the last days, new ways to view a topic
- **Devotional** -- conference talks, Come Follow Me, short-form content

None of those are the same as actually knowing the scriptures deeply. The Skousen and Joseph Fielding Smith books are out of print, two generations old, and there is really nothing like them now.

## The Vision

Help LDS adults who want to become scriptorians -- people with deep, thorough knowledge of the actual text of the scriptures.

- **The scriptures themselves**, not gospel topics, not commentary-heavy interpretation
- **Face value** -- what the scriptures actually say, not esoteric or novel readings
- **LDS understanding** -- Book of Mormon, D&C, Pearl of Great Price, and the Bible read together as a unified witness
- **Thorough and in-depth** -- not devotional, not surface-level
- **Series of books** that systematically cover the scriptures

## The Conviction

While pure religion is visiting the sick and afflicted, and it is more important to live a good Christian life than to study the scriptures, there is a place and a need for some people to become very knowledgeable about the scriptures. From great knowledge of the scriptures should come increased understanding of truth and the gospel -- not just of what the scriptures say, but of the gospel itself. But it is very hard to have a great understanding of the gospel without having a great knowledge of the scriptures.

The goal is not to replace living the gospel. The goal is to serve those who want depth.

## The Name

**Scriptorian** -- an LDS-familiar term for someone deeply knowledgeable in the scriptures.

## Inspiration

- Cleon Skousen: *The First 2000 Years*, *The Third Thousand Years*, *The Fourth Thousand Years*, *The Fifth Thousand Years*
- Joseph Fielding Smith: *Doctrines of Salvation*, *Answers to Gospel Questions*, etc.

These authors gave thorough, faithful, scripture-grounded treatments without looking beyond the mark. The knowledge gained from them was excellent. That is the standard.

## Structure: Tiered Progression

The site is organized as a tiered progression, not a buffet of disconnected books. Learners move through tiers, with freedom to choose their order within each tier.

### Tier 1 — Foundations

The first tier. Covers the factual and narrative landscape of the scriptures: people, places, times, events, and key doctrines across all four standard works.

- **Factual and informational** -- who, what, when, where. Building the mental map that everything else sits on.
- **Comprehensive, not selective** -- covers all of it, including the parts most people skip. A scriptorian needs to know the minor prophets, the later D&C sections, the war chapters, Jacob's allegory.
- **Not shallow, but not interpretive** -- facts are connected and contextualized, but the goal is knowledge of what's in the scriptures, not novel readings or deep doctrinal analysis. That comes in later tiers.
- **The foundation for everything that follows** -- later tiers assume this knowledge. Without it, deeper study falls flat.
- **Common scriptural and Christian terminology** -- the learner should come out knowing the basic terms used in Christian parlance beyond LDS culture. For example, "Pentateuch," "synoptic Gospels," "Pauline epistles," "Septuagint," "apocrypha," "eschatology," etc. LDS members often don't encounter these terms at church but a scriptorian should know them. Not everything the outside world uses, but at least the basics.

- **Heavy on names, facts, and details** -- the learner needs to know who Raphael was, even if there's not much on him. Who was Rahab? Who was Abish? Who were the men named Moroni? How many Nephis are there? Foundations requires learning a large volume of names, places, and facts. This is the Grammar stage of the Trivium -- it may be the least "fun" tier, but you just have to learn it. The reward comes in later tiers when all of this knowledge makes deeper study possible.
- **Strong sense of place and time** -- just like with history, if you don't know when and where the Romans and Greeks lived you are going to be severely hampered. The learner needs to know when the Kingdom of Israel split, when the Babylonian captivity happened, where Babylon was, which prophetic books were written before, during, and after the exile. They need to know where Palmyra is and when Joseph Smith was there, where Kirtland and Far West and Nauvoo are. They need to know where Zarahemla was relative to the land of Nephi, where King Noah was, when Alma lived relative to Captain Moroni. Time and place are the scaffolding that holds everything else together -- without them, people and events float unanchored.
- **Structure and organization of the scriptures** -- how many books are in the Old Testament, that Psalms is really five collections, that the Book of Mormon is mostly Mormon's abridgment but some books (Nephi's, Moroni's) are original, that D&C isn't chronologically ordered throughout. The learner should know how the scriptures are put together -- what's in each volume and how it's organized.
- **Basic authorship** -- who wrote each book and who is speaking. Moses wrote the Pentateuch. Mormon abridged most of the Book of Mormon but Moroni finished it and added his own material plus the Jaredite record. Paul wrote Romans. Some D&C sections are Joseph, some are the Lord speaking directly. Knowing who is talking in any given passage is foundational.
- **The narrative arc** -- beyond isolated facts, the learner should be able to tell the story from beginning to end. The grand throughline from the pre-earth council through the Second Coming as told across all four standard works. Not just "I know who Gideon is" but "I know where Gideon fits in the story."
- **Key passages and where to find them** -- a scriptorian should know where things are. Where is the Sermon on the Mount? Where is Alma's discourse on faith? Where is the vision of the degrees of glory? Where is Lehi's teaching on the Fall? Not memorized word for word, but "I know where to find it and what's in it."
- **How to use the study tools** -- the LDS scriptures come with a system: footnotes, cross-references, the Topical Guide, Bible Dictionary, JST appendix, chapter headings, the index to the triple combination. A scriptorian uses these constantly. Most members barely touch them. This is a practical skill that belongs in Foundations.

The person who finishes Foundations knows what's in the scriptures -- all the people, places, events, timelines, and key doctrines -- even if they haven't yet gone deep on any one part.

**Potential books in Foundations:**

Old Testament:
- The Pentateuch -- Creation, Fall, Flood, patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph), Exodus, Sinai, the Law, the wilderness
- Historical books -- Joshua and the conquest, the judges, the united monarchy (Saul, David, Solomon), the divided kingdom, exile to Babylon, return under Ezra/Nehemiah
- The prophets -- who each one was, when they prophesied, what kingdom they were in, what their core message was. At this level it's "know who Hosea is and when he fits" not "deeply understand Hosea"
- Wisdom literature -- Job, Psalms, Proverbs, Ecclesiastes. What's in them, basic themes

New Testament:
- The Gospels -- life of Christ from birth through resurrection, harmonized across all four accounts
- Acts -- the apostolic church, Paul's conversion and missions
- The Epistles -- who Paul was writing to and why, the general epistles, basic content of each letter
- Revelation -- overview of structure and major visions, not deep interpretation

Book of Mormon:
- Lehi through Mosiah -- the departure, the split, the kings, Benjamin, Abinadi, Alma's conversion
- Alma through Helaman -- the judges, the wars, the missionary accounts, the Gadianton robbers
- 3 Nephi through Moroni -- Christ's visit, the peace, the collapse, Mormon, Moroni, the Jaredites

D&C / Pearl of Great Price:
- Pearl of Great Price -- Moses, Abraham, Joseph Smith-History, Joseph Smith-Matthew, Articles of Faith. What's in each one
- D&C by period -- the New York revelations, Kirtland, Missouri, Nauvoo. Key sections and what they address. The learner should be able to place major sections in time and context at least roughly
- Official Declarations

### Tier 2 — Context

Go deeper into each scriptural book on its own terms. Historical setting, what was happening when a revelation was given, literary structure, what the author/prophet was trying to accomplish. This is where D&C sections come alive because you know what was happening in Kirtland or Liberty Jail. Isaiah makes sense because you know the Assyrian crisis. The Sermon on the Mount hits different when you understand what the Pharisees were teaching. Assumes Foundations.

**Potential books in Context:**

Old Testament in context:
- The ancient Near East -- what the world looked like when Abraham lived, when Moses led the Exodus, when Israel entered Canaan
- The Assyrian and Babylonian crises -- why the prophets were saying what they were saying
- The intertestamental period -- Greeks, Maccabees, Romans. The 400 years between Malachi and Matthew

New Testament in context:
- The Jewish world Jesus was born into -- Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes, the temple system, Roman occupation
- Each Gospel's audience and emphasis -- why Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John tell the story differently
- Paul's letters as responses to specific situations -- Corinth's problems, Galatia's confusion about the Law, Rome as a church he hadn't visited yet

Book of Mormon in context:
- What was happening in Jerusalem when Lehi left (connects to OT)
- The political and cultural structure of Nephite/Lamanite civilization
- Mormon as editor -- what he chose to include, what he skipped, his editorial comments and why they matter

D&C in context:
- Each revelation tied to what was happening in the Church at the time -- Section 76 in the context of the Sidney Rigdon partnership, Section 121 from Liberty Jail, Section 132 in Nauvoo
- The historical sequence of the Restoration -- what came when and why

Pearl of Great Price in context:
- The Book of Moses as JST Genesis -- what Joseph was doing when he received it
- The Book of Abraham -- when it was produced, what it contains, how it relates to Genesis

### Tier 3 — Connections

Cross-referencing across all four standard works. How does the Abrahamic covenant thread from Genesis through the Book of Mormon through D&C? How do temple themes appear everywhere? How does Alma 42 illuminate Romans? This is where the LDS perspective really shines -- four witnesses that illuminate each other. Typology lives here too (events that foreshadow Christ). Assumes Context.

**Potential books in Connections:**

Covenant threads:
- The Abrahamic covenant from Genesis through the Book of Mormon through D&C -- one continuous thread
- The covenant pattern: God offers, people accept, blessings/cursings follow. How it repeats everywhere

Typology:
- How OT events prefigure Christ -- the Passover, the brazen serpent, Abraham and Isaac, the Day of Atonement
- How Book of Mormon prophets explicitly taught typology (Nephi, Abinadi, Alma)

Parallel passages:
- Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7) vs. 3 Nephi 12-14 -- what's the same, what's different, what the differences tell you
- Isaiah chapters that appear in both the OT and the Book of Mormon
- Malachi in the OT, in 3 Nephi, and in D&C

Doctrinal threads across standard works:
- What the scriptures teach about the nature of God -- pulling from all four standard works together
- The doctrine of the Fall across Genesis, Moses, 2 Nephi, Alma
- The Atonement as taught across all four standard works -- not a gospel-topics treatment but "what does each scriptural author actually say about it?"

Temple in scripture:
- Temple themes, imagery, and language across all four standard works

### Tier 4 — Mastery

The hardest material and the integration of everything. Isaiah in the Book of Mormon cross-referenced with the OT originals. Revelation. The JST and what it changes. The most complex doctrinal passages. The ability to see the scriptures as a unified whole -- not just knowing the parts, but understanding the whole. Assumes everything below it.

**Potential books in Mastery:**

- Isaiah -- full, deep treatment chapter by chapter. The poetry, the layers, the messianic passages, the historical passages, and why Nephi and Christ both said to study Isaiah
- Revelation -- working through the actual text with all the OT and D&C cross-references needed to make sense of it
- The JST -- systematic study of what Joseph Smith changed and what it reveals. Not cherry-picked highlights but a thorough treatment
- The most complex doctrinal passages -- D&C 76, 84, 88, 93; D&C 121, 130, 131, 132; Jacob 5 (allegory of the olive tree); Alma 12-13 (Melchizedek, foreordination, the mysteries); Alma 40-42 (justice, mercy, resurrection, restoration); 2 Nephi 2 (the Fall and opposition)
- Ezekiel and Daniel -- prophetic visions that require OT context, NT awareness, and D&C connections to fully appreciate
- The scriptures as a unified whole -- the capstone. Seeing the entire scriptural canon as one continuous revelation from the pre-earth council through the Millennium

### Notes on tiers

- The progression follows the pattern found in every serious religious education system (Yeshiva, Catholic seminary, MDiv programs) and maps to the classical Trivium (Grammar → Logic → Rhetoric) and Bloom's Taxonomy: absorb the material, then reason about it, then synthesize.
- The LDS educational system (Seminary/Institute) is a flat rotation with no progression. Scriptorian fills that gap.
- 4 tiers is a starting point, not a hard limit. More tiers may emerge as the content takes shape -- don't be afraid to add them if the material calls for it.
- Within each tier, learners have freedom to choose which books to study and in what order. The tier structure provides progression; the books within each tier provide choice.

## Technical Approach

Same platform as Gospel 4 Kids and Best Subjects -- Next.js, AI tutor, live workbooks, graded exercises. But the content, tone, depth, and audience are entirely different: LDS adults, not children.
