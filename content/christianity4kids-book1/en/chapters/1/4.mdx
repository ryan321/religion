### Lesson 1.4 -- A Church Under Pressure

Imagine you're part of a brand-new church that's growing fast. People are excited, miracles are happening, and thousands are being baptized. But not everyone is happy about it. From the very beginning, the Church Jesus built faced serious pressure, both from the outside and from within.

**External persecution** started almost immediately. The Jewish leaders in Jerusalem didn't like the apostles preaching that Jesus was the Messiah. Peter and John were arrested and threatened (Acts 4:1-3). But the apostles refused to stop teaching. They said, "We ought to obey God rather than men" (Acts 5:29).

Then things got even more dangerous. A faithful believer named **Stephen** became the first Christian **martyr**, someone who dies for their faith. Stephen boldly testified of Jesus before the Jewish council. When they rejected his message, he said he could see heaven opened and Jesus standing at the right hand of God. This made the leaders so angry that they dragged Stephen out of the city and **stoned him to death** (Acts 7:54-60). Even as he died, Stephen prayed for those who were killing him, just like Jesus had done on the cross.

But the Church didn't just face problems from the outside. There were also **internal disagreements**. As the Church grew and spread to new places, questions came up that nobody had faced before. One of the biggest questions was this: Did non-Jewish people (called **Gentiles**) who wanted to join the Church need to follow all the Jewish laws, like circumcision and dietary rules?

This was a huge debate. Some members said yes, absolutely. Others said no. How did they settle it? They held what we call the **Jerusalem Council** (Acts 15). The apostles and elders gathered together, discussed the issue, and (most importantly) sought **revelation** from God. Peter shared a vision he had received. James, the brother of Jesus and leader of the Jerusalem church, helped summarize the decision. Together, guided by the Holy Ghost, they reached an answer that they sent out to all the churches.

This is a powerful example of how the Church was supposed to work. When disagreements arose, the apostles didn't just argue or vote. They turned to God for answers. As long as there were **living apostles** with priesthood authority and the gift of revelation, the Church could stay on track.

But what would happen if those apostles were taken away? That's the question we'll be exploring in the chapters ahead.
