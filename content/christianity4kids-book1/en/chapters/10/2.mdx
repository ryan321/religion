### Lesson 10.2 -- Cathedrals and Devotion

Have you ever seen a picture of a massive old church with towers reaching toward the sky and windows glowing with colored light? Those buildings are called **cathedrals**, and they tell us something important about what people believed during the Middle Ages.

Even though the priesthood and true authority were no longer on the earth, millions of people across Europe **sincerely loved God**. They wanted to worship Him, serve Him, and feel close to Him. One of the most amazing ways they showed this devotion was by building enormous, beautiful cathedrals.

Think about how long it takes to build a house -- maybe a year or two. Now imagine a building project that lasted **over a hundred years**. That's how long it took to build some of the great cathedrals like **Notre-Dame** in Paris, **Chartres** Cathedral in France, and **Canterbury** Cathedral in England. Entire communities -- grandparents, parents, children, and grandchildren -- poured their labor, money, and love into these structures. Many workers who started building a cathedral knew they would never live to see it finished. They built it anyway, as an offering to God.

The cathedrals were designed to make people feel the greatness of God. Their ceilings soared so high you could barely see them. Stained glass windows told stories from the Bible in brilliant colors. Every carved stone and painted wall was meant to teach and inspire.

**Pilgrimages** were another way people showed their faith. Ordinary men and women would travel **hundreds of miles** on foot to visit holy places -- a cathedral that held a relic, the city of Rome, or even Jerusalem itself. These journeys were difficult and dangerous, but pilgrims believed they brought them closer to God.

During this time, great **scholars** also studied and wrote about God. **Thomas Aquinas** was one of the most important. He spent his life trying to understand and explain Christian beliefs using reason and logic. His writings influenced the Church for centuries.

As Latter-day Saints, we can look at medieval Christians with **respect and compassion**. They didn't have the fullness of the gospel or priesthood authority, but many of them loved the Savior deeply and did their best to follow Him. God sees the hearts of all His children, and the devotion of these people was real and meaningful. They were proof that even during the **Apostasy**, the light of Christ never completely went out.
