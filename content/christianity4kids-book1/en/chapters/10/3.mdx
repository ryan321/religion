### Lesson 10.3 -- Indulgences and Corruption

Imagine you did something wrong and your parent said, "You're grounded for a week." But then someone told you, "Give me five dollars and I'll get your punishment taken away." Would that seem right? That's basically what **indulgences** were, and they became one of the biggest problems in the medieval Church.

The Catholic Church taught that when people sinned, they needed to do **penance**, acts of prayer, fasting, or good works to make up for what they'd done. Over time, Church leaders developed the idea that the Pope held a **"treasury of merit"**, a kind of spiritual bank account filled with the extra goodness of Jesus, Mary, and the saints. The Pope could supposedly draw from this treasury and reduce or cancel someone's punishment for sin.

At first, indulgences were connected to acts of devotion. But eventually, the Church started **selling them for money**. People were told they could buy forgiveness, not just for themselves, but even for dead relatives stuck in **purgatory** (a place the Church said souls went to be purified before heaven). One famous seller named **Johann Tetzel** had a catchy saying: "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs!"

Think about how wrong that is. Jesus taught that forgiveness comes through **repentance** and His grace, not through paying money to a church leader. But many people didn't know that because they couldn't read the Bible for themselves. It was only available in Latin, which most ordinary people didn't understand.

The corruption went beyond indulgences. Many **Church leaders lived in luxury**, wearing fine clothes, feasting at enormous banquets, and building grand palaces. Some bishops and even popes cared more about **political power** than about caring for people's souls. Positions in the Church were sometimes sold to the highest bidder, and some leaders held multiple positions just to collect the income.

This corruption slowly **eroded people's trust** in the Church. Ordinary believers who were trying their best to follow God could see that something was deeply wrong. How could leaders who lived like kings claim to represent Jesus, who was born in a stable and washed His disciples' feet?

From a Latter-day Saint perspective, this corruption shows why God didn't simply reform the existing church. The problems went so deep (the priesthood authority was gone, and the teachings had been changed so much) that only a complete **restoration** could fix things.
