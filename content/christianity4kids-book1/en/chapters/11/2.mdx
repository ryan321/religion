### Lesson 11.2 -- John Wycliffe

Imagine living in a world where the most important book ever written exists, but it's in a language you can't read. That was the reality for English-speaking people in the **1300s**. The Bible was only available in **Latin**, and only priests and scholars could understand it. One man decided to change that, and it nearly cost him everything.

**John Wycliffe** was an English scholar and professor at **Oxford University**, one of the most respected schools in the world. He was brilliant, well-educated, and deeply devoted to understanding scripture. The more he studied the Bible, the more troubled he became by what he saw in the Church around him.

Wycliffe criticized the Church's **wealth and luxury**. He argued that church leaders should live simply, as Jesus and the apostles had lived. He spoke out against **indulgences**, the practice of selling forgiveness for money. And he made a truly bold claim: he said the **Pope** was not the supreme authority over Christians. Instead, Wycliffe declared that the **Bible itself** should be the highest authority in matters of faith.

But Wycliffe's most revolutionary act was translating the **entire Bible into English** for the first time. He believed every person had the right to read God's word in their own language. He and his assistants spent years producing a complete English translation so that farmers, merchants, and tradespeople could read the scriptures for themselves.

The Church was furious. Officials **condemned** Wycliffe's teachings and tried to silence him. Powerful friends in England protected him during his lifetime, so he died of natural causes in **1384**. But the Church wasn't finished with him. Decades after his death, Church leaders were still so angry that they ordered his **bones dug up and burned**, then scattered the ashes in a river. They wanted to destroy even his memory.

They failed. Wycliffe's ideas **survived and spread**. His followers, called **Lollards**, carried on his work. They copied his English Bible by hand and shared it across England, often in secret. The Lollards faced persecution (some were arrested and even executed), but they kept spreading the scriptures.

Latter-day Saints honor Wycliffe as someone God used to prepare the way. He didn't have authority to restore the Church, but his insistence that people deserve to read the Bible for themselves was a crucial step toward the day when Joseph Smith would read James 1:5 and walk into the Sacred Grove.
