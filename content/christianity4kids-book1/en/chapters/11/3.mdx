### Lesson 11.3 -- Jan Hus

What would you do if someone promised you would be safe, and then broke that promise and tried to kill you? That's the tragic story of **Jan Hus**, a Czech priest whose courage and faith made him one of the most important reformers before the Reformation.

Jan Hus was born in the kingdom of **Bohemia** (today's Czech Republic) in the late 1300s. He became a popular **priest and preacher** in Prague, the capital city. Hus was inspired by the writings of **John Wycliffe**, the English scholar who had translated the Bible and criticized Church corruption. Hus read Wycliffe's works and agreed with many of his ideas.

Like Wycliffe, Hus **preached boldly against corruption** in the Church. He condemned indulgences, the selling of church positions, and the extravagant lifestyles of bishops and popes. He declared that the **Bible**, not the Pope, was the ultimate authority for Christians. He preached in the **Czech language** instead of Latin so ordinary people could understand. The people of Prague loved him, but Church leaders were alarmed.

In **1414**, Hus was summoned to the **Council of Constance**, a major meeting of Church leaders in Germany, to explain his beliefs. The Holy Roman Emperor **Sigismund** gave Hus a written guarantee of **safe passage**, promising he would not be harmed. Trusting this promise, Hus made the long journey to Constance.

It was a trap. Shortly after arriving, Hus was **arrested and thrown into prison**. The council put him on trial for heresy. They demanded he take back everything he had taught. Hus refused. He said he could not deny what he believed the Bible taught, unless someone could show him from scripture that he was wrong. No one could.

On **July 6, 1415**, Jan Hus was **burned alive at the stake**. He reportedly prayed and sang hymns as the flames rose around him. He was only about forty-four years old.

But his death didn't stop his movement. His followers, called **Hussites**, rose up in Bohemia and fought for decades to defend their right to worship as Hus had taught. The Hussite movement showed that the desire for reform could not be silenced by killing one man.

For Latter-day Saints, Jan Hus is a hero of conscience. He was willing to **die rather than deny truth** as he understood it. God was working through people like Hus, stirring hearts, challenging corruption, and preparing the world for the day when the fullness of the gospel would be restored.
