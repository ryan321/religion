### Lesson 12.1 -- Martin Luther

Can you imagine being so frustrated with something that you write a long list of complaints and nail it to a door? That's exactly what a German monk named **Martin Luther** did on **October 31, 1517**, and it changed the world forever.

Luther was an **Augustinian monk** and a professor of theology at the University of Wittenberg in Germany. He was deeply devoted to God and spent years studying the Bible. Like Wycliffe and Hus before him, the more Luther studied scripture, the more troubled he became by what he saw in the Church.

What finally pushed Luther over the edge was **indulgences**, the practice of selling forgiveness for money. A Dominican friar named **Johann Tetzel** was traveling through Germany selling indulgences with the slogan, "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs!" Tetzel claimed that people could buy their dead relatives out of purgatory, a place the Church said souls went to be purified before entering heaven. Luther was disgusted.

Luther sat down and wrote a document listing **95 problems** (called the **95 Theses**) with the Church's teaching and practice of indulgences. On October 31, he nailed this document to the door of the **Castle Church** in Wittenberg. In those days, church doors served as community bulletin boards, so this was like posting a public challenge for debate.

Here's what's important to understand: Luther **didn't intend to start a new church**. He was a loyal Catholic who wanted to **reform the Church from within**. He thought that if he pointed out the problems clearly enough, Church leaders would see the errors and fix them. He believed the Pope might not even know how badly indulgences were being abused.

But that's not what happened. Instead of listening, Church leaders pushed back hard. And Luther, rather than backing down, pushed back even harder. What started as a call for reform quickly became a **revolution** that would split Western Christianity in two.

Sometimes in history, a single act by a single person at just the right moment changes everything. Luther's 95 Theses were that act. The Reformation had begun, and nothing would ever be the same.
