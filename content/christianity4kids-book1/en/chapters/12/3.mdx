### Lesson 12.3 -- The Printing Press Changes Everything

What if Martin Luther had nailed his 95 Theses to the church door and nobody ever heard about it? That could easily have happened. After all, Wycliffe and Hus had said similar things, and the Church had managed to silence them. So what was different this time? The answer is one of the most important inventions in human history: the **printing press**.

Around **1440**, a German inventor named **Johannes Gutenberg** developed a printing press with **movable type**. Before Gutenberg, every book in Europe had to be copied by hand, usually by monks working in monasteries. A single Bible could take **months or even years** to produce. Books were incredibly rare and expensive, and most people never owned one.

Gutenberg's press changed everything. Suddenly, hundreds of copies of a book could be printed in the time it used to take to write just one. Books became **cheaper and more available**. More people learned to read because there was actually something for them to read. Information could spread faster than ever before.

When Luther posted his 95 Theses in 1517, someone quickly printed copies. Within **weeks**, Luther's ideas had spread across Germany. Within **months**, they reached every corner of Europe. The Church couldn't burn every copy fast enough. For the first time in history, **ideas traveled faster than the authorities could suppress them**.

Luther himself became one of the most published authors of his era. His pamphlets, sermons, and Bible translation rolled off printing presses by the thousands. Other reformers used the press too, spreading their own ideas and criticisms of the Church. The printing press turned what might have been a small local dispute into a **continent-wide revolution**.

The printing press also made it possible to put the **Bible directly into people's hands**. Luther's German Bible, Wycliffe's English Bible, and eventually many other translations could be printed and distributed widely. For the first time, ordinary Christians could read the scriptures and decide for themselves what God's word actually said.

Latter-day Saints believe that **God inspired the invention of the printing press**. It wasn't a coincidence that this technology appeared just before the Reformation. God was preparing the world, making sure that when the time came, truth could spread freely. The same technology that carried Luther's ideas across Europe would eventually help spread the **Book of Mormon** and the restored gospel to the entire world.
