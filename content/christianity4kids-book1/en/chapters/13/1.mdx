### Lesson 13.1 -- John Calvin and Geneva

Martin Luther started the Reformation, but he wasn't the only one who wanted to change how people worshipped God. A French lawyer named **John Calvin** became one of the most influential Protestant leaders in history, and his ideas still shape many Christian churches today.

Calvin was born in France in 1509. As a young man, he studied law and theology. When he became convinced that the Catholic Church had strayed from the Bible, he joined the Protestant movement. But France was dangerous for Protestants, so Calvin fled to the city of **Geneva, Switzerland**, where he would spend most of his life building a new kind of religious community.

Calvin agreed with Luther on many things: that salvation comes through faith, that the Bible is the ultimate authority, and that the Pope didn't have the right to lead all Christians. But Calvin added his own important idea: **predestination**. This is the belief that God, being all-knowing and all-powerful, has already decided who will be saved and who won't, even before they are born. People can't earn salvation or choose it. God already chose.

This might sound strange to Latter-day Saints, because we believe in **agency**, the ability to make our own choices. We believe God wants everyone to return to Him, and that our choices matter. But Calvin believed God's power was so complete that even salvation was His decision alone.

In Geneva, Calvin created a very **strict religious community**. Church leaders watched over people's behavior closely. Dancing, card games, and fancy clothing were discouraged or banned. Everyone was expected to attend church and live according to Calvin's interpretation of the Bible. The city became almost like a religious experiment, a place where every part of life was shaped by Calvin's teachings.

Calvin's ideas spread rapidly. His followers in France were called **Huguenots**. In Scotland, his ideas became the foundation of **Presbyterianism**. In England, people influenced by Calvin became known as **Puritans**, and many of them would eventually sail to America seeking religious freedom. Calvin's teachings also spread to the Netherlands, Hungary, and parts of Germany.

John Calvin died in 1564, but his influence was enormous. He trained pastors, wrote books, and built a system of church government that churches still use today. His ideas about God's power, strict moral living, and Bible study shaped Protestant Christianity across Europe and eventually across the Atlantic Ocean to the New World.

Calvin was sincere and devoted to God. But without priesthood authority or living prophets, even the most careful Bible study couldn't restore what had been lost in the Apostasy.
