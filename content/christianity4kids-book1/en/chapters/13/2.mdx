### Lesson 13.2 -- Other Reformers

Martin Luther and John Calvin are the most famous Protestant reformers, but they weren't alone. Across Europe, other bold leaders were reading the Bible for themselves and deciding that the Church needed to change. Each brought new ideas -- and each faced serious danger for speaking up.

In **Zurich, Switzerland**, a priest named **Huldrych Zwingli** began preaching reform around the same time as Luther. Zwingli believed the Bible should be the only guide for Christian life. He removed statues and images from churches, simplified worship services, and argued that the bread and wine used in communion were only **symbols** of Christ's body and blood -- not the actual body and blood, as Catholics taught. Zwingli and Luther actually disagreed about this, which shows that even reformers couldn't agree on what the Bible meant. Zwingli was killed in battle in 1531 when Catholic and Protestant regions of Switzerland went to war.

Another group took reform even further. The **Anabaptists** believed that baptism should only happen when a person was old enough to understand and choose it -- as an adult, by **immersion** (going completely under the water). They rejected infant baptism entirely. They also believed strongly in the **separation of church and state** -- the idea that government and religion should stay out of each other's business.

These ideas might sound reasonable today, but in the 1500s they were considered dangerous and radical. Both **Catholics and Protestants** persecuted the Anabaptists. They were arrested, tortured, drowned, and burned at the stake. It's a sad reminder that even people who wanted religious reform didn't always support religious freedom for everyone.

In **Scotland**, a fiery preacher named **John Knox** brought Calvin's ideas across the border from Geneva. Knox was bold, outspoken, and fearless. He challenged the Catholic queen of Scotland and helped establish **Presbyterianism** as the national religion. Presbyterian churches are governed by groups of elected elders rather than bishops or a pope -- an idea that came directly from Calvin's system of church government.

The Reformation was now spreading in many directions. Lutherans in Germany and Scandinavia, Calvinists in Switzerland and France, Presbyterians in Scotland, Anabaptists scattered across Europe -- all reading the Bible, all sincere, and all disagreeing with each other about what it meant.

From a Latter-day Saint perspective, this is exactly what you'd expect when people try to interpret the Bible without prophets or revelation to guide them. Good people, honest effort -- but no authority from God to settle the questions.
