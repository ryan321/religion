### Lesson 13.3 -- Henry VIII and the Church of England

Not every part of the Reformation was about theology or getting closer to God. Sometimes it was about **power**. The story of King Henry VIII of England is a perfect example.

Henry VIII became king of England in 1509. He was Catholic and actually defended the Pope against Martin Luther's criticisms. The Pope even gave Henry the title "Defender of the Faith." But Henry had a big problem: he wanted a **divorce**.

Henry was married to Catherine of Aragon, but she hadn't given him a son to inherit the throne. Henry wanted to marry a younger woman named Anne Boleyn, hoping she would give him a male heir. But the Catholic Church didn't allow divorce. Henry asked the Pope to annul (cancel) his marriage, and the **Pope refused**.

Henry's solution was dramatic. In 1534, he declared himself the **Supreme Head of the Church of England**, breaking away from the Pope's authority entirely. This new church is sometimes called the **Anglican Church**. Henry didn't change much about Catholic worship. The services, the prayers, and many traditions stayed the same. He simply removed the Pope from the picture and put himself in charge.

This is important to understand: Henry's break with Rome was **political, not theological**. He wasn't trying to reform Christian beliefs or return to the Bible like Luther or Calvin. He wanted control over his marriage, over Church property (he seized wealthy monasteries and took their land and treasure), and over religion in his kingdom.

The Church of England kept much of Catholic practice. It still had bishops, elaborate ceremonies, and many of the same teachings. The biggest change was that the king, not the Pope, was now the head of the Church. Over time, later leaders would push the Church of England in a more Protestant direction, but Henry himself wasn't interested in that.

Henry eventually married **six wives** in his search for a male heir. His personal life was messy and sometimes cruel. Two of his wives were executed. But his break with Rome permanently changed England and eventually affected millions of people around the world, as the Anglican Church spread to British colonies.

For Latter-day Saints, Henry's story shows that the Reformation wasn't always about seeking God's truth. Sometimes it was about human ambition and political power. The true nature of God, priesthood authority, and temple ordinances weren't going to be restored by a king wanting a divorce. Only God Himself could restore what was lost, and that was still centuries away.
