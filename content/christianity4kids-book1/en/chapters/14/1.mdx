### Lesson 14.1 -- Persecution Continues

You might think that once the Reformation happened and people started new churches, everyone would have celebrated religious freedom. But that's not what happened at all. In many ways, the years after the Reformation were just as violent and intolerant as the years before it.

The basic problem was this: most European countries had an **official religion**, and the government enforced it by law. If the king was Catholic, everyone in that country was expected to be Catholic. If the king became Protestant, everyone was expected to switch. People who refused could be fined, imprisoned, or killed.

**Catholics persecuted Protestants.** In France, thousands of Huguenots (French Calvinists) were massacred on St. Bartholomew's Day in 1572. Soldiers went door to door killing Protestant families. In Spain, the Inquisition hunted anyone suspected of Protestant beliefs. In parts of Germany, Catholic rulers tried to stamp out Lutheranism by force.

**Protestants persecuted Catholics** right back. In England, after Henry VIII broke with Rome, being a Catholic priest became a crime punishable by death. Catholic families had to practice their faith in secret, hiding priests in special rooms built into the walls of their homes. In parts of Switzerland and the Netherlands, Catholic worship was banned.

And both groups persecuted people who didn't fit into either camp. The **Anabaptists** -- who insisted on adult baptism and separation of church and state -- were hunted by Catholics and Protestants alike. **Quakers**, who believed in silent worship and that every person could receive direct revelation from God, were whipped, imprisoned, and banished.

The Thirty Years' War (1618-1648) was one of the deadliest conflicts in European history, and much of it was fought over religion. Catholic and Protestant armies destroyed cities, burned farms, and killed millions of people. Some regions of Germany lost a third of their population.

The Reformation had given people new choices about how to worship, but it hadn't given them the freedom to choose. Governments still decided what people could believe, and the punishment for disagreeing was severe.

For Latter-day Saints, this is a sobering lesson. Even good people who loved God were willing to hurt others who worshipped differently. True religious freedom -- the right to believe and worship according to your own conscience -- was still a long way off. And it would take a new land across the ocean to make it possible.
