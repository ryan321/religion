### Lesson 14.2 -- Seeking Freedom in the New World

With persecution raging across Europe, many Christians began looking for somewhere -- anywhere -- they could worship God in peace. Across the Atlantic Ocean, a vast new land was waiting. And group after group of believers set sail to find freedom in the **New World**.

The **Pilgrims** were among the first. They were a group of English Separatists who had broken away from the Church of England entirely. After years of hiding in the Netherlands, they decided to start fresh in America. In 1620, about 100 passengers boarded a small ship called the **Mayflower** and crossed the stormy Atlantic. They landed at **Plymouth**, in what is now Massachusetts. That first winter was brutal -- nearly half of them died from cold and disease. But they stayed, worshipped freely, and built a community based on their faith.

Ten years later, a much larger group called the **Puritans** arrived. They wanted to "purify" the Church of England by removing Catholic traditions they considered unbiblical. Thousands of Puritans settled in **Massachusetts Bay**, building towns, churches, and schools. Their communities were deeply religious, with strict rules about behavior and worship.

But here's something ironic: many groups that came to America seeking religious freedom **didn't extend that freedom to others**. The Puritans banished people who disagreed with their beliefs. Roger Williams was kicked out of Massachusetts for arguing that government and religion should be separate. Anne Hutchinson was expelled for holding Bible studies in her home that challenged Puritan leaders.

Other groups found different places to settle. The **Quakers**, led by William Penn, founded **Pennsylvania** as a colony where people of all faiths could live peacefully. Penn's colony was remarkably tolerant for its time. Meanwhile, **Catholics** -- who faced persecution in Protestant England -- established the colony of **Maryland** as a haven for Catholic families.

Each group had its own story, its own beliefs, and its own reasons for crossing the ocean. What they all shared was a desire to worship God according to their own understanding. They were brave, faithful, and willing to risk everything for their beliefs.

From a Latter-day Saint perspective, God was guiding this process. He was moving faithful people to a new land where, eventually, religious freedom would take root and grow. The New World wasn't just a place to escape persecution -- it was the land where God would one day restore His Church.
