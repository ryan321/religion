### Lesson 14.4 -- The First Amendment

After winning independence from Britain, the founders of the United States faced an enormous question: What role should religion play in the new nation? For thousands of years, governments had controlled religion. Kings decided which church was official, and everyone was expected to follow. Now America had a chance to try something completely different.

In 1791, the **First Amendment** to the Constitution was ratified. It begins with these powerful words: **"Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof."** Read that carefully. It says two things. First, the government cannot create an official national church. Second, the government cannot stop people from practicing their religion freely.

This was **revolutionary**. No major nation in history had ever made such a promise. In Europe, wars had been fought for centuries over which church the government would support. People had been burned, tortured, and killed for worshipping differently from the official religion. And now America was saying: the government will stay out of it. Believe what you want. Worship how you choose. The government won't force you into a church, and it won't stop you from attending one.

The founders understood this because many of them, or their parents and grandparents, had come to America fleeing religious persecution. They had seen firsthand what happened when governments tried to control people's faith. They decided that freedom of religion wasn't just a nice idea. It was essential to a free society.

Latter-day Saints believe this wasn't just a good political decision. We believe **God inspired the founding of America** specifically to prepare for the Restoration of His Church. The Book of Mormon teaches that God guided people to this land for sacred purposes (1 Nephi 13:12-19). And in modern revelation, the Lord declared that He "established the Constitution of this land, by the hands of wise men whom I raised up unto this very purpose" (D&C 101:77-80).

Think about it: Could the Restoration have happened in a country with an official church that persecuted other religions? Could Joseph Smith have organized The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in a nation where the government controlled worship? The First Amendment didn't just protect political freedom. It created the conditions God needed to restore His Church.

The stage was being set. A land of freedom. A nation built on the right to worship. Now something powerful was about to sweep across that land: a great spiritual awakening that would lead a young boy to ask a question that would change the world.
