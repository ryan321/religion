### Lesson 15.2 -- The Burned-over District

While the Second Great Awakening swept through the entire United States, one region experienced it more intensely than anywhere else. **Western New York**, the area around Rochester, Palmyra, and the Finger Lakes, became so saturated with revivals, camp meetings, and fiery preaching that people started calling it the **"Burned-over District."** The name came from the idea that the region had been "burned over" by so many waves of religious fire that there was hardly anything left to burn.

Why was this area so intense? Western New York was frontier country in the early 1800s, filled with families who had recently moved from New England. These were hardworking, religious people who took their faith seriously. When revival preachers arrived, the communities responded with tremendous enthusiasm. One revival after another rolled through the region like spiritual thunderstorms.

**Baptists, Methodists, and Presbyterians** were the three biggest groups competing for converts. Each sent their best preachers. Each held their own revival meetings. Each claimed that their church was the true path to God. And each warned that joining the wrong church could put your soul in danger.

The competition created **deep divisions**, not just between neighbors but within families. A father might become a Baptist while a mother joined the Methodists and an older brother became Presbyterian. Dinner table conversations turned into religious debates. Friendships were strained. Communities that had been united were pulled apart by competing claims.

Living in the middle of all this was a young boy named **Joseph Smith**. His family had moved to the Palmyra area from Vermont, and they were caught right in the center of the religious excitement. Joseph later wrote about this time, saying that "the whole district of country seemed affected" by the revivals and that "great multitudes united themselves to the different religious parties" (Joseph Smith--History 1:5).

Joseph's own family was divided. His mother, his brother Hyrum, and his brother Samuel leaned toward the Presbyterians. Others in the family had different feelings. Young Joseph, about **fourteen years old**, attended different meetings and listened to different preachers, genuinely wanting to know which church was right.

But the more he listened, the more confused he became. Every preacher seemed sincere. Every group quoted the Bible. And yet they completely contradicted each other. Joseph would later describe his situation simply: "What is to be done? Who of all these parties are right; or, are they all wrong together?" (Joseph Smith--History 1:10).

It was a question that no human being could answer.
