### Lesson 2.3 -- Peter and the Other Apostles

Paul gets a lot of attention (and he deserves it), but let's not forget the other apostles, especially **Peter**, the man Jesus chose to lead His Church.

Peter was a fisherman from Galilee. He wasn't rich or highly educated, but Jesus saw something special in him. In Matthew 16:18, Jesus said to him, **"Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church."** That "rock" was the principle of **revelation**, the ability to receive truth directly from God. Peter had just testified that Jesus was "the Christ, the Son of the living God," and Jesus told him this knowledge came from the Father in Heaven (Matthew 16:16-17). As the **senior apostle**, Peter held the **keys of the priesthood** and presided over the whole Church.

After the Day of Pentecost, Peter led the Church through its early growth. He performed miracles, preached boldly, and was arrested multiple times. But one of Peter's most important moments came when he received a remarkable **vision** from God.

In Acts chapter 10, Peter saw a vision of animals being lowered from heaven in a great sheet. A voice told him to eat, but Peter refused because some of the animals were considered unclean under Jewish law. The voice said, **"What God hath cleansed, that call not thou common"** (Acts 10:15). This vision taught Peter that the gospel was now to go to the **Gentiles**, people of all nations, not just the Jews. Shortly after, Peter baptized **Cornelius**, a Roman centurion, and his household. This was a huge turning point for the Church.

Notice how this worked: God didn't send an angel to every member. He gave the revelation to **Peter**, the presiding apostle. Then Peter shared it with the Church. That's the pattern: revelation flows through the one who holds the keys.

The other apostles were busy too. **James** (the brother of John) was killed by King Herod (Acts 12:2). According to tradition, **Thomas** traveled as far as India, **Andrew** went to modern-day Greece and Turkey, and other apostles scattered across the known world. They were spreading the gospel far and wide, but they were also **spreading thin**. There were only twelve of them (with replacements called as some were killed), and the Roman Empire was enormous.

As the apostles moved to distant lands and were killed one by one, it became harder and harder to keep the Church unified and on the right path. The foundation was strong, but the builders were disappearing.
