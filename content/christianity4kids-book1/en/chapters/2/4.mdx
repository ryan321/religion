### Lesson 2.4 -- The Epistles

Have you ever gotten a letter or message from a leader at church, maybe a bishop or a teacher, helping you understand something important? The early Christians received letters like that too, and some of those letters ended up in the Bible.

These letters are called **epistles**, and most of them were written by the Apostle **Paul**. After Paul established churches in cities across the Roman Empire, he couldn't stay in every place forever. So he wrote letters to help the members stay faithful, answer their questions, and correct problems. Paul wrote epistles to the Romans, the Corinthians, the Galatians, the Ephesians, the Philippians, the Colossians, the Thessalonians, and others. Other apostles wrote letters too. We have epistles from **Peter**, **James**, **John**, and **Jude** in the New Testament.

These letters are incredibly valuable because they give us a window into what the early Church was really like. And honestly? The picture isn't always pretty. The epistles show that even while the apostles were still alive, the Church was already **struggling** with serious problems.

Paul warned the Corinthians about divisions and arguing (1 Corinthians 1:10-13). He scolded the Galatians for turning to a "different gospel" (Galatians 1:6). He told Timothy to watch out for people who had "a form of godliness, but denying the power thereof" (2 Timothy 3:5). And he warned that **"grievous wolves"** would enter the flock and try to lead members astray (Acts 20:29).

Peter warned about **false teachers** who would secretly bring in destructive heresies (2 Peter 2:1). John wrote about people who had already left the Church and were teaching incorrect things (1 John 2:19). Even Jude urged believers to "earnestly contend for the faith" because ungodly men had crept into the Church unnoticed (Jude 1:3-4).

Do you see the pattern? While the apostles were alive, they could write letters, visit branches, and correct errors. They had the **authority** and the **revelation** to keep the Church on course. But what would happen when the apostles were gone? Who would correct false teachings? Who would receive revelation for the whole Church? Who would hold the priesthood keys?

Paul himself seemed to sense what was coming. In 2 Thessalonians 2:3, he warned that a great **"falling away"** would happen. The question was no longer if it would happen, but when.
