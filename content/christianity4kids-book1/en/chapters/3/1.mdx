### Lesson 3.1 -- Why Rome Persecuted Christians

Imagine living in a world where almost everyone worships different gods, celebrates wild festivals in their honor, and believes the emperor himself is practically divine. Now imagine telling everyone that there is only **one true God** and that you won't participate in any of those things. That's what early Christians did, and it made them very unpopular with the **Roman Empire**.

So why did Rome persecute Christians? There were several reasons, and understanding them helps us see just how brave the early Saints had to be.

First, Christians **refused to worship the emperor**. In the Roman world, burning incense to the emperor was more than religion. It was a sign of loyalty to the state. It was like saluting a flag, except with religious meaning. When Christians refused, Roman officials saw them as **disloyal** and even dangerous. It didn't matter that Christians were peaceful; refusing emperor worship looked like rebellion.

Second, Christians **refused to participate in pagan festivals**. Roman life was full of festivals honoring gods like Jupiter, Mars, and Venus. These celebrations included feasts, games, and public ceremonies. Christians wouldn't attend because they worshipped only Jesus Christ. This made their neighbors suspicious and annoyed. People depended on the gods' favor for good harvests and protection. What if the gods were angry because the Christians wouldn't honor them?

Third, Christians **met secretly**. Because they faced hostility, early believers often gathered in private homes or even underground in places called **catacombs**. They held their worship services quietly. But meeting in secret made Romans even more suspicious. Rumors spread that Christians were doing strange or evil things in their meetings, none of which were true.

Fourth, the Church was **growing fast**. What started as a small group in Jerusalem had spread across the empire. People of all backgrounds (Jews, Greeks, Romans, slaves, and free citizens) were joining. A movement that grew that quickly seemed threatening to Roman authorities who liked to keep things under control.

Finally, Christians were simply **different**. They didn't fit in. They talked about a coming kingdom (the Kingdom of God), which Romans misunderstood as a political threat. They called Jesus their **King**, which sounded like treason to Roman ears.

Being a Christian in the Roman Empire took real courage. You could lose your job, your home, your freedom, or even your life. And for many early Saints, that's exactly what happened.
