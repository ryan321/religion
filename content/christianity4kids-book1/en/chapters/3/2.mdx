### Lesson 3.2 -- Early Martyrs

The word **martyr** comes from a Greek word that simply means **"witness."** But over time, it came to mean something much more powerful: someone who dies rather than deny their testimony of Jesus Christ. The early Church had many martyrs, and their stories remind us of the incredible price some people have paid for their faith.

The first Christian martyr was **Stephen**, who we learned about in Chapter 1. He was stoned to death for testifying that Jesus was the Son of God (Acts 7). But Stephen was just the beginning. As the Church grew and persecution increased, many other faithful believers gave their lives.

The Apostle **James**, the brother of John and one of Jesus's closest disciples, was killed by King Herod Agrippa around AD 44. Acts 12:2 tells us simply that Herod "killed James the brother of John with the sword." James became the first of the twelve apostles to be martyred.

According to strong early Christian traditions, **Peter** was eventually arrested in Rome and sentenced to death by **crucifixion**. The tradition says that Peter asked to be **crucified upside down** because he didn't feel worthy to die in the same way as his Lord. Think about that. Peter loved Jesus so much that even in death, he wanted to show reverence for the Savior.

The Apostle **Paul**, after years of missionary journeys, imprisonments, and trials, was also put to death in Rome. Because Paul was a Roman citizen, he was given a different form of execution: he was **beheaded**. Tradition places his death around AD 64-67, during the reign of Emperor Nero.

Most of the other original apostles are believed to have been **martyred** as well, though the details come from tradition rather than scripture. Andrew is said to have been crucified on an X-shaped cross. Bartholomew was reportedly killed in Armenia. Thomas may have been speared in India. One by one, the apostles who had walked with Jesus, who held His priesthood authority and keys, were taken from the earth.

These men and women didn't die because they were foolish. They died because they **knew** what they had witnessed was true. They had seen the risen Christ, felt the power of the Holy Ghost, and experienced miracles. Their willingness to die rather than deny their faith is one of the strongest testimonies in all of history.

Their courage should inspire us. We may never face that kind of danger, but we can choose to stand for truth even when it's hard.
