### Lesson 3.3 -- The Great Persecutions

While Christians faced danger from the very beginning, there were certain times in history when the persecution became especially intense and organized. Two of the most famous periods of persecution were under the emperors **Nero** and **Diocletian**.

In **AD 64**, a massive fire swept through the city of **Rome**. The fire burned for days and destroyed large sections of the city. People were homeless, angry, and looking for someone to blame. Emperor **Nero** pointed his finger at the Christians. Most historians believe Nero himself may have started the fire (or at least did nothing to stop it), but blaming Christians was a convenient way to turn public anger away from himself.

What followed was horrifying. Nero had Christians arrested and killed in gruesome ways. Some were torn apart by wild animals in the arena. Others were covered in tar and set on fire to serve as human torches to light Nero's gardens at night. It was during this persecution that the apostles **Peter and Paul** are believed to have been martyred. Nero's persecution was brutal, but it was mostly limited to Rome and didn't last long after Nero's death in AD 68.

For the next two hundred years, persecution of Christians came and went. Some emperors left Christians alone. Others launched local crackdowns. But in **AD 303**, Emperor **Diocletian** began the most **systematic persecution** the Church had ever faced. This is sometimes called the **Great Persecution**.

Diocletian ordered Christian churches to be **destroyed**. He commanded that all copies of the **scriptures be burned**. Christians were stripped of their legal rights. Those who refused to offer sacrifices to the Roman gods were imprisoned, tortured, or killed. This persecution spread across the entire empire and lasted about ten years.

Yet through all of this, Christianity didn't disappear. In fact, it grew. An early Christian writer named **Tertullian** wrote a famous line: **"The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church."** What he meant was that every time Romans killed a Christian, other people were so impressed by the believer's courage and faith that they wanted to learn more about Christianity. Persecution meant to destroy the Church actually helped it spread.

The willingness of ordinary men, women, and even children to face death rather than deny Jesus Christ was a powerful witness to the world. Their faith couldn't be stamped out by swords, fire, or imperial decrees.
