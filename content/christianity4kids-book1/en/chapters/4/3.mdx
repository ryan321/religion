### Lesson 4.3 -- False Teachers

The apostles knew what was coming. Even while they were alive, they could see that false ideas were creeping into the Church, and they warned everyone about it.

**Paul** wrote to his young friend Timothy and said, "The time will come when they will not endure sound doctrine; but after their own lusts shall they heap to themselves teachers, having itching ears" (2 Timothy 4:3). What does "itching ears" mean? It means people would look for teachers who told them what they wanted to hear instead of what God actually said. Rather than listening to hard truths, they would find teachers who made things easy and comfortable.

**John** gave an even more direct warning. He wrote, "Little children, it is the last time: and as ye have heard that antichrist shall come, even now are there many antichrists" (1 John 2:18-19). John said these false teachers had actually been members of the Church but had left it. They went out and started teaching their own ideas as if they were God's truth.

**Peter** warned the members too. He wrote, "There shall be false prophets also among you, who privily shall bring in damnable heresies" (2 Peter 2:1). The word "privily" means secretly or quietly. These false teachers didn't announce, "Hey, I'm about to teach you something wrong!" They mixed their false ideas in with true ones so people couldn't easily tell the difference.

While the apostles were alive, they could push back against these false teachers. When a congregation had a problem, Paul wrote a letter to correct it. When someone taught false doctrine, Peter or John could say, "No, that is wrong. Here is what Jesus actually taught." The apostles were like referees in a game. They had the authority to call fouls and keep the game fair.

But once the apostles were gone, there was **no authoritative voice to correct** the false teachings. Imagine a classroom where the teacher leaves and never comes back. At first, the students try to follow the lesson plan. But soon, different students start arguing about what the teacher really meant. Without the teacher there to settle things, everyone just follows whoever sounds the most convincing. That's what happened to the early Church. Sincere people disagreed, and there was no one with God's authority to say, "This is the truth."
