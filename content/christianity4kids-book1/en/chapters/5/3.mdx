### Lesson 5.3 -- Doctrines Change

Once the apostles were gone and philosophy began to influence the Church, specific doctrines and practices started to change. These changes didn't happen because someone woke up one morning and decided to rewrite everything. They happened slowly, over generations, as leaders made decisions without the guidance of prophets.

**Baptism** was one of the first practices to change. Jesus was baptized by **immersion**. He went down into the water and came back up (Matthew 3:16). The word "baptize" itself comes from a Greek word meaning "to dip" or "to immerse." But over time, some church leaders began allowing **sprinkling** instead of immersion. It seemed easier, especially for sick people or infants. Eventually, sprinkling became common in many areas.

Speaking of infants, **infant baptism** was another change. In the New Testament, baptism was for people old enough to believe and repent. Jesus said, "He that believeth and is baptized shall be saved" (Mark 16:16). A baby can't believe or repent. But as ideas about original sin developed (the belief that all humans are born guilty because of Adam's sin), church leaders began baptizing babies to wash away that inherited guilt. This was a doctrine Jesus never taught.

The **priesthood** changed too. In Jesus's Church, ordinary men were called by God and ordained to serve: fishermen, tentmakers, tax collectors. But over the centuries, a **professional clergy** developed. Church leaders became highly educated specialists who studied for years, were paid for their work, and held powerful positions. The idea that God could call a simple farmer or fisherman to lead faded away.

Perhaps the biggest doctrinal change involved the **nature of God**. Jesus, His Father, and the Holy Ghost are **three separate beings** united in purpose. Jesus prayed to His Father. He said, "I and my Father are one," meaning one in purpose, not one in body (John 17:21-22). But over time, church councils developed the doctrine of the **Trinity**, the idea that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three "persons" but only one "substance" or being. This is a very difficult concept, and many people admit they don't fully understand it. That's because it didn't come from revelation. It came from debate and philosophical reasoning.

Each of these changes moved the Church further from what Jesus had originally established.
