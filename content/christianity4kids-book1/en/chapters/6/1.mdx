### Lesson 6.1 -- Constantine

For nearly 300 years after Jesus, being a Christian could get you killed. Roman emperors saw Christians as a threat because they refused to worship Roman gods or treat the emperor as divine. Christians were thrown to lions, burned alive, and imprisoned. Then, in the year 312 AD, everything changed because of one man: **Constantine**.

Constantine was a Roman general fighting for control of the empire. Before a crucial battle at the Milvian Bridge near Rome, he reportedly saw a **vision**, a cross of light in the sky with the words "In this sign, conquer." Constantine ordered his soldiers to paint the Christian symbol on their shields. They fought, and they won.

Constantine credited the **Christian God** for his victory. He didn't become a baptized Christian right away (he wasn't baptized until he was on his deathbed), but he became Christianity's most powerful protector. In **313 AD**, Constantine and his co-emperor issued the **Edict of Milan**, which made Christianity legal throughout the Roman Empire. Christians could now worship openly. They could build churches. They could hold property. The centuries of brutal persecution were finally over.

This was an enormous moment. Imagine being a Christian who had watched family members die for their faith, and suddenly the emperor himself was on your side. The relief and joy must have been overwhelming. Christians could come out of hiding and practice their religion without fear.

But this great change came with a **cost**, one that most people at the time couldn't see. When the most powerful man in the world becomes the protector of your religion, your religion starts to change. The emperor had his own reasons for supporting Christianity. Constantine wanted a **unified empire**, and he thought one religion could help hold it together. His support was real, but it was also political.

The Church went from being a persecuted group of humble believers meeting in homes and catacombs to being connected with the most powerful government on earth. That might sound like a good thing. But as we'll see, when the Church gained the emperor's favor, it also began to lose something it could never get back without a restoration.
