### Lesson 6.2 -- The Cost of Imperial Favor

Having the Roman emperor as your protector sounds amazing -- and in some ways it was. Christians were no longer hunted. They could build beautiful churches. Their leaders were treated with respect. But there was a price, and it was steep.

The first cost was **entanglement with politics**. When the emperor protects your church, the emperor also starts to influence your church. Constantine didn't just defend Christians -- he involved himself in Church decisions. He gave money to build churches. He gave church leaders special legal privileges. He even got involved in theological debates, not because he was a theologian, but because disagreements among Christians threatened the **political stability** he needed.

Think about it this way: if your school principal started deciding what your family should eat for dinner, that would feel wrong. The principal might be a good person, but dinner is not the principal's job. In a similar way, a Roman emperor deciding Church doctrine was a serious problem -- even if the emperor meant well.

The second cost was **wealth and power**. Before Constantine, Christian leaders were often poor, humble people who served at great personal risk. After Constantine, being a church leader became a position of influence and prestige. **Church leaders began dining with emperors.** Bishops received government salaries. Churches were given land and money. Some leaders started caring more about their political position than about serving God's children.

This doesn't mean every church leader became corrupt. Many remained humble and devoted. But the **system** had changed. The path to church leadership now ran through politics and education rather than through a call from God. Power and wealth entered the Church in ways that would have been unrecognizable to Peter, James, and John -- fishermen who left everything to follow Jesus.

The early apostles had no armies, no government support, and no palaces. They had priesthood authority from God and the guidance of the Holy Ghost. The Church after Constantine had armies, government support, and palaces -- but it no longer had apostles or priesthood keys. The trade wasn't a good one.

Jesus himself had taught, "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36). But after Constantine, the Church became deeply, thoroughly part of this world. And once it was woven into the fabric of the empire, it was nearly impossible to pull it back out.
