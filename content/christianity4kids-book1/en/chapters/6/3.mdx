### Lesson 6.3 -- The Council of Nicaea, 325 AD

One of the biggest debates in early Christianity was about the relationship between God the Father and Jesus Christ. Were they the same being? Were they separate? Were they equal? Different church leaders had very different answers, and the arguing was tearing the Church apart.

Constantine had a problem. He wanted Christianity to unite his empire, but Christians couldn't even agree on who God was. So in **325 AD**, he did something extraordinary. He called a great meeting of church leaders in the city of **Nicaea** (in modern-day Turkey). About **300 bishops** traveled from across the empire to attend. Constantine himself presided over parts of the meeting, even though he was not a church leader or theologian.

The main debate was between two sides. **Arius**, a popular preacher from Alexandria, taught that Jesus was created by the Father and was therefore not equal to Him. **Athanasius** and others argued that the Father and Son were of the exact same substance -- equal and eternal. The bishops debated, argued, and eventually **voted**. The result was the **Nicene Creed**, a statement declaring that Jesus Christ was "of one substance" with the Father, meaning they were the same being in a way that is difficult to explain or understand.

Stop and think about that for a moment. The nature of God, the most important question in all of religion, was decided by a **committee vote**, influenced by a Roman emperor who had political reasons for wanting everyone to agree. This was not how God's Church was supposed to work. In Jesus's Church, questions about doctrine were settled by **revelation**, by prophets and apostles asking God and receiving answers.

For Latter-day Saints, the Council of Nicaea is especially important because of what Joseph Smith experienced in the Sacred Grove. Joseph saw God the Father and Jesus Christ standing before him as **two separate, glorified beings**. They were not one substance. They were not an abstract mystery. They were real, distinct persons with real bodies. What Joseph saw **contradicted** the decision made at Nicaea.

The Nicene Creed became the foundation of most Christian churches' understanding of God. But it was a decision made by men debating and voting, not by God revealing truth through His prophets.
