### Lesson 6.4 -- Christianity Becomes the State Religion

Constantine made Christianity legal. But in **380 AD**, Emperor **Theodosius** took things much further. He declared Christianity the **official state religion** of the Roman Empire. This meant that Christianity wasn't just allowed. It was required. Every citizen was expected to be Christian. Every other religion was now in trouble.

Think about what this meant. **Pagan temples**, places where Romans had worshipped their traditional gods for centuries, were **closed**. Some were torn down. Others were turned into Christian churches. Traditional religious practices that had been part of Roman life for generations were suddenly illegal. People who held different beliefs were fined, had their property taken, or faced punishment.

**Other religions were suppressed.** Groups with different Christian beliefs were declared heretics and persecuted. Jews faced increasing hostility. People who practiced the old Roman religions were forced to stop. The government used its power to enforce religious conformity, something that would have horrified the early apostles.

Here is the painful irony: **the persecuted had become the persecutors.** For almost 300 years, Christians had been the ones thrown to lions, burned at the stake, and imprisoned for their beliefs. They knew what it felt like to be forced to worship something they didn't believe in. Yet within a few generations of gaining power, Christian leaders were doing the same things to others that had been done to them.

This was very far from what Jesus had taught. He had never forced anyone to follow Him. He invited people with love. He said, "Come, follow me," not "Follow me or else." The twelve apostles had taught by persuasion, by testimony, and by the power of the Holy Ghost. They had no armies. They had no government backing. They changed hearts, not laws.

Compare that to the Church in 380 AD. It had the full power of the Roman Empire behind it. It had wealth, political influence, and the authority to punish anyone who disagreed. But it did not have prophets. It did not have apostles. It did not have priesthood keys. It did not have revelation from God.

The humble Church that had started with twelve apostles in an upper room in Jerusalem was now a political institution backed by the Roman army. The need for a restoration, a time when God would bring back His authority and truth, had never been greater.
