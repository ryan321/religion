### Lesson 7.1 -- What Is a Creed?

Have you ever had a disagreement with a friend about what the rules of a game are? Imagine if there were no rule book, and the only people who knew the original rules had passed away. That's kind of what happened to the early Christian Church when it came to deciding what they believed.

A **creed** is a formal, written statement of belief. The word comes from the Latin word *credo*, meaning "I believe." Creeds are meant to spell out exactly what a group believes so there's no confusion. They sound like a good idea, but here's the important part: the original Church that Jesus established didn't need creeds.

Why not? Because the apostles had something better: **revelation**. They could pray and receive answers directly from God. When questions came up about what to teach or how to organize the Church, the apostles didn't have to sit around debating. They could ask Heavenly Father and receive guidance through the **Holy Ghost**. That's how the Church was supposed to work.

But after the apostles were killed and **priesthood authority** was lost, the Church had a big problem. There was no one left who had the authority to receive revelation for the whole Church. Bishops and other leaders still cared about getting things right, but they had to figure things out on their own. When disagreements arose (and they arose often), leaders had to settle them through **debate and voting**.

This is how creeds were born. Church leaders would gather at large meetings called **councils**. They would argue about questions of belief, sometimes for weeks or even months. Then they would take a vote. Whichever side won the vote got to write the official creed. Their ideas became the accepted teaching of the Church.

But what about the people who disagreed? They were often called **heretics**, people whose beliefs were considered dangerously wrong. Some were expelled from the Church, and some were even punished by the government. Losing a vote at a council could have very serious consequences.

Think about what a huge change this was. In Jesus's Church, truth came from God through revelation. Now, truth was being decided by whoever had the most votes or the most political power. That's a very different way to run a church.

As Latter-day Saints, we believe this shift from revelation to debate is one of the clearest signs that the **Apostasy** was real. Important truths about God were no longer being revealed. They were being argued about and voted on.
