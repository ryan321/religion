### Lesson 7.2 -- The Trinity

One of the biggest questions in the history of Christianity is this: What is the relationship between God the Father, Jesus Christ, and the Holy Spirit? The answer the ancient councils came up with is called the **Trinity**, and it's one of the places where Latter-day Saint beliefs are very different from most other Christian churches.

The story begins in the year **325 AD** at a city called **Nicaea** (in modern-day Turkey). The Roman Emperor **Constantine** called together hundreds of bishops to settle a fierce argument. Some Christians believed that Jesus was created by the Father and was not truly God. Others believed Jesus was fully God, equal with the Father. The debate had gotten so heated that it was causing problems throughout the empire.

After weeks of debate, the bishops voted and produced the **Nicene Creed**. This creed declared that the Father and the Son were "of one substance," meaning they were made of the same stuff and were one God. Later, at the **Council of Constantinople in 381 AD**, the creed was expanded to include the Holy Spirit. The final teaching said that God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit are **three persons but one God, one substance, one being**.

This is what most Christians today call the **Trinity**. But here's something interesting: the words used to explain the Trinity didn't come from the Bible. They came from **Greek philosophy**. Terms like "substance" and "essence" and "person" had very specific meanings in Greek thinking that were quite complicated. Many people, even adults, find the Trinity hard to understand. That's because it was built on philosophical ideas, not on simple revelation from God.

Members of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints do not accept the Trinity as described in these creeds. We believe that Heavenly Father, Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost are **three separate beings** who are perfectly united in purpose, love, and will, but they are not one being or one substance.

How do we know this? Because **Joseph Smith saw them**. In the **Sacred Grove** in 1820, Joseph saw God the Father and Jesus Christ standing together as two distinct, glorified beings. The Father spoke, pointing to His Son, and said, "This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!" (Joseph Smith--History 1:17). That one experience answered a question that had been debated and voted on for nearly 1,500 years.

Revelation is simpler and more reliable than philosophy.
