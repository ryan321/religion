### Lesson 7.3 -- More Councils

The Council of Nicaea in 325 AD was just the beginning. Over the next century and a half, church leaders held several more major councils to try to answer difficult questions about God, Jesus, and the Church. Each council brought more arguments, more creeds, and more divisions.

The **Council of Constantinople** met in **381 AD**. This council expanded the Nicene Creed to include a clearer statement about the Holy Spirit, declaring that the Holy Spirit was also fully God, equal with the Father and the Son. It also dealt with several groups whose teachings had been declared wrong at Nicaea but had continued to grow.

Fifty years later, in **431 AD**, the **Council of Ephesus** tackled another explosive question: What was the nature of Jesus Christ? Was He fully God? Fully human? Some mixture of both? And what about Mary, the mother of Jesus? Should she be called the "Mother of God" or just the "Mother of Christ"? This might sound like a small difference in words, but it caused enormous conflict. A bishop named **Nestorius** taught that Mary should be called "Mother of Christ" but not "Mother of God." The council ruled against him. Nestorius was removed from his position and eventually **exiled**, forced to leave his home and live far away. His followers were declared heretics, and the **Nestorian Church** split off and spread into Persia and Asia.

Twenty years after that, in **451 AD**, the **Council of Chalcedon** tried to settle the question of Christ's nature once and for all. The council declared that Jesus was "truly God and truly man," two natures united in one person, "without confusion, without change, without division, without separation." But not everyone agreed. Whole communities in Egypt, Syria, and Ethiopia rejected the council's decision. They became known as **Oriental Orthodox** churches and remain separate to this day.

Do you notice a pattern? Each council was supposed to bring unity, but each one actually created **more divisions**. Bishops were exiled. Entire Christian communities were declared heretical and cut off. And the decisions at these councils were shaped as much by **politics** as by theology. Emperors pressured bishops. Bishops formed alliances. Whoever had the most power often won the debate.

This is what happens when a church tries to answer the deepest questions about God without the gift of **revelation**. Instead of unity, you get an ever-growing list of splits and hard feelings.
