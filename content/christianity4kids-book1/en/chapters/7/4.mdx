### Lesson 7.4 -- What This Means

Let's step back and think about what the creeds and councils really show us. If you've been reading carefully, you've probably already noticed something important: the early Christians were asking the right questions, but they were trying to answer them the wrong way.

Questions like "What is God like?" and "How are the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit related?" are some of the most important questions anyone can ask. They matter deeply. The problem wasn't the questions. It was that the people trying to answer them no longer had the tools God had provided.

When the apostles were alive, questions like these could be answered through **revelation**. An apostle could pray, receive an answer from God, and share it with the Church. Done. It might take days or weeks, but the process was clear: ask God, receive an answer, teach the people.

Without apostles and without the **priesthood keys** to receive revelation for the whole Church, leaders had to rely on their own thinking. They borrowed ideas from **Greek philosophy**. They debated. They voted. They used political pressure. And when they couldn't agree, they split apart and called each other heretics.

Think about how different things could have been. Imagine if, at the Council of Nicaea in 325 AD, there had been a living **prophet** with real authority. The question of the Trinity, which took councils, creeds, debates, and centuries to sort through, could have been answered in a matter of moments. A prophet would have asked God and received a clear answer.

That's not just wishful thinking. That's exactly what happened. In **1820**, a 14-year-old boy named Joseph Smith knelt in a grove of trees and asked God a sincere question. God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to him as two separate, glorious beings. In that one experience, the question that had caused centuries of arguments, exile, and division was settled clearly and simply.

The nature of God is not something that should be decided by a **vote**. It's not something that should depend on which bishop had more political friends. It is something that can only be known through **revelation** from God Himself.

The creeds and councils teach us a powerful lesson: even when people are sincere and trying their best, they cannot replace what was lost in the Apostasy. Only God can restore His own truth. And that is exactly what He did, beginning in the **Sacred Grove**.

The history of the creeds is really the history of people reaching for answers they couldn't find on their own. The Restoration is the story of God reaching back.
