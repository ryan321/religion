### Lesson 8.1 -- Bishop of Rome Becomes Pope

After the fall of the Roman Empire in the West in **476 AD**, Europe was in chaos. Barbarian tribes had overrun the old Roman lands. Governments collapsed. Cities shrank. Roads crumbled. In the middle of all this confusion, one leader kept gaining more and more power, but it wasn't a king or a general. It was the **Bishop of Rome**.

Bishops were important leaders in the early Christian Church. Each major city had its own bishop who looked after the Christians in that area. The bishops of large, important cities (like Rome, Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem) had extra influence. But the Bishop of Rome gradually claimed to be the most important bishop of all.

Why Rome? The Bishop of Rome argued that his authority came from the **Apostle Peter**. According to tradition, Peter had traveled to Rome and been martyred there. Jesus had once said to Peter, "Thou art Peter, and upon this rock I will build my church" (Matthew 16:18). The bishops of Rome said this meant Peter had been given supreme authority, and that authority had been passed down to every Bishop of Rome after him.

Over time, the Bishop of Rome began to be called the **Pope**. This word comes from the Latin word *papa*, meaning "father." The Pope became the head of all Western Christianity. He claimed the right to make final decisions on matters of doctrine, to appoint other bishops, and to settle disputes throughout the Church.

Not everyone agreed with these claims. The bishops in the Eastern part of the old Roman Empire, especially the bishop (or **patriarch**) of Constantinople, did not accept that the Pope had authority over them. This disagreement would grow into a much bigger problem later. But in the West, the Pope's power was enormous and growing.

As Latter-day Saints, we understand something important here. After the apostles died and the priesthood keys were lost, no bishop, no matter how sincere, had the authority that Peter once held. Peter's authority came from Jesus Christ through **ordination**, not just from living in the right city. Real priesthood authority can't be passed down just by claiming a connection to Peter. It has to be given by God.

The rise of the Pope is another chapter in the story of the Apostasy: well-meaning leaders trying to fill a gap that only God could fill.
