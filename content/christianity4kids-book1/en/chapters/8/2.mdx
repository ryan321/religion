### Lesson 8.2 -- The Church Hierarchy

As the Pope's power grew, so did the structure of the Church beneath him. Over the centuries, the Catholic Church built an enormous **hierarchy**, a system of ranks where each level has authority over the ones below it. This hierarchy looked very different from anything Jesus had established.

At the very top was the **Pope**, who claimed to be Christ's representative on earth. Below the Pope were the **cardinals**, senior leaders who advised the Pope and eventually gained the right to elect new popes. Below the cardinals were **archbishops**, who oversaw large regions. Under them were **bishops**, who led smaller areas called **dioceses**. At the local level were **priests**, who served in individual churches called parishes. And then there were **monks** and **nuns**, who lived apart from the world in special communities called monasteries and convents.

This created a class of **professional clergy**, people whose full-time job was running the Church. Ordinary members were called the **laity**, and they were expected to follow, listen, and obey, but not to lead or teach on their own. The gap between the clergy and the laity grew wider and wider over time.

One of the biggest problems was the **Bible**. During the Middle Ages, the Bible was written in **Latin**, a language that most ordinary people couldn't read or understand. Church services were conducted in Latin too. If you wanted to know what the Bible said, you had to rely on the priests to tell you. You couldn't read it for yourself. This gave the clergy enormous control over what people believed.

Think about what this means. In Jesus's original Church, the gospel was meant for everyone. Ordinary fishermen like Peter and Andrew became apostles. The early Saints shared the gospel with each other and studied the scriptures together. But in the medieval Catholic Church, a huge distance had grown between the people and God. You couldn't read the scriptures. You couldn't hold priesthood authority. You had to go through layers and layers of clergy to get to God.

In The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, we believe that **every worthy member** can hold the priesthood (for men) and participate in sacred ordinances. We don't have a paid professional clergy. Bishops, teachers, and leaders in our wards and stakes are regular members who serve voluntarily. Everyone is encouraged to read the scriptures for themselves and to receive personal revelation.

The medieval hierarchy moved people further from God. The Restoration brought them back.
