### Lesson 8.3 -- Monasteries: Preserving What Was Good

Not everything about the medieval Church was a step away from truth. Even in the middle of the Apostasy, God's influence could still be seen, and one of the best examples is the **monasteries**.

A **monastery** is a place where **monks** live together, separated from the outside world, dedicating their lives to prayer, work, and study. Women who made similar commitments lived in **convents** and were called **nuns**. These men and women gave up wealth, marriage, and personal freedom to serve God the best way they knew how.

One of the most important things monks did was **preserve the Bible**. Remember, this was long before printing presses existed. Every copy of the Bible (and every other book) had to be written out by hand. This was slow, painstaking work. Monks spent years carefully copying manuscripts, one page at a time, in beautiful handwriting called **calligraphy**. Without their dedication, many parts of the Bible and other ancient writings could have been lost forever.

Monasteries also became centers of **learning**. In an age when most people couldn't read, monks maintained **libraries** and **schools**. They studied not just the scriptures but also history, science, medicine, and agriculture. When the Roman Empire collapsed and much of Europe's knowledge was in danger of disappearing, monasteries kept that knowledge alive.

Monks and nuns also served their communities in practical ways. They cared for the **sick**, fed the **poor**, and offered shelter to travelers. In a world that was often violent and chaotic, monasteries were places of peace and order. Many of the early hospitals in Europe were run by monks.

Does this mean the monasteries had priesthood authority or the full gospel? No. But it does show us something important: **God uses imperfect institutions to accomplish good things**. Even without revelation or priesthood keys, sincere people who loved God were able to do remarkable work. They preserved the scriptures that would later be available for people like William Tyndale and Joseph Smith. They kept learning alive during some of the darkest times in European history.

As Latter-day Saints, we can respect and appreciate the good that monks and nuns did. Their sacrifice and devotion helped prepare the way for the Restoration. The scriptures they so carefully copied would one day help a young boy in New York understand that he needed to ask God which church was true.

God's hand was at work, even in the darkest times.
