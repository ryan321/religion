### Lesson 9.2 -- What They Disagreed About

When the Eastern and Western churches started pulling apart, you might expect their disagreements to be about huge, obvious differences. But many of the things they argued about seem surprisingly small. The problem was that these small disagreements became explosive because of the **cultural distance** and **political rivalry** between the two halves of the old Roman Empire.

One of the most famous disputes was about a single word in the Nicene Creed. The original creed said that the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father." Western churches added the Latin word **filioque**, which means "and the Son," so it read that the Holy Spirit "proceeds from the Father **and the Son**." The Eastern churches were furious. They said the West had no right to change the creed without an official council. The West said the addition was theologically correct. This argument over one word lasted for centuries and has never been fully resolved.

Another disagreement was about whether **priests could marry**. In the Eastern Church, ordinary priests were allowed to be married (though bishops had to be unmarried). In the Western Church, the Pope eventually required all priests to be **celibate**, meaning they could never marry. Each side thought the other was wrong.

Even **bread** became a source of conflict. For the sacrament (or communion), Western churches used **unleavened bread** (flat bread without yeast, like the bread used at the Jewish Passover). Eastern churches used **leavened bread**, regular bread with yeast. Each side believed their tradition was the correct one and argued about it passionately.

And then there was the constant, underlying question of **authority**. Did the Pope in Rome have the right to make decisions for the Eastern churches? The Pope said yes. The Eastern patriarchs said no. Every other disagreement was made worse by this power struggle.

Now, you might be thinking, "These seem like things people could work out if they just talked!" And you'd be right. If there had been a **prophet** with authority to receive revelation from God, these questions could have been settled. Is the Holy Spirit from the Father alone or from the Father and the Son? Ask God. Can priests marry? Ask God. What kind of bread should be used? Ask God.

But there was no prophet. There was no revelation. So small questions turned into giant walls between people who all claimed to follow the same Savior.
