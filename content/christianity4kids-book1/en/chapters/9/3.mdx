### Lesson 9.3 -- The Split of 1054

For centuries, tensions between the Eastern and Western churches had been building. Disagreements about the filioque, priestly marriage, bread, icons, and (above all) the Pope's authority had pushed the two sides further and further apart. Then, in the year **1054 AD**, things finally broke.

The story of how it happened is dramatic. Pope Leo IX sent a representative, a cardinal named **Humbert**, to Constantinople to try to resolve the disputes. But Humbert was not a patient or diplomatic man. He was proud, stubborn, and convinced that the Pope's authority over the whole Church was absolute.

Negotiations went badly. Humbert and the **Patriarch of Constantinople**, a man named **Michael Cerularius**, clashed fiercely. Neither side was willing to give ground. Then, on July 16, 1054, Humbert marched into the great church of **Hagia Sophia**, one of the most magnificent buildings in the world and the heart of Eastern Christianity, and placed a document on the altar. That document was a **papal bull of excommunication**. It declared that Michael Cerularius and his followers were cut off from the Church.

The patriarch was not about to take that quietly. Michael Cerularius responded by **excommunicating Humbert and the Pope's representatives right back**. Each side declared the other side to be outside the true Church of Christ.

This event is known as the **Great Schism** (the word "schism" means a split or division). It formally divided Christianity into two branches: the **Roman Catholic Church** in the West, led by the Pope in Rome, and the **Eastern Orthodox Church** in the East, led by the patriarchs of Constantinople, Alexandria, Antioch, and Jerusalem.

The split has never been fully healed. Although leaders from both churches have met in modern times and expressed goodwill, the Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox churches remain separate to this day, nearly a thousand years later. They have different leaders, different traditions, and different understandings of authority.

Think about what this means. Both sides claimed to be the true Church of Christ. Both sides had sincere, devoted believers. But they could not stay together because there was no one with the **authority from God** to unite them. The Great Schism is one of the most visible and lasting signs of the Apostasy.

A church without revelation is a church that eventually breaks apart.
