### Lesson 14.1 -- What Happens When You Break a Covenant

Making a covenant with God is a sacred and wonderful thing. But what happens when you break one? Does God throw you away? Does He stop loving you? The answer is a firm and hopeful **no**.

When you break a covenant -- by sinning, by choosing something you know is wrong -- real consequences follow. The **Holy Ghost withdraws**. That doesn't mean He's gone forever, but His influence pulls back. You feel it. There is a heaviness, a loneliness, a sense that something isn't right. The light dims a little. The peace you once felt gets harder to find.

The blessings attached to your covenant are **suspended** -- put on hold. Think of it like a lamp that's been unplugged. The lamp isn't broken. The electricity is still there in the wall. But until you plug it back in, no light comes out. When you break a covenant, you unplug yourself from the blessings. But the connection point is still there, waiting for you to come back.

This is an important truth: the consequences are **real but not permanent** -- as long as you repent. God does not give up on you. He never has, and He never will. He is patient, loving, and always ready to welcome you back.

Jesus told a story about this in Luke 15. A young man asked his father for his inheritance early, left home, and wasted everything on foolish choices. He ended up hungry, alone, and feeding pigs. He had broken his relationship with his father. But when he finally came to his senses and decided to go home, his father didn't slam the door. His father **ran to meet him**, threw his arms around him, and celebrated his return.

That is the parable of the **prodigal son**, and it is a picture of how Heavenly Father feels about you when you stumble. He is not standing at the door with crossed arms, angry and disappointed. He is watching the road, hoping you'll come back -- and ready to run to you when you do.

Breaking a covenant is serious. It has real consequences. But it is not the end of the story. The way back is always open, and it is called **repentance**. You'll learn about that in the next lesson.
