### Lesson 10.3 -- Sacrifice and the Atonement

Every sacrifice we've talked about so far (Adam's firstlings, Abraham's willingness with Isaac, the daily offerings under the Law of Moses) all pointed to one thing. They all looked forward to the **ultimate sacrifice**: the Atonement of Jesus Christ.

Think about what Christ gave. He didn't offer an animal on an altar. He didn't give up a piece of land or a bag of gold. He gave **everything**. In the Garden of Gethsemane, He knelt down and took upon Himself the sins, sorrows, pains, and sicknesses of every person who has ever lived or ever will live. **He bled from every pore**. He suffered so deeply that even He, the Son of God, asked the Father if there was another way (**Luke 22:42**). But there wasn't, and He did it anyway.

Then came the cross. He was beaten, mocked, and crucified. He gave His life, not because He had to, but because He **chose to**. He could have called down legions of angels to rescue Him. But He didn't. He sacrificed Himself so that every one of us could be forgiven, healed, and brought back into God's presence.

Why does this matter for understanding faith? Because Christ's sacrifice is the foundation of everything. He gave everything so that **we could receive everything**: forgiveness, resurrection, eternal life, and the chance to become like our Heavenly Father.

Now, here's the important part: **our willingness to sacrifice mirrors His sacrifice**. We don't atone for anyone's sins. We can't. Only Christ could do that. But when we are willing to give things up for the gospel (our time, our comfort, our desires), we are showing that we **trust in the One who did atone**.

Think about it this way. When you choose to keep the commandments even when it's hard, you're saying, "I trust Jesus Christ. I believe His sacrifice was real, and I'm willing to follow His example." That act of trust is what faith looks like in action.

The Lectures on Faith teach that sacrifice is required for faith, and the Atonement is the greatest example of why. Christ didn't ask us to do anything He wasn't willing to do first. He went first. He gave the most. And now He invites us to follow, not to suffer as He suffered, but to trust as He trusted and to give as He gave.

Your sacrifices will never be as great as His. But your willingness to make them connects you to Him in a way that nothing else can.
