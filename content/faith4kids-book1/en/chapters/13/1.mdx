### Lesson 13.1 -- What If I Have Doubts?

Have you ever had a moment where you weren't sure about something you'd been taught? Maybe you wondered if God really hears your prayers, or if the scriptures are really true. If so, you're not alone, and having those questions doesn't make you a bad person. In fact, it makes you pretty normal.

The Lectures on Faith don't pretend that believing in God is always easy. They don't say, "Just believe and never ask questions." Instead, they lay out careful, logical reasons *why* we can trust God: His character, His attributes, and His nature. The lectures are actually answering the kinds of questions that doubts bring up. They take doubt seriously enough to respond to it.

There's a wonderful moment in the Bible where a father brings his sick child to Jesus and asks for help. Jesus tells him that all things are possible to those who believe. The father's response is one of the most honest prayers in all of scripture: **"Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief"** (Mark 9:24). He didn't pretend he had perfect faith. He admitted he was struggling and then asked the Lord to help him with that struggle. And Jesus healed his child anyway.

That's an important pattern. You don't have to have *perfect* faith to act on your faith. You just have to act on what you *do* know, even while you're still working through what you don't know.

The Lectures on Faith teach that the opposite of faith isn't doubt. It's **inaction**. If you sit around doing nothing because you're not 100% sure, that's when faith dies. But if you keep praying, keep studying, keep going to church, and keep trying to keep your covenants, even when you have questions, your faith stays alive and actually grows stronger.

Think about planting a seed. Alma 32 talks about this. You plant the seed not because you already know exactly what the tree will look like, but because you want to find out. You experiment. You give it a chance. That's what faith looks like when you have doubts. You keep planting seeds, keep watering them, and keep watching for growth.

Some of the greatest people in the scriptures had moments of doubt. Joseph Smith went to the Sacred Grove because he was confused and didn't know which church was right. His doubt led him to pray, and that prayer changed the world.

So if you have doubts, don't be afraid of them. Don't ignore them either. Bring them to God. Keep doing the things that invite the Spirit. Keep learning, keep praying, and keep keeping your covenants. That's exactly what the Lectures on Faith are asking you to do: act on what you know, and trust that more understanding will come.
