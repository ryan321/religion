### Lesson 13.3 -- What About "Blind Faith"?

You might have heard someone say, "I just have blind faith," meaning they believe in God without really knowing why. It sounds humble, and the intention is good. But the Lectures on Faith actually argue *against* that kind of faith. They teach that real faith isn't blind at all. It's wide open.

Think about it. The lectures don't just say, "Believe in God." They spend **five full lectures** explaining what you need to know about God before you can truly exercise faith in Him. Lecture 2 teaches you the history of how people learned about God from the beginning. Lecture 3 goes through God's character: that He is merciful, gracious, slow to anger, and full of goodness. Lecture 4 covers His attributes: knowledge, power, justice, judgment, mercy, and truth. Lecture 5 explains His nature and the Godhead.

Why would the lectures spend so much time on knowledge if faith were just about closing your eyes and jumping? They wouldn't. The whole point is that faith is *informed*. You learn the truth, you understand who God is, and *then* you act with confidence. That's not blind. That's seeing clearly and choosing to trust what you see.

The prophet Alma taught the same principle. He said, **"Experiment upon my words"** (Alma 32:27). An experiment isn't blind. It's a test. You try something, you watch what happens, and you learn from the results. Alma was saying, "Don't just take my word for it. Try it and see for yourself." That's the opposite of blind faith.

The Lectures on Faith take a similar approach. They present reasons, evidence, and logic for why you can trust God. They lay out the facts and then invite you to act on them. Your faith gets tested, proven, and grounded in something real.

Here's an example. If someone told you to jump off a cliff with your eyes closed, that would be blind faith. But if someone showed you a strong parachute, taught you how it works, helped you put it on correctly, and explained that thousands of people have used it safely, and *then* you jumped, that's informed faith. You're still making a leap, but you're doing it based on knowledge and preparation.

That's what the Lectures on Faith want for you. They want your faith to be built on a solid foundation of truth. They want you to know God so well that trusting Him feels as natural as trusting gravity. Not because you've turned off your brain, but because you've turned it on and pointed it toward the most important truths there are.

Real faith isn't blind. It's the clearest vision you'll ever have.
