### Lesson 7.1 -- Lecture 5: A Unique Teaching

Have you ever heard something at church that made you think, "Wait, that's different from what my friend at school believes"? One of the most unique teachings of the Restoration is what it says about God, specifically, what the **Godhead** actually is. Lecture 5 of the Lectures on Faith gives us one of the clearest early statements on this topic, and it's a big deal.

Most of the Christian world teaches that God is one being who appears in three forms (Father, Son, and Holy Ghost) all mysteriously blended together. But Lecture 5 teaches something very different. It says the **Godhead** is made up of **two personages**, God the Father and Jesus Christ the Son, both of whom have real, glorified **bodies of flesh and bones**. The third member, the **Holy Ghost**, is described as a **personage of Spirit**, meaning He is a real person but does not have a physical body.

Think about that for a moment. The Father has a body. The Son has a body. The Holy Ghost is a spirit person. Three distinct, separate beings, not one being wearing three masks or showing up in three different forms.

This teaching was later confirmed beautifully in **Doctrine and Covenants 130:22**, which says: "The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's; the Son also; but the Holy Ghost has not a body of flesh and bones, but is a personage of Spirit." That verse lines up perfectly with what Lecture 5 had already taught years earlier.

Why does this matter? Because if you want to have faith in God, you need to know what He actually is. You can't fully trust someone you don't understand. Lecture 5 pulls back the curtain and says, "Here is what the Godhead really looks like." There's no mystery or confusion, just clear truth.

This was revolutionary in the 1830s, and it's still distinctive today. When someone asks you what Latter-day Saints believe about God, you can point to this teaching: the Godhead is three separate beings (the Father, the Son, and the Holy Ghost) united perfectly in purpose but distinct as individuals. Over the next three lessons, we'll look at each member of the Godhead one at a time.
