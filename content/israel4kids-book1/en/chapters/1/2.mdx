### Lesson 1.2 -- What the Land Looks Like

You might think of Israel as a big, powerful country since so much of the Bible takes place there. But here is a surprise -- Israel is tiny. It is roughly the size of **New Jersey**. You could drive from the top to the bottom in just a few hours. Yet this little strip of land has some of the most dramatic geography on the planet.

Start at the west side. Along the **Mediterranean Sea**, you will find flat **coastal plains**. This is good farmland, and in ancient times it was where trade ships came and went. The **Philistines** -- the people Samson and David fought -- lived along this coast.

Move inland and the ground rises into **rolling hills and mountains**. This central hill country is where many of the most important Bible cities sit, including **Jerusalem** and **Bethlehem**. The hills made good natural defenses. Armies had a hard time attacking uphill, so the Israelites often settled in this rugged terrain.

Keep going east and you drop down -- fast. The **Jordan River** flows from north to south through a deep valley called the **Rift Valley**. The Jordan empties into the **Dead Sea**, which sits at the lowest point on earth -- about 1,400 feet below sea level. The Dead Sea is so salty that nothing lives in it. You could float on it without even trying.

Head south and you hit the **Negev**, a dry desert region. It gets very little rain. Some of the patriarchs, like Abraham, spent time in the Negev with their flocks, moving from well to well.

Now here is why all this geography matters: Israel sits at the **crossroads** of three continents. To the southwest is **Egypt**. To the northeast is **Mesopotamia** (modern Iraq), where empires like Assyria and Babylon rose. To the west, across the sea, are Greece and Rome. Any army marching between these empires had to pass right through Israel's land. That made Israel incredibly important -- and incredibly vulnerable. Throughout the Old Testament, you will see Israel caught between big empires, and now you know why.

God did not pick this land by accident. Its location meant that Israel would interact with every major civilization of the ancient world. The gospel, the scriptures, and the prophecies would spread from this tiny crossroads to the rest of the earth.
