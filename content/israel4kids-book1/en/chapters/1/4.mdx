### Lesson 1.4 -- The Twelve Tribes Settle the Land

Imagine your family owns a big piece of property and you need to divide it up among twelve relatives. Everyone wants the best spot. Some want farmland. Others want to be near water. Nobody wants the desert. Now multiply that by thousands of families, and you will get a sense of what Joshua had to do.

After the major battles were over, Joshua **divided the land of Canaan among the twelve tribes** of Israel. Each tribe received its own **territory**, its own region to settle, farm, and govern. This was not random. Joshua cast lots (a way of seeking God's will, similar to drawing names) to determine which tribe got which area.

Here is a quick tour of who lived where. **Judah** received a large territory in the south, including the area around what would later become Jerusalem. Judah was the biggest and most powerful tribe, and the royal line of David would come from Judah. **Ephraim** and **Manasseh** (the two tribes descended from Joseph) settled in the central hill country. If you have a patriarchal blessing that says you are from Ephraim, this is the tribe's original homeland. **Benjamin** got a small but important strip between Judah and Ephraim. **Dan** was assigned land along the coast, but they eventually migrated north because they could not hold their territory against the Philistines.

Other tribes (**Issachar**, **Zebulun**, **Naphtali**, and **Asher**) settled in the north, in the region later called Galilee. **Reuben**, **Gad**, and the other half of Manasseh actually settled east of the Jordan River, on land they had requested from Moses before the conquest even began.

One tribe got no land at all. The **Levites** were set apart for priestly service. Instead of a territory, they received 48 cities scattered throughout the other tribes' lands. They served in the tabernacle and later the temple, and the other tribes supported them with tithes.

Here is what made this setup unusual: there was **no central government**. No king. No capital city. No national army. Each tribe governed itself. They were connected by their shared covenant with God, the tabernacle worship, and their family ties. But day to day, they operated independently. It was more like a loose alliance than a unified country.

This system worked as long as the people stayed faithful. But when they drifted from the covenant, the lack of unity would become a serious weakness.
