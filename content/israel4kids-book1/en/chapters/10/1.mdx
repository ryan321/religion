### Lesson 10.1 -- Alexander the Great

Imagine being only twenty years old and leading an army that conquers most of the known world. That is basically what **Alexander the Great** did. By the time he was your parents' age, he had built one of the largest empires in history. And his conquests changed the land of Israel forever.

In **332 BC**, Alexander swept through the Near East with his Greek army. He had already defeated the mighty **Persian Empire**, which had controlled Judea for about two hundred years. Now Judea -- the land where the Jews lived -- fell under Greek control. According to an old Jewish tradition, when Alexander arrived in Jerusalem, the high priest came out to meet him and showed him the book of **Daniel**. Daniel's prophecies described a powerful Greek ruler who would conquer the Persians. Alexander was impressed. Whether or not that story happened exactly that way, it tells us something important: the Jews saw God's hand in these world-shaking events, and they believed their prophets had seen it all coming.

But here is the twist. Alexander died young -- only **32 years old** -- and he never named a successor. So his enormous empire got carved up among his generals, like a pizza split between friends who cannot agree on toppings. Two of those pieces matter most for our story: **Egypt**, ruled by a family called the **Ptolemies**, and **Syria**, ruled by a family called the **Seleucids**.

Guess where Judea sat? Right in the middle. The tiny Jewish homeland was sandwiched between these two powerful Greek kingdoms. Both wanted to control it because it sat on the land bridge connecting Africa to Asia. For the next century and a half, Judea would get passed back and forth like a football between the Ptolemies and Seleucids. Sometimes the handoff was peaceful. Sometimes it was not.

This pattern should sound familiar by now. Israel's location -- that promised land right in the center of everything -- kept putting it in the path of every empire that rose to power. Assyria, Babylon, Persia, and now Greece. God's covenant people lived on some of the most fought-over real estate in history.

Alexander may have died young, but his influence did not die with him. The culture he spread would create the biggest challenge the Jews had faced since Babylon. That challenge had a name: **Hellenism**. And we will talk about it next.
