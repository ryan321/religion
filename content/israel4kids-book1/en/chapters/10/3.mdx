### Lesson 10.3 -- Under the Ptolemies and Seleucids

After Alexander's empire split up, Judea first landed under the control of the **Ptolemies**, the Greek family that ruled Egypt. This actually was not too bad. For about a hundred years (roughly 301 to 198 BC), the Ptolemies mostly left the Jews alone. They collected taxes, of course (rulers always collect taxes), but they let the Jews worship in the temple, follow the Torah, and govern their own religious life. The high priest served as the local leader, and daily life in Jerusalem continued without too much interference.

During this time, something really important happened. The Jewish community in **Alexandria**, Egypt, had grown large, and many of those Jews spoke Greek better than Hebrew. So scholars translated the Hebrew scriptures into Greek. This translation is called the **Septuagint**. It was a big deal because it meant people all over the Greek-speaking world could now read the word of God. Later, when the apostles spread the gospel of Jesus Christ, they often quoted from the Septuagint. So this Greek translation helped prepare the world for the New Testament.

Then things changed. Around **198 BC**, the **Seleucids** (the Greek kingdom based in Syria) defeated the Ptolemies and took control of Judea. At first, the Seleucid rulers were fairly tolerant. They even gave the Jews special privileges. But the Seleucid Empire had problems. It was expensive to run, and it was losing territory to the growing power of Rome in the west. The kings needed money and they needed loyalty. Having one group of people in your empire who worshipped a different God, followed different laws, and refused to blend in was starting to look like a problem.

The real trouble came when **Antiochus IV** took the Seleucid throne. He was not the patient type. Antiochus did not care about Jewish traditions or the Torah. He wanted a unified empire where everyone worshipped the same gods and followed the same customs. Jewish distinctiveness was no longer something he was willing to tolerate.

Think of it like this: the Ptolemies were the easygoing substitute teacher who let you do your own thing. The early Seleucids were a stricter teacher who still gave you some freedom. Antiochus IV was the kind of ruler who demanded everyone do things his way, or face the consequences. And those consequences were about to get very, very serious.
