### Lesson 10.4 -- Antiochus and the Abomination

What happened in **167 BC** was one of the darkest moments in the history of God's covenant people. It was so terrible that both the Old Testament prophet Daniel and Jesus Christ himself would reference it.

**Antiochus IV** gave himself the nickname **"Epiphanes"**, which means "God Manifest." He literally claimed to be a god walking the earth. And he decided that the Jews needed to stop being Jewish. Not just in a casual way. He made it the law.

Here is what Antiochus ordered. **Circumcision** (the physical sign of the Abrahamic covenant) was banned. Mothers who circumcised their babies were killed, and their dead infants were hung around their necks as a warning. Keeping the **Sabbath** was outlawed. Owning or studying the **Torah** was punishable by death. Soldiers went through the land, burning every copy of the scriptures they could find.

Then came the worst part. Antiochus marched into the **temple in Jerusalem**, the holiest place on earth for the Jewish people, and set up a pagan altar to the Greek god Zeus right on top of the altar of God. He sacrificed a **pig** on it, knowing that pigs were unclean animals under the Law of Moses. He wanted to humiliate the Jews and destroy their faith.

The Jews called this the **"abomination of desolation."** Daniel had prophesied about it (Daniel 11:31), and centuries later Jesus would use the same phrase when warning his disciples about future destructions (Matthew 24:15). In Latter-day Saint understanding, this phrase points to more than one event. It is a pattern that repeats when sacred things are desecrated and God's people are persecuted.

Think about what this meant for ordinary families. Imagine being told that everything your family believes (your scriptures, your worship, your covenants) is now illegal. That you could be killed for praying or teaching your children the commandments. Some Jews gave in. They were scared, or they figured it was not worth dying over. But many refused. They chose faithfulness over safety.

Antiochus expected the Jews to surrender their identity and disappear into Greek culture. He was wrong. Instead of crushing their faith, his cruelty lit a fire. Faithful Jews decided that if they had to choose between their covenants and their lives, they would choose their covenants. A revolt was coming.
