### Lesson 11.2 -- Judah Maccabee and the Temple

After Mattathias died, his son **Judah Maccabee** took charge, and he turned out to be a military genius. Against every expectation, Judah led his ragged band of fighters to a series of stunning victories against the professional Seleucid army.

Judah used the hilly terrain of Judea to his advantage. He avoided open battles where the Seleucids' superior numbers and war elephants would crush him. Instead, he used **guerrilla tactics**: ambushes, night attacks, hit-and-run raids through mountain passes. The Seleucid generals kept sending larger and larger armies, and Judah kept defeating them. It was like a middle school basketball team beating the pros, except the stakes were life and death.

In **164 BC**, just three years after the revolt began, Judah and his fighters did something no one thought possible. They **recaptured Jerusalem**. When they reached the temple, they found it in terrible shape. The pagan altar to Zeus was still standing. The sacred objects had been stolen or defiled. The whole place was a mess.

Judah's men got to work. They tore down the pagan altar and built a **new altar** from uncut stones, following the instructions in the Torah. They made new sacred vessels, lit the **menorah**, and burned incense. Then they **rededicated** the temple to God. The celebration lasted eight days.

This rededication is what we know as **Hanukkah**, the **Festival of Dedication**. The word "Hanukkah" means "dedication" in Hebrew. You might know the later tradition about a small jar of oil miraculously lasting eight days, but the original celebration was about something even bigger: God's house was clean and holy again.

Here is something interesting. Jesus himself observed Hanukkah. In **John 10:22**, it says Jesus was in Jerusalem during the "Feast of Dedication." He walked in Solomon's porch of the temple and taught the people. So this holiday that started with the Maccabees was still being celebrated over 150 years later, and the Savior honored it with his presence.

The rededication of the temple was a moment of pure triumph. After years of persecution, desecration, and war, God's covenant people had taken back their holiest place and restored proper worship. It showed that even when sacred things are desecrated, they can be made holy again.
