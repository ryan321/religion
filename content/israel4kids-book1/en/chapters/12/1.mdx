### Lesson 12.1 -- Pompey and the Roman Conquest

In **63 BC**, the Roman general **Pompey** marched his legions into Jerusalem. He was not there to destroy the city, at least not this time. He was there because two squabbling Jewish princes had literally asked him to come settle their argument. Rome was happy to help. Rome was always happy to help, because "helping" meant taking over.

Pompey quickly took control of the city. But then he did something that shocked everyone. He walked straight into the **temple** and entered the **Holy of Holies**, the innermost room, the most sacred space in all of Judaism. Only the high priest was allowed in there, and only once a year on the Day of Atonement. For a pagan Roman general to barge in was an outrage.

What Pompey found (or rather, did not find) surprised him. Roman temples were full of statues of gods. Greek temples had massive idols. But the Holy of Holies was **empty**. No statue. No idol. Just an empty room where God's presence dwelt. Pompey could not understand a God you could not see or touch. But he had just stumbled onto one of the most important truths in the universe: the God of Israel is not a carved image. He is real, living, and invisible.

Pompey did not loot the temple treasury (that would come later with other Romans), but the damage was done in a different way. From this moment forward, **Judea was under Roman control**. The Jewish independence that the Maccabees had fought and died for was over. It had lasted about eighty years.

Rome did not always rule its territories the same way. Sometimes it appointed local kings. Sometimes it sent Roman governors. Sometimes it let existing leaders stay in place as long as they obeyed. Judea would experience all of these approaches over the coming decades. But the bottom line was always the same: Rome was in charge.

For the Jewish people, this was another painful chapter in a long story. Assyria, Babylon, Persia, Greece, and now Rome. Empire after empire had rolled over the promised land. But the prophets had said that one day a king from David's line would come and establish a kingdom that would never be destroyed. The Jews held onto that promise. They just did not yet understand what kind of kingdom it would be.
