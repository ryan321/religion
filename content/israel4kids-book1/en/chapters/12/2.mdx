### Lesson 12.2 -- Herod the Great

If you could describe **Herod the Great** in two words, they might be "brilliant" and "terrifying." He was one of the greatest builders in the ancient world -- and one of the most paranoid, cruel rulers who ever lived.

Rome appointed Herod **"King of the Jews"** in **37 BC**. He was not really Jewish by heritage. His family were **Idumeans** -- descendants of the Edomites -- who had been forced to convert to Judaism during the Hasmonean period. So right from the start, many Jews did not accept him as a legitimate king. He was not from the tribe of Judah. He was not from David's line. He was Rome's man, placed on the throne to keep order and collect taxes.

But Herod could build. He constructed massive **fortresses** like Masada and Herodium. He built the port city of **Caesarea Maritima**, complete with an artificial harbor, an aqueduct, a theater, and a hippodrome. His most ambitious project was expanding the **temple in Jerusalem**. Herod's Temple, as it came to be known, was one of the wonders of the ancient world. He doubled the size of the Temple Mount, built enormous retaining walls (the Western Wall that still stands today is part of Herod's construction), and covered the temple itself in gold and white stone that blazed in the sunlight. Even the rabbis said, "He who has not seen Herod's temple has never seen a beautiful building."

But behind all that grandeur was a monster. Herod was consumed by **paranoia** -- he constantly feared that someone was plotting to take his throne. He executed his own wife **Mariamne**, whom he reportedly loved. He killed three of his own **sons** because he suspected them of disloyalty. The Roman Emperor Augustus supposedly joked that it was safer to be Herod's pig than Herod's son.

This is the same Herod who appears in the Christmas story. When the wise men came looking for a newborn "King of the Jews," Herod saw the baby as a threat. He ordered the **massacre of all infant boys** in Bethlehem (Matthew 2:16). Joseph was warned in a dream and took Mary and Jesus to Egypt. Herod's cruelty was real, and it reached right into the story of the Savior's birth.

Herod died in **4 BC**, shortly after Jesus was born. His kingdom was divided among his surviving sons, none of whom had his talent -- though some inherited his cruelty.
