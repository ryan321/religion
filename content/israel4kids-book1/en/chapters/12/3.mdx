### Lesson 12.3 -- Life Under Rome

Living under Roman rule was a mixed experience. Rome brought some genuinely useful things -- and also made life miserable in other ways.

On the positive side, Rome built **roads**. Excellent roads that connected every corner of the empire. These roads made travel faster and safer than it had ever been. Rome also built **aqueducts** -- engineered channels that carried fresh water into cities. Roman **law** created a system of order and justice that, while far from perfect, was more organized than what most conquered peoples had before. And the **Pax Romana** -- the "Roman Peace" -- meant that large-scale wars between nations mostly stopped. The empire was too powerful for anyone to challenge.

But peace came at a steep price. **Taxes** were heavy. Rome taxed everything -- land, crops, goods, even the right to use certain roads. To collect these taxes, Rome hired local people called **tax collectors** (or publicans). These were Jews who worked for Rome, and they were widely despised. They often charged more than they were supposed to and kept the extra for themselves. When you read about tax collectors in the New Testament -- like Matthew or Zacchaeus -- now you understand why people hated them.

Roman **soldiers** were everywhere. They could force you to carry their gear for a mile (which is why Jesus said to go two miles -- Matthew 5:41). Pagan **temples** and Roman **culture** pressed in from all sides. The **high priesthood**, which was supposed to be a sacred calling, became a political appointment. Rome decided who got to be high priest, choosing men who would cooperate rather than men who were actually righteous.

Different Jews responded to all of this in different ways. **Tax collectors** cooperated with Rome and got rich. **Sadducees** worked within the system, controlling the temple and keeping things stable. **Pharisees** focused on keeping every detail of the law, creating a fence of rules around the Torah. **Essenes** gave up on the whole system and retreated to the desert to live pure lives and wait for God to act. And **Zealots** wanted to fight -- they plotted rebellion and dreamed of throwing Rome out by force.

Everyone was waiting for something. The prophets had promised a **Messiah** -- an anointed king who would save Israel. Under the weight of Roman occupation, that hope burned brighter than ever.
