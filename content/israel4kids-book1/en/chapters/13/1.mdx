### Lesson 13.1 -- Pharisees, Sadducees, and Others

By the time Jesus was born, the Jewish people were not all one big group that agreed on everything. They had split into several different parties, kind of like how a school might have different clubs, each with its own set of rules and ideas. If you want to understand why people reacted so differently to Jesus, you need to know who these groups were.

The **Pharisees** were the largest and most popular group. Their name means something like "strict observers." They believed in keeping every detail of the Law of Moses, and then some. Over the centuries, teachers had added hundreds of extra rules on top of the original commandments. These extra rules were called the **oral traditions**. For example, the Law of Moses said to keep the Sabbath day holy, but the Pharisees had detailed lists of exactly what counted as "work" on the Sabbath. They believed in the resurrection of the dead, in angels, and in a future Messiah. Many Pharisees were sincere and devoted. But Jesus often called out the ones who cared more about looking righteous than actually being righteous.

The **Sadducees** were a smaller but very powerful group. They were mostly wealthy priests and aristocrats who ran the temple. They were politically connected, friendly with whoever was in charge, whether that was a Jewish king or the Romans. Doctrinally, they only accepted the five books of Moses and rejected the oral traditions. They did not believe in the resurrection, angels, or spirits. You can see why they clashed with Jesus, who taught about all of those things.

The **Essenes** took a completely different approach. They thought the temple leadership was corrupt, so they withdrew from society. Some lived in desert communities, spending their days copying scriptures, praying, and keeping strict purity rules. The famous **Dead Sea Scrolls** were likely preserved by Essenes.

Finally, the **Zealots** were the fighters. They believed God wanted them to drive the Romans out by force. They were willing to use violence, even assassination, to achieve their goals. Their passion would eventually help spark a full-scale war with Rome.

Each of these groups thought they had the right answer for Israel. When Jesus came teaching with real authority, every group had to decide what to do with him. That tension runs through the entire New Testament.
