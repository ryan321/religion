### Lesson 13.2 -- The Temple in Jesus' Day

Imagine the biggest, most beautiful building you have ever seen. Now imagine it covered in white stone and gold, sitting on top of a hill, visible for miles in every direction. That was the temple in Jerusalem during Jesus' lifetime -- and it was one of the most impressive religious buildings in the entire ancient world.

This temple is often called **Herod's Temple** because King Herod the Great had it massively expanded and rebuilt. Herod wanted it to be stunning, and it was. The project took decades. Jewish teachers said, "Whoever has not seen Herod's temple has never seen a beautiful building." The temple mount itself was a huge platform -- roughly the size of several football fields.

The temple complex was organized into a series of **courts**, each one more restricted than the last. The outermost area was the **Court of the Gentiles**. Anyone could enter here -- Jewish or not. This is where the **money changers** and animal sellers set up shop. People traveling to Jerusalem needed to exchange their foreign coins for temple currency and buy animals for sacrifices. When Jesus overturned the money changers' tables, this is exactly where it happened. He said they had turned his Father's house into "a den of thieves."

Moving inward, you reached the **Court of Women**, where Jewish women could worship but go no further. Next came the **Court of Israel**, open only to Jewish men. Then the **Court of the Priests**, where the altar of sacrifice stood and only priests could serve. Finally, at the very center, stood the **Holy of Holies** -- the most sacred spot on earth to the Jewish people. Only the high priest entered it, and only once a year on the Day of Atonement.

The temple was not just a place of worship. It was the center of Jewish economic and political life too. The Sanhedrin met there. Tax money flowed through it. Priests held enormous influence. Losing the temple would mean losing everything -- which is exactly what made its eventual destruction so devastating.

For Latter-day Saints, temples are sacred places of covenant-making. The Jerusalem temple served a similar role in its day. It was the place where God's presence dwelt among his people, and everything pointed toward the Savior and his atoning sacrifice.
