### Lesson 13.3 -- Roman Governors and Local Rule

When Herod the Great died in 4 BC, his kingdom did not stay in one piece. Rome divided it among three of his sons. But those sons were not nearly as capable as their father, and Rome eventually got tired of the arrangement. In Judea -- the region around Jerusalem -- Rome replaced the local ruler with a Roman governor.

These governors were called **prefects** or **procurators**. They were mid-level Roman officials, not the most important people in the empire, but they held life-and-death power over the people they governed. The most famous of these governors was **Pontius Pilate**, who served from about 26 to 36 AD -- right during Jesus' ministry.

Pilate had a mixed reputation. Roman and Jewish sources describe him as stubborn and sometimes brutal. He did not always understand -- or care about -- Jewish religious feelings. But he also had to keep the peace, or Rome would replace him. That tension helps explain his behavior during Jesus' trial. He did not seem to think Jesus was guilty of a real crime, but he faced a crowd demanding execution and Jewish leaders threatening to report him to the emperor.

For local matters, especially religious ones, the Jews had their own governing council called the **Sanhedrin**. This was a group of about seventy leaders, drawn mostly from the Pharisees and Sadducees. The **high priest** served as its president. The Sanhedrin handled religious law, settled disputes, and managed daily affairs of Jewish life.

But here was the catch -- the Sanhedrin could not carry out a death sentence on its own. Only Rome had that authority. This is exactly why Jesus was tried twice: first before the Sanhedrin on charges of blasphemy, and then before Pilate on charges of claiming to be a king. The Sanhedrin needed Pilate's approval to have Jesus executed, and Pilate needed a political reason -- not just a religious one -- to grant it.

This system of shared power -- Roman military authority on top, Jewish religious authority underneath -- created constant friction. Both sides needed each other, and both sides resented it. That friction would eventually explode into full-scale war.
