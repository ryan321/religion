### Lesson 13.4 -- Galilee and Judea

If you read the New Testament carefully, you will notice that Jesus spent a lot of time traveling. He walked from town to town, teaching as he went. The geography of the land matters because it shaped how his ministry unfolded.

The land of Israel in Jesus' day was divided into several regions. The two most important for understanding the Gospels are **Galilee** in the north and **Judea** in the south.

**Galilee** was where Jesus grew up. It was a rural, agricultural region with green hills, farms, and fishing villages around the **Sea of Galilee** -- which is really more of a large freshwater lake, about thirteen miles long and eight miles wide. The towns of **Nazareth**, where Jesus was raised, and **Capernaum**, which became his base during his ministry, were both in Galilee. Most of his apostles were Galilean fishermen -- Peter, Andrew, James, and John all made their living on that lake.

People from Galilee had a reputation in the south. They spoke with a distinct accent and were sometimes looked down on as uneducated country people. When Nathanael first heard about Jesus, he said, "Can anything good come out of Nazareth?" That tells you something about how Galileans were viewed.

**Judea** was the religious and political center of the Jewish world. **Jerusalem** sat in the hills of Judea, and the temple dominated the city. This is where the Sanhedrin met, where the high priest served, and where Pontius Pilate came during major festivals to keep order. Every faithful Jew tried to travel to Jerusalem for the great feasts -- Passover, Pentecost, and the Feast of Tabernacles.

Between Galilee and Judea lay **Samaria**, home of the Samaritans. Most Jews avoided passing through Samaria because of centuries of bad blood between the two peoples. The **Jordan River** ran north to south, connecting the Sea of Galilee to the **Dead Sea**. Near the southern end of the Jordan sat **Jericho**, and just east of Jerusalem was **Bethany**, where Jesus' friends Mary, Martha, and Lazarus lived.

These are all real places. You can visit them today. Walking from Nazareth to Jerusalem would take about three or four days on foot. When you read the Gospels and picture these distances, the stories come alive in a new way. Jesus did not just teach ideas -- he walked dusty roads to reach real people in real places.
