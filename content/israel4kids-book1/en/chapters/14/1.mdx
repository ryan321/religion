### Lesson 14.1 -- The Church in Jerusalem

After Jesus was crucified, resurrected, and ascended into heaven, you might think the story pauses. But it does not. The apostles stayed in Jerusalem and got right to work building the Church.

The **early Church** in Jerusalem was a tight-knit community. Believers met in each other's homes for worship, prayer, and the breaking of bread. But they also continued going to the **temple**. That might surprise you. Why would followers of Jesus still go to the Jewish temple? Because at this point, they were still Jewish. They kept the **Law of Moses**. They observed the Sabbath. They saw themselves as Jews who had found the promised Messiah, not as members of a brand-new religion.

The Church grew quickly. On the day of **Pentecost**, Peter preached and about three thousand people were baptized. The apostles performed miracles, taught boldly, and shared everything they had with each other. It was an exciting time, but also a dangerous one.

The **Sanhedrin** (the same council that had pushed for Jesus' death) was not happy. They arrested Peter and John and ordered them to stop teaching. The apostles refused. The tensions kept building. A faithful man named **Stephen** became the first person killed for his testimony of Christ. He was **stoned to death** by an angry mob. Not long after, the apostle **James** (the brother of John) was executed by **Herod Agrippa**, a grandson of Herod the Great who ruled as a local king under Rome.

As persecution increased, many believers scattered to other cities and regions. This actually helped the gospel spread, but it also meant the Church in Jerusalem faced constant pressure.

Latter-day Saints understand that something else was happening during this period too. Over time, as apostles were killed and not replaced, the full authority of the priesthood was lost. Teachings were changed. The ordinances were altered. This is what we call the **Great Apostasy**, the gradual falling away from the fulness of the gospel that Jesus had established. It would not be restored until the Prophet Joseph Smith, nearly 1,800 years later.
