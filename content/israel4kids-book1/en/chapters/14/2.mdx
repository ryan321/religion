### Lesson 14.2 -- Growing Tensions with Rome

The decades after Jesus' death were a slow-burning fuse. From about 40 AD to the mid-60s AD, the relationship between the Jewish people and their Roman rulers got worse and worse. It was like two kids on a playground who keep bumping into each other, except both sides had real power, real grievances, and real weapons.

A big part of the problem was the **Roman governors**. Remember, after Pontius Pilate, Rome kept sending officials to govern Judea. Many of them were corrupt, greedy, or simply did not care about Jewish religious beliefs. Some stole from the temple treasury. Others deliberately offended Jewish customs. One governor set up pagan images in Jerusalem, which was a slap in the face to a people who believed deeply in the commandment against idols.

The Jewish people were not passive about any of this. There were **protests** and small **revolts** throughout this period. Crowds would gather and demand that offensive actions be reversed. Sometimes it worked. Sometimes the Romans responded with violence.

A group called the **Sicarii** (a more extreme branch of the Zealots) began carrying out assassinations. The name comes from the Latin word for a small dagger, or *sica*. They would slip into crowds during festivals, stab a Roman sympathizer or a Jewish leader they considered a traitor, and disappear. It was terrifying, and it made everyone suspicious of everyone else.

As the years went on, the **moderate** voices (people who wanted to work within the system and avoid war) were losing influence. A new generation was growing up that had never known anything but Roman occupation and abuse. They were angry, and they were done waiting.

Think of it like a pot of water on a stove. The heat had been turned up gradually for decades. By the early 60s AD, the water was starting to bubble. Everyone in Judea could feel that something big and terrible was coming. The only question was when it would boil over.

The answer came in 66 AD.
