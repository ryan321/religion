### Lesson 14.3 -- The Jewish Revolt Begins (66 AD)

In 66 AD, the pot finally boiled over.

The Roman governor at the time was a man named **Gessius Florus**, and he was one of the worst. He raided the temple treasury, taking a huge amount of money. When the people protested, his soldiers killed civilians in the streets. That was the last straw.

The Jewish people rose up in full **revolt**. Rebels seized control of Jerusalem. They attacked the Roman garrison (the soldiers stationed in the city) and wiped them out. In the fortress of **Masada**, far to the south near the Dead Sea, Zealots overran the Roman troops and took the stronghold. All across the land, Jewish fighters drove out Roman forces.

It was an astonishing start. For a moment, it must have felt like the days of the Maccabees all over again, a small, passionate people standing up to an empire. But there was a critical difference this time. Rome in 66 AD was not the declining Seleucid Empire. Rome was the most powerful military force in the world, and it was not going to let a small province break free.

Emperor **Nero** sent one of his best generals, **Vespasian**, to crush the rebellion. Vespasian brought a massive, well-trained army of around 60,000 soldiers. He did not rush. The Romans were patient and methodical. They started in **Galilee**, in the north, and conquered it region by region, town by town. One of the Jewish commanders in Galilee was a man named **Josephus**, who surrendered to the Romans and eventually became a historian. His writings are one of our main sources for everything that happened next.

After Galilee fell, Vespasian turned south toward Jerusalem. But then something unexpected happened. Emperor Nero died, and a power struggle erupted in Rome. Vespasian himself was declared emperor, so he left the war to his son, **Titus**. Titus would finish what his father started.

The rebels inside Jerusalem still held the city. But the Roman army was coming, and it was only a matter of time.
