### Lesson 15.1 -- The Siege of Jerusalem (70 AD)

In the spring of 70 AD, Roman general **Titus** arrived at the walls of Jerusalem with four full **legions** -- roughly 60,000 to 80,000 trained soldiers. The siege of Jerusalem had begun, and it would become one of the most devastating events in ancient history.

Titus surrounded the city and cut it off from the outside world. No food could get in. No one could get out. The Romans built a wall of their own around the entire city -- called a **circumvallation** -- to make sure the blockade was airtight. If you think about what that means for the people trapped inside, it is horrifying. The food stores had already been burned by the feuding factions. Now there was no way to replace them.

**Starvation** spread quickly. People ate leather, straw, anything they could find. A Jewish historian named **Josephus** -- the same man who had surrendered to the Romans in Galilee -- was present during the siege. He worked for the Romans now, and he wrote down everything he saw. His descriptions of the famine inside the walls are some of the most terrible passages in ancient literature. He wrote about families fighting over scraps and people dying in the streets.

Meanwhile, the Roman army went to work with its famous **engineering**. They built massive ramps of earth and stone against the walls. They brought up battering rams. They constructed siege towers so soldiers could fight from above. The Romans were not just strong -- they were patient and methodical. They had done this to dozens of cities before.

The Jewish defenders fought back with incredible courage. They made daring raids out of the city. They poured boiling oil on the attackers. They set Roman siege equipment on fire. But the numbers and resources were overwhelmingly against them. Every day, the defenders got weaker from hunger while the Romans stayed well supplied.

It took several months, but the Romans breached the outer walls, then the second wall, and then pushed into the city itself. The final target was the temple mount -- the heart of Jerusalem and the heart of the Jewish faith.
