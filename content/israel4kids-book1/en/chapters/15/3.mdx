### Lesson 15.3 -- What Was Lost

The destruction of the temple in 70 AD did not just destroy a building. It reshaped an entire religion.

For over a thousand years, the **temple** had been the center of Jewish worship. It was where sacrifices were offered, where priests served, where the presence of God dwelt. The entire system of the Law of Moses (the daily offerings, the festivals, the Day of Atonement rituals) depended on having a functioning temple. When the temple burned, all of that stopped. There has been no Jewish temple since.

**Animal sacrifices** ended permanently. The **Levitical priesthood** (the tribe of Levi, which had provided priests for generations) lost its central function. Without a temple, there was no altar, no Holy of Holies, no place for priests to perform their duties.

The **Sadducees**, who had built their power around the temple and the priesthood, simply disappeared as a group. With the temple gone, they had no base and no purpose. They faded from history within a generation.

What survived was the approach of the **Pharisees**. They had always emphasized personal study of the Torah, prayer, and the synagogue, things that did not require a temple. After 70 AD, their way of practicing Judaism became the foundation for what we know today as **Rabbinic Judaism**. Religious leadership shifted from priests to **rabbis**, teachers and scholars. Worship shifted from the temple to the **synagogue**. The focus moved from sacrifice to **Torah study**, prayer, and keeping the commandments in daily life.

In a way, the Pharisees' emphasis on learning and personal devotion saved Judaism from extinction. The religion adapted and survived, even without its most sacred building.

For Latter-day Saints, the destruction of the temple fits into a larger picture. Jesus fulfilled the Law of Moses through his Atonement. The sacrifices that pointed forward to him were no longer needed. And Jesus himself had foretold the temple's destruction. In the Book of Mormon, the Savior taught the Nephites that the law of sacrifice by the shedding of blood was done away, replaced by "a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (3 Nephi 9:19-20). The temple's fall was both a tragedy for the Jewish people and a fulfillment of prophecy.
