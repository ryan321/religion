### Lesson 2.3 -- Life During the Judges

What would it be like to live in a country with no president, no police, and no army? That is pretty close to what life was like for the Israelites during the time of the judges. It was a rough period, sometimes inspiring, sometimes chaotic, and often dangerous.

There was **no king** in Israel. There was **no capital city**. There was **no standing army**. Each of the twelve tribes governed itself in its own territory. If an enemy attacked one tribe, the other tribes might help, or they might not. It depended on relationships, distance, and whether anyone felt like showing up. This loose setup meant Israel was strong when united and weak when divided, and they were divided more often than not.

Worship was also scattered. The **tabernacle** (the portable temple the Israelites had carried through the wilderness) was set up at a place called **Shiloh**. It was supposed to be the center of worship for all the tribes. But traveling to Shiloh was not easy, especially for tribes living far away. So many people started worshiping at **local high places**, hilltop shrines that were convenient but problematic.

The problem with the high places was not just the location. Over time, Israelites began mixing their worship of the Lord with **Canaanite religious practices**. The Canaanites worshiped gods like Baal and Ashtoreth with rituals that were completely opposed to what God had commanded. Some Israelites tried to worship both, keeping the Lord's festivals while also bowing to idols. This blending of true worship with false worship is something prophets would condemn for centuries.

The book of Judges sums up this entire period with one blunt sentence: **"Every man did that which was right in his own eyes"** (Judges 21:25). That sounds like freedom, but in practice it meant chaos. Without shared standards and without leaders who pointed people toward God, everyone just made up their own rules. The result was not liberty. It was confusion, conflict, and decline.

Latter-day Saints might recognize a parallel in the Book of Mormon. After Christ visited the Nephites, there were about 200 years of peace and unity. But once the people stopped living the gospel, society fell apart quickly. The pattern is the same: when people abandon shared covenants, things unravel.

Life during the judges was not all bad. Families farmed, raised animals, celebrated harvests, and kept traditions alive. But without faithful leadership and covenant-keeping, the good times never lasted.
