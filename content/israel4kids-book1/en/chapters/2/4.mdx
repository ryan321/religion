### Lesson 2.4 -- Samuel: The Last Judge

Samuel's story starts before he was even born. His mother, **Hannah**, wanted a child desperately. Year after year, she prayed. Finally, she went to the tabernacle at Shiloh and made a vow: if God gave her a son, she would dedicate him to the Lord's service for his entire life. God answered her prayer, and she named the boy **Samuel**, which means "heard of God."

True to her word, Hannah brought young Samuel to the tabernacle and left him in the care of **Eli**, the high priest. Imagine being a small child, living in the tabernacle, helping the priest, and growing up surrounded by worship. That was Samuel's childhood.

One night, Samuel heard a voice calling his name. He thought it was Eli and ran to him. Eli sent him back to bed. It happened again. And again. Finally, Eli realized it was the Lord speaking to Samuel. He told the boy to answer, "Speak, Lord; for thy servant heareth" (1 Samuel 3:9). That night, God gave Samuel a message, and from that point on, everyone in Israel knew Samuel was a **prophet**.

Samuel served Israel in three roles at once: **judge**, **prophet**, and **priest**. He traveled a circuit through the land, settling disputes, teaching the people, and calling them to repentance. Under his leadership, Israel experienced real stability. He was honest, fair, and faithful to the Lord his entire life.

But as Samuel grew old, a problem emerged. His sons, **Joel** and **Abijah**, served as judges but were corrupt. They took bribes and perverted justice. The elders of Israel saw this and came to Samuel with a demand: **"Give us a king to judge us like all the nations"** (1 Samuel 8:5).

This request hurt Samuel deeply. But God told him something important: "They have not rejected thee, but they have rejected me, that I should not reign over them" (1 Samuel 8:7). The people were not just asking for a new form of government. They were saying that God's way of ruling through prophets and judges was not good enough. They wanted to be like everyone else.

Samuel warned them. A king would **tax** them, **draft** their sons into his army, and **take** their best land and flocks. The people listened to every word and said, "Nay; but we will have a king over us" (1 Samuel 8:19). So God told Samuel to give them what they asked for. The era of the judges was over. The era of the kings was about to begin.
