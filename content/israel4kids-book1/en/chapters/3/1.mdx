### Lesson 3.1 -- Israel Wants a King

For about 300 years, Israel had no king. God ruled through prophets and judges. But now the people had made up their minds -- they wanted a king, and they were not taking no for an answer.

The **elders of Israel** gathered and went to Samuel. Their argument had two parts. First, Samuel's sons were corrupt judges who took bribes. That was a real problem, and they were right to be upset about it. But their solution went further than just replacing bad judges. They said, **"Make us a king to judge us like all the nations"** (1 Samuel 8:5). That last phrase -- "like all the nations" -- was the real issue.

Israel was not supposed to be like all the nations. They were God's covenant people, set apart to live differently. Having a human king was not automatically wrong -- God had actually predicted it would happen (see Deuteronomy 17:14-15). But the people's motivation mattered. They were not asking for a king who would help them follow God better. They were looking at Egypt, Philistia, and Moab and saying, "We want what they have."

Samuel took the request to the Lord, and God told him to **warn the people** about what a king would cost. Samuel laid it out plainly. A king would take their sons and make them soldiers and servants. He would take their daughters to work in his kitchens. He would take the best of their fields, vineyards, and olive groves. He would take a tenth of their grain and flocks. "And ye shall cry out in that day because of your king," Samuel told them (1 Samuel 8:18).

The people heard every word. And they said no thanks to the warning. "Nay; but we will have a king over us; that we also may be like all the nations; and that our king may judge us, and go out before us, and fight our battles" (1 Samuel 8:19-20). They wanted someone visible -- a commander they could see riding at the head of an army. Trusting an invisible God was harder.

So God told Samuel to grant their request. He directed Samuel to a man from the tribe of **Benjamin** named **Saul**. Saul was tall, handsome, and from a good family. He looked the part. Samuel anointed him privately, and later Saul was presented to the nation. Israel had its first king.

Whether this would turn out to be a good idea -- well, that is the next lesson.
