### Lesson 3.2 -- Saul: A King Who Failed

Saul started strong. He was tall, head and shoulders above everyone else. He was humble at first, even hiding among the baggage when Samuel tried to present him to the nation. Early in his reign, he rallied the tribes and won battles. Things looked promising.

But Saul had a problem that showed up slowly and then all at once: he cared more about what people thought than what God commanded.

The first big failure came before a battle with the **Philistines**. Samuel told Saul to wait seven days for him to arrive and offer the sacrifice. Saul waited, but his soldiers started leaving. He panicked. Instead of trusting God's timing, he **offered the sacrifice himself**, something only a priest or prophet was authorized to do. When Samuel arrived, Saul made excuses. Samuel's response was devastating: "Thou hast done foolishly... now thy kingdom shall not continue" (1 Samuel 13:13-14).

The second failure was worse. God commanded Saul to completely destroy the **Amalekites** and everything they had. Saul won the battle but kept the best sheep and cattle alive and spared the Amalekite king, **Agag**. When Samuel confronted him, Saul claimed the animals were saved "to sacrifice unto the Lord." Samuel saw right through it: **"To obey is better than sacrifice"** (1 Samuel 15:22). Partial obedience is still disobedience.

After this, the **Spirit of the Lord departed** from Saul. He became moody, paranoid, and jealous, especially after a young shepherd named David started gaining attention. Saul's jealousy consumed him. He tried to kill David multiple times, hunted him across the wilderness, and even massacred an entire town of priests who had helped David.

Saul's story is a warning about what happens when someone has authority but stops listening to God. He was not a terrible person at the start. He had genuine gifts. But he made his own judgment more important than God's commands, and each wrong choice made the next one easier. By the end of his reign, he was consulting a witch for guidance, something the Law of Moses strictly forbade.

The lesson is uncomfortable but important: **looking like a king and being a good king are very different things.** Saul looked the part from day one. But faithfulness is not about appearance. It is about what you do when no one but God is watching. Israel's first experiment with kingship was a failure, but God already had someone better in mind.
