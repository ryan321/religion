### Lesson 3.4 -- The Covenant with David

Once David became king over all Israel, he did something that changed the nation forever. He conquered a fortress city held by a group called the **Jebusites** and made it his capital. That city was **Jerusalem**.

Jerusalem was a smart choice. It sat on the border between the northern and southern tribes, so neither side could claim favoritism. It was on high ground, easy to defend, and had a water source. David turned it into the political and spiritual center of Israel. He brought the **Ark of the Covenant** to Jerusalem with a huge celebration -- dancing, music, and sacrifices. For the first time in a long while, Israel had a united place of worship and government.

David wanted to build a permanent temple to house the Ark. He looked at his own palace made of cedar and felt it was wrong for the Ark of God to sit in a tent. But God sent the prophet **Nathan** with a surprising message. God told David he would not be the one to build the temple. Instead, God would build something for David -- a **house**, meaning a royal dynasty.

This is the **Davidic covenant**, and it is one of the most important promises in all of scripture. God told David: "Thine house and thy kingdom shall be established for ever before thee: thy throne shall be established for ever" (2 Samuel 7:16). A descendant of David would always sit on the throne. His line would rule forever.

Think about what "forever" means. Earthly kingdoms rise and fall. David's own kingdom would eventually split and be conquered. So how could this promise be fulfilled?

Latter-day Saints know the answer. The ultimate fulfillment of the Davidic covenant is **Jesus Christ**. The New Testament goes out of its way to establish that Jesus was born of David's lineage -- both Matthew and Luke include genealogies tracing the connection. The angel Gabriel told Mary that God would give her son "the throne of his father David: and he shall reign over the house of Jacob for ever" (Luke 1:32-33). Jesus is the eternal King from David's line.

The Book of Mormon also points to this. Prophets like Nephi and Abinadi taught that the Messiah would come through the house of Israel. The Davidic covenant is one thread in a larger tapestry that runs through the Old Testament, the New Testament, and the Restoration.

David was not a perfect man. He made serious sins later in life -- particularly with Bathsheba. But the covenant God made with him was not based on David's perfection. It was based on God's faithfulness to His own promises. David's repentance was deep and real, and God's plan moved forward through his lineage despite David's failures.

That is one of the most encouraging lessons in all of scripture: God keeps His covenants even when people stumble.
