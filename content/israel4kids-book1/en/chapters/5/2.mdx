### Lesson 5.2 -- The Northern Kingdom: Israel

After the split, the northern kingdom (called **Israel**) was the bigger of the two. It had **ten tribes**, more land, more people, and richer farmland. Its first capital was at **Shechem**, and later the capital moved to **Samaria**. On paper, the northern kingdom looked like the stronger nation.

But Jeroboam had a serious problem. The **temple** was in Jerusalem, and Jerusalem was in Judah, the southern kingdom. If his people kept traveling south to worship at the temple, Jeroboam worried they would eventually switch their loyalty back to Rehoboam. Their hearts would follow wherever they worshipped.

So Jeroboam came up with a solution. He made **two golden calves** and set them up at **Dan** in the far north and **Bethel** in the south. He told the people, "Here are your gods, O Israel, who brought you up out of Egypt." He also appointed priests who were not from the tribe of Levi and created his own religious festivals to replace the ones in Jerusalem.

Sound familiar? It should. The golden calf idea was basically the same mistake Aaron made in the wilderness while Moses was on Mount Sinai. Jeroboam knew the history. He did it anyway.

This was a **political decision** that became a **spiritual disaster**. Jeroboam was not trying to get people to stop believing in God altogether. He was trying to keep them from going to the temple. But once you start changing how God says to worship, things go downhill fast. Future kings of Israel kept the golden calves and added worse things on top of them: Baal worship, Asherah poles, and eventually even child sacrifice.

The Bible uses a phrase over and over about the northern kings: they "walked in the sins of Jeroboam." That is how bad his influence was. He set a pattern that almost no king after him was willing to break. One man's political calculation poisoned an entire nation's relationship with God for generations.

It is a good reminder that shortcuts in spiritual things almost never work out. Jeroboam thought he was being clever. He was actually leading his people away from God, and they would pay for it.
