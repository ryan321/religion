### Lesson 6.2 -- Elijah and Elisha

If there was ever a time the northern kingdom needed a prophet, it was during the reign of King **Ahab** and his wife **Jezebel**. Ahab was bad enough on his own, but Jezebel was something else entirely. She was a Phoenician princess who actively promoted **Baal worship** throughout Israel. She killed the Lord's prophets and replaced them with hundreds of priests of Baal. Israel's spiritual situation was about as bad as it could get.

Enter **Elijah**, one of the most dramatic prophets in all of scripture. God sent him straight into the mess. Elijah first announced a drought as punishment for Israel's wickedness. No rain fell for three and a half years. The land dried up. People suffered. And Ahab blamed Elijah for all of it.

Then came the showdown. Elijah challenged Ahab to a contest on **Mount Carmel**. He called out **450 priests of Baal**. The rules were simple: each side would prepare a sacrifice and call on their god to send fire. Whichever god answered with fire was the real God. The priests of Baal went first. They prayed, shouted, and danced all day. Nothing happened. Elijah even mocked them. "Maybe your god is sleeping," he said.

Then Elijah stepped up. He drenched his altar with water three times, just to make the point clear. He prayed one short, calm prayer. **Fire from heaven** fell and consumed the sacrifice, the stones, the dirt, and even the water in the trench. The people fell on their faces and cried, "The Lord, He is God!"

Elijah's ministry eventually ended in one of the most unique exits in the Bible. He was **taken to heaven in a chariot of fire**, translated, without dying. He still had work to do. Latter-day Saints know that Elijah later appeared in the Kirtland Temple in 1836, restoring the keys of sealing power to Joseph Smith.

Elijah's student, **Elisha**, continued the work. Elisha performed many miracles: healing a poisoned pot of food, multiplying oil for a widow, curing a foreign general named Naaman of leprosy, and even raising a child from the dead. Where Elijah was bold and fiery, Elisha was steady and compassionate. Together, they showed that God had not abandoned Israel, even when Israel had largely abandoned God.
