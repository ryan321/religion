### Lesson 7.4 -- Judah Survives -- Barely

The Northern Kingdom was gone. Assyria had swallowed it whole. Now the Assyrian war machine turned south, toward **Judah**. It looked like the end for God's people entirely.

In **701 BC**, the Assyrian king **Sennacherib** launched a massive invasion of Judah. He was not messing around. His army swept through the countryside, conquering city after city. He captured 46 fortified towns and claimed to have deported over 200,000 people. The great city of **Lachish** fell after a brutal siege. Sennacherib was so proud of that victory that he had detailed carvings made of it for his palace walls -- archaeologists have actually found them, and you can see them in the British Museum today.

Then Sennacherib turned to **Jerusalem**. He surrounded the city and sent messengers to King **Hezekiah** with a terrifying message: "No god has been able to save any nation from me. What makes you think your God is any different?"

That is a fair question, actually -- if you do not know who Israel's God really is.

But Hezekiah was not like the wicked kings of the north. He was one of Judah's best kings. The Bible says he "did that which was right in the sight of the Lord" (2 Kings 18:3). When he received Sennacherib's threatening letter, he did something powerful: he went to the temple, spread the letter out before the Lord, and **prayed**. He did not panic. He did not surrender. He asked God for help.

The prophet **Isaiah** sent word to Hezekiah: do not be afraid. The Lord himself will defend Jerusalem. Sennacherib will not even shoot an arrow into the city.

That night, something incredible happened. The **angel of the Lord** went through the Assyrian camp, and **185,000 soldiers died**. The next morning, the greatest army in the world was shattered. Sennacherib packed up and went home to Nineveh, where he was later murdered by his own sons.

Jerusalem was saved. Judah survived. But this was a reprieve, not a permanent rescue. The prophets kept warning that if the people turned away from God, destruction would come from another direction. Judah had maybe another century of independence left. The clock was ticking.

Still, the lesson of Hezekiah is powerful. When he faced an impossible situation, he turned to God -- and God answered. That is a pattern worth remembering in your own life.
