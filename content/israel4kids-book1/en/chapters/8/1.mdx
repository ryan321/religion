### Lesson 8.1 -- Babylon Replaces Assyria

Empires do not last forever. Even the scariest bully on the playground eventually meets someone tougher. That is exactly what happened to Assyria.

For over a century, Assyria had been the most powerful empire in the world. But by the late 600s BC, it was weakening. Internal problems, rebellions, and constant warfare had taken their toll. Meanwhile, a new power was rising in southern Mesopotamia: **Babylon**.

In **612 BC**, the Babylonians teamed up with the Medes (a people from modern-day Iran) and attacked **Nineveh**, the Assyrian capital. The city that had terrorized the world for generations fell. The prophet Nahum in the Bible had predicted exactly this -- the destruction of Nineveh. Within a few years, the Assyrian Empire was completely gone.

The man who built Babylon into a superpower was **Nebuchadnezzar**. He became king in 605 BC and ruled for over 40 years. Nebuchadnezzar was brilliant -- a skilled military commander and a massive builder. He turned Babylon into one of the most spectacular cities in the ancient world. The famous **Hanging Gardens of Babylon** (one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World) are traditionally credited to him, though historians debate whether they actually existed.

But for the tiny kingdom of **Judah**, the rise of Babylon was terrible news. Judah was stuck in an awful spot geographically -- right between two great powers. To the south was **Egypt**, still strong and looking to expand. To the north and east was Babylon, hungry for conquest. Judah was the land in between, and both empires wanted control of it.

Judah's kings made one bad decision after another. They kept **rebelling** against Babylon, thinking Egypt would save them. Egypt never really came through. And worst of all, they **ignored the prophets**. God sent warning after warning -- through Jeremiah, through Habakkuk, through others -- that trusting in foreign alliances instead of the Lord would lead to disaster.

The people did not listen. Judah's kings played dangerous political games with empires they could not control, while turning away from the God who had actually saved them before. Nebuchadnezzar was watching, and he was not patient.
