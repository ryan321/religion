### Lesson 9.1 -- Cyrus the Great

After about 50 years under Babylonian rule, the Jewish people must have wondered if they would ever go home. Then something amazing happened. A new empire rose, and its king turned out to be one of the most important figures in Jewish history, even though he was not Jewish himself.

In **539 BC**, the **Persian Empire** conquered Babylon. The Persian king was named **Cyrus**, and history remembers him as **Cyrus the Great**, and he earned that title.

Cyrus was different from every empire-builder before him. The Assyrians had ruled through terror. The Babylonians had ruled through force. Cyrus ruled through **tolerance**. His approach was revolutionary: instead of destroying conquered peoples and scattering them, he let them keep their cultures, their religions, and their identities. He even sent conquered peoples back to their homelands.

Soon after conquering Babylon, Cyrus issued a **decree** (an official royal order) allowing the Jews to **return to Jerusalem and rebuild their temple**. He even gave back the gold and silver vessels that Nebuchadnezzar had looted from Solomon's Temple. This decree is recorded in the Bible in Ezra 1:1-4, and a version of it was found on the famous **Cyrus Cylinder**, an ancient clay document that archaeologists discovered in Iraq.

But here is the part that should give you chills. Over **150 years** before Cyrus was even born, the prophet **Isaiah** called him by name. Isaiah 44:28 says that God would say of Cyrus, "He is my shepherd, and shall perform all my pleasure: even saying to Jerusalem, Thou shalt be built; and to the temple, Thy foundation shall be laid." Isaiah 45:1 goes even further, calling Cyrus the Lord's **"anointed"**, using the same word (in Hebrew, **mashiach**) that we get "Messiah" from.

Think about that. God named a Persian king over a century before he was born and called him his anointed. Cyrus probably did not even worship the God of Israel. But God used him anyway to fulfill prophecy and deliver His people. The Lord can work through anyone, even people who do not know they are doing His work.

The exile was ending. The door was open. It was time to go home.
