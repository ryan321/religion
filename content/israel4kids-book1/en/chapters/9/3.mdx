### Lesson 9.3 -- Ezra and the Law

The temple was rebuilt. But a building is not enough to keep a people faithful. The Jews needed something more -- they needed to remember who they were and what God expected of them. That is where **Ezra** comes in.

Ezra was a **priest** and a **scribe** -- meaning he was both a religious leader and an expert in the scriptures. Around **458 BC**, about 80 years after the temple was finished, Ezra led a second group of Jews from Babylon back to Jerusalem. The Persian king gave him authority and resources for the journey.

When Ezra arrived, he was shocked. The Jewish community in Jerusalem had been drifting. Many people -- including priests and leaders -- had **intermarried** with the surrounding peoples who worshipped other gods. They were starting to adopt foreign customs and forget the **Law of Moses**. The very thing that made them God's covenant people was slipping away.

This might seem harsh to us, but you have to understand the context. The whole reason Judah had been conquered and exiled in the first place was that the people kept turning to foreign gods. Intermarriage with idol-worshipping neighbors was exactly how that had started before. Ezra saw history about to repeat itself, and he was not going to let it happen.

Ezra's response was dramatic. He tore his clothes (a sign of deep grief in that culture), pulled hair from his head and beard, and fell on his knees in public prayer. The people were moved. They agreed to reform.

Then Ezra did something that changed Judaism forever. He gathered all the people together and **publicly read the Law of Moses** to them. The whole community stood and listened for hours. Levites moved through the crowd explaining the meaning of what was being read. People wept when they heard the law, because they realized how far they had drifted.

This moment was a **turning point**. From this point on, the Jewish people became intensely focused on the **written word of God** as the center of their religious life. The Torah -- the first five books of the Bible -- became the foundation of everything. Reading, studying, and obeying the scriptures became the core of what it meant to be Jewish.

Ezra helped the people remember that being part of God's covenant is not just about living in the right land or having a temple. It is about knowing God's word and living by it. That is a lesson that never gets old.
