### Lesson 1.2 -- The Announcements

When the time finally came for Jesus to be born, God didn't just let it happen quietly. He sent **angels** to make announcements so that the right people would know what was happening -- and be ready.

The first big announcement came to a young woman named **Mary** in the town of **Nazareth**. The angel **Gabriel** appeared to her and told her something incredible: she had been chosen to be the mother of the **Son of God** (Luke 1:26-35). This was not a normal birth announcement. Gabriel explained that the baby would be called Jesus, that He would be great, and that His kingdom would never end. Mary was humble and faithful. She simply said, "Be it unto me according to thy word."

Mary wasn't the only one with big news. Her cousin **Elisabeth** was already expecting a baby -- a boy who would grow up to be **John the Baptist**. Elisabeth was older and had never been able to have children, so her pregnancy was a miracle too. When Mary visited Elisabeth, the baby inside Elisabeth leaped for joy. Elisabeth was filled with the Holy Ghost and recognized that Mary was carrying the Lord (Luke 1:41-43).

Then there was **Joseph**, the man Mary was engaged to marry. When Joseph found out Mary was expecting a baby, he was confused and worried. But an angel appeared to him in a dream and said, "Fear not" (Matthew 1:20-21). The angel explained that this child was from God and that Joseph should name Him Jesus, because He would save His people from their sins. Joseph trusted the angel and stayed with Mary.

Meanwhile, on the other side of the world, announcements were happening too. In the Book of Mormon, a prophet named **Samuel the Lamanite** stood on a city wall and told the people about signs that would appear when Jesus was born. He said there would be **a day, a night, and a day with no darkness** -- as if the sun never set (Helaman 14:3-4). He also said a **new star** would appear in the sky (Helaman 14:5-6). These were bold, specific predictions. Some people believed Samuel. Many did not.

But every announcement -- whether from Gabriel, a dream, or a prophet on a wall -- carried the same message: the Savior of the world was about to arrive.
