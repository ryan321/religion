### Lesson 11.4 -- The Betrayal

While Jesus was still in the garden, still recovering from the most painful experience in all of eternity, the silence was broken. A crowd appeared in the darkness -- armed with **swords and clubs**, carrying torches, sent by the **chief priests** and elders (Matthew 26:47). And leading them was someone Jesus knew well: **Judas Iscariot**, one of His own twelve apostles.

Judas had arranged a signal with the mob. He would identify Jesus with a **kiss** -- a greeting that was normally a sign of friendship and respect. Judas walked up to Jesus and said, "Hail, master," and kissed Him (Matthew 26:49). Jesus responded, "Friend, wherefore art thou come?" (Matthew 26:50). Even in that moment, Jesus called him "friend." Think about that. The man who was handing Him over to be killed, and Jesus still addressed him with kindness.

What happened next was chaos. The men seized Jesus. **Peter** -- loyal, impulsive Peter -- pulled out a sword and swung at the **high priest's servant**, cutting off his right ear. The servant's name was **Malchus** (John 18:10). Peter was ready to fight. He would have taken on the whole crowd if Jesus had let him.

But Jesus stopped him immediately. "Put up again thy sword into his place," He said, "for all they that take the sword shall perish with the sword" (Matthew 26:52). Then He did something remarkable. He reached out and **healed** the servant's ear (Luke 22:51). Even while being arrested, even while being betrayed, Jesus showed mercy. He healed the enemy's wound.

Jesus also pointed out something important. He told the crowd, "Thinkest thou that I cannot now pray to my Father, and he shall presently give me more than twelve legions of angels?" (Matthew 26:53). A Roman legion was about 6,000 soldiers. Twelve legions of angels -- over 72,000 -- could have rescued Him in an instant. He wasn't helpless. He wasn't trapped. He **chose** to be taken. He let it happen because the scriptures had to be fulfilled.

Then the worst part: **"All the disciples forsook him, and fled"** (Matthew 26:56). Every single one. The men who had followed Him for three years, who had promised to stand by Him, ran away into the night. Jesus was completely alone.

The betrayal shows the contrast between human weakness and divine strength. Judas chose money over loyalty. Peter chose violence before being told to stop. The disciples chose fear over faithfulness. And Jesus chose love, mercy, and obedience -- all the way to the end.
