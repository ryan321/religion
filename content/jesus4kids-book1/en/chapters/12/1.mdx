### Lesson 12.1 -- The Trials

After His arrest in Gethsemane, Jesus was dragged through a series of **trials** that lasted through the night and into the morning. These trials were illegal in several ways: held at night, rushed, and based on false testimony. But the men in charge didn't care about fairness. They had already decided what they wanted to do. They just needed an excuse.

First, Jesus was taken to **Annas**, a former high priest who still had a lot of influence behind the scenes (John 18:13). Annas questioned Him briefly and then sent Him to **Caiaphas**, the current high priest.

At the house of Caiaphas, the **Sanhedrin**, the ruling council of Jewish religious leaders, had gathered (Matthew 26:57-68). They brought in **false witnesses** to testify against Jesus, but the witnesses couldn't even agree with each other (Mark 14:56). Their stories fell apart. The whole trial was falling apart. So Caiaphas took matters into his own hands.

He stood up and asked Jesus directly: **"Art thou the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?"** (Mark 14:61). It was the most important question anyone had ever asked. And Jesus answered clearly: **"I am"** (Mark 14:62). He went further, saying they would see the Son of Man sitting on the right hand of power and coming in the clouds of heaven.

Caiaphas tore his robes, a dramatic gesture that showed outrage, and declared it **blasphemy** (Matthew 26:65). The council agreed. They condemned Jesus to death. They spat in His face. They blindfolded Him, struck Him, and mocked Him, saying, "Prophesy unto us, thou Christ, Who is he that smote thee?" (Matthew 26:67-68).

But there was a problem. The Sanhedrin could declare someone guilty, but under Roman rule they did not have the authority to carry out a death sentence. Only the **Roman governor** could do that. So they needed to bring Jesus to **Pontius Pilate**.

The trials reveal something powerful about Jesus. He was completely innocent. Even His enemies couldn't find real evidence against Him. The only "crime" He committed was telling the truth: that He was the Son of God. And that truth, spoken calmly in the middle of a rigged trial, was enough to seal His fate. He did not fight back. He did not defend Himself with miracles. He stood there and spoke the truth, knowing exactly where it would lead.
