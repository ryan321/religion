### Lesson 14.2 -- Appearances to the Disciples

The news spread fast, but not everyone believed it right away. Two disciples were walking from Jerusalem to a village called **Emmaus**, about seven miles down the road (Luke 24:13-35). They were sad and confused. They had hoped Jesus was the Messiah, but now He was dead. Or so they thought.

A stranger joined them on the road and asked what they were talking about. They were surprised He did not seem to know about the crucifixion -- it was all anyone was discussing. The stranger began to explain the scriptures to them, showing how the prophets had foretold that the Messiah would suffer and then be glorified. Their hearts burned within them as He spoke.

When they arrived at Emmaus, they invited the stranger to stay for supper. As He sat at the table, He took bread, blessed it, and broke it. In that moment, their eyes were opened. They recognized Him -- it was Jesus. And then He vanished from their sight (Luke 24:31). They jumped up and ran all the way back to Jerusalem to tell the others.

That same evening, Jesus appeared to the apostles in a locked room in Jerusalem (John 20:19-20). They were hiding behind closed doors because they were afraid of the Jewish leaders. Suddenly, Jesus stood among them. "Peace be unto you," He said. He showed them the wounds in His hands and His side. They were overjoyed.

But one apostle was missing. **Thomas** was not there that night. When the others told him what had happened, he refused to believe. "Except I shall see in his hands the print of the nails, and put my finger into the print of the nails, and thrust my hand into his side, I will not believe," he said (John 20:25).

A week later, Jesus appeared again. This time Thomas was present. Jesus looked straight at him and said, "Reach hither thy finger, and behold my hands; and reach hither thy hand, and thrust it into my side: and be not faithless, but believing" (John 20:27).

Thomas fell to his knees. "My Lord and my God," he said (John 20:28).

Then Jesus taught a lesson for everyone who would come after: "Blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed" (John 20:29). That includes you. You have not seen the risen Lord with your own eyes, but you can still believe -- and that belief is counted as a blessing.
