### Lesson 2.2 -- Growing Up in Nazareth

After the family returned from Egypt, they settled in a small town called **Nazareth** in the region of **Galilee** (Matthew 2:23). This is where Jesus spent most of His childhood and young adult years. It was an ordinary place -- a quiet village with maybe a few hundred people. Nothing fancy.

**Joseph** was a **carpenter**, which means he worked with wood, stone, and other building materials. In those days, a carpenter's son would learn his father's trade. So Jesus almost certainly spent years cutting wood, shaping beams, and building things alongside Joseph. He learned to work with His hands. He understood what it meant to be tired at the end of a long day.

The scriptures don't tell us a lot about Jesus' childhood. But Luke gives us one important verse: "And Jesus increased in wisdom and stature, and in favour with God and man" (Luke 2:52). That single sentence tells us four things. He grew **intellectually** -- He learned and got smarter. He grew **physically** -- He got taller and stronger. He grew **spiritually** -- His relationship with Heavenly Father deepened. And He grew **socially** -- people liked Him and respected Him.

That verse is actually a great pattern for your own life. You're growing in all four of those areas right now. School helps you increase in wisdom. Sports, sleep, and good food help you increase in stature. Prayer, scripture study, and church help you grow in favour with God. And being a good friend and family member helps you grow in favour with other people.

Here is something to think about: Jesus was the **literal Son of God**. He had a divine Father. He had power and potential beyond anything any other person has ever had. And yet He grew up like a regular kid. He lived in a small town. He learned a trade. He played with other children. He obeyed His parents. He went to **synagogue** on the Sabbath and studied the scriptures.

Why would the Son of God live such a normal childhood? Because He needed to understand what it was like to be human. He needed to experience real life so that later, when He taught and served and suffered, He would truly know what people go through. He wasn't pretending to be one of us. He was one of us.
