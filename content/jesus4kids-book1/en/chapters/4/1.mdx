### Lesson 4.1 -- The Twelve Apostles

Jesus had a lot of followers. Everywhere He went, people came to listen, to watch, and to be healed. But out of all those people, He needed to choose a small group who would help Him lead His church. These wouldn't just be fans in the crowd. They would be His closest helpers, His witnesses, and eventually the leaders who would carry on after He returned to heaven.

Before making this huge decision, Jesus did something important. He went up on a mountain and prayed all night long (Luke 6:12). Think about that. The Son of God, someone who already knew everything, still turned to His Father in Heaven before making a major choice. That tells you something about how important prayer is, even when you already have a good idea of what to do.

When morning came, He called His followers together and chose twelve of them. We call them the **Twelve Apostles**. The word **apostle** means "one who is sent." These men weren't just students. They were being sent out to represent Jesus and do His work.

Here are the twelve He chose (Luke 6:13-16): **Peter** (also called Simon), **Andrew** (Peter's brother), **James** and **John** (sons of Zebedee), **Philip**, **Bartholomew** (also known as Nathanael), **Matthew** (the former tax collector), **Thomas**, **James** (son of Alphaeus), **Thaddaeus**, **Simon the Zealot**, and **Judas Iscariot**, who would later betray Him.

These men came from different backgrounds. Some were fishermen. One had worked for the Roman government collecting taxes. One had been part of a political group. They didn't all think alike or come from the same place. But Jesus saw something in each of them.

He gave the Twelve real **authority**, not just permission to tag along. They could preach the gospel, heal the sick, and cast out devils in His name (Mark 3:14-15). This was serious work, and it set a pattern we still follow today. In The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, the **Quorum of the Twelve Apostles** carries on that same calling, chosen by God, given authority, and sent out to serve.

Jesus didn't try to do everything alone. He organized His work, called specific people, and gave them power to act. That pattern of order and authority matters.
