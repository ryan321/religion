### Lesson 4.4 -- Growing Crowds, Growing Questions

As Jesus traveled, preached, and healed, His fame spread everywhere. People came from all over to see Him and hear Him. The **crowds kept growing**, but so did something else: questions. And not always friendly ones.

Some of the things Jesus did made the religious leaders very uncomfortable. For example, when the paralyzed man was lowered through the roof, Jesus didn't just heal him. He said, **"Thy sins be forgiven thee"** (Mark 2:5). The scribes sitting nearby were shocked. In their minds, only God could forgive sins. If Jesus was forgiving sins, He was either claiming to be God or He was a blasphemer. That was a serious charge.

Then there was the company He kept. Jesus ate dinner with **tax collectors and sinners** (Mark 2:15-16). Tax collectors were hated because they worked for Rome and often cheated people. "Sinners" was a label for anyone the religious crowd had written off. The **Pharisees** -- a group of religious leaders who prided themselves on following every rule -- couldn't understand why a so-called holy man would sit down with those kinds of people. Jesus answered them simply: "They that are whole have no need of the physician, but they that are sick: I came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance" (Mark 2:17).

Jesus also **healed on the Sabbath**, which the Pharisees said was against the rules. The Sabbath was supposed to be a day of rest, with no work allowed. But Jesus made it clear that doing good and helping people was never wrong, no matter what day it was (Mark 3:1-5). He said, "The sabbath was made for man, and not man for the sabbath" (Mark 2:27).

The Pharisees weren't really asking, "Is this the Messiah?" They were asking, **"Who does He think He is?"** They saw someone breaking their traditions, challenging their authority, and drawing huge crowds. That felt dangerous to them.

This is where the **conflict** between Jesus and the religious leaders started to take shape. It would only get worse from here. The very people who should have recognized the Messiah were the ones most determined to stop Him. Jesus wasn't what they expected, and rather than change their expectations, they decided to fight Him.
