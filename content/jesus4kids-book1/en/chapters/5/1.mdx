### Lesson 5.1 -- The Beatitudes

One day, Jesus went up on a mountainside, sat down, and began to teach. The crowd that gathered to listen heard one of the most famous sermons ever given -- what we call the **Sermon on the Mount** (Matthew 5-7). It covers everything from prayer to forgiveness to how to treat your enemies. But it starts with something unexpected: a list of blessings called the **Beatitudes**.

The word "beatitude" comes from a Latin word meaning "blessed" or "happy." Each one begins with "Blessed are..." and describes a kind of person that God values. Here they are (Matthew 5:3-10):

**Blessed are the poor in spirit** -- people who are humble and know they need God. **Blessed are they that mourn** -- people who feel sadness and will be comforted. **Blessed are the meek** -- people who are gentle and patient, not pushy or proud. **Blessed are they which do hunger and thirst after righteousness** -- people who really want to do what's right. **Blessed are the merciful** -- people who show kindness and forgiveness. **Blessed are the pure in heart** -- people whose intentions are clean and honest. **Blessed are the peacemakers** -- people who work to bring others together. **Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness' sake** -- people who do right even when it costs them.

Now, look at that list again. It's not what the world usually admires. The world tends to celebrate the rich, the powerful, the famous, and the tough. Jesus flipped that completely upside down. He said the people who matter most to God are the humble, the kind, the honest, and the brave -- even if the world overlooks them.

This is a **different kind of greatness**. You don't have to be the strongest or the smartest or the loudest. You just have to have the right heart. That's the kind of person God is looking for.

The Beatitudes aren't just nice sayings to hang on a wall. They're a description of what a follower of Jesus looks like from the inside out. They set the tone for everything else in the Sermon on the Mount.
