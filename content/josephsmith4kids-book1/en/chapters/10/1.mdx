### Lesson 10.1 -- The Idea of Zion

Have you ever dreamed of a perfect place, somewhere everyone gets along, shares what they have, and lives close to God? Joseph Smith did not just dream about it. The Lord actually told him where that place would be.

In 1831, the Lord gave Joseph a revelation that said the land of **Zion**, the place where the Saints would gather and build a holy city, was in **Jackson County, Missouri** (D&C 57:1-3). This was a big deal. The idea of Zion was not just about a nice neighborhood. It was about building a community so righteous that the Lord Himself would dwell among His people. The Saints called it the **New Jerusalem**.

Joseph traveled to Missouri that summer with a small group of Church members. When they arrived, they found rolling prairies and open land. Joseph **dedicated the ground** for a future temple in a place called Independence. He felt the Spirit strongly and believed this was exactly where the Lord wanted His people to be.

Word spread quickly, and Saints began **moving to Missouri** in large numbers. They were excited to build Zion. Families packed up and traveled hundreds of miles to start new lives in Jackson County. They bought land, built homes, and set up farms. It felt like the beginning of something amazing.

But there were several problems. The people already living in Missouri did not want the Saints there. The **existing settlers** were suspicious and sometimes hostile. There were real reasons for the tension. The Saints practiced an unfamiliar religion that their neighbors thought was strange or even dangerous. Large numbers of Church members were arriving all at once, which made the old settlers worry about being outnumbered. Many of the Saints came from **Northern states** and openly opposed slavery, which angered Missouri settlers who supported it. And the Saints tended to **vote together as a group**, which threatened the political power of those already living there.

The conflict started almost immediately. What had seemed like a promised land quickly became a place of danger. The Saints believed God had called them to Missouri, but the people of Missouri did not want them there. This tension between what God had promised and what the world would allow became one of the most painful chapters in Joseph's life, and in the history of the Church.

Zion was real, but building it would cost more than anyone expected.
