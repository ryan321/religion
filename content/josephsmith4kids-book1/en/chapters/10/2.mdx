### Lesson 10.2 -- Driven from County to County

Imagine building a house, planting a garden, and finally feeling at home -- and then a mob shows up and burns it all down. That is exactly what happened to the Saints in Missouri, and it happened more than once.

In **1833**, armed mobs attacked the Saints in **Jackson County**. They **burned homes**, destroyed a printing press that was publishing Church materials, and **tarred and feathered** several Church members. Tar and feathering was a cruel punishment where hot tar was poured on a person's skin and feathers were stuck to it. The pain and humiliation were terrible. The Saints were told to leave or face worse violence. With no army to protect them and local authorities refusing to help, the Saints had no choice but to flee.

They moved north to **Clay County**, where the people were friendlier at first. But after a couple of years, the welcome wore thin there too. The Saints were asked to move again. They relocated to a newly created county called **Caldwell County**, with its main town of **Far West**. The idea was that Caldwell would be "the Mormon county" -- a place just for them. For a while, it worked. The Saints built homes, farms, and even began planning a temple.

But the pattern repeated. As the Church grew and members spread into neighboring counties, tensions flared again. Old fears and new conflicts pushed Missouri toward another crisis.

When Joseph heard about the suffering in Jackson County, he did not just send prayers. In **1834**, he organized **Zion's Camp** -- a march of about 200 men from Ohio to Missouri to help the Saints reclaim their land. They walked nearly a thousand miles. It was hard, hot, and dangerous. Some men got sick, and a few died of cholera along the way.

When they arrived, the Lord revealed that this was not the time to fight. He **disbanded the camp** before any battle took place. Many of the men were confused and disappointed. Why march all that way for nothing? But it was not for nothing. The Lord was testing their faith and training future leaders. Many of the men who marched in Zion's Camp later became apostles and other Church leaders. They had proven they were willing to sacrifice everything when the Lord asked.

The Saints kept getting pushed from place to place. But through it all, Joseph kept leading, and the people kept believing that God had not forgotten them.
