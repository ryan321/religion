### Lesson 10.3 -- The Extermination Order and Haun's Mill

Some moments in history are so terrible that they are hard to believe. In October 1838, the governor of Missouri did something that should never happen in a free country. **Governor Lilburn Boggs** issued what became known as the **Extermination Order**. His exact words were chilling: "The Mormons must be treated as enemies, and must be exterminated or driven from the State."

Read that again. A governor of an American state ordered that an entire group of people should be destroyed or forced out -- not because they had committed crimes, but because of their religion. This was not a law. It was an order for violence, and mobs took it as permission to attack.

Just three days after the order was issued, a mob of over 200 armed men rode into a small settlement called **Haun's Mill**. The Saints there were not soldiers. They were families -- men, women, and children going about their daily lives. The mob opened fire without warning. When the shooting stopped, **17 men and boys** lay dead or dying. Some were killed after they had already surrendered. A ten-year-old boy named **Sardius Smith** was shot after hiding under a blacksmith's bellows. The attackers showed no mercy.

The survivors were left to bury their dead. There was no time for coffins. They lowered the bodies into an unfinished well and covered them with dirt. Mothers who had lost husbands and sons had to keep moving, with nowhere safe to go.

When Joseph heard about the massacre and the Extermination Order, he faced an impossible choice. He could fight, which would lead to more bloodshed and give the state an excuse to wipe out the Church entirely. Or he could **surrender himself** to protect his people. Joseph chose to surrender.

He was arrested and taken before a military court. The commanding general, Samuel Lucas, **court-martialed Joseph** and sentenced him to be **shot the next morning** in the public square at Far West. But one officer, **General Alexander Doniphan**, refused to carry out the order. He called it **"cold-blooded murder"** and said he would hold the officers personally responsible if they went through with it. His courage saved Joseph's life -- at least for the moment.

Joseph was not freed. Instead, he was sent to jail. But he was alive. And the Church, though battered and bleeding, survived.
