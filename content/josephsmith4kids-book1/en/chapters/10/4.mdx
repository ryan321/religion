### Lesson 10.4 -- Liberty Jail

Picture the coldest, darkest room you can imagine. Now picture being locked inside it for months with no way out. That was **Liberty Jail**.

During the winter of **1838-39**, Joseph Smith and several companions were imprisoned in a small jail in Liberty, Missouri. The building was made of thick stone walls with a dungeon-like lower level where the ceiling was so low the men could barely stand up straight. The food was terrible -- sometimes **rotten** and barely edible. There were reports that the guards even tried to feed them poisoned meat. The jail was freezing cold, and the prisoners had little to keep warm. Guards **taunted** them and mocked their faith.

While Joseph sat in that miserable cell, he knew his people were suffering too. The Saints were being driven out of Missouri in the dead of winter -- women, children, and the elderly trudging through snow and mud with almost nothing. Joseph could not help them. He could not lead them. He could only sit in the dark and wait.

In one of the lowest moments of his life, Joseph cried out in prayer: **"O God, where art thou? And where is the pavilion that covereth thy hiding place?"** (D&C 121:1). He poured out his heart, asking why the Saints had to suffer so much, why God seemed far away, and how long it would all last.

The Lord answered. And His answer became some of the most beautiful and comforting scripture in all of the Doctrine and Covenants: **"My son, peace be unto thy soul; thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment; and then, if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high"** (D&C 121:7-8). The Lord did not take away the suffering. But He promised that it had a purpose and that it would not last forever.

Joseph also received revelation about how priesthood power works -- that it must be used with **"persuasion, long-suffering, gentleness, meekness, and love unfeigned"** (D&C 121:41). These words, written from a dungeon, have guided Church leaders ever since.

Later in his life, Joseph looked back on Liberty Jail and called it a kind of **"temple."** That might sound strange for a cold, filthy prison. But Joseph meant that he had learned things there -- about God, about himself, about suffering -- that he could never have learned in comfort. Sometimes the hardest moments teach us the most important lessons.

Joseph was eventually allowed to escape while being transferred to another location. He made his way to Illinois, where his people were already gathering. The Missouri period was over. But what Joseph learned in Liberty Jail stayed with him forever.
