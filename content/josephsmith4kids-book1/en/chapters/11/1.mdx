### Lesson 11.1 -- From Swamp to City

After everything the Saints had been through in Missouri -- the mobs, the Extermination Order, Liberty Jail -- they needed a fresh start. In **1839**, Joseph escaped custody and joined his people in Illinois, where they had already begun to gather along a swampy bend of the **Mississippi River**.

The land was cheap, and there was a good reason for that. It was a **swamp**. Standing water, thick mud, and clouds of mosquitoes made the area miserable and dangerous. Almost immediately, people started getting sick. **Malaria** swept through the settlement, and many Saints died from the disease. Joseph himself fell ill but reportedly got up from his sickbed to bless and heal others. Entire families were laid low with fever.

But the Saints did not give up. They **drained the swamps**, cleared the land, and began to build. Joseph named the settlement **Nauvoo**, a word he said came from Hebrew meaning "beautiful place." It was a bold name for a muddy riverbank, but Joseph could see what it would become.

And it became something remarkable. Joseph directed the **construction** of homes, businesses, streets, and public buildings. He planned the city on a grid pattern, with wide streets and neat lots. He welcomed **thousands** of new members who were pouring in -- not just from other parts of America, but from England and other countries where missionaries had been baptizing converts by the hundreds.

Within just a few years, Nauvoo grew into one of the **largest cities in Illinois** -- bigger than Chicago at the time. The population reached somewhere between 10,000 and 12,000 people. It had its own newspaper, its own university, and plans for a grand temple on the bluff overlooking the river.

Joseph was at the center of all of it. He was not just a religious leader giving sermons on Sunday. He was personally involved in **planning** the city, organizing construction projects, meeting with new arrivals, and solving the daily problems of a booming frontier town. He wanted Nauvoo to be the Zion that Missouri never let them build.

The transformation was incredible. In just a few years, the Saints turned a disease-ridden swamp into a thriving city. It showed what they could accomplish when they had a place to call their own -- and a prophet to lead them.
