### Lesson 11.2 -- Joseph as Mayor and Military Leader

In Nauvoo, Joseph Smith held more titles and responsibilities than almost any other person in America at the time. He was not just the prophet and president of the Church. He was also the **mayor of Nauvoo** and the **lieutenant general of the Nauvoo Legion**. That combination of religious, political, and military power made him one of the most unusual leaders in American history.

The **Nauvoo city charter**, granted by the Illinois state legislature in 1840, gave the city a lot of independence. It allowed Nauvoo to have its own courts, make its own laws, and form its own militia. Joseph became mayor and used the position to protect the Saints. After what had happened in Missouri, where the government itself had ordered their destruction, the Saints wanted the legal power to defend themselves.

The **Nauvoo Legion** was the city's militia, and it was no small force. It grew to **several thousand men**, making it one of the largest military bodies in the United States at the time. Joseph held the rank of **lieutenant general**, a title that at that point in American history had only been held by George Washington. The Legion trained regularly, held parades, and served as a visible reminder that the Saints would not be pushed around again.

Joseph took these responsibilities seriously. He led city council meetings, heard legal disputes, and made decisions that affected thousands of people. He also drilled with the Legion, wore a military uniform, and reviewed troops on horseback. For the Saints, seeing their prophet also serve as their mayor and military commander was inspiring. It meant he was willing to protect them in every way possible.

But not everyone saw it that way. **Critics** both inside and outside the Church worried about so much **power concentrated** in one person. Newspaper editors, politicians, and former Church members accused Joseph of being a tyrant. They feared that a man who led a church, ran a city, and commanded an army could become too powerful to control. Some of these critics had honest concerns. Others simply hated the Saints and wanted any excuse to oppose them.

This tension between Joseph's desire to protect his people and his enemies' fear of his power would define the final years of his life. The same strength that kept Nauvoo safe also made Joseph a target.
