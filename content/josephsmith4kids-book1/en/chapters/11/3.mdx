### Lesson 11.3 -- Joseph the Man in Nauvoo

Most of what we know about Joseph Smith's personality comes from the **Nauvoo period**. By this time, thousands of people lived near him, and many of them wrote down what he was like in their journals and letters. What they described was a man who was both powerful and playful, both tender and tough.

Joseph was **physically impressive**. He stood **over six feet tall**, which was quite tall for his time, and had broad shoulders and a strong build. People who met him often commented on his striking blue eyes and the energy he carried. He looked like a leader, but he did not act like someone who thought he was better than everyone else.

In fact, Joseph loved to have fun. He **wrestled** with young men and was known to be quite good at it. He **played ball**, pulled sticks (a strength contest where two people sit facing each other and pull on a stick), and even **raced horses**. He once said that when he was with the boys, he liked to be a boy himself. People remembered him laughing, joking, and being the kind of person who made everyone around him feel at ease.

But there was another side to Joseph, a deeply **tender** side. He **visited the sick** constantly, walking from house to house to lay his hands on people and give them blessings. He **blessed children** and took time to comfort those who were **grieving**. When friends died or when he spoke about sacred experiences, Joseph **wept openly**. He was not embarrassed to show emotion. In a time when many men considered crying a weakness, Joseph let his tears flow freely.

People who knew him described a man of contrasts. One moment he could be wrestling in the yard, and the next he could be standing before a congregation, speaking with the power of a prophet about things he had seen and heard in vision. He could command thousands, yet he would stop to comfort a single widow.

This combination of physical strength and emotional tenderness, playfulness and prophetic power, was what made Joseph so memorable to the people of Nauvoo. He was not a distant, untouchable leader. He was their neighbor, their friend, and their prophet all at once. They loved him not just for what he taught but for who he was.
