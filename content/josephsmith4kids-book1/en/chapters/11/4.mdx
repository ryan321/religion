### Lesson 11.4 -- Family Life in Nauvoo

Being a prophet is demanding. Being a mayor and military leader on top of that is exhausting. But Joseph Smith was also a **husband and father**, and that part of his life came with its own joys and sorrows -- especially the sorrows.

Joseph and **Emma Smith** had many children, but the heartbreak of their family life is hard to imagine. Several of their babies **died in infancy**. In an era before modern medicine, losing children was tragically common, but that did not make it any less painful. Joseph and Emma buried child after child. The grief was **constant** and deep. Joseph wept over each loss. For all his strength and faith, he was a father who could not save his own children, and that broke his heart.

Despite the demands on his time, Joseph was a **devoted father** to the children who survived. He played with them, taught them, and tried to be present even when Church responsibilities pulled him in every direction. People who visited the Smith home remembered seeing Joseph with his children on his lap or holding them close.

**Emma Smith** was remarkable in her own right. She managed the household, oversaw the family's finances, and kept the home running while Joseph was away -- which was often. She was also the first president of the **Relief Society**, the women's organization of the Church, founded in Nauvoo in 1842. Emma was intelligent, strong-willed, and deeply committed to the work, even when it cost her greatly.

One of the hardest things about life in the Smith home was the total lack of **privacy**. The home was almost never quiet or empty. **Visitors** arrived constantly -- Church members needing counsel, refugees from Missouri needing a place to stay, missionaries reporting from the field, and leaders making decisions about the city and the Church. Joseph and Emma's home served as a meeting place, a boarding house, and a refuge all at once.

Emma fed, housed, and cared for an endless stream of guests, sometimes with very little money or food to go around. The demands were enormous. Imagine never being able to close your door and just be a family. That was the reality for the Smiths in Nauvoo.

Through it all, Joseph and Emma held on to each other. Their relationship was tested by loss, pressure, and sacrifice. But they shared a life that was, in its own way, extraordinary -- not because it was easy, but because they gave everything they had to a cause they believed was bigger than themselves.
