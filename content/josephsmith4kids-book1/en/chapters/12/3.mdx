### Lesson 12.3 -- The King Follett Discourse

On **April 7, 1844**, just weeks before his death, Joseph Smith stood before thousands of Saints at a general conference in Nauvoo and delivered what many consider the greatest sermon of his life. It was a funeral sermon for a man named **King Follett**, a Church member who had recently died in an accident. But Joseph used the occasion to teach truths that shook the Christian world.

The **King Follett Discourse**, as it came to be known, covered some of the deepest and most daring topics Joseph ever addressed: the **nature of God**, the **potential of human beings**, and the **eternal nature of the soul**.

Joseph taught that the human soul is **eternal**. It was never created from nothing and it can never be destroyed. "The mind of man, the intelligent part, is as immortal as, and is co-eternal with, God Himself," he said. This was a direct challenge to the traditional Christian idea that God created human souls out of nothing at some point in time.

Then Joseph said something even more startling. He declared that **"God himself was once as we are now, and is an exalted man."** God the Father, Joseph taught, had once lived a mortal life. He had progressed, grown, and become God. And because He had walked that path, He understood His children perfectly.

But Joseph did not stop there. If God was once like us, then we have the **potential to become like God**. This teaching, sometimes called **eternal progression** or **exaltation**, meant that human beings are not just God's creations. They are His **children**, with the capacity to grow and eventually inherit all that the Father has.

These ideas **astonished** some listeners and **offended** others. For those who had spent their lives believing that God was completely unlike humans, a being beyond comprehension who existed outside of time and space, Joseph's words sounded like blasphemy. But Joseph taught with **absolute confidence**. He was not guessing, and he was not philosophizing. He had stood in the presence of God the Father and Jesus Christ. He had **seen** them. He knew what they looked like, how they spoke, and who they were. He was not speculating about God's nature. He was describing what he had personally witnessed.

The King Follett Discourse was Joseph Smith at his boldest. He knew his enemies were closing in. He may have known he did not have much time left. And so he taught the deepest truths he knew, holding nothing back.
