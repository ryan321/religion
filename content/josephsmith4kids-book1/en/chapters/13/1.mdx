### Lesson 13.1 -- Enemies from Within

Have you ever had a friend turn against you? Maybe someone you trusted said unkind things about you or tried to get others to dislike you. It hurts much more when the person was close to you than when a stranger is mean. Joseph Smith experienced this kind of deep betrayal during his final years in Nauvoo.

Not all of Joseph's opposition came from outside the Church. Some of the most painful attacks came from people who had once been his close friends and trusted leaders. One of the most important of these was **William Law**, who had served as a member of the **First Presidency**, one of the highest positions in the Church. William Law had been a loyal supporter of Joseph for years, but over time he turned against the Prophet. He disagreed with some of Joseph's teachings and decisions, and instead of working through those concerns privately, he began organizing opposition.

William Law was not alone. Other former leaders and members joined him. Some had held positions of trust and responsibility. Some had been with the Church almost from the beginning. When they turned against Joseph, they did not simply walk away quietly. They actively worked to bring him down, spreading accusations, writing letters, and trying to turn public opinion against the Prophet.

This was something Joseph had dealt with before. Throughout the history of the Church, people like **Thomas Marsh**, **Oliver Cowdery**, and others had left and sometimes fought against the work. But the betrayals in Nauvoo felt especially dangerous because they came at a time when outside enemies were also growing bolder. Internal and external opposition was building at the same time.

Joseph understood that this kind of **dissent**, open disagreement meant to divide, was part of the cost of standing for truth. The Savior Himself had been betrayed by Judas, one of His own Twelve Apostles. Joseph took comfort in knowing that prophets throughout history had faced similar trials. But that knowledge did not make the pain any less real.

The gathering storm inside the Church would soon combine with political pressures outside it. Together, these forces would push Joseph toward the most difficult days of his life. The enemies from within gave ammunition to the enemies from without, and the results would be devastating.
