### Lesson 13.2 -- Political Tensions

Have you ever noticed how people can get really worked up about politics? In Joseph Smith's day, political feelings ran just as hot as they do today, maybe even hotter. As the city of **Nauvoo** grew, so did the political influence of the Latter-day Saints, and that influence made a lot of people nervous.

Nauvoo had become one of the largest cities in Illinois. The Saints tended to vote together on issues that affected their safety and freedom, which gave them real power in elections. Politicians from both major parties (the **Whigs** and the **Democrats**) sometimes tried to win the Saints' support and sometimes attacked them for having too much influence. Either way, the growing strength of Nauvoo made Joseph a political target.

In **1844**, Joseph Smith did something unexpected. He announced that he was running for **President of the United States**. Now, Joseph did not really expect to win the election. He knew that was extremely unlikely. But he had important reasons for running. The Saints had been driven from Missouri, attacked, robbed, and even killed, and no government had stepped in to help them. Joseph had personally appealed to President **Martin Van Buren**, who famously told him, "Your cause is just, but I can do nothing for you."

Joseph's presidential platform focused on ideas that mattered deeply to him: **religious liberty** for all people, ending slavery through gradual, peaceful means, and reducing the size and power of government. He wanted to bring national attention to the mistreatment of the Saints and to stand up for the rights of all Americans to worship as they chose.

But the **candidacy** only added to the tension. Joseph's enemies saw it as proof that he wanted too much power. Combined with the **Nauvoo Charter**, which gave the city unusual independence, including its own militia and courts, critics accused Joseph of trying to build a kingdom within the United States. These fears were exaggerated, but they were real to the people who held them.

The political tensions created a dangerous situation. Joseph's enemies now had political reasons to want him removed, not just religious ones. State and local officials who might have otherwise left the Saints alone now saw Nauvoo as a political threat. The stage was being set for a confrontation that would go far beyond any election.
