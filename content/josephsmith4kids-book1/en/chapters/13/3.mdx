### Lesson 13.3 -- The Nauvoo Expositor

Have you ever seen someone twist the truth to make another person look bad? It is one thing to disagree with someone honestly. It is another thing to use half-truths and accusations to destroy them. In June of 1844, that is exactly what happened in Nauvoo.

The dissenters who had turned against Joseph Smith, including **William Law** and others, decided to publish a newspaper. They called it the **Nauvoo Expositor**. The word "expositor" means someone who explains or reveals, and the publishers claimed they were revealing the truth about Joseph and the Church. The first and only issue came out on **June 7, 1844**.

The Expositor attacked Joseph's character, accused Church leaders of wrongdoing, and called for the repeal of the Nauvoo Charter. It was designed to stir up opposition both inside Nauvoo and among the surrounding communities that already distrusted the Saints. The publishers knew exactly what they were doing. They wanted to create enough outrage to bring Joseph down.

Joseph and the **Nauvoo City Council** faced a difficult decision. After lengthy debate, the council declared the Expositor a **public nuisance** and ordered the printing press destroyed. Under the laws of the time, cities did have the legal authority to declare something a nuisance and take action. But destroying a printing press was an extreme step, and it immediately became the most **controversial** decision Joseph ever made.

Even though the action may have been legal, it gave Joseph's enemies exactly what they wanted: a reason to act against him. Critics across Illinois pointed to the destruction of the press as proof that Joseph was a tyrant who would silence anyone who disagreed with him. Newspapers throughout the region demanded justice. Politicians who had been looking for an excuse now had one.

Joseph was charged with **riot** for his role in ordering the press destroyed. He was ordered to appear in **Carthage**, the county seat, to face trial. The legal charges may have seemed routine, but everyone knew this was about more than a courtroom. Joseph's enemies had been waiting for this moment, and the destruction of the Expositor gave them the opening they needed.

The path to Carthage was now set. What waited there would change everything.
