### Lesson 13.4 -- "Like a Lamb to the Slaughter"

Have you ever had to do something you knew would be really hard, but you did it anyway because it was the right thing? Joseph Smith faced the hardest decision of his life in late June of 1844, and the way he handled it shows us what real courage looks like.

When the charges came down and Joseph was ordered to Carthage, some of his closest friends urged him to **flee west**. The Rocky Mountains were vast and unsettled. Joseph could escape, regroup, and lead the Saints to safety from there. It was not a cowardly plan; it was practical. Joseph himself seriously considered it. He and his brother **Hyrum** actually crossed the **Mississippi River**, heading away from Carthage and toward the frontier.

But then word came back from Nauvoo. Some members were upset. They felt Joseph was **abandoning** them. If he fled, the government might punish the entire city. His enemies would say he ran because he was guilty. The people he loved would suffer for his escape.

Joseph made his decision. He turned around. He would go to Carthage and face whatever was coming. He knew what that likely meant. He had received warnings. He understood the hatred that waited for him there. But he would not let his people pay the price for his safety.

As he prepared to leave, Joseph said words that have echoed through history. To those around him, he declared: **"If my life is of no value to my friends, it is of none to myself."** He was not being dramatic. He was stating a simple truth. He would rather die than abandon the people who depended on him.

Then came the words recorded in **Doctrine and Covenants 135:4**: **"I am going like a lamb to the slaughter; but I am calm as a summer's morning; I have a conscience void of offense towards God, and towards all men."** Think about what that means. He knew he was walking toward danger. He may have sensed he was walking toward death. And yet he was at peace. His conscience was clear. He had done what God asked him to do.

Joseph crossed back over the Mississippi and rode toward **Carthage**. He looked back at Nauvoo, the beautiful city the Saints had built from a swamp, and said, "This is the loveliest place and the best people under the heavens." He did not know if he would see them again.

He rode forward anyway.
