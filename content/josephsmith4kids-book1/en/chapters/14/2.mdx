### Lesson 14.2 -- The Last Hours

The afternoon of **June 27, 1844**, settled over Carthage Jail with a heavy stillness. Joseph, Hyrum, John Taylor, and Willard Richards were together in the upstairs room. They knew danger was near. But instead of giving in to fear, they turned to the things that mattered most: faith, friendship, and the quiet witness of the Spirit.

At some point during those final hours, Joseph asked **John Taylor** to sing a hymn. The song he requested was called **"A Poor Wayfaring Man of Grief."** It tells the story of a person who helps a suffering stranger again and again (giving food, drink, comfort, and shelter) only to discover at the end that the stranger was the Savior Himself. The message of the hymn is simple and powerful: when we serve others, we serve Christ.

John Taylor sang the hymn once. Then Joseph asked him to sing it again. Think about why Joseph might have wanted to hear those words twice. He had spent his life serving others, often at great personal cost. He had been beaten, tarred, imprisoned, and driven from his home, all because he tried to do what God asked. The hymn must have spoken directly to his heart.

The mood in the room was **somber but peaceful**. Joseph was not panicking. He was **preparing**. He bore his **testimony** to the guards who came near, telling them of the truthfulness of the Book of Mormon and the gospel of Jesus Christ. Even in the shadow of death, Joseph's instinct was to share the message that had defined his life.

Joseph also took time to write a **letter to Emma**, his beloved wife. We do not have every word he may have written that day, but we know his heart was with his family. Emma had stood beside him through every trial: poverty, persecution, the loss of children, separation. She was never far from his thoughts.

These last hours reveal something important about Joseph Smith. He did not spend them cursing his enemies or plotting escape. He spent them doing what he had always done: testifying of Christ, loving his family, and drawing close to the people who mattered most. He faced the end of his life the same way he had lived it: with faith, with love, and with his eyes turned toward heaven.
