### Lesson 14.3 -- The Attack

What happened on the afternoon of June 27, 1844, is one of the most sorrowful events in the history of the Restoration. It should be spoken of with reverence, because the lives that were lost that day were given in the cause of truth.

Late in the afternoon, a mob of roughly **150 men** approached Carthage Jail. Many had **painted their faces** with mud to disguise themselves. They overwhelmed the small guard -- some of whom may have been sympathetic to the mob, and stormed toward the upstairs room where Joseph, Hyrum, John Taylor, and Willard Richards were held.

The attackers fired through the door. **Hyrum Smith** was struck almost immediately. As he fell, he spoke his last words: **"I am a dead man."** Joseph rushed to his brother's side. Hyrum, faithful, steady, loyal Hyrum, was gone. He had stood beside Joseph through everything, and now he had given his life alongside him.

The mob continued firing into the room. **John Taylor** tried to escape through the window but was hit by multiple bullets. He fell back inside, severely wounded. One bullet struck his **pocket watch**, which absorbed the force that would have killed him. That watch, stopped at 5:16, is preserved to this day as a witness of what happened in that room. **Willard Richards**, remarkably, was barely touched, fulfilling a promise Joseph had once made to him.

In the chaos, **Joseph** moved toward the window. He was struck by gunfire from both inside the room and from outside. He fell from the **second-story window** to the ground below. The Prophet of the Restoration was gone. He was **thirty-eight years old**.

It is important to pause and feel the weight of this moment. Joseph Smith was not just a historical figure. He was a husband, a father, a brother, and a friend. He was a man who had spoken with God and angels, translated ancient scripture, and built a church that would bless millions. And now he lay still on the ground outside a small jail in Illinois.

Hyrum was forty-four. He had never sought the spotlight. He had simply loved his brother and loved the Lord, and he served faithfully to the very end.

The attack at Carthage was an act of cruelty, but it could not destroy what Joseph and Hyrum had helped to build.
