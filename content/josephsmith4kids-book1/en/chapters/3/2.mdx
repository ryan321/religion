### Lesson 3.2 -- The Leg Surgery

When Joseph Smith was about **seven years old**, he went through one of the most painful experiences a child could face. It was an experience that showed remarkable courage for someone so young.

It started with a terrible illness. A **typhoid fever epidemic** swept through the area where the Smiths were living in Lebanon, New Hampshire. Several of the Smith children got sick. Joseph's case was severe. As he recovered from the fever, a painful **infection** settled into his left leg. The infection was deep, reaching into the bone itself. His leg swelled. The pain was agonizing. For weeks, his mother Lucy barely left his side.

The local doctors examined Joseph's leg and gave the family terrible news: they recommended **amputation**. Cutting off the leg seemed like the only way to save the boy's life. But **Lucy refused**. She would not allow her son to lose his leg. She pleaded with the doctors to try something else.

A surgeon named **Dr. Nathan Smith** (no relation to the family) from Dartmouth Medical College offered an alternative. He proposed an **experimental surgery**: cutting into the leg and removing the infected portions of bone. This procedure was extremely rare at the time. There was no anesthesia in 1813. No painkillers. No way to numb the agony of having bone scraped and cut from a living leg.

Joseph's parents prepared him for what was coming. The doctors offered Joseph **alcohol** to help dull the pain. Joseph refused. He also asked his **mother to leave the room** so she would not have to watch him suffer. Instead, he asked his **father to hold him**. Think about that. A seven-year-old boy, facing surgery without any pain relief, was concerned about his mother's feelings.

The surgery was brutal. The doctors cut into Joseph's leg and removed large pieces of infected bone. Joseph endured it. He screamed, he cried, but he endured it. The surgery was successful. His leg was saved, though it healed with some shortness, and Joseph walked with a slight **limp** for the rest of his life. He was on **crutches for years** afterward.

This experience tells you something important about Joseph Smith's character. Even as a small child, he had extraordinary **courage**. He could face pain, make hard decisions, and think of others even in his own suffering. These were qualities he would need again and again throughout his life.
