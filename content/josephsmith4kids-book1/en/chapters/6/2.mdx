### Lesson 6.2 -- Marriage

Since Isaac Hale refused to give his permission, Joseph and Emma made a bold decision. On **January 18, 1827**, they **eloped**, meaning they got married without her father's blessing. The ceremony took place in **South Bainbridge, New York**, at the home of Squire Tarbill, a local justice of the peace.

Eloping wasn't an easy choice. Emma loved her family, and going against her father's wishes must have been painful. But she believed in Joseph, and she believed that their life together was the right path. Joseph, for his part, was deeply grateful to have a partner who trusted him when so few people did.

After the wedding, the young couple moved in with **Joseph's family** in Manchester, New York. The Smith household was crowded and busy, since Joseph's parents and several siblings were still living there. It wasn't exactly a dream honeymoon setup, but the Smiths welcomed Emma warmly. Lucy Mack Smith, Joseph's mother, grew to love Emma like her own daughter.

From the very beginning, this was a **genuine partnership**. Emma wasn't just along for the ride. She understood that Joseph had a sacred mission, and she chose to support it fully. When Joseph talked about the gold plates, the angel Moroni, and the work God had called him to do, Emma didn't roll her eyes or tell him to be practical. She listened. She believed. She helped.

That doesn't mean everything was easy. The Smiths were poor. The work was hard. People in the community whispered about Joseph and his "gold bible." There was real **hardship, danger, and heartbreak** ahead, more than either of them could have imagined on their wedding day.

But they faced it together. That's what made their marriage so important to the story of the Restoration. Joseph didn't do this work alone. Behind every late night of translation, every flight from enemies, and every moment of revelation, Emma was there. Steady, faithful, and strong.

Their partnership was one of the great love stories of the Restoration, even though it wasn't always easy. Real partnerships rarely are.
