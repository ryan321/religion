### Lesson 7.3 -- The Translation

Have you ever tried to write a really long school report? Maybe a few pages? Now imagine writing **500 pages** -- without any notes, without going back to fix things, and without taking breaks to look anything up. That's essentially what Joseph Smith did when he translated the Book of Mormon.

The translation was done by the **gift and power of God**. Joseph didn't sit down with a dictionary and figure out the ancient language on the plates word by word. Instead, he used sacred instruments -- the Urim and Thummim and later a **seer stone** -- to receive the English words as God revealed them. Oliver Cowdery and other scribes wrote down what Joseph dictated.

Here's what makes the translation truly remarkable: the bulk of the Book of Mormon was completed in roughly **65 working days**. Think about that. In just over two months, Joseph dictated a 500-page book containing a **complex narrative** that covers a thousand years of history, with dozens of characters, detailed geography, distinct writing styles, poetry, sermons, letters, and deep doctrinal teaching.

Joseph didn't use any reference materials. He didn't outline the book first. He didn't go back and revise chapters he had already finished. Each day, he simply picked up **right where he had left off** the day before, even after breaks of days or weeks. His scribes confirmed this -- there was no review, no revision, no starting over.

The book contains **distinct writing styles** for different ancient authors. Nephi writes differently than Alma, who writes differently than Mormon. The doctrinal teachings are consistent throughout, even though they come from prophets separated by centuries. Military strategies, legal customs, and cultural details are woven throughout in ways that scholars still study today.

For Latter-day Saints, the Book of Mormon itself is the best **evidence** of Joseph's prophetic calling. No 23-year-old farm boy with limited education could have produced such a work on his own. The book invites every reader to put it to the test -- to read it, ponder it, and ask God whether it is true (see **Moroni 10:3-5**).

Joseph always gave the credit to God. He never claimed to be a brilliant writer. He simply said he translated the record by the gift and power of God, and the finished book stands as a witness of that claim.
