### Lesson 15.3 -- The End of Kings

Back in Zarahemla, the Nephites had a big problem. King **Mosiah** was getting old, and the nation needed a new leader. But there was a catch -- none of his sons wanted the job. They had all left on missions to the Lamanites. So who would be the next king?

Mosiah thought carefully about this question, and he came to an important conclusion. Maybe having a king at all was a bad idea. He had seen firsthand what a wicked king could do. **King Noah** had taxed the people unfairly, killed a prophet, led the people into sin, and then abandoned them when danger came. One bad king could destroy an entire nation.

Mosiah explained his reasoning to the people. A **good king** is wonderful -- someone like King Benjamin, who loved his people and served them. But the problem is that you can't guarantee the next king will be good. And if you get a wicked king, he can do enormous damage before anyone can stop him (Mosiah 29:16-17).

So Mosiah proposed something revolutionary. Instead of a king, the Nephites would choose **judges** -- leaders selected by the **voice of the people**. If a judge was bad, he could be removed. If the people made good choices, they would be blessed. If they made bad choices, they would suffer the consequences. But at least the power would be spread out instead of resting in one person's hands.

This was a huge change. The Nephites had been ruled by kings for their entire history -- from Nephi all the way through Mosiah. Kings were all they knew. But Mosiah convinced them that judges chosen by the people were safer and fairer.

Mosiah also warned the people that with this new system came new responsibility. Under a king, you could blame the king if things went wrong. But under judges chosen by the people, **the people themselves were accountable**. If the majority chose wickedness, God would hold the whole nation responsible (Mosiah 29:26-27).

The people agreed to Mosiah's plan. They gave up the monarchy. No more kings. From now on, the Nephites would govern themselves through elected judges. It was one of the most important changes in the entire Book of Mormon, and it shaped everything that happened for the next several hundred years.
