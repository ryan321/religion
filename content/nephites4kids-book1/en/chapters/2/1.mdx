### Lesson 2.1 -- Why They Needed the Plates

Lehi's family had barely settled into their wilderness camp when the Lord gave another important command. Lehi was told to send his sons **back to Jerusalem** to get something critical: the **brass plates** from a man named **Laban** (1 Nephi 3:2-4).

Why would God send them back to the dangerous city they had just escaped? Because without those plates, Lehi's family would be in serious trouble over time. Not physically, but spiritually and culturally.

The **brass plates** were a set of metal records that contained the **five books of Moses** (Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy), a **history of the Jewish people** from the beginning down to the reign of King Zedekiah, and the **prophecies** of many prophets, including some whose writings we don't have in the Bible today. They also contained Lehi's own **genealogy**, proving his family's lineage back through the tribe of Joseph.

Think about what would happen without these records. Lehi's family was heading into the wilderness with no plan to return. Within a generation or two, their children and grandchildren would begin to forget. They would forget the **language** of their fathers. They would forget their **history**, where they came from and why they left. Most importantly, they would forget the **commandments of God** and the covenants he had made with their ancestors.

This actually happened to other groups in the Book of Mormon. The Mulekites, who also fled Jerusalem around the same time, arrived in the promised land **without scriptures**. When the Nephites eventually found them, the Mulekites' language had become corrupted and they had lost their knowledge of God. The scriptures made the difference.

The Lord knew that preserving the word of God in written form was essential. Lehi's family couldn't survive as a **covenant people** on memory alone. They needed the actual records (the law, the prophecies, and the promises) written down and preserved for every future generation.

Getting the plates wasn't optional. It was necessary for everything that would follow.
