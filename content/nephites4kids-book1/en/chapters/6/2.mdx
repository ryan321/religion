### Lesson 6.2 -- The Split

For years, the only thing holding Lehi's family together was **Lehi himself**. He was the father, the prophet, the peacemaker. As long as he was alive, there was at least a chance that the family could stay united. But once Lehi died, that thread snapped.

Almost immediately, Laman and Lemuel turned on Nephi again. They were **angry** -- angry that Nephi had always seemed to be the favored one, angry that he had rebuked them so many times, angry that he claimed authority over them even though they were older. Their resentment had been building for over a decade, and now there was no father left to hold them back.

They wanted to **kill Nephi** (2 Nephi 5:1-4).

This wasn't just a threat. The Lord Himself warned Nephi that his life was in danger and that he needed to leave. So Nephi gathered everyone who was willing to follow him -- his brother **Sam**, his younger brothers **Jacob and Joseph**, his sisters, **Zoram**, and others who believed in the warnings of God -- and they **departed into the wilderness** (2 Nephi 5:5-6).

They traveled for many days until they found a place where they could settle safely. They called it the **land of Nephi**. The people who followed Nephi became known as the **Nephites**. The people who stayed with Laman became known as the **Lamanites**.

This was the moment the family split **permanently**. What had started as an argument between brothers became a division between two entire civilizations. Nephites and Lamanites would be rivals, enemies, and occasionally allies for the next thousand years of Book of Mormon history.

Think about how sad this is. These were brothers -- sons of the same father and mother. They had walked the same desert, eaten the same food, sailed the same ocean. They had seen the same miracles. But they made **different choices**, and those choices divided them forever.

The split wasn't really about land or politics or who was in charge. It was about **faith**. Nephi and his followers chose to trust God. Laman and Lemuel chose resentment and pride. Two different responses to the same experiences produced two completely different civilizations.

This divide would shape everything that happened in the Book of Mormon from this point forward.
