### Lesson 6.4 -- The Lamanites

While the Nephites were building temples and teaching from the scriptures, the Lamanites were building something very different: a culture rooted in **anger and resentment**.

After the split, the Lamanites became a **hunting people**. They lived in tents, moved from place to place, and survived off the land. They didn't build the same kinds of permanent structures the Nephites did. Their way of life was simpler and more nomadic, but it was also shaped by something darker: a deep, burning **hatred toward the Nephites**.

Where did this hatred come from? It started with Laman and Lemuel, but it didn't stay with them. They **taught their children** that Nephi had wronged them. They told their sons and daughters that Nephi was a thief who had **stolen their birthright**. They said that as the eldest sons, Laman and Lemuel should have been the leaders, but Nephi had taken that from them. They said Nephi had "**robbed**" them (Mosiah 10:12-16).

They also taught that Lehi had been wrong to leave Jerusalem in the first place. In their version of the story, the family never should have gone into the wilderness. Everything bad that happened to them was Nephi's fault for going along with their father's foolish visions.

The most damaging part was that this story got **passed down from generation to generation**. Children who had never met Nephi or Laman grew up hating the Nephites because their parents and grandparents told them to. A family disagreement became a **cultural identity**. Being a Lamanite, in many ways, came to mean being someone who was angry at the Nephites.

This is how small grievances become enormous conflicts. What started as two brothers being jealous of a younger sibling turned into a **civilizational divide** that lasted for hundreds of years. Wars were fought, cities were destroyed, and thousands of people died, all because of bitterness that was never resolved and resentment that was carefully **taught** instead of healed.

The Book of Mormon doesn't tell this story to say the Lamanites were all bad. In fact, later in the record, some of the most faithful people in the entire book are Lamanites who chose to break the cycle. But that comes later. For now, the divide was real, it was deep, and it was growing wider with every generation.

What started as one family's journey from Jerusalem had become **two nations**, and their story was just beginning.
