### Lesson 7.3 -- Nephi's Psalm

Have you ever felt frustrated with yourself? Maybe you know what's right, but you keep making the same mistakes. If so, you'll connect with one of the most honest and personal passages in all of scripture -- a section often called **Nephi's Psalm**, found in **2 Nephi 4:15-35**.

Up to this point, Nephi might seem almost too perfect. He always obeyed, always had faith, always did the right thing. But in this passage, Nephi opens his heart and shows us that he struggled too.

He starts by saying how much he **rejoices in the scriptures** and how his heart ponders them constantly. But then his tone shifts. He cries out, **"O wretched man that I am!"** (2 Nephi 4:17). He admits that his soul is weighed down by his sins. He's troubled by weaknesses and even mentions struggling with his **temper** -- something that had caused him grief in his dealings with his brothers.

Wait -- Nephi? The guy who built a ship, crossed an ocean, and received visions from God? Yes, even Nephi felt burdened by his own imperfections.

But here's what makes this passage so powerful. Nephi doesn't stay in that dark place. He catches himself and begins to remember all the blessings God has given him. He recalls the visions, the guidance, the protection. And then he turns to God with a beautiful plea for help:

He asks God to make him strong, to help him shake off his sins, to close the gates of temptation around him. He chooses to **trust in the Lord** rather than trust in his own strength.

This is one of the most **vulnerable** moments in all of scripture. Nephi -- the great prophet and leader -- standing before God and admitting he can't do it alone. He needed the **Savior's grace** just like everyone else.

And that's the whole point. If someone as faithful as Nephi needed the Atonement of Jesus Christ, then it's okay for you to need it too. In fact, recognizing that need is one of the most important steps in growing closer to God.

Nephi's Psalm teaches us that being a good person doesn't mean being perfect. It means being **honest about your weaknesses** and turning to God for the strength you don't have on your own.
