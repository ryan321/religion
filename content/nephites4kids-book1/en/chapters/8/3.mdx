### Lesson 8.3 -- Enos's Prayer

Have you ever prayed so hard about something that you lost track of time? That's exactly what happened to **Enos**, the son of Jacob. His story is one of the most beautiful examples of prayer in all of scripture.

Enos tells us that one day he went out into the forest to **hunt** (Enos 1:1-8). While he was alone among the trees, his father's teachings about Jesus Christ began to sink deep into his heart. Something stirred inside him, and he felt a powerful need to talk to God. So he knelt down and began to pray.

And he didn't just pray for a few minutes. Enos **wrestled with God in prayer** all day long and into the night. That word "wrestled" is important. It means this wasn't casual or easy. Enos was pouring out his whole soul, struggling with his own feelings and desperately seeking an answer.

What happened next shows us a beautiful pattern of how real prayer can work.

**First**, Enos prayed for **himself**. He wanted to know that his sins were forgiven. After hours of pleading, a voice came to him saying his sins were forgiven because of his faith in Christ. Imagine the relief and joy he must have felt!

**Second**, once Enos felt that peace, his heart immediately turned to others. He began praying for the **Nephites**, his own people. He asked God to bless and protect them.

**Third**, his concern expanded even further. He started praying for the **Lamanites**, the very people who were enemies of the Nephites! Enos asked God to preserve the sacred records so that one day, even the Lamanites could receive the gospel. God promised him that the record would be **preserved** and would eventually come forth to the Lamanites.

Do you see the pattern? Enos started by thinking about himself, then his people, then even his enemies. His circle of concern grew wider and wider as his prayer deepened. That's a powerful **model for how our own prayers can grow**.

You don't have to pray all day in the woods to follow Enos's example. But you can start by praying about your own needs, then thinking about your family and friends, and then expanding outward to pray for people you might not naturally think about, even people who are hard to love.
