### Lesson 8.4 -- The Small Books and the Passage of Time

After Enos, the Book of Mormon enters a stretch that can feel a little different from what you're used to. Instead of long, detailed stories, we get a series of **very short books** -- some only a few verses long. But don't skip past them! These little books cover **centuries** of Nephite history, and they connect some really important dots.

After Enos passed the plates to his son **Jarom**, the record continued through a chain of keepers: **Omni, Amaron, Chemish, Abinadom**, and finally **Amaleki**. Each one added just a little bit to the record. Chemish, for example, wrote only a single verse! Some admitted they weren't great writers. Others simply didn't have much to say.

But even in these short entries, we learn important things. **Wars** were constant between the Nephites and Lamanites. Some generations kept the commandments and were blessed. Others drifted away and suffered the consequences. The familiar cycle of **obedience and rebellion** continued rolling along, just as it always does in the Book of Mormon.

The biggest event tucked into these small books is a major move. The Nephites, led by a king named **Mosiah** (the first Mosiah -- the grandfather of the more famous King Benjamin), left the **land of Nephi** and traveled through the wilderness until they discovered a place called **Zarahemla**.

And here's where it gets really interesting. When they arrived in Zarahemla, they found a whole group of people already living there -- the **Mulekites**. Who were the Mulekites? They were descendants of a man named **Mulek**, who was a son of King Zedekiah of Jerusalem. That means the Mulekites had also come from Jerusalem around the same time Lehi's family left! They had crossed the ocean on their own journey, but they hadn't brought any scriptures with them, so they had lost their language and much of their history.

The Nephites and Mulekites joined together in Zarahemla, with Mosiah as their king. This combined group would become the nation we follow for the rest of the Book of Mormon.

These small books remind us that even brief entries in the record matter. Faithful people kept the plates safe through difficult centuries, making sure the chain of record keepers was never broken.
