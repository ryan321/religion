### Lesson 1.2 -- Nehor

Not everyone was happy with the new government. In the very first year of the reign of the judges, a man named **Nehor** showed up and started causing big problems. He was large, strong, and a very convincing speaker, and his teachings were dangerously wrong.

Nehor went around telling the people that **priests and teachers should be paid** by the people and should be popular and well-liked. He also taught that **everyone would be saved** no matter what they did -- that God had created everyone and that in the end every single person would have eternal life. That might sound nice at first, but think about it. If everyone is saved no matter what, then why would anyone try to keep the commandments? Why would anyone repent? Nehor's teachings took away the need for Jesus Christ and His Atonement (see Alma 1:3-4).

A lot of people liked what Nehor had to say. It was easy to believe, and it made people feel good without asking them to change. He became so popular that he started his own church and began getting rich from the money people gave him.

But then something terrible happened. Nehor ran into an old hero of the faith named **Gideon**, the same Gideon who had stood up to wicked King Noah years before. Gideon argued against Nehor's false teachings using the words of God. Nehor got so angry that he drew his sword and **killed Gideon** (see Alma 1:7-9).

Nehor was brought before **Alma the chief judge** to be tried. Alma told Nehor that this was the first time **priestcraft** had been introduced among the people. **Priestcraft** means preaching just to get popular and rich instead of truly serving God. Alma said that if priestcraft were allowed to spread, it would destroy the entire nation. Nehor was found guilty of murder and was **executed** for his crime (see Alma 1:12-15).

But here's the sad part: even though Nehor was gone, **his ideas didn't die with him**. Many people kept believing and teaching his false doctrines. These ideas would pop up again and again throughout the book of Alma, causing all sorts of trouble. Nehor's story reminds us that false teachings can sound appealing but lead people far away from the truth.

We should always compare what people teach us with what the **scriptures** and **living prophets** say. That's how we can tell truth from error.
