### Lesson 10.1 -- Moroni's Angry Letter

Have you ever been so frustrated that you said something you later wished you hadn't? That happened to one of the greatest heroes in the Book of Mormon: Captain Moroni.

The war against the Lamanites had been dragging on for years. Moroni and his soldiers were out on the front lines, fighting and dying to protect the Nephite people. They were running low on food, weapons, and men. Moroni had sent message after message to the government in Zarahemla asking for **supplies and reinforcements**, but nothing was coming. Soldiers were starving. Cities were falling. Moroni was desperate.

So Moroni did something bold. He sat down and wrote a **furious letter** to Pahoran, the chief judge. And when we say furious, we mean it. This was not a polite request. Moroni accused Pahoran and the government leaders of sitting in comfort on their thrones while soldiers bled and died on the battlefield. He called them **thoughtless** and said they were guilty of **great neglect**. He even said their laziness was almost like treason, betraying their own people.

Moroni didn't stop there. He made a **threat**. He told Pahoran that if the government didn't send help immediately, he would leave the battlefield and **march his army to the capital city** of Zarahemla. He said he would take what his soldiers needed by force if he had to. He warned that God would punish the leaders if they didn't do their duty (see Alma 60).

Now, here's the important thing to understand. Moroni's **passion** for his people was real. He truly cared about the soldiers who were suffering. His anger came from love for the men he was losing every day. But Moroni made a big mistake. He **assumed the worst** about Pahoran without knowing the full situation. He didn't ask what was going on. He didn't wonder if maybe there was a good reason the supplies hadn't come. He jumped straight to accusations and threats.

Have you ever blamed someone for something before you knew the whole story? Maybe you got mad at a friend for not showing up, only to find out they were sick. That's basically what Moroni did. He let his frustration take over, and he judged Pahoran without having all the facts.

Moroni's letter teaches us that even **good people** can act unfairly when they're angry or stressed. Being passionate about the right things is wonderful, but we still need to be careful about how we treat others, especially before we know the full story.
