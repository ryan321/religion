### Lesson 10.2 -- Pahoran's Response

Have you ever had someone yell at you or accuse you of something that wasn't your fault? How did you react? Did you yell back? Most of us probably would. But Pahoran, the chief judge of the Nephites, showed us a different, and much harder, way to respond.

When Pahoran received Moroni's angry letter full of harsh accusations and threats, he had every right to be offended. He was being called lazy, neglectful, and practically a traitor. But here's the incredible part: **Pahoran didn't get angry back**. His response is sometimes called the **most gracious letter in all of scripture**, and when you read it, you'll understand why.

First, Pahoran **explained what had actually happened**. He hadn't been sitting around ignoring the soldiers. A group of **rebels called the king-men** had risen up and overthrown the government in Zarahemla! These wicked men wanted to bring back a king and had driven Pahoran out of the city. He had been fighting his own battle just to survive. He couldn't send supplies because he didn't even control the capital anymore (see Alma 61).

But the most remarkable part of Pahoran's letter isn't the explanation. It's his **attitude**. Instead of defending himself with anger, Pahoran wrote some of the most beautiful words in the Book of Mormon. He told Moroni, **"I do joy in the greatness of your heart"** (Alma 61:9). Think about that. Moroni had just called him terrible things, and Pahoran responded by saying he admired Moroni's passion and courage. He didn't take the insults personally. He understood that Moroni's anger came from love for his soldiers.

Pahoran **refused to take offense**. He could have written back with his own list of accusations. He could have pointed out how unfair Moroni had been. Instead, he focused on the **solution**. He told Moroni to bring his army to Zarahemla, and together they would defeat the rebels first, then finish the war against the Lamanites. He turned Moroni's threat into an **invitation to work together**.

This is one of the greatest examples of choosing not to be offended in all of scripture. Pahoran teaches us that when someone says hurtful things, even unfair things, we don't have to fire back. We can choose to understand where they're coming from, forgive them, and focus on fixing the problem instead of making it worse.

How different would our friendships, our families, and our world be if more people responded the way Pahoran did?
