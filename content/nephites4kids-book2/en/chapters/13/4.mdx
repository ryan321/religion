### Lesson 13.4 -- The Cycle Continues

You might think that after everything the Nephites went through (the murder of the chief judge, Nephi's prophecies being proven true, and the terrible famine) the people would have learned their lesson for good. But if you've been reading the Book of Mormon carefully, you already know what happened next. They fell right back into wickedness.

This is one of the most frustrating patterns in the Book of Mormon, and it's called the **pride cycle**. It goes like this: the people are righteous, so God blesses them. Then they become proud and wealthy. Pride leads them into sin. Sin brings suffering through wars, famines, or other consequences. The suffering humbles them, they repent, and God blesses them again. And then the whole thing starts over.

After the famine ended and the rains returned, the Nephites had a few years of peace and prosperity. But it didn't take long before **contention** crept back in. People started fighting and arguing again. The **Gadianton robbers**, who had been driven into the mountains, came back stronger than ever. They grew in number and power until they became a serious threat to the entire Nephite nation once more (see Helaman 11:24-37).

Prophets continued to preach and warn the people, but most Nephites ignored them. Some even got angry and tried to hurt the prophets for speaking the truth. It was as if the people had completely forgotten the famine, the miracles, and the mercy God had shown them just a few years earlier.

This pattern teaches an important lesson for us today. **Prophets can warn, but they can't choose for us.** Each person has to decide for themselves whether they will follow God or follow the world. No amount of miracles, signs, or even suffering can force someone to be righteous. Agency (our ability to choose) is one of God's greatest gifts, but it also means we are responsible for our own choices.

The good news is that the cycle can be broken. If we choose to stay humble and remember God even when things are going well (especially when things are going well) we don't have to keep repeating the same mistakes. We can learn from the Nephites instead of copying them.

The question isn't whether God will be faithful to us. He always is. The question is whether we will be faithful to Him.
