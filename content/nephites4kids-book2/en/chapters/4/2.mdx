### Lesson 4.2 -- The Zoramites and the Rameumptom

After Korihor was trampled among the Zoramites, Alma heard something troubling. The **Zoramites**, a group of Nephites who had broken away from the Church, were falling into serious wickedness. Alma decided that preaching the word of God was the best way to help, so he gathered a group of missionaries and went to the Zoramite lands.

What they found when they got there was shocking.

The Zoramites had built a very strange way of worshiping. In the center of their synagogues, they had constructed a high tower called the **Rameumptom**, which means "the holy stand." This tower was so tall that only one person could stand on top at a time. And every week, the Zoramites would take turns climbing to the top and reciting the **exact same prayer** (Alma 31:12-23).

The prayer itself was the real problem. Standing on the Rameumptom, each person would stretch out their hands and say: "Holy God, we believe that thou hast separated us from our brethren. We believe that thou hast elected us to be thy holy children. We believe that thou hast made it known unto us that there shall be no Christ."

They **thanked God that they were better than everyone else**. They rejected Jesus Christ. They told God how special and chosen they were. And then (here's the part that really bothered Alma) they went home and **never thought about God again for the rest of the week**. Their worship was completely empty. It was just a performance they did once and then forgot about.

Alma was so upset by what he saw that he prayed with his whole heart. He asked God for strength and comfort as he worked among these people. It wasn't just that their beliefs were wrong. Their hearts were full of pride. They had turned worship into a way of showing off instead of a way of connecting with God.

The Rameumptom is one of the most memorable images in the Book of Mormon because it teaches such a clear lesson. Real worship isn't about standing above other people and declaring how great you are. It's about humility. It's about actually living what you believe every day, not just performing once a week and forgetting the rest of the time.

The Zoramites had everything backward. They thought religion was about being better than others, when it's really about becoming better yourself.
