### Lesson 6.4 -- Moroni's Faith

It would be easy to read about Captain Moroni and think of him only as a military genius -- a man of strategy, strength, and action. But the Book of Mormon makes it clear that Moroni's greatest weapon wasn't his sword. It was his **faith**.

Throughout the war chapters, we see Moroni doing something that great warriors don't always do: he **fasted and prayed**. Before major decisions, he sought God's guidance. Before battles, he asked for divine help. He didn't rely only on his own intelligence or strength. He knew that without God's blessing, no amount of preparation would be enough.

Moroni also made sure his soldiers knew why they were fighting. He didn't just give military orders. He reminded them of their covenants with God. He pointed them to their families, their freedom, and their faith. He made sure the cause was bigger than just winning a battle -- it was about **defending everything that mattered most**.

At the same time, the Book of Mormon is honest about the fact that Moroni was not perfect. He was **passionate** and sometimes **angry**. When the government failed to send supplies and reinforcements to his soldiers, Moroni wrote a furious letter to Pahoran, the chief judge. He accused Pahoran of sitting in comfort while soldiers were dying. He threatened to march his army to the capital and take action if help didn't come.

It turned out that Pahoran wasn't lazy or neglectful at all. He had been driven out of the capital by a group of rebels and was fighting his own battle. Pahoran wrote back with remarkable patience and kindness, explaining the situation. He wasn't offended by Moroni's harsh words -- he just wanted to work together to solve the problem.

This moment teaches us something valuable. Moroni was a great man, but he jumped to conclusions and let his anger get ahead of him. **Being great doesn't mean being perfect.** Even the most faithful people make mistakes, misjudge situations, and need to be corrected.

What made Moroni truly great wasn't that he never stumbled. It was that his life was **devoted to God, his family, and freedom**. His faith was the foundation for everything he did. He wasn't a perfect man -- he was a faithful man. And in the end, that's what the Book of Mormon celebrates about him.

Captain Moroni reminds us that strength without faith is incomplete, and that even imperfect people can do extraordinary things when they trust in God.
