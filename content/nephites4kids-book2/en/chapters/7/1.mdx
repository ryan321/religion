### Lesson 7.1 -- Amalickiah the Traitor

Captain Moroni was not the only powerful leader among the Nephites. A man named Amalickiah was also brilliant, persuasive, and determined. The difference was that Amalickiah cared only about himself. He wanted power, and he was willing to do anything to get it.

Amalickiah was a Nephite -- born and raised among the people of God. He knew the gospel. He knew the laws. He knew how the government worked. But none of that mattered to him as much as sitting on a throne. Amalickiah wanted to be king of the Nephites, and he set out to make it happen.

He started by flattering people. He told certain Nephites what they wanted to hear -- that they deserved more power, more wealth, more authority. He was so smooth with his words that he convinced many people to follow him. Some of these followers were lower judges who liked the idea of having more control. Others were just ordinary people who got swept up in his promises.

When Amalickiah tried to tear apart the Nephite government, Captain Moroni stepped in. Moroni raised the title of liberty and rallied the faithful Nephites to defend their freedom. The people responded. They came together and made it clear that they would not give up their liberty for a would-be king.

Amalickiah saw that he could not win -- not yet, anyway. So he gathered up the people who were still loyal to him and fled to the land of the Lamanites. This was not a retreat out of fear. It was a calculated move. Amalickiah had a new plan. If he could not rule the Nephites from the inside, he would conquer them from the outside.

This is what makes Amalickiah one of the most dangerous villains in the Book of Mormon. He was not some foreign enemy who did not know better. He was a Nephite dissenter -- someone who had been part of God's covenant people and chose to walk away. Throughout the Book of Mormon, dissenters often caused more damage than any outside enemy because they knew exactly how the Nephites thought, how they fought, and where they were weak.

Amalickiah's story is told in Alma chapters 46 and 47, and it is a warning that talent and intelligence can be used for terrible things when a person has no integrity. He had every gift a leader could want -- except honesty and love for others.
