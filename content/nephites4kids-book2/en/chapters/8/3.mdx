### Lesson 8.3 -- Teancum's Courage

Among Captain Moroni's best officers was a man named Teancum. He was brave, skilled, and fiercely loyal to the Nephite cause. While Moroni was the overall commander and Helaman led the stripling warriors, Teancum was the captain who did what others could not, or would not, do. His courage changed the course of the war more than once.

Teancum's most famous act came at a critical moment. Amalickiah had led a massive Lamanite invasion and captured several Nephite cities along the eastern coast. The Nephites were retreating, and things looked desperate. Then, on the last night of the year, Teancum crept into the Lamanite camp alone.

He found Amalickiah asleep in his tent. Quietly, Teancum threw a javelin that struck the Lamanite king in the heart, killing him instantly. Amalickiah died without even waking up. Teancum slipped back to his own camp before anyone discovered what had happened. When the Lamanite soldiers found their king dead the next morning, they were so shaken that they pulled back. Alma 51:33-34 records this bold act that stopped an enemy advance cold.

But Amalickiah's death did not end the war. His brother Ammoron took over as king and continued the fight with the same hatred for the Nephites. The wars dragged on for years, and the Nephites kept suffering.

Near the end of the long conflict, Teancum decided to try the same thing again. He crept into the Lamanite camp at night and found Ammoron. This time, he threw his javelin and struck Ammoron. But Ammoron did not die immediately. He woke up and called for his guards before he died from the wound. The guards found Teancum and killed him before he could escape. Alma 62:36 records this moment.

Teancum gave everything he had, including his life, for his people. He did not fight for glory or power. He fought because he believed in freedom and because he loved the Nephite nation. Mormon described him as a man who had "fought valiantly" and been "a true friend to liberty."

Not every hero survives. Teancum's story teaches us that courage sometimes means accepting the highest cost. He is remembered not because he lived to celebrate victory, but because he was willing to give everything for what mattered most.
