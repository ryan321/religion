### Lesson 10.3 -- Moroni Takes Over

After Mormon was killed, his son **Moroni** was left completely alone. Think about what that means. His father was dead. His family was gone. His friends were gone. His entire civilization (every city, every village, every community he had ever known) was destroyed. Moroni was one of the last Nephites alive on the face of the earth.

Moroni wrote about his situation in heartbreaking words: "My father hath been slain in battle, and all my kinsfolk, and I have not friends nor whither to go" (Moroni 1:1-3). He had no people. He had no home. He had no place of safety. The Lamanites were actively hunting down anyone with Nephite blood, and they would kill him on sight.

Despite all of this, Moroni picked up the sacred record his father had spent a lifetime creating, and he **kept writing**. He could have buried the plates immediately and tried to disappear. Instead, he chose to add to the record. He felt there were important things still to be said, things that future readers would need.

Moroni wandered alone for **decades**. We don't know exactly how many years, but his writings span a long period of time after Cumorah. Imagine being completely alone for year after year, moving from place to place, never able to settle down, always watching for enemies. No companionship, no community, no one to talk to except God.

During those lonely years of wandering, Moroni continued to write. He finished his father's record, added his own testimony, included the record of the Jaredites (the book of Ether), and wrote the final chapters that now bear his name (the book of Moroni). He also included important teachings about faith, hope, charity, spiritual gifts, and how the Church of Christ should be organized.

What kept Moroni going through all those years alone? **Faith**. He believed that God had a purpose for the record, even though he couldn't see it. He trusted that someday, someone would find the plates and bring them to the world. He wrote with future readers in mind, often speaking directly to us as if he could see our day.

Moroni's story teaches us that we are never truly alone when we have God. Even in the darkest and loneliest times, the Lord is with us. Moroni's long years of faithful service, with no audience, no praise, and no visible reward, are a powerful reminder that obedience to God matters even when no one else is watching. Sometimes the most important work is done quietly, by people nobody sees.
