### Lesson 11.4 -- A Warning About Kings

After the Jaredites arrived in the promised land, they settled in and began to grow as a people. They had families, built homes, and established communities. Life was good. But then the people made a request that would shape, and eventually destroy, their entire civilization. They wanted a **king**.

The brother of Jared was deeply troubled by this request. He warned the people plainly that having a king would lead to **captivity** (see Ether 6:22-23). He understood something important about human nature: when you give one person total power over everyone else, it almost always leads to problems. Kings can become proud. They can become tyrants. And the people who gave them power often end up suffering for it.

But the people insisted. They wanted a king anyway. The brother of Jared's warning went unheeded, just like so many prophetic warnings throughout scripture. The people got what they asked for, and it set a **pattern** that would repeat itself throughout the entire Jaredite history.

This pattern should sound familiar because it's the same mistake other groups in the scriptures made. The Israelites in the Old Testament demanded a king even though the prophet Samuel warned them against it. God told Samuel that the people weren't rejecting Samuel; they were rejecting **God** as their king (see 1 Samuel 8:7). The Nephites also had their share of trouble with kings. King Noah was one of the most wicked rulers in the Book of Mormon, and his reign brought terrible suffering to his people.

Why does wanting a king cause so many problems? Because it puts trust in **a person** instead of in God. When a righteous king rules, things can go well for a time. But eventually an unrighteous king takes power, and the people suffer. The brother of Jared understood that no system built on the power of one person is safe because people are imperfect.

The Jaredites' choice to have a king didn't cause immediate disaster. Some of their early kings were good men who tried to lead righteously. But the foundation had been laid for future problems. Over the centuries that followed, wicked kings would rise to power, secret combinations would form around the throne, and the competition for the kingdom would eventually tear the Jaredite nation apart.

The lesson for us is about where we place our **trust**. When we depend on God's guidance and follow His prophets, we're building on a foundation that can't be corrupted. But when we look to popular leaders or powerful people to solve all our problems, we set ourselves up for disappointment. People will always let us down eventually. God never will.
