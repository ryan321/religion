### Lesson 12.2 -- Prophets Rejected

Throughout the long history of the Jaredites, God never stopped sending **prophets** to warn the people. Every time the Jaredites drifted into wickedness, the Lord raised up men to call them to repentance. Some of these prophets were listened to, and the people turned back to God. But far too often, the prophets were **rejected** -- mocked, threatened, and cast out.

The last and perhaps most important Jaredite prophet was **Ether**. He lived at the very end of the Jaredite civilization, when things were falling apart completely. The Jaredite nation was tearing itself to pieces with civil war, and Ether was called by God to deliver a final message.

Ether's prophecies were remarkable. He didn't just warn the Jaredites about their coming destruction -- he also spoke of things far in the future. He prophesied about the **New Jerusalem**, a holy city that would one day be built on the American continent. He testified of **Jesus Christ** and His role as the Savior of the world. And he told the Jaredites plainly that if they did not repent, they would be utterly destroyed (see Ether 13:2-8).

The people rejected Ether completely. They didn't want to hear his message. They didn't want to change their ways. They "cast him out" and refused to listen (see Ether 13:13-14). Ether had to **hide in a cave** to survive. From the mouth of that cave, he watched in sorrow as the events he had prophesied began to unfold. The nation he loved was destroying itself, and they had refused the one thing that could have saved them -- repentance.

Imagine what that must have been like for Ether. He had been given a message from God that could save his entire people. All they had to do was listen and repent. But they chose destruction instead. Ether watched from his hiding place as everything fell apart, writing down what he witnessed so that future generations would know what happened.

The pattern of **rejecting prophets** is one of the saddest and most repeated patterns in scripture. The Jaredites did it. The Nephites did it. The people of Jerusalem did it to Lehi and to Jesus Christ Himself. And it always leads to the same result -- suffering that could have been avoided.

This is why the scriptures urge us to **listen** to God's servants. Prophets don't deliver messages people always want to hear. Sometimes their words are uncomfortable or challenging. But they speak for God, and ignoring them has consequences. Ether's story reminds us that God always gives a warning before destruction -- the question is whether we will listen.
