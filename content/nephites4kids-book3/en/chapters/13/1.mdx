### Lesson 13.1 -- The Last Nephite

Try to imagine being the very last person from your entire people. Everyone you knew (your family, your friends, your neighbors) all gone. That was Moroni's life after the terrible battle at Cumorah.

After the Nephite nation was destroyed around 385 AD, **Moroni was left completely alone**. His father Mormon had been killed. The armies of the Nephites had been wiped out. Hundreds of thousands of people had fallen. And Moroni, somehow, survived.

But surviving didn't mean he was safe. The **Lamanites** were hunting down any Nephite who was still alive. Moroni had no home to return to. He had no city to shelter in. He had no companion to travel with. He was a man without a people, wandering through a land that used to be his home but was now filled with danger at every turn.

Moroni described his situation in his own words: **"I wander whithersoever I can for the safety of mine own life"** (Moroni 1:3). Think about what that means. Every single day, he had to keep moving just to stay alive. He couldn't settle anywhere. He couldn't trust anyone. He was always looking over his shoulder.

And yet, Moroni carried something incredibly precious with him through all those years of wandering: the **sacred records**. The gold plates that his father Mormon had spent a lifetime compiling. Moroni knew that these records were more important than his own comfort or safety. He protected them with his life because he knew they had a purpose that went far beyond his own time.

Moroni wandered for **more than 36 years** like this, from roughly 385 AD to at least 421 AD. That's longer than most adults have been alive! Imagine spending that many years alone, hiding, moving from place to place with no one to talk to and no safe place to rest. It would take incredible **faith and determination** to keep going.

Why did he do it? Because Moroni believed that God had a plan for the records he was carrying. He believed that someday, somehow, those plates would come forth and bless the lives of people he would never meet. That faith kept him walking, day after day, year after year, when most people would have given up.

Moroni's example teaches us that **faithfulness doesn't require comfort**. Sometimes doing what God asks means being lonely, tired, or afraid. But when we trust in His plan, we can keep going, even when the road is long and hard.
