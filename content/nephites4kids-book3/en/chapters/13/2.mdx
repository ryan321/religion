### Lesson 13.2 -- Mormon's Letters

Even though Mormon was dead, his voice was not lost. As Moroni worked on the record during his long years of wandering, he included something deeply personal: **two letters from his father**. These letters are found in Moroni chapters 8 and 9, and they give us a window into the heart of a loving father and a faithful prophet.

The **first letter** deals with an important question that had come up among the people: should little children be baptized? Some people apparently thought that babies and small children needed to be baptized to be saved. Mormon wrote firmly and clearly that this was **wrong**.

Mormon taught that **little children are alive in Christ** and do not need baptism (see Moroni 8:8-12). Why? Because Jesus Christ's Atonement automatically covers little children. They haven't reached an age where they can truly understand right and wrong, so they are **innocent before God**. Mormon called the idea of baptizing infants a "solemn mockery before God" and said it denied the mercies of Christ. This was strong language, and it showed how important this truth was to him.

This teaching still matters today. In The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, children are not baptized until they are **eight years old**, the age of accountability. Mormon's letter helps us understand why.

The **second letter** is very different in tone. It describes the **horrifying wickedness** of the Nephites during their final days. Mormon wrote about terrible things that the Nephite soldiers were doing: cruelty, violence, and behavior so awful that Mormon could barely write about it. He told Moroni that the people had lost all feeling and had gone beyond the point of caring about right and wrong.

Reading this second letter is hard because it shows how far people can fall when they completely turn away from God. But Mormon didn't write it just to describe darkness. Even in the middle of all that horror, he reminded Moroni to stay faithful. He told his son that **Christ had not forgotten him** and that there was still reason to hope.

These two letters are remarkable because they preserve a **father's voice across centuries**. Mormon wrote them to his son during a time of terrible suffering, never knowing that millions of people would eventually read his words. His teachings about little children and his honesty about the wickedness of his time both carry important messages for us today.

Through these letters, we learn that **God's truth doesn't change**, no matter how dark the world becomes. And a parent's love and counsel can reach far beyond their own lifetime.
