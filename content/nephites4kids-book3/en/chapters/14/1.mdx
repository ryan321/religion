### Lesson 14.1 -- Moroni's Farewell

Have you ever had to say goodbye to someone, not knowing if you'd ever see them again? Moroni experienced something even harder than that. He had to say goodbye to the **entire world** (through writing) because there was no one left to say it to in person.

What makes Moroni's farewell so moving is that he **didn't expect to still be alive**. Several times throughout the record, Moroni wrote what he thought would be his final entry. He would finish a section, basically say goodbye, and assume he would die soon. But then he kept living. He kept wandering. And so he kept writing.

In Mormon chapter 8, Moroni wrote as if he was about to finish the record for good. Then in Ether, he added an entire abridgment of the Jaredite record, something he probably didn't plan on doing. Each time he thought he was done, the Lord gave him more time and more to write.

Finally, Moroni reached **chapter 10 of his own book**, the book of Moroni. This is his **true farewell**. By this point, Moroni had nothing left to lose. He had no home, no family, no people, and no guarantee of another day of life. But he also had **everything to give**. He had a lifetime of faith, experience, and testimony, and he poured all of it into this final chapter.

Moroni 10 is not the farewell of a man who has given up. It's the farewell of a man who has **held on through everything** and still believes. Despite decades of loneliness, despite watching his entire civilization be destroyed, despite living as a hunted man in the wilderness, Moroni's faith was strong. Maybe even stronger than ever.

There's something powerful about a person who has been through the worst life can offer and still stands firm in their testimony. Moroni didn't write his farewell from a comfortable home. He wrote it from the wilderness, probably cold, hungry, and tired. And yet his words burn with conviction and love.

When we read Moroni 10, we're reading the words of someone who had **every reason to quit but chose not to**. He held on to his faith when there was nothing else to hold on to. And he used his final words not to complain about his suffering, but to testify of Christ and invite every future reader to come to know Him.

That's the kind of farewell that changes lives, and it has, for nearly two hundred years.
