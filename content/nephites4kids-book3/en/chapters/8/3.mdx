### Lesson 8.3 -- The Abridger

Mormon was a prophet. He was a general. But his greatest work wasn't preaching or fighting. It was **writing**. More specifically, it was reading, selecting, summarizing, and organizing nearly a thousand years of Nephite records into the book we hold today.

Think about how massive the original records were. Starting with Nephi around 600 BC and stretching to Mormon's own time around 400 AD, prophets, kings, and record keepers had been writing on metal plates for about **a thousand years**. There were the large plates of Nephi (which contained the full history), the small plates of Nephi (which focused on spiritual matters), and many other records, letters, and accounts. The sheer volume was enormous.

Mormon's job was to **abridge** all of this. To abridge means to shorten or condense. He had to read through that vast library of plates, decide what to include and what to leave out, write his summaries onto new plates, and organize everything into a narrative that would make sense to readers far in the future.

This was an incredible literary achievement. Mormon didn't just copy things word for word. He **selected** the stories, sermons, and events that he felt were most important. He **added his own commentary**, often stepping into the narrative to explain what a particular story means or why it matters. When you read phrases like **"and thus we see"** in the Book of Mormon, that is Mormon talking directly to you, pointing out the lesson he wants you to learn.

He also **organized** the narrative carefully. He chose to arrange events in a way that would teach principles, not just report history. He made decisions about pacing, sometimes covering decades in a single verse, other times slowing down to spend whole chapters on a single sermon or battle. Every one of these choices shaped the book.

Because of this, the Book of Mormon is truly **Mormon's book**. His fingerprints are on every page. He decided which prophets got long sections and which got brief mentions. He decided which wars to describe in detail and which to skip. He chose which of Jesus Christ's teachings to include when He visited the Nephites.

Mormon did all of this while leading armies, watching his civilization collapse, and knowing his people were headed for destruction. He wrote by firelight in war camps. He carried heavy metal plates through hostile territory. And he never stopped, because he knew the book he was creating would matter more than anything else he could do.
