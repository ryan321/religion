### Lesson 9.1 -- Mormon Leads Armies

Mormon didn't just write about war -- he lived it. For **decades**, he served as the commander of the Nephite armies, leading his people through an endless series of battles against the Lamanites. It was the job he had been given at age sixteen, and he carried it faithfully for most of his life.

But leading the Nephite armies was not like leading a righteous cause. The Nephites were no longer a good people. They had become deeply wicked. They **boasted** about their own strength instead of trusting God. They **cursed God** when things went badly, blaming Him for their problems instead of looking at their own choices. And when they won battles, they didn't give thanks -- they sought **revenge**, wanting to crush their enemies rather than find peace (Mormon 2-3).

Mormon kept fighting anyway. He was not fighting because he thought war was glorious or because he enjoyed it. He fought because he loved his people and hoped -- desperately hoped -- that the suffering of war would cause them to **repent** and turn back to God. He had seen it happen before in Nephite history. Other generations had been humbled by war and returned to the Lord. Mormon prayed that his people would do the same.

They didn't.

No matter how many battles they lost, no matter how much suffering they experienced, the Nephites refused to change. Mormon wrote about this with deep sadness. He wanted so badly to see his people humble themselves, cry out to God, and be saved. Instead, they "did curse God, and wish to die" (Mormon 2:14). Their sorrow was not the godly sorrow that leads to repentance. It was the "sorrowing of the damned" -- they were miserable but unwilling to change.

Imagine being in Mormon's position. You are a prophet who knows the truth. You can see exactly what your people need to do to be saved. But they will not listen. And every day you have to stand on the battlefield and watch them die, knowing it did not have to be this way.

Mormon carried this burden for years. He led and fought and prayed, and his people kept sinking deeper into darkness. It was one of the loneliest, most heartbreaking assignments any prophet in scripture ever received.
