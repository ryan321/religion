### Lesson 9.2 -- Mormon Refuses to Lead

There comes a point when even the most patient person reaches a limit. For Mormon, that moment came after years of watching his people grow worse instead of better. Despite all his efforts, all his prayers, and all his pleading, the Nephites refused to repent. And finally, Mormon made a painful decision.

**"I did utterly refuse from this time forth to be a commander and a leader of this people"** (Mormon 3:11).

Mormon quit. He stepped down as the general of the Nephite armies. This was not a casual decision. He had been leading these armies since he was sixteen years old. This was his life's work. But the Nephites had crossed a line. They had begun swearing oaths of vengeance and going on the offensive, attacking the Lamanites aggressively rather than defending their families. They boasted that they would destroy their enemies by their own power, giving no credit to God.

Mormon could not lead people in a cause like that. He was a prophet of God, and he would not put God's name on a war driven by hatred and revenge. So he stepped back and became what he called an **"idle witness"** (Mormon 3:16). He watched the battles. He recorded what happened. But he would not command.

This must have been agonizing. Mormon loved his people. Even in their wickedness, he loved them. Standing on the sidelines while they marched toward destruction went against every instinct he had as a leader and a prophet. But sometimes the most righteous thing you can do is refuse to participate in something wicked, even when people you love are involved.

Mormon stayed in this position for some time, watching his people continue to lose ground. The wars grew worse. The Nephites kept falling. And eventually, something in Mormon's heart moved him to act one last time.

Out of pure **love** (not because he thought they deserved it, and not because he believed they would repent), Mormon agreed to lead the Nephite armies **one final time** (Mormon 5:1). He knew it was hopeless. He knew the end was coming. But he could not stand by and watch his people be destroyed without trying one more time to help.

That decision, to lead a doomed army out of love for a people who didn't deserve it, tells you everything you need to know about Mormon's character. He was a man of deep, stubborn, unbreakable love.
