### Lesson 9.4 -- The Last Battle

The Lamanites came. And when they attacked, the destruction was total.

Mormon had organized his 230,000 warriors into groups of ten thousand, each with a commander. As the battle raged, Mormon watched from the hill as group after group was cut down. He recorded the names of the fallen commanders and their people with heartbreaking precision: "my ten thousand who were with me... and Moroni and his ten thousand... and Gidgiddonah and his ten thousand..." (Mormon 6:10-15). One by one, the groups fell. Ten thousand here, ten thousand there, until the entire Nephite army lay destroyed.

Of the **230,000 warriors** who had gathered at Cumorah, only **twenty-four survived** -- including Mormon and his son Moroni (Mormon 6:11). Twenty-four people out of nearly a quarter of a million. The numbers are almost impossible to comprehend. And that count was only the soldiers. Countless women, children, and elderly people perished as well.

Mormon himself was wounded in the battle. Looking out over the battlefield, he saw his people -- an entire civilization -- lying dead. And in one of the most powerful passages in all of scripture, he cried out to them:

**"O ye fair ones, how could ye have departed from the ways of the Lord! O ye fair ones, how could ye have rejected that Jesus, who stood with open arms to receive you!"** (Mormon 6:17).

He called them "fair ones" -- beautiful ones, precious ones. Even after everything they had done, even after all their wickedness and rebellion, Mormon still saw them as God's children. He mourned for what they could have been. He mourned for the potential that was destroyed.

His cry continued: "Ye have fallen... and I mourn your loss" (Mormon 6:19-20). A thousand years of civilization -- the entire Nephite nation, stretching back to Lehi leaving Jerusalem -- was gone. The cities, the temples, the prophecies, the covenants, the generations of families -- all of it ended on the slopes of Cumorah.

Mormon would die shortly after, leaving Moroni alone with the plates. But Mormon's final words were not words of anger. They were words of love and sorrow from a father, a prophet, and a leader who never stopped caring about his people, even when they had given up on themselves.

The Nephite story was finished. But the record Mormon created -- the Book of Mormon -- was just beginning its journey to the world.
