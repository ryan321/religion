### Lesson 11.4 -- Temple Work for the Dead

Now that we understand why everyone needs ordinances, let's talk about how the Church actually makes this happen -- through **temple work for the dead**.

In temples around the world, members of the Church perform sacred ordinances on behalf of people who have died. These include **baptisms**, **confirmations**, **endowments**, and **sealings** -- the same ordinances living people receive, performed by proxy for those who can no longer do them for themselves. Young men and women starting at age 12 can participate in baptisms for the dead, and it's often one of the first temple experiences a young person has.

But here's something crucial to understand: performing an ordinance for someone who has died does **not** force that person to accept it. Agency -- the power to choose -- continues in the spirit world. The person's spirit is completely free to accept or reject the ordinance. We are simply making the ordinance **available** to them, so that if they choose to accept the gospel, the required ordinance is already done. As the prophet Joseph Smith taught, the ordinance stands ready, like an open door -- but only the person can choose to walk through it (see D&C 128:18).

This is why **family history** is such an important part of the gospel. Before you can perform an ordinance for someone, you need to know who they are. Finding the names of your ancestors -- your grandparents, great-grandparents, and beyond -- connects directly to the plan of salvation. When you search for your family's names and take them to the temple, you are literally helping the plan work for people who couldn't do it for themselves.

The prophet Malachi said that before the great and dreadful day of the Lord, God would send Elijah the prophet to "turn the heart of the fathers to the children, and the heart of the children to their fathers" (Malachi 4:5-6). Latter-day Saints believe Elijah came to the Kirtland Temple in 1836 and restored the sealing power that makes this work possible (see D&C 110:13-16). That event set in motion the vast work of connecting families across the centuries.

Temple work is one of the most unselfish things a person can do. You give your time and effort to help someone you may never have met -- someone who lived long ago and has been waiting for this blessing. It's a beautiful picture of how the plan of salvation brings all of God's children together, living and dead, in one great family united by covenants.
