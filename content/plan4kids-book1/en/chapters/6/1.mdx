### Lesson 6.1 -- The Two Commandments

In the Garden of Eden, God gave Adam and Eve two commandments. First, they were told to multiply and have children (Moses 2:28). Second, they were told not to eat the fruit of the tree of knowledge of good and evil (Moses 3:17). At first glance, these two commandments seemed to conflict with each other -- and that's one of the most important things to understand about the Fall.

Here's why. In their innocent state in the Garden, Adam and Eve could not have children. The scriptures later confirm this -- without the Fall, "they would have had no children" (2 Nephi 2:23). So how could they obey the commandment to multiply if they stayed in the Garden and never ate the fruit? They couldn't fulfill both commandments at the same time.

This wasn't a mistake on God's part. It was by design. God needed the Fall to happen, but He needed it to happen by choice, not by force. If God had simply kicked Adam and Eve out of the Garden or forced them to eat the fruit, there would have been no real agency involved. The whole point of the plan was that God's children would choose for themselves.

So God set up a situation where Adam and Eve had to think, consider, and decide. He gave them their agency -- "Nevertheless, thou mayest choose for thyself" (Moses 3:17) -- and then let them work it out. The Fall wasn't something that went wrong. It was something that had to happen, and it had to happen through a real choice made by real people.

This is what makes the Fall different from a simple act of disobedience. It wasn't just breaking a rule for no reason. It was choosing one path forward when staying put was no longer an option for fulfilling God's purposes. The Fall was part of the plan all along.

Understanding this changes how we see the entire story of Adam and Eve. They weren't foolish people who ruined everything. They were brave spirits who faced an impossible situation and made the choice that moved the plan of salvation forward for all of God's children.
