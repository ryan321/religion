### Lesson 1.2 -- Authority vs. Power

When we talk about the priesthood, two words come up a lot: **authority** and **power**. They might sound like the same thing, but they're actually quite different, and understanding the difference matters more than you might think.

**Authority** is the right to act in God's name. A person receives this authority through **ordination** -- when someone who already holds the priesthood lays their hands on your head and confers it upon you. Once you're ordained, you have the official right to perform certain duties. Think of authority like a badge. A police officer has a badge that gives them the right to enforce the law. Without the badge, they're just a regular person.

**Power**, on the other hand, is the actual ability to act effectively with that authority. You can have the badge, but if you don't live in a way that invites the Spirit, the priesthood won't work the way it should. Power comes from **righteous living**: being honest, prayerful, kind, and obedient.

The Lord made this crystal clear in one of the most important scriptures about the priesthood. In Doctrine and Covenants 121:36, He said that "the rights of the priesthood are inseparably connected with the powers of heaven, and that the powers of heaven cannot be controlled nor handled only upon the principles of righteousness." Read that again slowly. The powers of heaven only work through righteousness. You can't cheat your way into priesthood power. You can't bully your way into it. You earn it by how you live every single day.

The verses that follow (D&C 121:37-39) warn that when a man tries to use the priesthood to control others, or to be prideful or unrighteous, his authority becomes meaningless. "The heavens withdraw themselves; the Spirit of the Lord is grieved." The badge is still there, but the power behind it is gone.

This is actually great news for you. It means that priesthood power isn't reserved for people who are rich, famous, or super talented. It's available to anyone who lives righteously. A humble twelve-year-old deacon who prays, keeps the commandments, and serves others can have tremendous priesthood power, maybe even more than someone twice his age who isn't living right.

So when you think about preparing for the priesthood, remember: ordination gives you the **authority**, but your choices give you the **power**. Both matter. Both are needed. And both are gifts from a loving Heavenly Father who trusts you to use them well.
