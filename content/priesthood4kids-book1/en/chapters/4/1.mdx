### Lesson 4.1 -- The Aaronic Priesthood

When Jesus Christ's Church is on the earth, He gives His people two levels of priesthood authority. The lower level is called the **Aaronic Priesthood**. It is named after **Aaron**, the brother of Moses, because Aaron and his sons were the first priests called to serve in this way under the Law of Moses. The Lord explained this in Doctrine and Covenants 107:13, saying that the lesser priesthood "is called the Priesthood of Aaron, because it was conferred upon Aaron and his seed, throughout all their generations."

The Aaronic Priesthood is sometimes called the **lesser priesthood** or the **preparatory priesthood**. That does not mean it is unimportant. It means it prepares people for greater things. Think of it like learning to ride a bicycle before you drive a car. Riding the bike is real and valuable, but it also gets you ready for something bigger down the road.

Why is it called "preparatory"? Doctrine and Covenants 84:26 teaches that the lesser priesthood holds "the key of the ministering of angels and the preparatory gospel." It helps people take the first steps of faith, repentance, and baptism. It opens the door so they can later receive the higher blessings of the Melchizedek Priesthood.

Holders of the Aaronic Priesthood have the authority to perform certain ordinances. They can **baptize** people who are ready to enter the covenant. They can **prepare and bless the sacrament** so that members can renew their baptismal covenants each week. These are sacred and essential responsibilities.

However, the Aaronic Priesthood does **not** include the authority to do everything. It does not give the power to **confirm** someone a member of the Church, to **bestow the gift of the Holy Ghost**, or to **ordain** someone to the Melchizedek Priesthood. Those ordinances require the higher priesthood. Understanding this difference helps us see why both levels of priesthood are needed in Christ's Church.

Today in The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, young men begin receiving the Aaronic Priesthood at age twelve. It is the beginning of a lifetime of service. Every deacon, teacher, and priest who worthily exercises the Aaronic Priesthood is doing something that traces all the way back to Aaron in ancient times and forward to the Savior's plan for each of us.
