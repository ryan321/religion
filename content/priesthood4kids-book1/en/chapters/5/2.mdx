### Lesson 5.2 -- Keys vs. Authority vs. Power

Three words come up often when we talk about the priesthood: **authority**, **keys**, and **power**. They sound similar, but each one means something different. Understanding all three helps you see the full picture of how the priesthood works.

**Authority** is the right to act in God's name. A young man receives priesthood authority when he is **ordained** by someone who already holds that authority. Once ordained a deacon, teacher, or priest, he has the right to perform the duties of that office. Authority comes through the laying on of hands and is real and permanent. It does not go away unless it is formally removed.

**Keys** are the right to **direct** the use of the priesthood. Keys come through being **set apart** to a specific leadership calling. Not everyone who holds the priesthood holds keys. For example, every deacon in a quorum has priesthood authority, but only the **deacons quorum president** holds the keys for that quorum. He has the right to direct the work of the quorum, assigning who passes the sacrament, organizing service projects, and leading quorum meetings. His keys are specific to his stewardship and last only as long as he serves in that calling.

**Power** is the spiritual strength and capacity that comes from **righteous living**. This is the one you have the most control over. Doctrine and Covenants 121:36 warns that "the rights of the priesthood are inseparably connected with the powers of heaven, and that the powers of heaven cannot be controlled nor handled only upon the principles of righteousness." A person can hold authority and even keys, but if he is not living right (if he is unkind, dishonest, or prideful), the power of the priesthood withdraws from him. He still has the title, but the heavenly power behind it fades.

You need all three for the priesthood to work as God intends. Authority gives you the right. Keys give you the direction. Power gives you the heavenly backing. Think of a flashlight: authority is owning the flashlight, keys determine when and where you turn it on, and power is the battery that makes it actually shine.

A deacon who is kind, prayerful, and obedient may not hold keys, but he carries real priesthood power. A quorum president who holds keys but is careless in his living will find that his service lacks the Spirit. The Lord wants all three working together.
