### Lesson 8.3 -- What Unrighteous Dominion Looks Like

Now that we know what the Lord says about unrighteous dominion in D&C 121, let's talk about what it actually looks like in real life. This is important because sometimes people use their authority in wrong ways without even realizing it.

Unrighteous dominion is using priesthood authority -- or any authority -- to control, manipulate, or force other people. It can be loud and obvious, or it can be quiet and sneaky. Here are some examples.

A father who demands obedience through fear rather than love is exercising unrighteous dominion. If his children obey him only because they are afraid of being yelled at or punished harshly, something is wrong. God does not want obedience that comes from fear. He wants obedience that comes from understanding, love, and trust.

A Church leader who bullies, shames, or pressures people into doing things is exercising unrighteous dominion. A quorum president who makes fun of a boy who misses a meeting. A teacher who embarrasses someone for giving a wrong answer. A leader who makes people feel guilty to get them to do what he wants. These are all forms of control, and they drive the Spirit away.

Another form of unrighteous dominion is thinking that the priesthood makes you better than other people. It does not. The priesthood is not a badge of superiority. It is a responsibility to serve. Jesus made this clear when He washed His Apostles' feet. He was the Son of God -- the most powerful being on earth -- and He knelt down and washed dirty feet (see John 13:12-17). He then said, "I have given you an example, that ye should do as I have done to you."

Here is the key principle: the priesthood is not about power OVER people. It is about power to SERVE people. The moment it becomes about you -- your ego, your control, your status -- it stops working. As the Savior taught in Mark 10:43-44, "Whosoever will be great among you, shall be your minister: and whosoever of you will be the chiefest, shall be servant of all."

This applies to every priesthood holder, whether you are a deacon or an apostle. The priesthood is given to bless other people, not to lift yourself up. And if you ever catch yourself using your authority to make yourself feel important or to push someone around, that is a signal to stop and change course immediately.
