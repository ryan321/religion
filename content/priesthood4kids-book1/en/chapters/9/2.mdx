### Lesson 9.2 -- Persuasion, Long-suffering, and Gentleness

Let's look more closely at the first three qualities the Lord lists in D&C 121:41 for how priesthood power should be exercised: persuasion, long-suffering, and gentleness. These are not just nice words. They are deeply practical, and each one takes real strength.

**Persuasion** means influencing people through reason, testimony, and example -- not through threats, pressure, or manipulation. When you persuade someone, you help them see the truth and choose it for themselves. You explain, you teach, you share your own experience, and then you let them decide. A father who persuades his children helps them understand why something matters. A quorum president who persuades invites and inspires rather than guilts and demands. Persuasion respects the other person's agency -- their God-given right to choose. As Joshua declared in Joshua 24:15, "Choose you this day whom ye will serve; ... but as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord." He stated his commitment and invited others to make theirs. That is persuasion.

**Long-suffering** means patience -- especially with people who are slow, difficult, or frustrating. It means enduring without giving up on someone. It is easy to be patient with people who do what you want right away. Real long-suffering is being patient with the person who keeps making the same mistake, who struggles to understand, or who does not seem to be trying very hard. The Lord is the greatest example of long-suffering. In 2 Peter 3:9, we read that "the Lord is not slack concerning his promise ... but is longsuffering to us-ward, not willing that any should perish, but that all should come to repentance." God does not give up on us when we are slow to learn. Priesthood holders should not give up on others either.

**Gentleness** means being kind and tender even when you have the authority to be harsh. A gentle leader does not bark orders. He does not humiliate people for their mistakes. He corrects with care. He builds people up rather than tearing them down. Gentleness is not weakness. It takes far more strength to remain gentle when you are frustrated than to lash out in anger. As Paul taught in 2 Timothy 2:24-25, "The servant of the Lord must not strive; but be gentle unto all men, apt to teach, patient, in meekness instructing those that oppose themselves."

These three qualities -- persuasion, long-suffering, and gentleness -- are signs of strength, not weakness. They are marks of a man who trusts God enough to lead God's way.
