### Lesson 1.3 -- Doctrine Changes

Once the apostles were gone, something predictable happened. Without prophets to receive revelation from God, people started figuring out the gospel on their own. And when people rely on their own ideas instead of God's guidance, things go wrong, sometimes slowly, sometimes in big ways.

At first, local leaders tried their best. But they disagreed with each other. Questions came up that nobody could answer with authority. What exactly is the nature of God? How should baptism be performed? What happens after we die? These were important questions, and without a prophet to ask God for answers, people turned to **debate and philosophy** instead of revelation.

Over the next few centuries, church leaders held **councils** -- big meetings where scholars and bishops argued about doctrine. One of the most famous was the **Council of Nicaea** in AD 325. At this council, leaders debated the nature of God, Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost. Instead of relying on scripture and revelation, many of them mixed in ideas from **Greek philosophy**. The result was the **Nicene Creed**, which described God in ways that would have been unfamiliar to the early apostles. The simple truth that God the Father, Jesus Christ, and the Holy Ghost are three separate beings was replaced with a confusing idea that they are one single substance.

**Baptism** changed too. In the New Testament, baptism was done by **immersion** -- going all the way under the water (Romans 6:4). Over time, some churches started sprinkling water on people's heads instead. The form of the ordinance was altered by human decision, not by revelation.

The **plan of salvation** (the beautiful truth about where we came from, why we're here, and where we're going) was largely lost. Ideas about heaven and hell became vague or mixed with pagan beliefs. The understanding of a premortal life disappeared entirely from mainstream Christianity.

This didn't happen overnight. It was a **gradual process** that took place over hundreds of years. That's why we call it the **Great Apostasy**. "Apostasy" means falling away from the truth. It wasn't one big moment where everything changed. It was a slow drift, a little here, a little there, until the original gospel of Jesus Christ was barely recognizable.

The important thing to understand is that these weren't bad people trying to destroy the gospel. Most of them were sincere. They just didn't have the authority or the revelation to get it right. That's what happens when the priesthood and prophets are missing.
