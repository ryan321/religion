### Lesson 11.1 -- Zion in Missouri

The Lord had big plans for Missouri. Through revelation, He told Joseph Smith that the land of **Zion**, the **New Jerusalem**, would be built in **Jackson County, Missouri** (Doctrine and Covenants 57:1-3). This was exciting news. The Saints believed they were going to build a holy city, a place where the righteous could gather and prepare for the Second Coming of Jesus Christ.

Settlers began moving to Missouri. Families packed up their belongings and traveled hundreds of miles to start new lives in this promised land. They arrived with hope and faith, ready to build Zion.

But problems started almost immediately. The people who already lived in Missouri, the **old settlers**, did not like the Latter-day Saints one bit. There were several reasons for this tension.

First, the Saints had a **strange religion**. They talked about prophets, revelations, and angels. They read the Book of Mormon alongside the Bible. The old settlers thought this was bizarre and maybe even dangerous.

Second, the Saints **voted as a bloc**. When election time came, the Latter-day Saints tended to vote together for the same candidates. The existing residents worried that the Saints would take over local government and run things their way.

Third, many of the Saints came from **Northern states** and held **anti-slavery views**. Missouri was a slave state. The old settlers were deeply suspicious of Northerners who might oppose slavery and upset the social order.

Fourth, the Saints just **kept coming**. More and more families arrived every month. The old settlers felt like they were being overrun. They feared they would become outnumbered in their own county.

None of these reasons justified what the old settlers would eventually do. But it helps you understand why the tension was so high. The Saints were different, and the people of Missouri did not want them there.

The Lord had called Missouri "Zion," but living there was going to be anything but peaceful. The Saints were about to face some of the darkest days in Church history. Their faith would be tested in ways they never imagined.
