### Lesson 11.2 -- Driven Out

The tension between the Latter-day Saints and the old settlers in Missouri did not stay tension for long. It turned into violence.

In **1833**, mobs in Jackson County attacked the Saints. They broke into the Church's **printing press** and destroyed it, scattering pages of the Book of Commandments (an early version of the Doctrine and Covenants) into the street. They **tarred and feathered** Church leaders -- a painful, humiliating punishment where hot tar is poured on someone's skin and then feathers are stuck to it. They burned homes and threatened families.

The mobs **drove the Saints from their homes** in the middle of winter. Families fled through snow and freezing cold with little food and few belongings. Women carried children. Men tried to protect their families while being chased by armed groups. It was brutal and heartbreaking.

The Saints relocated to other counties in Missouri -- places like **Clay County** and later **Caldwell County**. For a few years, things were somewhat calmer. But by **1838**, the tensions exploded again. Old conflicts returned. Fights broke out. Both sides committed acts of violence.

Then came one of the darkest moments in American religious history. The governor of Missouri, **Lilburn W. Boggs**, issued what became known as the **Extermination Order**. On October 27, 1838, he wrote that the Mormons "must be treated as enemies, and must be **exterminated** or driven from the State." Read that again. A governor of an American state officially ordered that an entire group of people be killed or forced to leave -- simply because of their religion.

This was not just words on paper. The order gave mobs and militias legal cover to attack the Saints. Thousands of Latter-day Saints were forced to flee Missouri. They lost their homes, their farms, their livestock, and in some cases their lives.

The Missouri period taught the Saints a painful lesson: following the Lord does not always mean life will be easy. Sometimes faith means enduring things that are deeply unfair. But the Saints held on to their testimonies even when everything else was taken from them.
