### Lesson 11.3 -- Haun's Mill

Just three days after Governor Boggs issued the Extermination Order, one of the most tragic events in Church history took place. On **October 30, 1838**, an armed mob attacked a small Latter-day Saint settlement called **Haun's Mill** in eastern Missouri.

The settlement was a small, peaceful community built along Shoal Creek. About thirty families lived there. They had a blacksmith shop, a mill for grinding grain, and simple homes. These were ordinary people: farmers, tradespeople, mothers, fathers, and children, just trying to live their lives.

The mob rode in on horseback, roughly **240 armed men**. The attack was sudden and vicious. The Saints had almost no warning. Some of the men tried to defend themselves, but they were badly outnumbered. Many ran to the blacksmith shop for cover, but the mob fired through the gaps in the logs, turning it into a death trap.

When the shooting stopped, **17 men and boys lay dead**. Among them was **Sardius Smith**, a boy just **ten years old**. He was hiding under the bellows in the blacksmith shop when a mob member found him and killed him in cold blood. Several more people were badly wounded.

The **women and children** survived by hiding in the woods along the creek. They listened to the gunfire and screaming, not knowing if their fathers, husbands, and brothers were alive or dead. When they finally came back, they found a scene of horror. There was no time for proper burials. The survivors lowered the bodies into an unfinished well.

The attack at Haun's Mill was **brutal and unprovoked**. These people had not attacked anyone. They were simply living in Missouri and practicing their religion.

Three days after the massacre, **Joseph Smith** and other Church leaders **surrendered** to the Missouri militia. Joseph did this to prevent more bloodshed. He knew that if he gave himself up, the mobs might leave the rest of the Saints alone. He was arrested and thrown into prison without a proper trial.

Joseph's willingness to sacrifice his own freedom to protect his people showed the kind of leader he was. Even in the darkest moments, he put others first.
