### Lesson 11.4 -- Liberty Jail

After surrendering to the Missouri militia, Joseph Smith and several other Church leaders were taken to a small jail in **Liberty, Missouri**. They arrived in late 1838 and spent the entire **winter of 1838-1839** locked inside.

**Liberty Jail** was a miserable place. The building was made of rough stone with walls several feet thick. The ceiling was so low that Joseph, a tall man, could barely stand up straight. The cell was **cold, dark, and cramped**. There were no real beds, just a hard stone floor. The food the guards brought was **rotten** and sometimes so disgusting the prisoners could barely eat it. There were even rumors that human flesh was mixed into their food to torment them.

While Joseph suffered inside the jail, the Saints outside were being forced to leave Missouri in the dead of winter. Thousands of men, women, and children marched through snow and mud, sick and starving, heading east toward Illinois. Joseph could do nothing to help them. He could only pray.

At his lowest point, Joseph cried out to the Lord: **"O God, where art thou? And where is the pavilion that covereth thy hiding place?"** (Doctrine and Covenants 121:1). He was not angry at God. He was heartbroken. He wanted to know why the Lord would let His people suffer so much.

The Lord answered Joseph with some of the most powerful words in all of scripture: **"My son, peace be unto thy soul; thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment; and then, if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high"** (D&C 121:7-8).

Several sections of the Doctrine and Covenants (including parts of sections 121, 122, and 123) were received as revelations during this time. These are among the most beloved scriptures in the Church. Joseph later called Liberty Jail a **"temple of God"** because of the sacred revelations he received there.

Liberty Jail teaches us something important. Sometimes our hardest moments become our most sacred ones. When we turn to the Lord in our pain, He can turn our prisons into temples.
