### Lesson 12.1 -- A New Beginning

After months of suffering in Missouri, the Saints finally escaped. Joseph Smith managed to get away from his captors in early **1839**, and he rejoined his people as they crossed into **Illinois**. They were exhausted, poor, and heartbroken. But they were not defeated.

The Saints settled on a swampy bend of the **Mississippi River** in western Illinois. The land was cheap for a reason -- it was wet, muddy, and full of mosquitoes that carried **malaria**. Many Saints got terribly sick. Some died. It did not seem like a promising place to start over.

But the Saints went to work. They **drained the swamps**, cleared the land, and began building homes. They named their new city **Nauvoo**, a word that comes from Hebrew and means **"beautiful."** It was an act of faith to call a swamp "beautiful," but that is exactly what the Saints turned it into.

Over the next few years, Nauvoo grew at an astonishing rate. Thousands of converts arrived from the eastern United States and from England. The city expanded quickly. The Saints built **homes, shops, schools**, and even a **university** -- the University of the City of Nauvoo. Streets were laid out in an orderly grid. Gardens and orchards were planted.

By the early 1840s, Nauvoo had grown to rival **Chicago** as one of the largest cities in Illinois. At its peak, the population reached somewhere around 12,000 people. This was remarkable for a group of refugees who had arrived with almost nothing just a few years earlier.

Nauvoo became a place of **hope and renewal** for the Saints. After being driven from Ohio and Missouri, they finally had a city they could call their own. The state of Illinois granted them a **city charter** that gave them the right to govern themselves, form a militia, and establish courts.

The Saints had been knocked down again and again. But each time, they got back up and kept building. Nauvoo was proof that faith and hard work can turn even a swamp into something beautiful.
