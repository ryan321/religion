### Lesson 14.1 -- Growing Threats

By 1844, the city of **Nauvoo, Illinois** was one of the largest cities in the state. The Saints had built something remarkable there: a thriving community with homes, businesses, schools, and a temple rising on the bluff above the Mississippi River. But not everyone was pleased with what the Saints had built. **Opposition was growing** from both outside and inside the Church.

Some former members of the Church had turned against Joseph. They were angry, and they decided to fight him publicly. In June 1844, they published a newspaper called the **Nauvoo Expositor**, which attacked Joseph and the Church. It was filled with accusations meant to stir up hatred and turn people against the Saints.

The **Nauvoo city council** met and determined the newspaper was a public nuisance. They voted to have the **printing press destroyed**. This decision, while legal under the standards of that time, gave Joseph's enemies exactly what they wanted: a legal reason to come after him. They charged Joseph with **riot** and demanded he stand trial in Carthage, the county seat.

Joseph knew that going to Carthage was dangerous. His enemies were there, and feelings were running extremely high. Some of his friends urged him not to go. For a brief time, Joseph even crossed the Mississippi River, considering whether to head west to escape. But when word came that some Saints felt he was abandoning them, Joseph turned back. He would not leave his people.

As he rode toward Carthage, Joseph spoke words that have become some of the most well-known in Church history. He said: "I am going like a **lamb to the slaughter**; but I am calm as a summer's morning; I have a conscience void of offense towards God, and towards all men" (Doctrine and Covenants 135:4).

He knew what might be waiting for him. He went anyway. Not because he was reckless, but because he trusted the Lord and he would not abandon the Saints who needed him. Joseph rode toward Carthage knowing he might never come back.
