### Lesson 14.2 -- Carthage Jail

Joseph arrived in Carthage and surrendered himself to the authorities. He was accompanied by his brother **Hyrum Smith**, along with **John Taylor** and **Willard Richards**, both members of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. The four men were taken to **Carthage Jail**, a small stone building that served as the county jail.

The **governor of Illinois, Thomas Ford**, had promised Joseph protection. He assured Joseph that he would be safe and that the law would be followed. But that promise did not hold. Governor Ford left Carthage to visit Nauvoo, and the troops assigned to guard the jail were largely made up of men who were hostile to the Saints. The protection Joseph had been promised was quietly **withdrawn**.

The four men were held in an **upstairs bedroom** on the second floor of the jail, not in a cell, but in the jailer's own quarters. They spent their time talking, singing hymns, and reading scripture. On the afternoon of **June 27, 1844**, the mood in the jail was somber. Joseph asked John Taylor to sing "A Poor Wayfaring Man of Grief," a hymn about showing kindness to a stranger who turns out to be the Savior. Taylor sang it through once, and then Joseph asked him to sing it again.

That afternoon, everything changed. A **mob** of around 150 to 200 men gathered outside the jail. Many of them had **painted their faces** with mud and gunpowder to disguise themselves. They rushed the building, overwhelming the few guards who remained. The mob surged up the stairs toward the room where the four men were held.

The men inside heard the commotion and knew what was coming. They pressed against the door, trying to hold it shut, but there was no lock strong enough to stop what was about to happen. Bullets began tearing through the wooden door. In a matter of moments, the room became a scene of violence and chaos.

What happened next would change the Church and the lives of the Saints forever.
