### Lesson 14.4 -- The Saints' Grief

Word of the killings traveled quickly from Carthage to Nauvoo. When it arrived, the grief was overwhelming. The Saints had lost their prophet, the man who had led them from the very beginning. The man through whom God had restored the gospel, the priesthood, the temple, and the Book of Mormon. Joseph was not just a leader to them. He was their friend, their brother, and God's chosen servant.

**Thousands of Saints** lined the streets as the bodies of Joseph and Hyrum were brought back to Nauvoo. The next day, an estimated **ten thousand people** came to view the bodies and pay their respects. Men and women wept openly. Children who had played with Joseph now stood beside their grieving parents. The entire city mourned.

But beyond the grief, there was a pressing and frightening question: **What now?**

Joseph Smith had been the prophet of the Restoration from the very first day. He had received the First Vision. He had translated the Book of Mormon. He had received the priesthood and organized the Church. For fourteen years, every major decision, every revelation, and every direction had come through him. Now he was gone. The Saints had never faced anything like this.

The enemies of the Church were watching closely. Many of them assumed, and even celebrated the idea, that the **Church would collapse** without Joseph. They believed that once the prophet was dead, his followers would scatter and the whole movement would fade into nothing. History was full of religious movements that died with their founders. Joseph's enemies expected the same thing to happen here.

But they were wrong.

What those enemies did not understand was that the Church was not built on Joseph Smith. It was built on **Jesus Christ**. Joseph was the instrument, but the Lord was the foundation. And before his death, Joseph had done something his enemies knew nothing about. He had **conferred all the priesthood keys** on the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. The authority to lead the Church had not died with Joseph. It had been carefully, deliberately passed on.

The days ahead would be difficult and uncertain. But the Restoration was not over. It was about to enter a new chapter.
