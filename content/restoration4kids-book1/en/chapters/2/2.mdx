### Lesson 2.2 -- The Reformation

After a thousand years of spiritual darkness, brave men and women began to push back. They saw problems in the way religion was being practiced, and they spoke up, even when it was dangerous. This movement is called the **Reformation**, and it was one of the most important steps in preparing the world for the Restoration.

One of the most famous reformers was **Martin Luther**, a German monk and professor. In 1517, Luther nailed a document called the **95 Theses** to the door of a church in Wittenberg, Germany. His list challenged many practices of the Catholic Church, especially the selling of **indulgences**, the idea that you could pay money to have your sins forgiven. Luther believed that forgiveness came through faith in Jesus Christ, not through payments to a church. His ideas spread quickly and sparked an enormous debate across Europe.

Another reformer, **John Calvin**, taught in Geneva, Switzerland. Calvin emphasized the importance of reading the Bible and living according to its teachings. His ideas influenced millions and led to new churches being formed, churches that were separate from the Catholic Church for the first time in centuries.

One of the bravest reformers was **William Tyndale**, an Englishman who believed that every person should be able to read the Bible in their own language. At the time, Bibles were only available in Latin, and translating them was illegal. Tyndale translated the Bible into **English** anyway. For this, he was hunted down, arrested, and eventually **executed** -- strangled and burned at the stake in 1536. But his translation survived. Much of the language in the King James Bible comes directly from Tyndale's work.

Here is something important to understand: the reformers were not restoring the gospel. They didn't have priesthood authority, and they didn't claim to receive revelation from God. What they did was **open the door**. They challenged the idea that one church should control all religious thought. They put the Bible into the hands of ordinary people. They made it acceptable, and eventually legal, to worship differently.

The Reformation didn't bring back the true Church. But it broke the grip of religious control and planted the seeds of **religious freedom**. Without the Reformation, the conditions for the Restoration could never have existed. God was using courageous people to prepare the way.
