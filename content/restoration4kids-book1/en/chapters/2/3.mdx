### Lesson 2.3 -- The Printing Press and the Bible

If you wanted a copy of the Bible in the year 1400, here's what it would take. A trained scribe would sit at a desk and copy the entire thing by hand, word by word, page by page. It could take a year or more. The finished book would be incredibly expensive -- so expensive that only churches, monasteries, and the very wealthy could own one. Most people would never hold a Bible in their entire lives.

Then, around **1440**, a German inventor named **Johannes Gutenberg** changed the world. He developed the **printing press with movable type** -- a machine that could arrange metal letters, ink them, and press them onto paper over and over again. For the first time in history, books could be **mass-produced** quickly and cheaply.

The first major book Gutenberg printed was the Bible -- now known as the **Gutenberg Bible**. It was a masterpiece, but more importantly, it proved that the technology worked. Within just a few decades, printing presses spread across Europe. Suddenly, books that once took a year to copy could be printed in days. The cost dropped dramatically. Ideas could travel faster than ever before.

This was a game-changer for religion. Before the printing press, the Catholic Church controlled most of what people knew about the scriptures. Priests would read passages in Latin and explain them however they chose. Ordinary people had no way to check. But once Bibles could be printed in large numbers -- and especially once they were translated into languages like English, German, and French -- **people could read the scriptures for themselves**.

Think about how important that is. If you've never read the scriptures yourself, you have to take someone else's word for what they say. But once you can read them on your own, you can ask your own questions. You can notice things. You can search for truth. That's exactly what started happening across Europe.

The printing press also helped the Reformation spread. Martin Luther's 95 Theses, for example, were printed and distributed across Germany within weeks. Without the press, his ideas might have stayed local. With it, they reached millions.

God was preparing the world. The printing press put the scriptures into the hands of the people, and that was a critical step toward the day when the gospel would be restored. People needed access to God's word before they could recognize the need for more of it.
