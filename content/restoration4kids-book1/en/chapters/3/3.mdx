### Lesson 3.3 -- The Religious Confusion

The revivals in the Palmyra area didn't just affect the community -- they hit the Smith family directly. Joseph watched as his own family members were drawn to different churches, and it troubled him.

**Lucy**, Joseph's mother, and some of his siblings leaned toward the **Presbyterian** faith. They attended meetings and were drawn to the Presbyterian way of worship. Joseph's father, meanwhile, was more cautious. He believed in God and had spiritual experiences, but he was wary of joining any organized church. He wasn't sure any of them had it right.

Young Joseph himself felt drawn to the **Methodists**. He liked some of what they taught, and he attended their meetings. But he wasn't ready to commit. Something held him back -- a feeling that he couldn't just pick one and hope for the best. The decision felt too important for guessing.

The problem was that **every church claimed to be right**, and they all said the others were wrong. Joseph later described the confusion this way: "So great were the confusion and strife among the different denominations, that it was impossible for a person as young as I was ... to come to any certain conclusion who was right and who was wrong" (Joseph Smith--History 1:8).

Think about what that felt like. You're a teenager. You believe in God. You want to follow Jesus Christ. But the adults around you -- the preachers, the church leaders, the people you respect -- can't agree on what following Jesus actually looks like. The Baptists say you need to be immersed in water. The Presbyterians say sprinkling is fine. The Methodists have their own approach. Some preach that God chose who would be saved before they were even born. Others say you can choose for yourself. The disagreements go on and on.

Joseph wasn't just casually wondering about this. It weighed on him. He described feeling **deep anxiety** about the state of his soul. He genuinely wanted to know the truth -- not to win an argument, not to prove someone wrong, but because he believed it mattered for his eternal life.

The religious confusion in Palmyra did something important. It forced Joseph to realize that **he couldn't solve this by listening to more preachers**. They all contradicted each other. If he was going to find the truth, he would need to look somewhere else. And that realization was about to lead him to one of the most important verses in the Bible.
