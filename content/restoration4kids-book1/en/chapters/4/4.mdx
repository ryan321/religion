### Lesson 4.4 -- The Aftermath

After the light faded and Joseph Smith found himself lying on his back in the Sacred Grove, he was filled with wonder. He had just seen **God the Father and Jesus Christ**. He had received a direct answer to his prayer. You might think everyone would be excited to hear about it. But that is not what happened.

When Joseph came home, he leaned against the fireplace. His mother could tell something had happened and asked what was wrong. Joseph told her he had learned that the **Presbyterian church was not true** -- that none of the churches had the fulness. He began to share his experience with others, including a local **Methodist minister** he thought he could trust.

The minister's reaction surprised Joseph. The man dismissed the whole thing. He said visions and revelations had ended long ago and that Joseph's experience was from the **devil**. Instead of kindness and interest, Joseph got **rejection and mockery**.

It did not stop with one minister. As word spread, people from many churches began to **persecute** Joseph. He was just a young, uneducated farm boy. Important, educated men turned against him. They made fun of him. They said terrible things about him. Joseph later compared himself to the Apostle Paul, who was also mocked after telling people about a vision from heaven.

But here is what makes Joseph remarkable. He **never denied it**. Not once. No matter how hard things got, he stood by what he had seen. He later wrote one of the most powerful statements in all of scripture: **"I had seen a vision; I knew it, and I knew that God knew it, and I could not deny it"** (Joseph Smith--History 1:25).

Joseph could not pretend it had not happened. He could not say he made it up just to make life easier. The vision was real, and he knew it. He was willing to face **ridicule, hatred, and persecution** rather than lie about what God had shown him.

This tells us something about **testimony**. When you know something is true, you do not let other people talk you out of it. It does not matter if they laugh at you or disagree. Truth is truth. Joseph learned that lesson early, and he held onto it for the rest of his life.

The persecution Joseph faced also shows us that **opposition** often follows sacred experiences. Doing the right thing does not always make life easy. But standing firm is always worth it.
