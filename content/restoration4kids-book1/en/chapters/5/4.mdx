### Lesson 5.4 -- Receiving the Plates

The day finally arrived. On **September 22, 1827**, Joseph Smith went to the Hill Cumorah for the fifth time. This time, Moroni allowed him to **take the plates**. After four years of preparation, Joseph was ready.

Joseph received three sacred objects from the stone box: the **gold plates**, the **Urim and Thummim** (the translating instruments), and a **breastplate**. Moroni gave Joseph a solemn charge -- he was personally responsible for keeping these items safe. If Joseph was careless or negligent with them, he would be **cut off**. But if he did everything in his power to protect them until Moroni came back for them, they would be preserved.

Joseph took this responsibility seriously, and he needed to. Almost immediately, people tried to **steal the plates**. Word spread quickly that Joseph Smith had a set of gold plates, and greedy people wanted them. Some thought they could melt the gold down for money. Others just wanted to cause trouble.

Joseph had to be creative about hiding the plates. He wrapped them in a cloth and hid them in a **hollowed-out log** in the woods. When that was not safe enough, he brought them home and hid them **under the hearthstones** by the fireplace. At one point, he buried them in a **barrel of beans**. He also hid them in his father's cooper shop under the floorboards.

People broke into the Smith home searching for the plates. Mobs gathered. Joseph and Emma eventually had to **leave the area entirely**, traveling to Harmony, Pennsylvania, where Emma's family lived, just to get some peace and begin the work of translation.

The opposition was intense, but it did not stop the work. Joseph had the plates. He had the tools to translate them. And he had a commandment from God to bring forth this sacred record. No amount of threats or break-ins or mobs could stop what God had set in motion.

This part of the story shows us how **real** the plates were. People could feel them through cloth. They were heavy -- about **sixty pounds**. Joseph had to physically carry, move, and hide them. This was not imaginary. The gold plates were a real, physical object, and the work of translating them was about to begin.
