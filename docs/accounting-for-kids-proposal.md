# Accounting for Kids — Book Proposal

## Series Context

This proposal is for 2 books that teach accounting in the same style as the Economics for Kids series (Books 1–8). Same format, tone, age target, and lesson structure. These books fill the gap between "understanding how businesses work" (econ Books 6–8) and actually being able to read, create, and interpret financial records.

**Differentiation from Econ Book 6 (Business Finance):**
Book 6 teaches a business owner to *read* financial information — what the three statements are, profit vs. cash, cost structure. The Accounting series teaches how that information gets *created and analyzed* — the recording system, the logic behind it, and how to use statements to evaluate a business. Book 6 is "what the dashboard tells you." Accounting is "how the dashboard gets built, and how to read it like a pro."

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters per book, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300–500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7–12
- Real-world examples from a kid's life (lemonade stands, allowance tracking, school fundraisers, small shops)

## Math expectations

- Addition, subtraction, and basic comparison throughout — these are within range for the age group
- Multiplication for simple calculations (units x price, etc.)
- Percentages introduced gently in Book 2 (margins, ratios) — explained from scratch, not assumed
- No algebra, no complex formulas
- The math is always in service of understanding, not computation for its own sake
- Where possible, use round numbers that make the arithmetic invisible so the concept stays front and center

## Prerequisites

- Recommended: Economics for Kids Book 1 (scarcity, choices) and Book 6 (business finance basics)
- Not strictly required — the accounting books are self-contained and re-introduce concepts as needed

## Running example

Both books use a recurring example business — a kid's snack shop — that grows in complexity as concepts build. This gives continuity: the student isn't learning in the abstract, they're building the accounting for one business across the whole series. Individual lessons also use other examples (lawn mowing, babysitting, a pet store) but the snack shop is the thread.

## Book 1: How Accounting Works

**Subtitle:** Recording, Organizing, and Reporting

The goal: by the end of this book, the student has built a complete set of financial statements for a pretend business by hand. They understand the accounting equation, debits and credits, journals, ledgers, and how transactions flow through the system to produce an income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement.

### Chapter 1: Why We Keep Records

**Core concepts:** Purpose of accounting, who uses it, why it matters

- 1.1 — What Is Accounting?
  - Accounting is the system for recording, organizing, and reporting what happens with money in a business. It's the business's memory. Without it, you're guessing.
- 1.2 — Who Needs Accounting?
  - The owner (am I making money?), investors (is this a good business?), lenders (can they pay me back?), the government (are they paying taxes?). Different people use the same records for different reasons.
- 1.3 — Accounting vs. Bookkeeping
  - Bookkeeping is recording transactions — writing down what happened. Accounting is the bigger picture: organizing those records, interpreting them, and using them to make decisions. Bookkeeping is part of accounting.
- 1.4 — The Language of Business
  - Accounting is often called "the language of business" because it's how businesses communicate their financial health. Learning accounting is like learning to read a language everyone in business speaks.

**Test:** 4 questions on what accounting is, who uses it, bookkeeping vs. accounting, and why it's called the language of business.

### Chapter 2: Transactions — Where It All Starts

**Core concepts:** What a transaction is, examples, source documents

- 2.1 — What Is a Transaction?
  - A transaction is any event that affects the business's money or what it owns. Selling a product, buying supplies, paying rent, receiving a loan — these are all transactions.
- 2.2 — Every Transaction Has Two Sides
  - This is the most important idea in accounting. When you buy supplies for $10, two things happen: you get supplies (something gained) and you lose $10 cash (something given up). Always two sides.
- 2.3 — Source Documents
  - A source document is proof that a transaction happened. Receipts, invoices, bank statements, contracts. These are the evidence. Without them, you're just trusting memory — and memory is unreliable.
- 2.4 — Recording Transactions
  - You record a transaction as soon as it happens, not later when you remember it. The sooner you write it down, the more accurate your records will be. Waiting leads to mistakes and missing entries.

**Test:** 4 questions on what transactions are, the two-sides idea, source documents, and when to record.

### Chapter 3: The Accounting Equation

**Core concepts:** Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity, what each means

- 3.1 — Assets: What You Own
  - Assets are things of value the business owns or controls. Cash, inventory, equipment, money customers owe you. If it has value and belongs to the business, it's an asset.
- 3.2 — Liabilities: What You Owe
  - Liabilities are debts — money the business owes to others. Loans, unpaid bills, money owed to suppliers. If someone can come collect from you, it's a liability.
- 3.3 — Owner's Equity: What's Left
  - Owner's equity is what's left after you subtract liabilities from assets. It's the owner's share. If the business has $100 in assets and $30 in liabilities, the owner's equity is $70.
- 3.4 — The Equation That Always Balances
  - Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity. This equation always balances. Every single transaction keeps it balanced. If it doesn't balance, something is wrong. This is the foundation of all accounting.

**Test:** 4 questions on assets, liabilities, owner's equity, and why the equation always balances.

### Chapter 4: Accounts — Categories for Everything

**Core concepts:** Types of accounts, chart of accounts, naming conventions

- 4.1 — What Is an Account?
  - An account is a category for tracking one type of thing. "Cash" is an account. "Supplies" is an account. "Sales Revenue" is an account. Each account collects all the transactions related to that one thing.
- 4.2 — The Five Types of Accounts
  - Assets, Liabilities, Owner's Equity, Revenue, and Expenses. Every account in every business fits into one of these five types. Revenue and Expenses are subcategories that flow into Owner's Equity.
- 4.3 — The Chart of Accounts
  - A chart of accounts is the master list of all accounts a business uses. A simple business might have 10-15 accounts. A big company might have hundreds. But they all fit into the same five types.
- 4.4 — Naming Accounts Clearly
  - Account names should be obvious. "Cash" not "Money Stuff." "Office Supplies" not "Things We Bought." Clear names mean anyone can look at the records and understand them.

**Test:** 4 questions on what accounts are, the five types, the chart of accounts, and clear naming.

### Chapter 5: Debits and Credits

**Core concepts:** The debit/credit system, which accounts increase with which, T-accounts

- 5.1 — Debits and Credits Are Just Left and Right
  - Debit means left side. Credit means right side. That's it. Forget what these words mean in everyday English — in accounting, they just mean left and right. Every transaction records an amount on the left side of one account and the right side of another.
- 5.2 — Which Side Increases Which?
  - Assets and Expenses increase with debits (left side). Liabilities, Owner's Equity, and Revenue increase with credits (right side). This is the rule you memorize. Once you know it, everything else follows.
- 5.3 — T-Accounts: A Simple Picture
  - A T-account is a quick way to see an account. Draw a T. Write the account name on top. Debits go on the left. Credits go on the right. The balance is the difference between the two sides.
- 5.4 — Debits Always Equal Credits
  - In every transaction, the total debits must equal the total credits. This is what keeps the accounting equation balanced. If debits don't equal credits, you made a mistake somewhere.

**Test:** 4 questions on what debits and credits mean, which side increases which account type, T-accounts, and why debits must equal credits.

### Chapter 6: The Journal — Recording Transactions

**Core concepts:** Journal entries, date/account/amount, the general journal

- 6.1 — What Is a Journal?
  - The journal is where you first record every transaction. It's like a diary for the business — things go in chronological order as they happen. Each entry shows the date, accounts affected, and amounts.
- 6.2 — Writing a Journal Entry
  - A journal entry has: the date, the account debited (listed first, on the left), the account credited (listed second, indented), and the amounts. A short description explains what happened.
- 6.3 — Simple Entries and Compound Entries
  - A simple entry affects two accounts — one debit, one credit. A compound entry affects three or more accounts. Example: you buy supplies partly with cash and partly on credit — that's three accounts in one entry.
- 6.4 — Practice: Recording a Day's Transactions
  - Walk through a full day at the snack shop: buying supplies, selling snacks, paying for a sign. Record each as a journal entry. See how the journal tells the story of what happened that day.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a journal is, parts of a journal entry, simple vs. compound entries, and recording transactions.

### Chapter 7: The Ledger — Organizing by Account

**Core concepts:** Posting from journal to ledger, account balances, the general ledger

- 7.1 — What Is a Ledger?
  - The journal records things in order of when they happened. The ledger reorganizes the same information by account. All cash transactions together, all supply transactions together, and so on.
- 7.2 — Posting: Journal to Ledger
  - Posting means copying each journal entry into the correct ledger accounts. The journal entry for "bought supplies with cash" gets posted to both the Supplies account and the Cash account.
- 7.3 — Account Balances
  - After posting, each account has a running balance. Add up the debits, add up the credits, find the difference. That's the account balance. It tells you where that account stands right now.
- 7.4 — The General Ledger
  - The general ledger is the collection of all accounts. It's the master record of the entire business. Everything from the journal ends up here, organized and totaled. If the journal is the diary, the ledger is the filing cabinet.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a ledger is, posting, how to find an account balance, and the general ledger.

### Chapter 8: The Trial Balance — Checking Your Work

**Core concepts:** What a trial balance is, how to prepare one, finding errors

- 8.1 — What Is a Trial Balance?
  - A trial balance is a list of all accounts and their balances at a specific date. It checks that total debits equal total credits. If they don't, there's an error somewhere in the records.
- 8.2 — Preparing a Trial Balance
  - List every account. Write its balance. Put debit balances in one column and credit balances in another. Add up each column. The totals should match. Simple, but powerful.
- 8.3 — What a Trial Balance Catches
  - It catches math errors and one-sided entries (forgetting the second half of a transaction). It does NOT catch every error — you could debit the wrong account and the trial balance would still balance.
- 8.4 — Finding and Fixing Errors
  - If the trial balance doesn't balance, work backwards. Check the addition. Check the posting. Check the journal entries. The mistake is in there somewhere. Patience and careful checking are the tools.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a trial balance is, how to prepare one, what it catches, and finding errors.

### Chapter 9: Revenue and Expenses

**Core concepts:** Recognizing revenue, matching expenses, the period concept

- 9.1 — Revenue: When You Earn It
  - Revenue is earned when you deliver the product or service, not when you receive the cash. If you sell a snack today but the customer pays next week, the revenue happens today. This is called the revenue recognition principle.
- 9.2 — Expenses: When You Use Them Up
  - Expenses are the costs of earning revenue. You record an expense when you use something up, not when you buy it. Buying supplies is getting an asset. Using those supplies to make snacks is the expense.
- 9.3 — Matching: Expenses Follow Revenue
  - The matching principle says expenses should be recorded in the same period as the revenue they helped create. If you buy ingredients in January and sell the snacks in January, both go in January's records.
- 9.4 — Periods: Slicing Time
  - Accounting divides time into periods — months, quarters, years. Each period gets its own report. This lets you compare: "Did we do better this month than last month?" Without periods, everything is just one big blur.

**Test:** 4 questions on when to recognize revenue, when to record expenses, matching, and why periods matter.

### Chapter 10: The Income Statement

**Core concepts:** Building an income statement, revenue minus expenses, net income

- 10.1 — What Is an Income Statement?
  - The income statement answers one question: did the business make money or lose money during this period? It lists revenue at the top, subtracts expenses, and shows what's left: net income (profit) or net loss.
- 10.2 — Revenue Section
  - The top of the income statement shows all revenue earned during the period. Sales revenue, service revenue, any other money the business earned by doing its job. This is the starting point.
- 10.3 — Expense Section
  - Below revenue, list all expenses: supplies used, rent, wages, utilities, anything the business spent to operate. Group similar expenses together so the reader can see where money went.
- 10.4 — Net Income: The Bottom Line
  - Revenue minus expenses equals net income. Positive means profit. Negative means loss. This number is literally called "the bottom line" because it's at the bottom of the statement. It's the single most looked-at number in business.

**Test:** 4 questions on what an income statement shows, revenue, expenses, and net income.

### Chapter 11: The Balance Sheet

**Core concepts:** Building a balance sheet, the snapshot idea, reading it

- 11.1 — What Is a Balance Sheet?
  - The balance sheet shows what the business owns, what it owes, and what's left for the owner — at one specific moment in time. The income statement covers a period. The balance sheet is a snapshot of a single date.
- 11.2 — Assets Section
  - Assets are listed in order of liquidity — how quickly they can be turned into cash. Cash first, then accounts receivable, then inventory, then equipment. Current assets (convertible within a year) are listed before long-term assets.
- 11.3 — Liabilities and Owner's Equity
  - Liabilities come next: accounts payable, loans, anything owed. Then owner's equity: what the owner put in plus accumulated profits minus any money taken out. Assets must equal Liabilities + Owner's Equity. Always.
- 11.4 — Reading a Balance Sheet
  - A balance sheet tells you: Is the business mostly funded by debt or by the owner? Does it have enough cash to pay its bills? Is inventory piling up? These are the questions the balance sheet answers.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a balance sheet shows, asset ordering, the equation check, and what you can learn from reading one.

### Chapter 12: The Cash Flow Statement

**Core concepts:** Why cash flow matters, operating/investing/financing, cash vs. profit

- 12.1 — Cash Is Not the Same as Profit
  - A business can be profitable and still run out of cash. How? Timing. You recorded revenue when you sold the product, but the customer hasn't paid yet. Profit is on paper. Cash is in the bank. Both matter.
- 12.2 — What the Cash Flow Statement Shows
  - The cash flow statement tracks actual cash moving in and out during a period. It answers: where did cash come from, and where did it go? It bridges the gap between the income statement and reality.
- 12.3 — Three Sections
  - Operating activities: cash from the normal business (selling, paying suppliers). Investing activities: cash spent on equipment or long-term assets. Financing activities: cash from loans, owner investment, or repaying debt.
- 12.4 — Reading Cash Flow
  - Healthy businesses usually generate positive operating cash flow — the core business brings in more cash than it spends. Negative operating cash flow is a warning sign, even if the income statement shows profit.

**Test:** 4 questions on cash vs. profit, what the cash flow statement shows, the three sections, and healthy cash flow.

### Chapter 13: Adjusting Entries

**Core concepts:** Why adjustments exist, prepaid expenses, accruals, depreciation basics

- 13.1 — Why Adjustments?
  - Some things happen gradually, not in one clear transaction. Rent covers a whole month. Equipment wears out slowly. At the end of a period, you adjust the records to reflect what actually happened during that period.
- 13.2 — Prepaid Expenses
  - If you pay rent for three months upfront, you haven't "used" all that rent yet. Each month, you adjust: move one month's worth from the prepaid asset to rent expense. The cash left in January, but the expense happens over three months.
- 13.3 — Accrued Expenses and Revenue
  - Accrued means "earned or owed but not yet recorded." You worked all week but payday is next week — the expense is accrued. A customer used your service but hasn't been billed yet — the revenue is accrued.
- 13.4 — Depreciation: Spreading Big Costs Over Time
  - A piece of equipment lasts for years, but you don't expense the whole cost in year one. Depreciation spreads the cost over the equipment's useful life. $600 equipment lasting 3 years = $200 expense per year. Simple and fair.

**Test:** 4 questions on why adjustments exist, prepaid expenses, accruals, and depreciation.

### Chapter 14: Closing the Books

**Core concepts:** Closing entries, temporary vs. permanent accounts, the accounting cycle

- 14.1 — Temporary and Permanent Accounts
  - Revenue and expense accounts are temporary — they reset to zero at the end of each period so you can start fresh. Asset, liability, and equity accounts are permanent — they carry forward. This is how you know "this month's" revenue vs. "all-time."
- 14.2 — Closing Entries
  - Closing entries move the balances from temporary accounts into owner's equity. Revenue and expenses get zeroed out, and the net income (or loss) gets added to the owner's equity account. The books are "closed" for the period.
- 14.3 — The Accounting Cycle
  - The full cycle: transactions happen → record in journal → post to ledger → trial balance → adjustments → financial statements → closing entries → start the next period. Every business follows this cycle, period after period.
- 14.4 — Starting Fresh
  - After closing, revenue and expense accounts are at zero. The balance sheet accounts carry their balances into the next period. A new period begins, and the cycle starts again. Clean and organized.

**Test:** 4 questions on temporary vs. permanent accounts, closing entries, the accounting cycle, and why we close.

### Chapter 15: Putting It All Together

**Core concepts:** Full walkthrough, building complete statements from transactions, review

- 15.1 — A Full Month at the Snack Shop
  - Walk through an entire month of transactions for the snack shop: opening with cash, buying supplies, selling products, paying rent, receiving payments from customers who owed money, buying a small piece of equipment.
- 15.2 — From Journal to Statements
  - Record every transaction in the journal. Post to the ledger. Prepare the trial balance. Make adjusting entries. Prepare the income statement, balance sheet, and cash flow statement. The full cycle, start to finish.
- 15.3 — What the Statements Tell You
  - Read the statements you just built. Did the snack shop make money? Does it have enough cash? How much does it owe? How much is the owner's share? The numbers tell a story — and now you can read it.
- 15.4 — You Are an Accountant
  - You now understand the system that every business in the world uses to track its finances. From a lemonade stand to a Fortune 500 company, the logic is identical. The scale changes, the principles don't.

**Test:** 4 questions that require building or reading financial records from a set of transactions.

## Book 2: Reading and Using Financial Statements

**Subtitle:** What the Numbers Really Tell You

The goal: by the end of this book, the student can pick up a real company's financial statements and understand what they're reading. They know what EBITDA is, how to calculate margins, how to spot red flags, and how businesses use financial information to make decisions.

### Chapter 1: Reading Financial Statements Like a Detective

**Core concepts:** What to look for, asking questions of the numbers, healthy vs. unhealthy signs

- 1.1 — Numbers Tell a Story
  - Financial statements aren't just numbers — they're a story about what happened in a business. Your job is to read that story. Is the business growing? Shrinking? Healthy? Struggling? The numbers will tell you if you know what to look for.
- 1.2 — Start with the Big Picture
  - Before diving into details, look at the big numbers first. Is revenue going up or down? Is the business profitable? Does it have more assets than liabilities? Big picture first, details second.
- 1.3 — Comparing Periods
  - One month's numbers alone don't tell you much. But comparing this month to last month, or this year to last year, shows trends. Trends matter more than any single number.
- 1.4 — Asking "Why?"
  - The numbers tell you what happened. You have to figure out why. Revenue dropped 20% — why? Expenses jumped — why? The statements show the symptoms. Understanding the cause requires thinking.

**Test:** 4 questions on reading statements as a story, big-picture first, comparing periods, and asking why.

### Chapter 2: Revenue Analysis

**Core concepts:** Revenue growth, revenue streams, concentration risk

- 2.1 — Is Revenue Growing?
  - Revenue growth means the business is selling more. Compare this period to the same period last year (not just last month — seasons matter). Growing revenue is usually a good sign, but it's not the whole story.
- 2.2 — Where Does Revenue Come From?
  - Most businesses have multiple sources of revenue. A restaurant earns from dine-in, takeout, and catering. Knowing which sources are growing and which are shrinking tells you a lot.
- 2.3 — Revenue Concentration
  - If 90% of revenue comes from one customer or one product, that's risky. What happens if that customer leaves? Diversified revenue is more stable. Concentration is a red flag.
- 2.4 — Revenue vs. Growth at Any Cost
  - Growing revenue by selling at a loss or giving things away isn't real growth. Revenue only matters if the business can eventually turn it into profit. Growth that loses money on every sale is a problem, not a success.

**Test:** 4 questions on revenue growth, revenue sources, concentration risk, and healthy vs. unhealthy growth.

### Chapter 3: Cost of Goods Sold and Gross Profit

**Core concepts:** COGS, gross profit, gross margin

- 3.1 — Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)
  - COGS is the direct cost of making or buying the products you sold. If you sell lemonade, COGS is the lemons, sugar, and cups. It doesn't include rent or your phone bill — those are separate.
- 3.2 — Gross Profit
  - Revenue minus COGS equals gross profit. This tells you how much money is left after paying for what you sold, before paying for everything else. It's the first layer of profit.
- 3.3 — Gross Margin
  - Gross margin is gross profit as a percentage of revenue. If you sell $100 of lemonade and COGS is $40, gross profit is $60 and gross margin is 60%. Higher is better — it means more of each dollar stays in the business.
- 3.4 — Why Gross Margin Matters
  - Gross margin tells you whether the core business works. If gross margin is tiny or negative, no amount of cost-cutting elsewhere will save you. The product itself isn't making enough money.

**Test:** 4 questions on COGS, gross profit, gross margin calculation, and why gross margin matters.

### Chapter 4: Operating Expenses

**Core concepts:** SG&A, operating income, expense categories

- 4.1 — What Are Operating Expenses?
  - Operating expenses are the costs of running the business that aren't directly tied to making the product. Rent, salaries, marketing, office supplies, insurance. The business needs these to function, even though they don't go into the product.
- 4.2 — Selling, General, and Administrative (SG&A)
  - SG&A is the main category of operating expenses. Selling costs (marketing, sales commissions), general costs (rent, utilities), and administrative costs (office staff, legal, accounting). This is a big bucket.
- 4.3 — Operating Income
  - Gross profit minus operating expenses equals operating income. This tells you how much the core business earns after ALL the costs of running it. It's a more complete picture than gross profit alone.
- 4.4 — Watching Operating Expenses Grow
  - Operating expenses should grow slower than revenue. If revenue doubles but operating expenses triple, the business is getting less efficient, not more. Watch the relationship, not just the number.

**Test:** 4 questions on operating expenses, SG&A, operating income, and expense growth vs. revenue growth.

### Chapter 5: EBITDA

**Core concepts:** What EBITDA is, why it exists, what it shows, its limits

- 5.1 — What Is EBITDA?
  - EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. It's a way to see how much money the business's operations are generating before certain costs that have nothing to do with the day-to-day work.
- 5.2 — Why Strip Those Things Out?
  - Interest depends on how the business is financed. Taxes depend on where it's located. Depreciation and amortization are accounting entries, not cash leaving the door. Stripping them out lets you compare the core operations of different businesses more fairly.
- 5.3 — How to Calculate EBITDA
  - Start with operating income. Add back depreciation and amortization. That's EBITDA. Or start with net income and add back interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. Same number, different starting points.
- 5.4 — EBITDA Isn't Perfect
  - EBITDA ignores real costs. Interest has to be paid. Taxes have to be paid. Equipment wears out and has to be replaced. EBITDA is a useful tool, but businesses that only talk about EBITDA might be hiding problems below that line.

**Test:** 4 questions on what EBITDA stands for, why those items are stripped out, how to calculate it, and its limitations.

### Chapter 6: Net Income and the Bottom Line

**Core concepts:** Net income, earnings per share (basic), retained earnings

- 6.1 — Net Income: What's Really Left
  - Net income is what's left after everything — COGS, operating expenses, interest, taxes, all of it. This is the true profit. It's called the bottom line because it's literally the last line on the income statement.
- 6.2 — From EBITDA to Net Income
  - Start at EBITDA, subtract depreciation and amortization (back to operating income), subtract interest expense, subtract taxes. What's left is net income. Each subtraction represents a real cost the business faces.
- 6.3 — What Happens to Net Income
  - Net income either gets paid out to owners (dividends or distributions) or stays in the business (retained earnings). Retained earnings add to owner's equity on the balance sheet. This is how profits make the business stronger over time.
- 6.4 — Profit Margins
  - Net profit margin is net income divided by revenue, as a percentage. If a business earns $100 in revenue and keeps $10 as net income, the margin is 10%. This tells you how many cents of each dollar the business actually keeps.

**Test:** 4 questions on net income, the path from EBITDA to net income, retained earnings, and profit margins.

### Chapter 7: Reading the Balance Sheet

**Core concepts:** Current vs. long-term, liquidity, what a strong balance sheet looks like

- 7.1 — Current Assets
  - Current assets are things the business can turn into cash within a year: cash itself, accounts receivable (money customers owe), and inventory. These are the business's short-term resources.
- 7.2 — Long-Term Assets
  - Long-term assets last more than a year: equipment, buildings, patents, vehicles. They're valuable but not easily converted to cash. A business needs both current and long-term assets to function.
- 7.3 — Current vs. Long-Term Liabilities
  - Current liabilities are due within a year (bills, short-term loans). Long-term liabilities are due later (mortgages, multi-year loans). The balance between these tells you about the business's obligations and timeline.
- 7.4 — A Strong Balance Sheet
  - More assets than liabilities: good. Current assets greater than current liabilities: the business can pay its near-term bills. Not too much debt relative to equity: the business isn't overleveraged. These are the basics of a healthy balance sheet.

**Test:** 4 questions on current vs. long-term assets, current vs. long-term liabilities, and signs of a strong balance sheet.

### Chapter 8: Financial Ratios — Liquidity

**Core concepts:** Current ratio, quick ratio, working capital

- 8.1 — What Are Financial Ratios?
  - A ratio compares two numbers to give you useful information. $50,000 in current assets means nothing by itself. But $50,000 in current assets vs. $25,000 in current liabilities — that's a 2:1 ratio, and that's informative.
- 8.2 — Current Ratio
  - Current ratio = current assets ÷ current liabilities. A ratio above 1 means the business has more short-term resources than short-term debts. Below 1 is a warning: the business might struggle to pay its bills.
- 8.3 — Quick Ratio
  - Quick ratio is like the current ratio but excludes inventory (because inventory might be hard to sell quickly). Quick ratio = (cash + accounts receivable) ÷ current liabilities. It's a stricter test of whether the business can pay its bills right now.
- 8.4 — Working Capital
  - Working capital = current assets minus current liabilities. It's the cushion the business has for day-to-day operations. Positive working capital means breathing room. Negative means the business is stretching to cover short-term obligations.

**Test:** 4 questions on what ratios do, current ratio, quick ratio, and working capital.

### Chapter 9: Financial Ratios — Profitability

**Core concepts:** Gross margin, operating margin, net margin, return on equity

- 9.1 — Margin Ratios: How Much You Keep
  - Margin ratios tell you what percentage of revenue the business keeps at each stage. Gross margin, operating margin, and net margin are like checkpoints — each one subtracts more costs and shows what's left.
- 9.2 — Gross Margin and Operating Margin
  - Gross margin = gross profit ÷ revenue. Operating margin = operating income ÷ revenue. Comparing these tells you whether the problem (or strength) is in making the product or running the business around it.
- 9.3 — Net Profit Margin
  - Net margin = net income ÷ revenue. This is the final answer: of every dollar earned, how many cents does the business keep? Different industries have very different normal margins. A grocery store at 2% can be healthy. A software company at 2% is struggling.
- 9.4 — Return on Equity (ROE)
  - ROE = net income ÷ owner's equity. This tells you how much profit the owner's investment is generating. High ROE means the business is using the owner's money efficiently. It's one of the most watched numbers in business.

**Test:** 4 questions on margin ratios, gross vs. operating margin, net margin by industry, and ROE.

### Chapter 10: Financial Ratios — Efficiency and Leverage

**Core concepts:** Inventory turnover, accounts receivable turnover, debt-to-equity

- 10.1 — Inventory Turnover
  - Inventory turnover = COGS ÷ average inventory. It tells you how many times the business sold through its inventory during the period. Higher is usually better — it means products aren't sitting on shelves collecting dust.
- 10.2 — Accounts Receivable Turnover
  - AR turnover = revenue ÷ average accounts receivable. It measures how quickly customers pay. Slow collection means cash is tied up in IOUs instead of in the bank. Fast collection means healthier cash flow.
- 10.3 — Debt-to-Equity Ratio
  - Debt-to-equity = total liabilities ÷ owner's equity. It shows how much the business relies on borrowed money vs. the owner's money. Higher means more leverage — more risk, but potentially more return. Too high and the business is fragile.
- 10.4 — Using Ratios Together
  - No single ratio tells the whole story. A business with great margins but terrible liquidity could run out of cash. A business with low debt but shrinking revenue could be dying slowly. Use multiple ratios like a dashboard, not a single gauge.

### Chapter 11: Comparing Companies

**Core concepts:** Benchmarking, industry norms, size differences, apples to apples

- 11.1 — Why Compare?
  - A 10% margin is great in one industry and terrible in another. You need context. Comparing a business to similar businesses tells you whether its numbers are strong, weak, or average for its type.
- 11.2 — Industry Norms
  - Every industry has typical ranges for key ratios. Restaurants have thin margins and fast inventory turnover. Software companies have high margins but might burn cash on growth. Knowing the norms is essential.
- 11.3 — Size Differences
  - A corner store and a national chain are both retail businesses, but comparing their raw numbers is misleading. That's why percentages and ratios exist — they let you compare businesses of different sizes fairly.
- 11.4 — Apples to Apples
  - Make sure you're comparing the same things. Same time period. Same accounting methods. Same definitions. A company that capitalizes costs and one that expenses them will look different even if the underlying business is identical.

### Chapter 12: Red Flags and Warning Signs

**Core concepts:** Declining margins, growing debt, cash flow problems, inventory buildup

- 12.1 — Declining Margins Over Time
  - If gross margin or operating margin is shrinking period after period, something is wrong. Costs are rising, prices are falling, or the product is becoming less competitive. Margins trending down is an early warning.
- 12.2 — Revenue Growing, Cash Shrinking
  - This is one of the scariest patterns. The business looks like it's growing (revenue up!) but cash keeps dropping. This usually means the business isn't collecting money fast enough or is spending too much to grow.
- 12.3 — Debt Growing Faster Than the Business
  - Borrowing to grow is normal. But if debt grows much faster than revenue or profit, the business is getting more fragile. Eventually the interest payments can become overwhelming.
- 12.4 — Inventory Piling Up
  - Rising inventory without rising sales means products aren't selling. The business is making (or buying) more than customers want. That inventory might have to be sold at a discount or written off as a loss.

### Chapter 13: Budgeting and Forecasting

**Core concepts:** What a budget is, building a simple budget, forecasting, variance analysis

- 13.1 — What Is a Budget?
  - A budget is a financial plan — your best estimate of what revenue and expenses will be in the future. It's not a prediction that has to be perfect. It's a plan you measure against.
- 13.2 — Building a Simple Budget
  - Start with expected revenue. Then list expected expenses by category. The difference is expected profit. Use last period's actual numbers as a starting point, then adjust for what you expect to change.
- 13.3 — Forecasting: Looking Ahead
  - Forecasting means estimating future numbers based on trends, plans, and assumptions. "We sold 100 last month and we're adding a new product, so we expect 120 next month." Simple and honest beats complicated and overconfident.
- 13.4 — Variance: Budget vs. Actual
  - At the end of the period, compare what actually happened to what you budgeted. The difference is the variance. Positive variance (beat the budget) is good. Negative variance (missed) means understanding why and adjusting.

### Chapter 14: Cost Accounting Basics

**Core concepts:** Direct vs. indirect costs, allocating overhead, break-even point

- 14.1 — Direct Costs vs. Indirect Costs
  - Direct costs are clearly tied to a product (ingredients for a sandwich). Indirect costs support the whole business but aren't tied to one product (rent, electricity, the boss's salary). Both are real costs, but they're tracked differently.
- 14.2 — Allocating Overhead
  - Indirect costs need to be shared across products somehow. If you make sandwiches and salads, how much rent does each product "use"? Allocation is the process of dividing shared costs fairly. There's no perfect method, but reasonable is the goal.
- 14.3 — Break-Even Point
  - Break-even is when revenue exactly equals total costs — no profit, no loss. Knowing your break-even point tells you how much you need to sell before you start making money. Below break-even: losing money. Above: making money.
- 14.4 — Using Cost Information for Decisions
  - Should you drop a product? Add a product? Raise prices? Lower costs? Cost accounting gives you the numbers to decide. Without it, you're guessing which products are helping and which are hurting.

### Chapter 15: Accounting in the Real World

**Core concepts:** Who does accounting, auditing, ethics, technology, careers

- 15.1 — Who Does Accounting in Real Life?
  - Small businesses: often the owner or a part-time bookkeeper. Medium businesses: accounting staff or an outsourced firm. Big companies: entire accounting departments. The scale changes but the system is the same one you learned.
- 15.2 — Auditing: Checking the Checkers
  - An audit is when an independent person reviews the financial records to make sure they're accurate and follow the rules. Audits build trust — investors and lenders rely on audited statements because someone independent verified them.
- 15.3 — Ethics: Honesty in Accounting
  - Accounting only works if people are honest. Faking numbers, hiding expenses, or inflating revenue is fraud. When accounting fraud is discovered, businesses collapse, people lose jobs, and trust is destroyed. Honesty isn't optional — it's the foundation.
- 15.4 — You Can Read the Numbers Now
  - You now understand how financial records are created, organized, reported, and analyzed. You can read an income statement, a balance sheet, and a cash flow statement. You know what EBITDA is. You can calculate ratios and spot red flags. That puts you ahead of most adults.
