# Grammar for Kids — Book Proposal

## Why These Books

Grammar — with a capital G — is not about English class rules or diagramming sentences in one language. It's about how concepts relate to each other through terms. Every language is a surface representation of deeper structures: ways of naming, defining, categorizing, relating, and combining concepts into meaningful statements. These books teach that deeper structure.

Most people never learn what a "term" really is, how terms refer to things at different levels, what the fundamental types of relationships between concepts are, or how those relationships are expressed across languages. This is the foundation of the classical Trivium — Grammar comes first because you can't reason (Logic) or persuade (Rhetoric) without first understanding how meaning is structured.

These three books give a kid the conceptual vocabulary to understand any language they encounter, and more importantly, to think clearly about how meaning works.

## Series Context

Three books. This is the Grammar portion of the Trivium (Grammar → Logic → Rhetoric). Grammar here means the structure of meaning through terms and their relationships — not the rules of any particular language.

- **Book 1: Terms** — What the building blocks of meaning are
- **Book 2: Relationships Between Terms** — How concepts relate to each other
- **Book 3: Propositions and Structures** — How relationships are expressed in complete, structured thought

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters per book, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300–500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7–12
- Real-world examples from a kid's life, with examples drawn from multiple languages to show universality

## Math expectations

- None. This is a concepts-and-vocabulary subject.

## Prerequisites

- None required. These books stand alone.
- No prior knowledge of any language besides English needed, though examples from other languages are used to illustrate universal concepts.

## Cross-language approach

These books are not about any single language. When a concept is introduced, examples show how it works in English and at least one or two other languages (Latin, Turkish, Finnish, Navajo, Korean, Chinese, Russian, etc.) to demonstrate that the concept is universal — only the surface expression differs. The goal is for the student to see the grammar *underneath* all languages.

## Book 1: Terms — The Building Blocks of Meaning

**Subtitle:** What Words Really Are

### Chapter 1: What Is a Term?

**Core concepts:** Terms as units of meaning, terms vs. mere words, what terms refer to

- 1.1 — Words and Meaning
  - You use words every day, but have you ever thought about what a word actually does? A word is a sound (or a set of letters) that carries meaning. But not every sound is meaningful, and the same sound can mean different things. A term is a word used with a definite meaning. Understanding terms is the starting point for understanding how meaning works.
- 1.2 — What Is a Term?
  - A term is a word or phrase used to stand for something — a thing, an idea, a quality, an action. "Dog" is a term that stands for a certain kind of animal. "Justice" is a term that stands for a concept. "Running" is a term that stands for an action. A term is the basic unit of structured thought.
- 1.3 — Terms vs. Noise
  - Not every word in a sentence is a term in the strict sense. Words like "the," "and," "but," and "of" help connect and structure terms, but they don't stand for things by themselves. They're like the mortar between bricks — essential, but not the bricks. The terms are the bricks: the words that carry meaning about things, qualities, or actions.
- 1.4 — Every Language Has Terms
  - English has terms. So does Latin, Korean, Navajo, Finnish, and every other language. The words sound completely different, but they're all doing the same job: standing for things in the world and in the mind. The surface changes. The function is universal.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a term is, terms vs. other words, the function of terms, and universality across languages.

### Chapter 2: What Terms Refer To

**Core concepts:** Signification, things vs. concepts, the triangle of meaning

- 2.1 — The Triangle of Meaning
  - When you say "dog," three things are involved: the word itself (a sound or written letters), the concept in your mind (your idea of what a dog is), and the actual thing in the world (real dogs). The word connects to the concept, and the concept connects to the thing. This triangle — word, concept, thing — is how meaning works.
- 2.2 — Signification: What a Term Means
  - Signification is what a term means — the concept it calls up in your mind. "Dog" signifies the concept of a dog. "Triangle" signifies the concept of a three-sided shape. Signification is the link between the word and the idea. Two different words can have the same signification — "dog" in English and "perro" in Spanish both signify the same concept.
- 2.3 — Terms Can Refer to Different Levels
  - "Dog" can refer to an actual dog sitting in front of you. "Dog" can also refer to the kind of thing a dog is — the species. And "dog" can refer to the word itself — "Dog is a three-letter word." Same word, three different levels of reference. Keeping these levels straight prevents confusion.
- 2.4 — Abstract and Concrete Terms
  - "Chair" is concrete — you can point to one. "Fairness" is abstract — you can't point to it, but it's real and meaningful. "Red" is concrete when it describes something you can see, but "color" is more abstract. The distinction between concrete and abstract terms matters because it affects how we define, argue about, and understand things.

**Test:** 4 questions on the triangle of meaning, signification, levels of reference, and abstract vs. concrete.

### Chapter 3: First and Second Imposition

**Core concepts:** Terms about things vs. terms about words, recognizing the level

- 3.1 — First Imposition: Words About Things
  - Most words refer to things in the world or concepts about the world. "Tree," "happy," "running," "seven" — these all point outward to reality or our experience of it. These are called terms of first imposition. They're the most common and the most natural.
- 3.2 — Second Imposition: Words About Words
  - Some words refer to other words or to language itself. "Noun" is a word about a type of word. "Syllable" is a word about a part of a word. "Sentence" is a word about a structure of words. These are terms of second imposition — language talking about language.
- 3.3 — Why the Distinction Matters
  - Confusing the two levels creates problems. "Dog is an animal" (first imposition — talking about the thing). "Dog is a noun" (second imposition — talking about the word). Both are true, but they're true in completely different ways. Mixing them up leads to nonsense. When someone says something confusing, check which level they're operating on.
- 3.4 — Practice: Spotting the Level
  - "Seven is a prime number" — first imposition (about the number). "Seven has five letters" — second imposition (about the word). "Spanish is beautiful" — could be either (the language as a system of sounds, or the culture it represents). Paying attention to which level a term is operating on is a skill that makes all thinking clearer.

**Test:** 4 questions on first imposition, second imposition, why the distinction matters, and identifying the level in examples.

### Chapter 4: First and Second Intention

**Core concepts:** Concepts about things vs. concepts about concepts, how we categorize thought

- 4.1 — First Intention: Thinking About Things
  - When you think "dog," you're thinking about the animal — a thing in the world. When you think "tree" or "justice" or "running," you're thinking about things, qualities, or actions. These are first intentions — concepts that refer to reality.
- 4.2 — Second Intention: Thinking About Thinking
  - When you think "species," you're not thinking about a thing in the world — you're thinking about a category of thought. "Dog" is a first intention. "Species" (the category that "dog" belongs to) is a second intention. "Genus," "category," "definition," "subject," "predicate" — these are all second intentions. They're concepts about how we organize concepts.
- 4.3 — The Two Levels
  - First intention: the mind reaches out to the world. Second intention: the mind turns back on itself and examines its own operations. Both are valid and necessary. Science mostly uses first intentions (studying things). Logic and grammar mostly use second intentions (studying how we think and speak about things).
- 4.4 — Why This Matters
  - If someone says "a dog is a species," they're mixing levels. A dog is an animal (first intention). "Dog" belongs to a species (second intention — a category). The thing itself and the category it belongs to are different. This distinction prevents a huge class of errors that people make all the time without realizing it.

**Test:** 4 questions on first intention, second intention, the two levels, and spotting level confusion.

### Chapter 5: Categories of Being

**Core concepts:** Substance, quantity, quality, relation, and other categories; what can be said about a thing

- 5.1 — What Can You Say About a Thing?
  - Pick any thing — a dog, a table, a person. What can you say about it? What it is (a dog). How big it is (large). What it's like (brown, friendly). Where it is (in the yard). When it exists (right now). What it's related to (its owner). These different kinds of things you can say correspond to categories of being — the most basic ways we describe reality.
- 5.2 — Substance: What a Thing IS
  - Substance is the most fundamental category — what a thing is in itself. A dog is a dog. A table is a table. Substance is what remains when you strip away all the qualities, sizes, locations, and relationships. It's the thing itself, the subject that has all the other properties.
- 5.3 — Accidents: Properties of a Thing
  - Everything else you can say about a thing is an accident — not "accident" as in a mistake, but a property that a thing has. A dog's color, size, location, age, and relationships are all accidents. They can change without the dog becoming a different kind of thing. A brown dog that turns gray is still a dog. The substance stays; the accidents change.
- 5.4 — The Main Categories
  - The classical categories (from Aristotle): substance (what it is), quantity (how much/many), quality (what it's like), relation (how it relates to other things), place (where), time (when), position (its posture or arrangement), state (its condition), action (what it does), and passion (what is done to it). Every statement about anything fits into one of these.

**Test:** 4 questions on what categories are, substance vs. accidents, the main categories, and classifying statements into categories.

### Chapter 6: Univocal, Equivocal, and Analogical Terms

**Core concepts:** Same word same meaning, same word different meanings, same word related meanings

- 6.1 — Univocal Terms: One Meaning
  - A term is univocal when it means the same thing every time it's used. "Triangle" means a three-sided shape whether you're talking about geometry, a musical instrument shape, or a traffic sign. The meaning stays constant. Univocal terms are the clearest and least likely to cause confusion.
- 6.2 — Equivocal Terms: Different Meanings
  - A term is equivocal when the same word has completely different meanings. "Bank" can mean a financial institution or the side of a river. "Bat" can mean a flying animal or a piece of sports equipment. The word is the same but the meanings are unrelated. Equivocal terms are a major source of confusion and bad arguments.
- 6.3 — Analogical Terms: Related Meanings
  - A term is analogical when it's used with related but not identical meanings. "Healthy" can describe a person (the body is functioning well), food (promotes health), and a complexion (shows signs of health). The meanings are connected but not the same. Analogical terms are the trickiest because the connection is real but the meanings shift.
- 6.4 — Why This Matters for Clear Thinking
  - Many arguments go wrong because people use the same word with different meanings without realizing it. "Freedom" in a political sense, a philosophical sense, and a personal sense are related but different. If you argue using "freedom" as if it means the same thing in every context, your reasoning will be flawed. Spotting equivocation is one of the most useful thinking skills.

**Test:** 4 questions on univocal, equivocal, and analogical terms, and spotting equivocation in an argument.

### Chapter 7: Definition

**Core concepts:** What a definition is, genus and differentia, good vs. bad definitions, types of definition

- 7.1 — What Is a Definition?
  - A definition tells you what something is by placing it in a category and then distinguishing it from other things in that category. "A triangle is a polygon (category) with exactly three sides (what makes it different)." A definition draws a boundary around a concept so you know what's inside and what's outside.
- 7.2 — Genus and Differentia
  - The genus is the broader category the thing belongs to. The differentia is what makes it different from other members of that category. "A human is an animal (genus) that reasons (differentia)." "A square is a rectangle (genus) with all sides equal (differentia)." This structure works for defining almost anything.
- 7.3 — Good Definitions vs. Bad Definitions
  - A good definition is precise enough to include everything it should and exclude everything it shouldn't. "A chair is something you sit on" is too broad — you sit on benches and floors too. "A chair is a piece of furniture designed for one person to sit on, with a back and usually four legs" is better. Too broad or too narrow and the definition fails.
- 7.4 — Types of Definition
  - Essential definition: what the thing IS (genus + differentia). Descriptive definition: what the thing is LIKE (listing properties). Etymological definition: where the word comes from. Operational definition: what the thing DOES or how you identify it. Each type is useful for different purposes.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a definition is, genus and differentia, evaluating definitions, and types of definition.

### Chapter 8: Supposition

**Core concepts:** How a term is being used in context, material vs. formal supposition, personal supposition

- 8.1 — What Is Supposition?
  - Supposition is about how a term is being used in a particular statement. The same term can "stand for" different things depending on context. "Man is a species" — here "man" stands for the concept. "Man is running" — here "man" stands for a particular individual. The term is the same; what it stands for in that moment is different.
- 8.2 — Material Supposition
  - Material supposition is when a term stands for the word itself, not for what the word means. "'Dog' has three letters." Here "dog" stands for the word, not for the animal. You're talking about the word as a physical thing (letters, sounds), not about what it refers to.
- 8.3 — Formal Supposition
  - Formal supposition is when a term stands for what it means — either the concept (simple supposition) or actual things in the world (personal supposition). "Dog is a species" — simple supposition (the concept). "A dog is in the yard" — personal supposition (an actual dog). Formal supposition is the normal way we use terms.
- 8.4 — Why Supposition Matters
  - Many confusions come from not recognizing how a term is being used. "Nothing is better than perfect happiness. A sandwich is better than nothing. Therefore, a sandwich is better than perfect happiness." This is funny because "nothing" shifts its supposition between the two statements. Recognizing supposition shifts prevents being tricked by bad arguments.

**Test:** 4 questions on what supposition is, material vs. formal, types of formal supposition, and spotting supposition shifts.

### Chapter 9: Parts of Speech as Functions

**Core concepts:** Naming, asserting, modifying, connecting — universal functions across languages

- 9.1 — Parts of Speech Are Functions, Not Labels
  - Every grammar class teaches nouns, verbs, adjectives. But these are labels for something deeper: functions. A noun names a thing. A verb asserts an action or state. An adjective modifies a noun. These functions exist in every language — the labels just describe what role a term plays in expressing meaning.
- 9.2 — The Naming Function
  - Every language needs a way to name things — objects, people, places, ideas. In English, nouns do this. In Latin, nouns do this with case endings that show their role. In Chinese, context and word order do much of the work. The function (naming) is universal. The strategy differs.
- 9.3 — The Asserting Function
  - Every language needs a way to say something is happening, exists, or is true. This is the verb function — asserting action, existence, or state. English uses word order and helper words (is, was, will). Latin puts the information into the verb's ending. Turkish agglutinates (stacks) meanings onto the verb. Same function, different tools.
- 9.4 — The Modifying and Connecting Functions
  - Modifiers add information to other terms — "big" modifies a noun, "quickly" modifies a verb. Connectors join terms or ideas — "and," "but," "because." Every language has ways to modify and connect, though some do it with separate words, some with endings, and some with word position. The needs are universal; the methods vary.

**Test:** 4 questions on parts of speech as functions, the naming function, the asserting function, and modifying/connecting.

### Chapter 10: The Copula and Predication

**Core concepts:** Subject-predicate structure, the copula (is/are), affirming and denying, types of predication

- 10.1 — Subject and Predicate
  - The most basic structure of a meaningful statement: something you're talking about (the subject) and something you're saying about it (the predicate). "The dog (subject) is brown (predicate)." "Snow (subject) is cold (predicate)." Every complete assertion has this structure — you pick a thing and say something about it.
- 10.2 — The Copula: "Is"
  - The copula is the word that links subject to predicate — in English, forms of "to be" (is, are, was). "The sky is blue." The copula doesn't describe an action — it asserts a connection between the subject and what's being said about it. Some languages don't use an explicit copula (Russian often drops it; Chinese doesn't have one in the same way), but the linking function still exists.
- 10.3 — Affirming and Denying
  - Every predication either affirms or denies. "The cat is black" affirms. "The cat is not black" denies. This is the most basic distinction in statements — you're either saying something is the case or saying it isn't. Every language has a way to negate.
- 10.4 — Types of Predication
  - You can predicate different categories: "The dog is an animal" (substance — what it is), "The dog is large" (quality), "The dog is in the yard" (place), "The dog is mine" (relation). What you predicate about the subject corresponds to the categories of being from Chapter 5. Predication is where terms and categories come together.

**Test:** 4 questions on subject and predicate, the copula, affirming vs. denying, and types of predication.

### Chapter 11: Extension and Comprehension

**Core concepts:** How broad a term is vs. how much it contains, the inverse relationship

- 11.1 — Extension: How Many Things a Term Covers
  - The extension of a term is the set of all things it applies to. "Animal" has a very large extension — it covers dogs, cats, fish, birds, insects, and millions more. "Poodle" has a much smaller extension — only poodles. The broader the term, the larger its extension.
- 11.2 — Comprehension: How Much a Term Contains
  - The comprehension (or intension) of a term is the set of qualities that define it. "Poodle" contains a lot — it's an animal, a mammal, a dog, a specific breed with specific characteristics. "Animal" contains very little — just "living thing that isn't a plant." The more specific the term, the richer its comprehension.
- 11.3 — The Inverse Relationship
  - As extension increases, comprehension decreases — and vice versa. "Living thing" covers almost everything alive (huge extension) but tells you almost nothing about what it's like (tiny comprehension). "My neighbor's three-year-old brown poodle named Max" covers exactly one thing (tiny extension) but tells you a great deal (huge comprehension). This tradeoff is fundamental.
- 11.4 — Why This Matters
  - When you define something, you're trying to find the right balance. Too broad (large extension, low comprehension) and the definition is useless — "a chair is a thing." Too narrow (small extension, high comprehension) and it excludes too much. Understanding extension and comprehension helps you define terms precisely and spot when others are being too vague or too restrictive.

**Test:** 4 questions on extension, comprehension, the inverse relationship, and applying the concept to definitions.

### Chapter 12: Division and Classification

**Core concepts:** Dividing a genus into species, classification hierarchies, exhaustive and exclusive division

- 12.1 — Division: Splitting a Category
  - Division is the process of splitting a broader term into its narrower parts. "Animal" divides into mammals, birds, reptiles, fish, amphibians, insects, and more. "Vehicle" divides into cars, trucks, bikes, boats, planes. Division is the opposite of definition — definition combines genus and differentia; division separates a genus into its species.
- 12.2 — Rules of Good Division
  - A good division is exhaustive (every member of the genus falls into one of the species — nothing left out) and exclusive (the species don't overlap — nothing counted twice). "People divided into children and adults" is exhaustive and exclusive. "People divided into tall people and smart people" is neither — some are both, some are neither.
- 12.3 — Classification Hierarchies
  - You can keep dividing. Animal → Mammal → Dog → Poodle → Toy Poodle. Each level is more specific. This creates a hierarchy — a tree of categories from the most general to the most specific. Classification hierarchies organize all knowledge, from biology to libraries to how you organize files on a computer.
- 12.4 — Basis of Division
  - Every division uses a basis — the criterion you're splitting on. Dividing animals by "number of legs" gives one set of species. Dividing by "habitat" gives a completely different set. The basis of division must be stated clearly — different bases give different (and equally valid) classifications. Confusion arises when people divide on an unclear or shifting basis.

**Test:** 4 questions on division, rules of good division, classification hierarchies, and basis of division.

### Chapter 13: Ambiguity and Clarity

**Core concepts:** Sources of ambiguity, lexical vs. structural, vagueness vs. ambiguity, achieving clarity

- 13.1 — What Is Ambiguity?
  - Ambiguity means a word, phrase, or sentence can be understood in more than one way. "I saw the man with the telescope" — did you use a telescope to see him, or did you see a man who was holding a telescope? Ambiguity is one of the biggest obstacles to clear communication and clear thinking.
- 13.2 — Lexical Ambiguity
  - Lexical ambiguity comes from a single word having multiple meanings. "Bank" (financial institution or river edge), "light" (not heavy or not dark), "right" (correct or a direction). This is equivocation at the word level. Context usually resolves it, but not always — and in arguments, unresolved lexical ambiguity can be exploited.
- 13.3 — Structural Ambiguity
  - Structural ambiguity comes from the arrangement of words, not the words themselves. "Visiting relatives can be boring" — is visiting them boring, or are the relatives who are visiting boring? The individual words are clear; the structure allows two readings. Every language has its own forms of structural ambiguity.
- 13.4 — Vagueness vs. Ambiguity
  - Ambiguity means multiple distinct meanings. Vagueness means the boundary is unclear. "Tall" is vague — where exactly does tall start? "Bank" is ambiguous — it has two distinct meanings. Ambiguity can be resolved by choosing a meaning. Vagueness is harder — there may be no sharp boundary to find. Both interfere with clarity, but in different ways.

**Test:** 4 questions on ambiguity, lexical vs. structural, vagueness vs. ambiguity, and resolving ambiguity.

### Chapter 14: How Different Languages Express Terms

**Core concepts:** Inflection, word order, particles, agglutination — different strategies for the same needs

- 14.1 — Inflection: Changing the Word Itself
  - Latin, Russian, Finnish, and many other languages change the form of a word to show its role. In Latin, "canis" (dog) becomes "canem" when it's the thing being acted upon, "canis" when it's the one acting, "cani" when something is given to it. The ending tells you the relationship. English mostly lost this ability.
- 14.2 — Word Order: Position Shows Role
  - English and Chinese rely heavily on word order. "The dog chased the cat" means something different from "The cat chased the dog." The position of the word in the sentence tells you who did what. In Latin, you could scramble the word order and the meaning would stay the same because the endings carry the information.
- 14.3 — Particles and Helper Words
  - Japanese and Korean use particles — small words that attach to terms to mark their role. In Japanese, "wa" marks the topic, "ga" marks the subject, "wo" marks the object. Chinese uses word order plus particles for relationships that English handles with prepositions. Each language finds its own strategy.
- 14.4 — Agglutination: Stacking Meanings
  - Turkish, Finnish, and Navajo build complex meanings by stacking pieces onto a word. In Turkish, "evlerimde" means "in my houses" — ev (house) + ler (plural) + im (my) + de (in). One word, four pieces of meaning. This is agglutination — building terms from smaller meaningful parts. Different from inflection (changing a word's form), this is assembling meaning from blocks.

**Test:** 4 questions on inflection, word order, particles, and agglutination as strategies for expressing term relationships.

### Chapter 15: Terms as the Foundation of Thought

**Core concepts:** Review, terms as building blocks, preparing for Logic, the power of precision

- 15.1 — What You've Learned
  - Review: what terms are, what they refer to, levels of imposition and intention, categories of being, definition, supposition, the functions of parts of speech, predication, extension and comprehension, division and classification, ambiguity, and how different languages express terms. These are the building blocks of all structured thought.
- 15.2 — Terms and Clear Thinking
  - Every argument, every explanation, every law, every scientific statement, every contract is built from terms and their relationships. When the terms are clear, thinking is clear. When terms are ambiguous, vague, or confused across levels, thinking goes wrong — and people don't even realize it.
- 15.3 — Terms Across All Languages
  - No language is more "logical" than another. Each language has its own tools for expressing the same underlying concepts: naming, defining, categorizing, relating, predicating. Understanding the universal concepts underneath lets you learn any language faster and understand any argument more clearly.
- 15.4 — What Comes Next
  - Terms combine into propositions. Propositions combine into arguments. Arguments combine into persuasion. That's the Trivium: Grammar (terms and their relationships), Logic (valid reasoning from propositions), and Rhetoric (persuasive communication of reasoned arguments). You now have the foundation. Next comes what you can build on it.

**Test:** 4 questions reviewing key concepts, applying them to a new example, connecting terms to clear thinking, and looking ahead to Logic.

## Book 2: Relationships Between Terms

**Subtitle:** How Concepts Connect

### Chapter 1: What Is a Relationship Between Terms?

**Core concepts:** Terms don't exist in isolation, types of connections, relationships as the grammar of thought

- 1.1 — Terms Need Each Other
  - A single term — "dog" — names something, but it doesn't say anything. It's only when you connect terms that meaning happens. "The dog runs." "The big dog." "My dog." Each connection between terms creates a relationship, and that relationship carries meaning. Grammar is fundamentally about these relationships.
- 1.2 — Different Kinds of Relationships
  - Terms can be connected in many ways. One term can describe another (modification). One term can act on another (action). One term can be identified with another (predication). Terms can possess, receive, cause, locate, compare, and much more. Each type of connection is a different relationship.
- 1.3 — Relationships Are Universal
  - Every language expresses relationships between terms. English does it with word order and prepositions. Latin does it with case endings. Turkish does it with suffixes. Korean does it with particles. The relationships themselves — agent, patient, location, possession — are the same. Only the expression differs.
- 1.4 — Relationships Are the Grammar
  - When people say "grammar," they usually mean "the rules of English." But real Grammar is the system of relationships between concepts. The rules of any particular language are just that language's way of expressing those relationships. Understanding the relationships themselves is understanding Grammar at the deepest level.

**Test:** 4 questions on why terms need relationships, types of connections, universality, and relationships as the core of Grammar.

### Chapter 2: Predication — Saying Something About Something

**Core concepts:** Subject-predicate revisited in depth, essential vs. accidental predication, predicables

- 2.1 — The Most Fundamental Relationship
  - Predication is the act of saying something about something. Every meaningful statement has it: pick a subject, say something about it. "Snow is white." "Birds fly." "Justice is a virtue." This is the most basic relationship in all of Grammar — and every other relationship is a variation of it.
- 2.2 — Essential Predication
  - Essential predication says what something IS — part of its very nature. "A dog is an animal." "A triangle has three sides." You can't remove these without the thing ceasing to be what it is. Essential predications express the substance and definition of a thing.
- 2.3 — Accidental Predication
  - Accidental predication says something true about a thing that could be otherwise. "The dog is brown." "The triangle is large." These are true but not essential — the dog could be black, the triangle could be small. Accidental predications express qualities, quantities, relations, and other properties.
- 2.4 — The Predicables
  - The classical predicables organize what can be predicated about a thing: genus (the broader category — "animal"), species (the specific kind — "dog"), differentia (what distinguishes it — "domesticated"), property (something that always follows from its nature — "able to bark"), and accident (something incidental — "brown"). These five cover everything you can say about anything.

**Test:** 4 questions on predication, essential vs. accidental, the predicables, and classifying predications.

### Chapter 3: Modification — Adding Information

**Core concepts:** Attribution, adverbial modification, degree, how modification works across languages

- 3.1 — What Is Modification?
  - Modification is when one term adds information to another term without changing what it refers to. "Big dog" — "big" modifies "dog." The dog is still a dog; "big" narrows down which kind. "Runs quickly" — "quickly" modifies "runs." The running is still running; "quickly" tells you how. Modification adds detail.
- 3.2 — Modifying Names (Attribution)
  - When a modifier attaches to a naming term (a noun), it's called attribution. "Red ball," "tall building," "ancient language." The modifier specifies a quality, quantity, or type. In English, modifiers usually go before the noun. In French, many go after. In Japanese, they always go before. The relationship is the same; the position differs.
- 3.3 — Modifying Actions and Other Modifiers
  - Modifiers can attach to action terms (verbs) or even to other modifiers. "Runs quickly" (modifies the action). "Very quickly" (modifies the modifier). "Almost never" (modifies the modifier). These create layers of specification — each modifier adds another layer of precision to the meaning.
- 3.4 — Degree
  - Many modifiers have degrees: big, bigger, biggest. Fast, faster, fastest. In English, we use "-er" and "-est" or "more" and "most." In Latin, the endings change. In Turkish, a prefix is added. Degree is a universal concept — every language has a way to express that one thing has more of a quality than another.

**Test:** 4 questions on modification, attribution, modifying actions, and degree across languages.

### Chapter 4: Case Relationships — Who Did What to Whom

**Core concepts:** Agent, patient, instrument, recipient, beneficiary — the semantic roles

- 4.1 — Roles in an Action
  - When something happens, different terms play different roles. "The girl hit the ball with a bat." The girl is the one doing it (the agent). The ball is the thing being affected (the patient). The bat is the tool used (the instrument). These roles exist in every description of every action in every language.
- 4.2 — The Agent
  - The agent is the one who performs the action. "The dog chased the cat." The dog is the agent. In English, the agent usually comes first. In Latin, the agent takes the nominative case ending. In Japanese, the agent is marked with the particle "ga." Different signals, same role.
- 4.3 — The Patient
  - The patient is the thing affected by the action. "The dog chased the cat." The cat is the patient — it's being chased. In English, the patient comes after the verb. In Latin, it takes the accusative case ending. In Korean, it's marked with "eul/reul." The patient receives the action.
- 4.4 — Other Roles
  - Instrument (with what — "cut with a knife"), recipient (to whom — "gave the book to her"), beneficiary (for whom — "made dinner for the family"), source (from where — "came from the store"), goal (to where — "went to the park"), location (where — "sat in the chair"). Each is a distinct relationship that every language must express.

### Chapter 5: Case Systems

**Core concepts:** How languages mark case, Latin cases, comparison with other systems

- 5.1 — What Is a Case System?
  - A case system is a language's method of marking which role each term plays. Latin marks case with word endings. "Canis videt felem" (the dog sees the cat) — "canis" (nominative = agent) and "felem" (accusative = patient). You could rearrange the words and the meaning wouldn't change because the endings carry the information.
- 5.2 — The Latin Cases
  - Nominative (the subject/agent), accusative (the direct object/patient), genitive (possession — "of the dog"), dative (the indirect object/recipient — "to the girl"), ablative (instrument, source, cause — "with a bat," "from the city"). Each case is a different relationship. Latin has a case for each of the most common relationships.
- 5.3 — Rich Case Systems
  - Finnish has about 15 cases, including cases for "in," "into," "out of," "on," "onto," and "off of." Hungarian has a similar number. Russian has 6. Turkish builds them with suffixes. These languages can express very precisely what relationship a term has to the rest of the statement — all through the form of the word.
- 5.4 — Languages Without Case Endings
  - English has almost no case endings (just "I/me/my" and "who/whom"). Instead, it uses word order and prepositions. Chinese has none and uses word order, particles, and context. These languages express the same relationships — they just use different strategies. No method is better; they're different tools for the same job.

**Test:** 4 questions on what case systems do, Latin cases, rich case systems, and languages without case endings.

### Chapter 6: Possession and Identity

**Core concepts:** How terms express belonging, types of possession, identity and equivalence

- 6.1 — Possession: What Belongs to What
  - Possession is the relationship of belonging. "The dog's bone." "My house." "The city's mayor." In English, we use "'s" or "of." In Latin, the genitive case. In Turkish, a possessive suffix. Possession is one of the most common relationships between terms.
- 6.2 — Types of Possession
  - Not all possession is the same. Ownership ("my bike" — I own it), part-whole ("the car's engine" — the engine is part of the car), association ("the team's captain" — the captain belongs to the team in a role), origin ("the painter's style" — the style comes from the painter). The word "of" or "'s" handles all of these in English, but they're different relationships.
- 6.3 — Identity: Two Terms, One Thing
  - Identity is when two terms refer to the same thing. "The morning star is the evening star." "Mark Twain is Samuel Clemens." Identity uses the copula but goes beyond predication — it says these aren't just related, they're the same thing referred to by different terms.
- 6.4 — Equivalence and Difference
  - Some relationships express similarity or difference. "This is like that" (comparison). "This is the same as that" (equivalence). "This is different from that" (contrast). These are fundamental for reasoning — all classification, analogy, and argument depend on recognizing what's the same, what's similar, and what's different.

**Test:** 4 questions on possession, types of possession, identity, and equivalence/difference.

### Chapter 7: Spatial Relationships

**Core concepts:** Location, direction, source, goal, path — how terms relate in space

- 7.1 — Location: Where Something Is
  - Location describes where a thing is in relation to another thing. "The book is on the table." "The cat is under the bed." "The store is near the school." In English, prepositions handle this. In Finnish, specific cases handle it. In Navajo, the verb itself changes to express spatial relationship.
- 7.2 — Direction and Movement
  - Direction adds motion to space. "Into" (movement to inside), "out of" (movement from inside), "toward" (movement in a direction), "away from" (movement from). These are different from static location — "in the house" (location) vs. "into the house" (direction). Finnish has separate cases for "in," "into," and "out of."
- 7.3 — Source and Goal
  - Source is where something comes from. Goal is where something is going. "She walked from the school to the park." School is the source; park is the goal. These spatial concepts extend to non-physical uses — "He changed from happy to sad." The same relationship structure applied to emotions instead of locations.
- 7.4 — How Languages Handle Space
  - English uses prepositions (in, on, under, near, from, to). Latin uses cases and prepositions together. Turkish suffixes handle most of it. Korean uses particles. Some languages (like Tzeltal, a Mayan language) don't use left/right at all — they use compass directions even for small spaces. The spatial relationships are universal; the way of expressing them is not.

**Test:** 4 questions on location, direction, source/goal, and how different languages express spatial relationships.

### Chapter 8: Temporal Relationships

**Core concepts:** When things happen, sequence, duration, simultaneity, before/after

- 8.1 — Time as a Relationship Between Events
  - Just as spatial relationships locate things in space, temporal relationships locate events in time. "Before lunch," "after the game," "during class," "since Tuesday," "until Friday." These describe when one event happens relative to another event or time point.
- 8.2 — Sequence: Before, After, Then
  - Sequence puts events in order. "First she ran, then she jumped." "He ate before he left." "After the storm, the sun came out." Sequence is one of the most basic temporal relationships — it tells you which event came first.
- 8.3 — Duration and Simultaneity
  - Duration is how long something lasts. "She studied for two hours." "The game lasted all day." Simultaneity is when things happen at the same time. "While you were sleeping, it snowed." These are different temporal relationships from sequence — they describe overlap and length, not order.
- 8.4 — Tense: How Languages Mark Time
  - Most languages have a way to mark when an action happens relative to "now." Past, present, future. English uses helper words (was, is, will) and endings (-ed). Latin changes the verb ending. Chinese uses context and time words ("yesterday," "tomorrow") rather than changing the verb. Mandarin verbs don't change for tense at all — the relationship to time is expressed differently.

**Test:** 4 questions on temporal relationships, sequence, duration/simultaneity, and tense across languages.

### Chapter 9: Causal Relationships

**Core concepts:** Cause and effect, purpose, reason, condition — why and because

- 9.1 — Cause and Effect
  - One of the most important relationships: one thing causes another. "The rain made the ground wet." "She studied hard, so she passed." Cause and effect links two events where one brings about the other. This relationship is fundamental to science, law, everyday reasoning, and virtually every argument.
- 9.2 — Purpose: In Order To
  - Purpose expresses intention — why someone does something. "She studied in order to pass." "He saved money so he could buy a bike." Purpose is about the future goal that motivates an action. It's different from cause (which is about what made something happen) — purpose is about what you're trying to make happen.
- 9.3 — Reason: Why
  - A reason explains why something is the case. "He's tired because he stayed up late." "The store closed because it lost money." Reasons can be causes, but they can also be justifications or explanations that aren't strictly causal. "I believe it because the evidence supports it" — the evidence doesn't cause the belief in a physical sense, but it provides the reason.
- 9.4 — Condition: If...Then
  - A conditional relationship says one thing depends on another. "If it rains, the game is cancelled." "Unless you study, you won't pass." Conditionals are about dependency — one thing is true only if another thing is true. This is one of the most powerful relationship types and is central to Logic (which comes next in the Trivium).

**Test:** 4 questions on cause/effect, purpose, reason, and conditional relationships.

### Chapter 10: Comparison and Degree

**Core concepts:** Similarity, difference, degree, superlatives, analogy, proportion

- 10.1 — Similarity and Difference
  - Comparison is the relationship of being alike or unlike. "This is like that." "These are different." "A whale is similar to a fish in shape but different in biology." Every act of classification depends on comparison — putting things in the same category means they're similar in some way.
- 10.2 — Degree: More and Less
  - Many qualities exist on a spectrum. Bigger, smaller, faster, slower, more expensive, less popular. Degree relationships express where something falls on that spectrum relative to something else. "Taller than," "as fast as," "the most popular." Every language has tools for expressing degree.
- 10.3 — Superlatives: The Extreme
  - A superlative marks the extreme of a quality within a group. "The tallest student in class." "The cheapest option." Superlatives require a reference group — you're the tallest compared to a specific set. Without the group, a superlative is meaningless.
- 10.4 — Analogy and Proportion
  - An analogy says two relationships are similar. "A captain is to a ship as a principal is to a school." The individual terms are different, but the relationship between them is parallel. Analogy is one of the most powerful tools in language and reasoning — it lets you explain unfamiliar things in terms of familiar ones.

**Test:** 4 questions on similarity/difference, degree, superlatives, and analogy.

### Chapter 11: Quantification

**Core concepts:** All, some, none, specific vs. general, universal and particular terms

- 11.1 — How Many Are We Talking About?
  - "Dogs bark." All dogs? Most dogs? Some dogs? Quantification tells you how many members of a group you're talking about. "All dogs bark" is very different from "Some dogs bark." The quantity changes the meaning of the entire statement. This is one of the most important and most overlooked aspects of Grammar.
- 11.2 — Universal Terms: All and Every
  - A universal statement covers every member of a group. "All triangles have three sides." "Every student took the test." Universal statements are strong claims — one exception disproves them. That's why they're powerful when true and risky when not.
- 11.3 — Particular Terms: Some, Many, Few
  - A particular statement covers part of a group. "Some birds can't fly." "Many students passed." "Few people came." Particular statements are safer — they don't claim to cover everyone. But they're also less informative. "Some" could mean 2 or 2 million.
- 11.4 — Definite and Indefinite
  - "The dog" (a specific dog) vs. "a dog" (any dog). "This book" (definite) vs. "some book" (indefinite). Whether a term refers to a specific thing or any member of a category changes the meaning significantly. English uses articles (the, a) and demonstratives (this, that). Many languages handle this differently — Russian has no articles at all.

**Test:** 4 questions on quantification, universal statements, particular statements, and definite vs. indefinite.

### Chapter 12: Active, Passive, and Voice

**Core concepts:** Who is in focus, voice as perspective, middle voice, how languages handle voice

- 12.1 — Active Voice: The Agent in Focus
  - "The dog chased the cat." Active voice puts the agent (the doer) as the subject. The action flows from the agent to the patient. Active voice is the most direct way to describe an action — who did what to whom, in that order.
- 12.2 — Passive Voice: The Patient in Focus
  - "The cat was chased by the dog." Passive voice puts the patient (the thing affected) as the subject. The agent can even be dropped entirely: "The cat was chased." Passive voice is useful when the patient matters more than the agent, or when the agent is unknown.
- 12.3 — Why Voice Matters
  - Voice changes emphasis and focus without changing the facts. "The window was broken" (passive — focuses on the window, might hide who did it). "Jack broke the window" (active — focuses on Jack). Politicians, lawyers, and writers choose voice deliberately to control how information lands. Recognizing voice helps you see what's being emphasized and what's being hidden.
- 12.4 — Middle Voice and Other Voices
  - Some languages have more voices. Greek and Sanskrit have a middle voice — the subject acts on or for itself ("He washed himself"). Some languages have an antipassive (focusing on the agent while demoting the patient). Others have applicative voices that promote indirect objects. The underlying concept is the same: voice is about perspective on the action.

**Test:** 4 questions on active voice, passive voice, why voice matters, and additional voices in other languages.

### Chapter 13: Coordination and Subordination

**Core concepts:** Joining equals, nesting relationships, how complexity builds

- 13.1 — Coordination: Joining Equals
  - Coordination connects terms or ideas of equal status. "Dogs and cats." "She ran and jumped." "Smart but lazy." The connected parts have the same grammatical role — both are subjects, both are actions, both are descriptions. Coordination builds outward — adding more to the same level.
- 13.2 — Subordination: Nesting Relationships
  - Subordination puts one idea inside another, making it dependent. "The dog that chased the cat is tired." The part "that chased the cat" is subordinate — it's inside the main statement, adding information about the dog. Subordination builds depth — adding layers of meaning within a statement.
- 13.3 — Types of Subordination
  - Subordinate ideas can serve different functions. They can modify a term ("the dog that barked"), express time ("when it rained"), express cause ("because she was tired"), express condition ("if you study"), or serve as a term itself ("what he said surprised me"). Each type embeds a different kind of relationship.
- 13.4 — Building Complexity
  - Simple statements combine through coordination and subordination to create complex ones. "The dog chased the cat" + "the dog is brown" + "the chase happened yesterday" = "The brown dog that chased the cat yesterday is now sleeping." All the relationships are there — modification, action, time, state. Complex meaning is built from simple relationships stacked together.

**Test:** 4 questions on coordination, subordination, types of subordination, and how complexity is built.

### Chapter 14: Negation, Questions, and Commands

**Core concepts:** Denying, questioning, directing — beyond assertion

- 14.1 — Negation: Saying What Isn't
  - Negation reverses a statement. "The dog is brown" → "The dog is not brown." Every language has a way to negate. English uses "not" and "no." Latin modifies the verb. Japanese adds a suffix. What gets negated matters — "Not all dogs bark" and "All dogs don't bark" mean different things in logic, even though they might sound similar.
- 14.2 — Scope of Negation
  - Where the negation falls changes the meaning. "I didn't eat because I was hungry" could mean "I ate, but not because of hunger" or "I didn't eat, and the reason was hunger." The scope — what the "not" applies to — is critical. Languages handle scope differently, and ambiguity about scope is a frequent source of miscommunication.
- 14.3 — Questions: Requesting Information
  - A question reverses the direction of communication — instead of providing information, it requests it. "Is the dog brown?" (yes/no question). "What color is the dog?" (open question). Questions presuppose certain things ("What color is the dog?" presupposes the dog exists and has a color). Understanding what a question presupposes is a subtle but important skill.
- 14.4 — Commands and Mood
  - Commands direct action. "Close the door." "Don't run." Commands aren't true or false — they're about what should happen, not what is the case. This is the imperative mood. Statements about what is the case are the indicative mood. Wishes and hypotheticals ("If I were taller...") are the subjunctive mood. Mood expresses the speaker's relationship to the content.

**Test:** 4 questions on negation, scope, questions and presupposition, and mood.

### Chapter 15: The Web of Relationships

**Core concepts:** Review, how all relationships interconnect, preparing for propositions (Book 3)

- 15.1 — The Relationships You've Learned
  - Review: predication, modification, case relationships (agent, patient, instrument, recipient, etc.), possession, identity, spatial, temporal, causal, comparison, quantification, voice, coordination, subordination, negation, mood. These are the types of connections between concepts that make meaning possible.
- 15.2 — Every Statement Is a Web
  - Even a simple sentence like "My older brother quickly gave his best friend two books at school yesterday because she asked" contains: possession (my, his), modification (older, best, two), agent (brother), action (gave), recipient (friend), patient (books), location (at school), time (yesterday), cause (because she asked). A single statement can contain a dozen relationships.
- 15.3 — Relationships Are Universal, Expression Is Not
  - Every language expresses these relationships. The strategies differ — endings, word order, particles, agglutination — but the relationships themselves are the same. A child in Tokyo, a farmer in Finland, and a lawyer in Rome are all expressing agents, patients, causes, conditions, and modifications. The Grammar beneath is shared.
- 15.4 — What Comes Next
  - You know what terms are (Book 1) and how they relate to each other (Book 2). Next: how these relationships combine into complete propositions — statements that can be true or false — and how the structure of propositions affects what can be validly derived from them. That's where Grammar meets Logic.

## Book 3: Propositions and Structures

**Subtitle:** How Meaning Is Built

### Chapter 1: What Is a Proposition?

**Core concepts:** Propositions vs. sentences, truth value, simple vs. compound propositions

- 1.1 — From Relationships to Statements
  - Book 1 covered terms. Book 2 covered relationships. Now we put them together into propositions — complete statements that assert something that can be true or false. "The dog is brown" is a proposition. "Is the dog brown?" is not (it's a question). "Close the door" is not (it's a command). Propositions are the unit of truth.
- 1.2 — What Is a Proposition?
  - A proposition is the meaning behind a statement — the claim it makes. "Snow is white" is a proposition. It's either true or it isn't. The same proposition can be expressed in different languages, different words, even different sentence structures — but the claim stays the same. Propositions are about meaning, not about particular words.
- 1.3 — Sentences vs. Propositions
  - A sentence is a string of words in a particular language. A proposition is the meaning that sentence expresses. "The cat is on the mat" (English) and "Le chat est sur le tapis" (French) are different sentences expressing the same proposition. One proposition, many possible sentences. This distinction is fundamental.
- 1.4 — Simple and Compound Propositions
  - A simple proposition makes one claim. "The dog is brown." A compound proposition combines claims. "The dog is brown and the cat is black." Compound propositions use connectors — and, or, if...then, because — that create relationships between propositions themselves, not just between terms. This is where Grammar starts reaching toward Logic.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a proposition is, truth value, sentences vs. propositions, and simple vs. compound.

### Chapter 2: The Structure of Simple Propositions

**Core concepts:** Subject, copula, predicate in depth; quality and quantity of propositions

- 2.1 — Subject-Copula-Predicate
  - Every simple proposition has three parts: a subject (what you're talking about), a copula (the link), and a predicate (what you're saying about it). "All dogs (subject) are (copula) animals (predicate)." This structure is the skeleton of every simple claim. Even when the copula is hidden in the verb ("Dogs bark" = "Dogs are things-that-bark"), it's there.
- 2.2 — Quality: Affirmative and Negative
  - A proposition's quality is whether it affirms or denies. "Dogs are animals" (affirmative). "Dogs are not reptiles" (negative). Quality is one of only two fundamental properties of a proposition. Every proposition is either affirmative or negative.
- 2.3 — Quantity: Universal and Particular
  - A proposition's quantity is how much of the subject it covers. "All dogs are animals" (universal). "Some dogs are large" (particular). Quantity is the other fundamental property. Combined with quality, you get four basic types: All S is P, No S is P, Some S is P, Some S is not P. These four types are the foundation of classical Logic.
- 2.4 — The Four Types (A, E, I, O)
  - Logicians label the four types with letters: A (All S is P — universal affirmative), E (No S is P — universal negative), I (Some S is P — particular affirmative), O (Some S is not P — particular negative). Every simple proposition fits one of these four categories. This classification system has been used for over 2,000 years because it captures everything a simple proposition can do.

**Test:** 4 questions on the three parts, quality, quantity, and the four types.

### Chapter 3: Tense — Locating Propositions in Time

**Core concepts:** Past, present, future; how tense works, tense across languages, tenseless languages

- 3.1 — What Tense Does
  - Tense locates an action or state in time relative to "now." Past tense: it already happened. Present tense: it's happening now. Future tense: it will happen. Tense doesn't change what the proposition claims — it changes when the claim applies.
- 3.2 — How English Handles Tense
  - English uses verb forms and helper words. "She walked" (past), "She walks" (present), "She will walk" (future). English also has more complex tenses: "She has walked" (present perfect — past action relevant to now), "She was walking" (past continuous — ongoing past action). Each combination of time and completion creates a different tense.
- 3.3 — How Other Languages Handle Tense
  - Latin changes the verb ending for each tense. Turkish adds suffixes. Chinese doesn't change the verb at all — it uses time words ("yesterday," "tomorrow") and context particles (like "le" for completed actions). Some languages have more tenses than English (some African languages distinguish near past from far past). Some have fewer.
- 3.4 — Languages Without Tense
  - Some languages (like Mandarin, Malay, and some sign languages) don't mark tense on the verb at all. They use other tools to indicate time — context, time words, particles. This proves that tense is one strategy for expressing time, not the only one. The concept of time is universal; grammatical tense is not.

**Test:** 4 questions on what tense does, English tenses, tense in other languages, and tenseless languages.

### Chapter 4: Aspect — Completion and Duration

**Core concepts:** Perfective vs. imperfective, progressive, habitual, how aspect differs from tense

- 4.1 — Tense vs. Aspect
  - Tense tells you when. Aspect tells you the shape of the action — is it complete, ongoing, repeated, or habitual? "She read the book" (complete — perfective). "She was reading the book" (ongoing — imperfective). Both are past tense, but the aspect is different. Tense and aspect are independent — any tense can have any aspect.
- 4.2 — Perfective and Imperfective
  - Perfective aspect views the action as a complete whole — start and finish included. "He ate lunch." Imperfective aspect views the action as ongoing or in progress — no clear endpoint in focus. "He was eating lunch." The difference matters: "When the phone rang, he was eating" (eating was in progress) vs. "When the phone rang, he ate" (he started and finished eating).
- 4.3 — Habitual and Progressive
  - Habitual aspect describes recurring actions. "She walks to school" (she does this regularly). Progressive aspect describes actions in progress at a specific moment. "She is walking to school" (right now). Some languages mark these differently. English uses "is walking" for progressive and simple present for habitual. Russian uses verb pairs (perfective and imperfective) for this distinction.
- 4.4 — Aspect Across Languages
  - Slavic languages (Russian, Polish, Czech) have aspect built deeply into the verb system — most verbs come in perfective/imperfective pairs. Chinese uses particles like "le" (completed), "zhe" (ongoing), and "guo" (experienced). Arabic distinguishes perfective and imperfective as its main verb categories, not past and present. Aspect is universal; how it's expressed varies enormously.

**Test:** 4 questions on tense vs. aspect, perfective/imperfective, habitual/progressive, and aspect across languages.

### Chapter 5: Mood — The Speaker's Attitude

**Core concepts:** Indicative, subjunctive, imperative, conditional, epistemic modality

- 5.1 — What Is Mood?
  - Mood expresses the speaker's attitude toward the proposition. Is it a statement of fact? A wish? A command? A hypothetical? The same content — "he goes" — changes meaning with mood: "He goes" (fact — indicative), "If he went..." (hypothetical — subjunctive), "Go!" (command — imperative). Mood is the speaker's stance.
- 5.2 — Indicative: Stating Facts
  - The indicative mood presents something as factual. "The sun rises in the east." "She is a student." Most everyday statements are indicative. It's the default mood — when you make a claim about what is, was, or will be, you're using the indicative.
- 5.3 — Subjunctive: Wishes, Doubts, and Hypotheticals
  - The subjunctive mood expresses things that aren't (or might not be) factual. "If I were taller..." (hypothetical). "I wish she were here" (wish). "It's important that he be on time" (necessity). English has a weak subjunctive (mostly just "were" instead of "was"). Latin and Spanish have full, rich subjunctive systems used constantly.
- 5.4 — Imperative, Conditional, and Beyond
  - Imperative: commands and requests. "Sit down." "Please help." Conditional: what would happen under certain circumstances. "If it rains, we'll stay inside." Some languages have additional moods — Korean has a politeness system built into verb endings. Turkish has a mood for reported speech (things you heard but didn't witness). Mood reflects how the speaker relates to the content.

**Test:** 4 questions on mood types, indicative, subjunctive, and additional moods in other languages.

### Chapter 6: Agreement — Terms Matching Each Other

**Core concepts:** What agreement is, number, gender, person, agreement across languages

- 6.1 — What Is Agreement?
  - Agreement means related terms must match in certain features. In English, "The dog runs" (singular subject, singular verb). "The dogs run" (plural subject, plural verb). The verb form agrees with the subject's number. Agreement is a way languages reinforce which terms are connected.
- 6.2 — Number Agreement
  - Singular and plural is the most common agreement. English marks it on verbs weakly ("runs" vs. "run") and on nouns clearly ("dog" vs. "dogs"). Other languages have dual (for exactly two) or even trial (for exactly three). Arabic has singular, dual, and plural. Some languages have no number agreement at all.
- 6.3 — Gender Agreement
  - Many languages assign gender to nouns — not just people, but all nouns. In Spanish, "mesa" (table) is feminine, "libro" (book) is masculine. Adjectives and articles must match: "la mesa bonita" (the beautiful table — all feminine). German has three genders. Swahili has over a dozen noun classes. English doesn't have grammatical gender for objects.
- 6.4 — Person Agreement
  - Verbs often agree with who's doing the action. "I am," "you are," "she is" — different forms for different persons. Latin marks person, number, tense, mood, and voice all in the verb ending. A single Latin verb form like "amabamus" means "we were loving" — five pieces of information in one word.

**Test:** 4 questions on what agreement is, number, gender, and person agreement across languages.

### Chapter 7: Compound Propositions — Connecting Claims

**Core concepts:** Conjunction (and), disjunction (or), conditional (if...then), biconditional (if and only if)

- 7.1 — Joining Propositions with "And"
  - "It is raining and the ground is wet." This compound proposition joins two simple propositions. Both must be true for the compound to be true. If it's raining but the ground is dry (maybe it's covered), the compound is false. "And" is called conjunction — both parts are needed.
- 7.2 — Joining Propositions with "Or"
  - "We'll eat pizza or we'll eat tacos." This is true if either part (or both) is true. "Or" is called disjunction. In everyday language, "or" sometimes means "one or the other but not both" (exclusive or). In logic, "or" usually means "at least one" (inclusive or). The distinction matters.
- 7.3 — "If...Then" Propositions
  - "If it rains, then the game is cancelled." This is a conditional — the second part depends on the first. The first part is the condition (antecedent). The second is the result (consequent). Conditionals are the most powerful connectors because they express dependency — and they're the gateway to logical reasoning.
- 7.4 — "If and Only If"
  - "The light is on if and only if the switch is up." This is a biconditional — the two parts are true together and false together. It's stronger than a regular conditional. "If it rains, the ground is wet" (conditional — the ground could be wet for other reasons). "The ground is wet if and only if it rains" (biconditional — rain is the only cause). This is a precise and powerful relationship.

**Test:** 4 questions on conjunction, disjunction, conditional, and biconditional.

### Chapter 8: Subordinate Propositions

**Core concepts:** Embedded propositions, relative clauses, noun clauses, adverbial clauses

- 8.1 — Propositions Inside Propositions
  - A subordinate proposition lives inside another proposition, playing a role within it. "The dog that bit me was brown." The main proposition is "The dog was brown." Inside it, "that bit me" is a subordinate proposition that identifies which dog. Complex meaning is built by nesting propositions within propositions.
- 8.2 — Relative Clauses: Modifying Terms
  - A relative clause is a subordinate proposition that modifies a term. "The book that I read was interesting." "That I read" identifies which book. In English, relative clauses use "that," "which," "who." In Latin, the relative pronoun changes case to match its role within the clause. In Japanese, the modifying clause simply goes before the noun with no connecting word.
- 8.3 — Noun Clauses: Propositions as Terms
  - A noun clause is a subordinate proposition that acts as a term. "What she said surprised me." "What she said" is the subject — a whole proposition functioning as a term. "I believe that he is honest." "That he is honest" is the object. Propositions can fill any role a term can fill.
- 8.4 — Adverbial Clauses: Propositions as Modifiers
  - An adverbial clause modifies the main proposition by expressing time, cause, condition, or purpose. "When the bell rang, everyone left" (time). "Because it was cold, we stayed inside" (cause). "If you finish early, you can play" (condition). Each one embeds a relationship — temporal, causal, conditional — as a subordinate proposition.

**Test:** 4 questions on embedded propositions, relative clauses, noun clauses, and adverbial clauses.

### Chapter 9: Definiteness and Reference

**Core concepts:** Definite vs. indefinite, specific vs. generic, demonstratives, how languages handle reference

- 9.1 — Definite: A Specific Thing
  - "The dog" refers to a specific dog — one that both the speaker and listener can identify. Definite reference points to a particular thing. English uses "the." Many languages have similar articles. Some languages (like Russian and Latin) have no articles at all and express definiteness through word order and context.
- 9.2 — Indefinite: Any Member of a Kind
  - "A dog" refers to any dog — no specific one. "I'd like a sandwich" means any sandwich will do. Indefinite reference introduces something new or unspecified. English uses "a" and "an." In Hungarian, the verb changes form depending on whether the object is definite or indefinite.
- 9.3 — Generic: The Whole Kind
  - "Dogs are loyal" doesn't mean a specific dog or any random dog — it means dogs in general, as a kind. Generic reference talks about a category. English handles this awkwardly (no article? "the"? "a"? all can work for generics). Other languages have clearer strategies.
- 9.4 — Demonstratives and Deixis
  - "This book" vs. "that book." Demonstratives point to things relative to the speaker — near (this/these) or far (that/those). Deixis is the broader concept of language that depends on context: "here," "there," "now," "then," "I," "you" all change meaning depending on who's speaking, where, and when.

**Test:** 4 questions on definite, indefinite, generic reference, and demonstratives/deixis.

### Chapter 10: Information Structure

**Core concepts:** Topic and comment, focus, given vs. new information, word order as information tool

- 10.1 — Topic: What You're Talking About
  - In "As for the weather, it's beautiful today," the weather is the topic — what the statement is about. The rest is the comment — what you're saying about it. Topic and comment organize information. In Japanese, the particle "wa" explicitly marks the topic. In English, word order and stress do this work more subtly.
- 10.2 — Given and New Information
  - In conversation, some information is already known (given) and some is being introduced for the first time (new). "I saw a dog. The dog was huge." "A dog" is new (first mention). "The dog" is given (you already know about it). Languages arrange sentences to put given information first and new information later.
- 10.3 — Focus: The Important Part
  - Focus highlights the most important or contrastive information. "JOHN ate the cake" (not Mary). "John ATE the cake" (he didn't just look at it). In English, stress marks focus. In Hungarian, the focused word moves to a specific position in the sentence. In Mandarin, word order and particles handle it.
- 10.4 — Word Order as an Information Tool
  - Languages with free word order (Latin, Russian, Navajo) use that freedom to arrange information — putting the topic first, the focus where emphasis falls. Languages with fixed word order (English, Chinese) use other tools — stress, particles, special constructions ("It was John who ate the cake"). Word order serves two masters: Grammar (marking relationships) and information (organizing meaning for the listener).

### Chapter 11: Reported Speech and Evidentiality

**Core concepts:** Quoting others, indirect speech, evidentiality, the source of information

- 11.1 — Direct and Indirect Speech
  - Direct speech quotes exact words: She said, "I am tired." Indirect speech reports the meaning: She said that she was tired. The shift from direct to indirect changes pronouns, tense, and perspective. Every language handles this, but the mechanics differ — some languages have complex tense-shifting rules, others barely change anything.
- 11.2 — Why Reported Speech Matters
  - Much of what we know comes from other people's statements. Understanding reported speech means understanding attribution — who said what. "The news reported that..." "My friend told me..." "Scientists say..." The source of a claim matters as much as the claim itself. Reported speech is how we track sources.
- 11.3 — Evidentiality: How Do You Know?
  - Some languages require you to say how you know something. Turkish has a special verb form for information you didn't witness personally. Quechua has endings that mark whether you saw it yourself, heard it from someone, or are making an inference. English doesn't require this, but the concept is important — the basis for a claim matters.
- 11.4 — Source and Reliability
  - "It will rain" (I'm asserting it). "The forecast says it will rain" (I'm reporting someone else's claim). "It looks like it will rain" (I'm making an inference). These express different levels of certainty and different sources. Even in languages that don't grammatically require evidentiality, being aware of the source makes communication clearer and more honest.

### Chapter 12: Performatives and Speech Acts

**Core concepts:** Language that does things, promises, declarations, requests, speech act types

- 12.1 — Language That Does Things
  - Most propositions describe the world. But some language changes the world. "I promise to help." The act of saying it creates the promise. "I name this ship the Queen Mary." The saying is the doing. "You're fired." The words don't describe a firing — they are the firing. These are called performatives.
- 12.2 — Types of Speech Acts
  - Assertives: stating facts ("The store is closed"). Directives: requesting or commanding ("Please sit down"). Commissives: committing to something ("I'll be there at 5"). Expressives: expressing feelings ("I'm sorry"). Declaratives: changing reality by saying ("I pronounce you married"). Every utterance is a speech act of some kind.
- 12.3 — Indirect Speech Acts
  - "Can you pass the salt?" is grammatically a question but functionally a request. "It's cold in here" might be a statement but functionally a request to close the window. Language often works indirectly — the literal meaning and the intended meaning differ. Understanding speech acts means understanding what people are actually doing with their words.
- 12.4 — Conditions for Speech Acts
  - A promise requires the ability to follow through. A declaration requires authority. "I declare you president" doesn't work if you have no authority. Speech acts have conditions — they only work when the right circumstances are in place. Understanding these conditions helps you recognize when language is functioning properly and when it's empty.

**Test:** 4 questions on performatives, types of speech acts, indirect speech acts, and conditions.

### Chapter 13: Presupposition and Implication

**Core concepts:** What a statement takes for granted, what it implies without saying, pragmatic inference

- 13.1 — Presupposition: What's Taken for Granted
  - "Have you stopped eating candy?" presupposes that you used to eat candy. Whether you answer yes or no, the presupposition stands. Presuppositions are assumptions built into the structure of a statement. They're powerful because they slip in claims without asserting them directly.
- 13.2 — Types of Presupposition
  - Existential: "The king of France is bald" presupposes there is a king of France. Factive: "She realized she was wrong" presupposes she was wrong. Change of state: "He stopped running" presupposes he was running. Each type sneaks in an assumption that can be challenged.
- 13.3 — Implication: What's Suggested Without Saying
  - "Some students passed" implies (but doesn't say) that some didn't. "She's a good student" on a recommendation letter implies she's not exceptional. Implications go beyond what's literally stated. They're the meaning between the lines. Good communicators control implications. Good listeners notice them.
- 13.4 — Pragmatic Inference
  - If someone asks "Is there a gas station nearby?" and you say "There's one around the corner," you're implying it's open and functioning — even though you didn't say so. Listeners draw inferences based on context and the assumption that speakers are being helpful and relevant. This is pragmatics — the study of meaning in context.

### Chapter 14: Translation and Universality

**Core concepts:** What's preserved across languages, what's lost, untranslatable concepts, universal Grammar

- 14.1 — What Translation Preserves
  - Translation preserves the proposition — the core meaning. "Il fait froid" (French) and "It's cold" (English) express the same proposition in different structures. The term relationships, the claim, the truth value — these survive translation because they're deeper than any particular language.
- 14.2 — What Translation Loses
  - Connotation, rhythm, wordplay, cultural associations, and sometimes concepts that don't exist in the target language. "Saudade" (Portuguese) doesn't have an exact English equivalent. Japanese politeness levels don't map to English. Translation is always an approximation because languages encode more than just propositions.
- 14.3 — Untranslatable Concepts
  - Some languages have terms for concepts others lack. "Schadenfreude" (German — pleasure at someone else's misfortune). "Han" (Korean — a deep, unresolved resentment). "Hozho" (Navajo — beauty, balance, harmony as a way of being). These aren't untranslatable in principle — you can explain them — but the single-term packaging doesn't exist in every language.
- 14.4 — Universal Grammar
  - Beneath all languages lies a shared structure. Every language has terms, relationships, propositions. Every language can name, predicate, modify, quantify, negate, ask, command, express time, and report speech. The strategies differ. The underlying Grammar — the system of concepts and relationships — is universal. That's what these three books have been about.

### Chapter 15: Grammar as the Foundation of Thought

**Core concepts:** Review, Grammar → Logic → Rhetoric, how Grammar enables clear thinking

- 15.1 — What You've Learned Across Three Books
  - Book 1: what terms are, what they refer to, how they're defined, how they can be ambiguous. Book 2: how terms relate — predication, modification, case, time, space, cause, comparison, quantity, voice. Book 3: how relationships combine into propositions, how tense/aspect/mood/agreement work, how compound and subordinate structures build complexity, how meaning works in context.
- 15.2 — Grammar Is the Structure of Meaning
  - Grammar isn't a set of rules to memorize. It's the system that makes meaning possible. Every thought you've ever had, every argument you've ever heard, every book you've ever read is built from terms, their relationships, and the propositions that combine them. Understanding this system means understanding the structure of thought itself.
- 15.3 — From Grammar to Logic
  - Grammar gives you the building blocks. Logic tells you what you can validly derive from them. If "All dogs are animals" and "Rex is a dog," then "Rex is an animal." That's Logic — but it's built entirely on Grammar: terms, predication, quantification, and the structure of propositions. Without Grammar, Logic has nothing to work with.
- 15.4 — From Logic to Rhetoric
  - And once you can reason validly, Rhetoric teaches you to communicate that reasoning persuasively. The Trivium is a progression: Grammar (the structure of meaning) → Logic (the extraction of knowledge) → Rhetoric (the communication of knowledge). You've completed the first stage. The next two build directly on everything you've learned here.
