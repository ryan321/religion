# Law for Kids — Book Proposal

## Why This Book

Law affects everyone every day, but almost nobody learns how it actually works until they're already in a situation that requires it — or never. Most adults can't explain the difference between civil and criminal law, don't know what "burden of proof" means, don't understand how a contract works, and have no idea what happens in a courtroom beyond what they've seen on TV (which is mostly wrong).

This book teaches law the same way the econ series teaches economics: the actual concepts, explained clearly, with real examples. Not "how to be a lawyer" — but how the legal system works, what the key concepts are, and how they apply to real life. A kid who finishes this book will understand how laws are made, what rights are, how courts work, what contracts mean, and what happens when someone breaks the rules — all in plain language.

## Series Context

One book. Law is broad, but the core concepts fit in 15 chapters without needing to go into the details of specific statutes or case law. This covers the framework — the concepts and vocabulary that make everything else in law understandable.

Based on U.S. law as the primary reference point, but the core concepts (what a law is, how courts work, burden of proof, rights, contracts, evidence) are largely universal. Where something is U.S.-specific (the Constitution, the court system structure), it's noted but taught as an example of a broader concept.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300–500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7–12
- Real-world examples from a kid's life (school rules, family rules, neighborhood disputes, fairness on the playground)

## Math expectations

- None. This is a concepts-and-vocabulary subject.

## Prerequisites

- None required. This book stands alone.

## Law for Kids

**Subtitle:** How Rules, Rights, and Justice Work

### Chapter 1: What Is Law?

**Core concepts:** What law is, why societies have laws, written vs. unwritten rules, law vs. rules

- 1.1 — Why Do We Have Rules?
  - Imagine a basketball game with no rules. Anyone can grab the ball, push people, and run anywhere. It wouldn't work. Rules make cooperation possible. Laws are rules for a whole society — they set expectations so millions of people can live, work, and interact without chaos.
- 1.2 — What Is a Law?
  - A law is an official rule created by a government that everyone in that place must follow. Laws are different from family rules or school rules because they apply to everyone and are enforced by the government. Breaking a law can have serious consequences — fines, restrictions, or even jail.
- 1.3 — Written Law
  - Most laws are written down — in constitutions, statutes (laws passed by legislatures), and regulations (detailed rules created by agencies). Written law has a huge advantage: everyone can read it, look it up, and know what the rules are. There's no "I didn't know" when the law is publicly available.
- 1.4 — Laws Change Over Time
  - Laws aren't permanent. They're created, changed, and sometimes removed as society changes. Slavery was once legal. Women couldn't always vote. Laws evolve because people's understanding of fairness and justice evolves. The legal system has a process for making those changes.

**Test:** 4 questions on why laws exist, what a law is, written law, and how laws change.

### Chapter 2: Where Laws Come From

**Core concepts:** Constitutions, legislatures, statutes, regulations, common law, precedent

- 2.1 — Constitutions: The Foundation
  - A constitution is the highest law of a country or state. It sets up the government's structure and protects basic rights. In the U.S., the Constitution is the supreme law — no other law can contradict it. Other countries have their own constitutions with similar purposes.
- 2.2 — Legislatures: Making New Laws
  - A legislature is a group of elected people who create laws. In the U.S., Congress (the Senate and House of Representatives) makes federal laws. Each state has its own legislature too. A proposed law is called a bill. If enough legislators vote for it and the executive signs it, it becomes a statute.
- 2.3 — Regulations: Filling in the Details
  - Legislatures write broad laws. Government agencies write regulations that fill in the specific details. A law might say "food must be safe." A regulation spells out exactly what that means — temperatures, labeling requirements, inspection schedules. Regulations have the force of law.
- 2.4 — Common Law and Precedent
  - Some law comes from judges' decisions in court cases, not from legislatures. When a judge decides a case, that decision can set a precedent — a standard that future courts follow in similar cases. Over time, these decisions build up into a body of law called common law. Precedent creates consistency — similar cases get similar outcomes.

**Test:** 4 questions on constitutions, how legislatures create laws, regulations, and precedent.

### Chapter 3: Types of Law

**Core concepts:** Criminal law, civil law, the key differences, other areas of law

- 3.1 — Criminal Law
  - Criminal law deals with actions that society considers harmful enough that the government itself prosecutes the person who did it. Theft, assault, fraud, murder — these are crimes. The government (not the victim) brings the case. If found guilty, the punishment can include fines, probation, or prison.
- 3.2 — Civil Law
  - Civil law deals with disputes between people, companies, or organizations. One party (the plaintiff) claims another party (the defendant) wronged them and asks the court for a remedy — usually money or an order to do (or stop doing) something. No one goes to jail in a civil case.
- 3.3 — The Key Differences
  - Criminal: government vs. the accused. Purpose is punishment and public safety. Burden of proof is "beyond a reasonable doubt" (very high). Civil: person vs. person. Purpose is to resolve a dispute and make the injured party whole. Burden of proof is "preponderance of the evidence" (more likely than not — a lower bar).
- 3.4 — Other Areas of Law
  - Family law (divorce, custody, adoption), contract law (agreements between people), property law (who owns what), constitutional law (rights and government power), administrative law (government agencies and regulations), international law (between countries). Law has many branches, but criminal and civil are the two main trunks.

**Test:** 4 questions on criminal law, civil law, key differences between them, and other areas of law.

### Chapter 4: Rights

**Core concepts:** What rights are, constitutional rights, the Bill of Rights, rights have limits

- 4.1 — What Is a Right?
  - A right is something you are legally entitled to do, have, or be free from. The right to speak freely, the right to a fair trial, the right to own property. Rights aren't permissions — they're protections. They exist to prevent the government (and sometimes other people) from treating you unfairly.
- 4.2 — Constitutional Rights
  - In the U.S., the Constitution protects fundamental rights. The first ten amendments are called the Bill of Rights. Freedom of speech, freedom of religion, the right to bear arms, protection from unreasonable searches, the right to a trial by jury — these are constitutional rights.
- 4.3 — Other Rights
  - Not all rights come from the Constitution. Federal and state laws create additional rights — the right to a minimum wage, the right to attend public school, protections against discrimination. International agreements recognize rights too, like the rights of children or the rights of refugees.
- 4.4 — Rights Have Limits
  - Freedom of speech doesn't mean you can say anything anywhere without consequences. You can't yell "fire" in a crowded theater. You can't threaten someone. Most rights have limits — they stop where another person's rights begin. Understanding the limits is as important as understanding the rights.

**Test:** 4 questions on what rights are, the Bill of Rights, where other rights come from, and limits on rights.

### Chapter 5: The Court System

**Core concepts:** Trial courts, appeals courts, the Supreme Court, jurisdiction, federal vs. state

- 5.1 — Trial Courts: Where Cases Start
  - Most cases begin in a trial court. This is where evidence is presented, witnesses testify, and a judge or jury decides the outcome. Each side tells their story, and the court makes a decision based on the facts and the law.
- 5.2 — Appeals Courts: Reviewing Decisions
  - If you lose in trial court and believe the law was applied incorrectly, you can appeal. An appeals court reviews the case — not to retry it, but to check whether the trial court made a legal error. Appeals courts don't hear new evidence or new witnesses. They review the record.
- 5.3 — The Supreme Court
  - The U.S. Supreme Court is the highest court in the country. Its decisions are final and apply everywhere. The Supreme Court chooses which cases to hear — typically cases involving important constitutional questions or conflicts between lower courts. It hears only about 70-80 cases per year out of thousands of requests.
- 5.4 — Federal vs. State Courts
  - Federal courts handle cases involving federal law, the Constitution, and disputes between states. State courts handle most everyday cases — crimes under state law, family disputes, local contract issues. Both systems have trial courts, appeals courts, and a top court. Most cases happen in state courts.

**Test:** 4 questions on trial courts, appeals, the Supreme Court, and federal vs. state jurisdiction.

### Chapter 6: People in the Legal System

**Core concepts:** Judges, juries, lawyers, plaintiffs, defendants, prosecutors, witnesses

- 6.1 — Judges
  - A judge oversees the case, makes sure the rules are followed, decides legal questions, and in some cases decides the outcome. Judges are neutral — they don't take sides. In a jury trial, the judge runs the process and explains the law; the jury decides the facts.
- 6.2 — Juries
  - A jury is a group of ordinary citizens who listen to the evidence and decide the outcome. In criminal cases, the jury decides guilty or not guilty. In civil cases, the jury decides who's at fault and what the remedy should be. The right to a jury trial is a fundamental protection.
- 6.3 — Lawyers
  - Each side has a lawyer (also called an attorney) who presents their case, questions witnesses, and argues the law. In criminal cases, the prosecutor represents the government and the defense attorney represents the accused. In civil cases, each side hires their own lawyer. A lawyer's job is to be their client's advocate.
- 6.4 — Parties and Witnesses
  - The parties are the people or organizations involved in the case. In criminal cases: the government (prosecution) and the defendant (the accused). In civil cases: the plaintiff (the one suing) and the defendant (the one being sued). Witnesses are people who have relevant information and testify about what they know.

**Test:** 4 questions on the role of judges, juries, lawyers, and identifying parties in a case.

### Chapter 7: How a Case Works

**Core concepts:** Filing, pleadings, discovery, motions, settlement, trial, verdict

- 7.1 — Starting a Case
  - A civil case begins when the plaintiff files a complaint — a document explaining what happened and what they want the court to do. A criminal case begins when the government files charges. The other side is notified and given a chance to respond. This exchange of documents is called the pleadings.
- 7.2 — Discovery: Gathering Information
  - Before trial, both sides share information through discovery. They exchange documents, answer written questions (interrogatories), and take depositions (recorded interviews under oath). Discovery prevents trial by ambush — both sides should know the evidence before the trial starts.
- 7.3 — Settlement and Plea Bargains
  - Most cases never go to trial. In civil cases, the parties often reach a settlement — an agreement to resolve the dispute without a trial. In criminal cases, the defendant may accept a plea bargain — pleading guilty to a lesser charge in exchange for a lighter sentence. Trials are expensive and uncertain for everyone.
- 7.4 — Trial and Verdict
  - If the case goes to trial, both sides present evidence and arguments. Witnesses testify. Lawyers make opening and closing statements. Then the judge or jury delivers a verdict — the decision. In criminal cases: guilty or not guilty. In civil cases: liable or not liable, and what remedy to award.

**Test:** 4 questions on how cases start, discovery, why most cases settle, and what happens at trial.

### Chapter 8: Evidence

**Core concepts:** What evidence is, types of evidence, admissibility, burden of proof

- 8.1 — What Is Evidence?
  - Evidence is anything presented in court to prove or disprove a fact. Documents, photographs, physical objects, testimony from witnesses, electronic records, video footage. Evidence is how you show the court what actually happened. Without evidence, you just have claims.
- 8.2 — Types of Evidence
  - Direct evidence directly proves a fact (an eyewitness who saw it happen). Circumstantial evidence indirectly suggests a fact (muddy footprints leading from the scene). Documentary evidence is documents and records. Physical evidence is tangible objects. Each type has strengths and weaknesses.
- 8.3 — Admissibility: What the Court Will Accept
  - Not all evidence is allowed in court. Evidence must be relevant (related to the case), reliable (trustworthy), and obtained legally. Hearsay (repeating what someone else said) is generally not allowed because the person who said it isn't there to be questioned. Rules of evidence keep trials fair.
- 8.4 — Burden of Proof
  - The burden of proof is the responsibility to prove your case. In criminal cases, the prosecution must prove guilt "beyond a reasonable doubt" — the highest standard. In civil cases, the plaintiff must show their claim is "more likely than not" — a lower standard. The defendant doesn't have to prove anything — they just have to create enough doubt.

**Test:** 4 questions on what evidence is, types of evidence, admissibility, and burden of proof.

### Chapter 9: Arguments and Reasoning

**Core concepts:** Legal arguments, claims and support, precedent in arguments, logical reasoning

- 9.1 — What Is a Legal Argument?
  - A legal argument is a structured claim supported by evidence and law. It's not an angry disagreement — it's a logical presentation. "My client is not liable because [evidence shows X] and [the law says Y]." Every legal argument has a claim, support from evidence, and a basis in law.
- 9.2 — Building an Argument
  - State your position. Present the facts that support it. Show how the law applies to those facts. Address the other side's strongest points. A good argument doesn't ignore inconvenient facts — it explains why its conclusion is right despite them.
- 9.3 — Using Precedent
  - Lawyers argue that past court decisions (precedents) support their position. "In a similar case, the court ruled that..." Precedent gives arguments authority beyond just "I think so." If your situation is like a case that was already decided, the same reasoning should apply.
- 9.4 — Logical Reasoning
  - Good legal arguments follow logical steps. If the law says "anyone who does X is liable" and the evidence shows the defendant did X, then the defendant is liable. This is deductive reasoning. Weak arguments have logical gaps — they jump from facts to conclusions without connecting them properly.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a legal argument is, building one, using precedent, and identifying logical reasoning.

### Chapter 10: Torts

**Core concepts:** What a tort is, negligence, intentional torts, strict liability, damages

- 10.1 — What Is a Tort?
  - A tort is a wrongful act (other than a breach of contract) that causes harm to someone. If someone's carelessness damages your property, that's a tort. If someone deliberately hurts you, that's a tort. Tort law lets the injured person sue for compensation. The word comes from the French word for "wrong."
- 10.2 — Negligence
  - Negligence is the most common type of tort. It means someone failed to be reasonably careful, and that failure caused harm. A driver who runs a red light and causes an accident was negligent. To prove negligence, you show: they had a duty of care, they breached that duty, the breach caused the harm, and there was actual damage.
- 10.3 — Intentional Torts
  - Some torts are done on purpose. Assault (threatening harm), battery (actually causing physical contact), trespassing (entering someone's property without permission), defamation (making false statements that damage someone's reputation). The key difference from negligence: the person meant to do it.
- 10.4 — Strict Liability and Damages
  - Strict liability means you're responsible for harm even if you weren't careless or didn't mean to cause it. If your dog bites someone, you may be liable regardless of intent. Damages are the money a court awards to compensate the injured person — for medical bills, lost property, lost income, or pain and suffering.

**Test:** 4 questions on what torts are, negligence elements, intentional torts, and damages.

### Chapter 11: Contracts

**Core concepts:** What a contract is, offer and acceptance, consideration, breach, remedies

- 11.1 — What Is a Contract?
  - A contract is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties. "I'll mow your lawn for $20" — if both sides agree, that's a contract. Contracts can be written or verbal, simple or complex. The key idea: both sides make a promise, and the law holds them to it.
- 11.2 — The Elements of a Contract
  - A valid contract needs: an offer (one party proposes something), acceptance (the other party agrees), and consideration (both sides give something of value — money, services, goods, a promise). Without all three, it's not a contract. "I'll give you $50 for nothing" isn't a contract because there's no consideration from your side.
- 11.3 — Breach of Contract
  - A breach happens when one side doesn't keep their promise without a valid excuse. If you hire a painter and they never show up, that's a breach. If you agree to pay someone and then refuse, that's a breach. The law provides remedies because people and businesses need to be able to rely on agreements.
- 11.4 — Remedies for Breach
  - When a contract is breached, the injured party can seek remedies. The most common is damages — money to cover what you lost because of the breach. Sometimes the court orders specific performance — forcing the person to do what they promised. The goal is to put the injured party in the position they would have been in if the contract had been honored.

**Test:** 4 questions on what contracts are, the three elements, breach, and remedies.

### Chapter 12: Criminal Law Concepts

**Core concepts:** Elements of a crime, intent, degrees of offenses, defenses, sentencing

- 12.1 — Elements of a Crime
  - Most crimes require two things: a guilty act (actus reus) and a guilty mind (mens rea). The act is what you did. The mind is whether you meant to do it. Accidentally bumping someone isn't a crime. Deliberately hitting them might be. Both elements usually must be present for a conviction.
- 12.2 — Intent and Mental States
  - The law recognizes different mental states. Intentional: you meant to do it. Reckless: you knew the risk and did it anyway. Negligent: you should have known the risk. Strict liability: no mental state required (some offenses, like selling to a minor, don't require intent). The mental state affects what you can be charged with and the severity of punishment.
- 12.3 — Degrees and Categories
  - Crimes range from minor to severe. Infractions (traffic tickets) are the mildest. Misdemeanors (petty theft, minor offenses) carry fines or short jail time. Felonies (robbery, assault, serious fraud) carry significant prison time. Murder has degrees — first degree (planned), second degree (intentional but not planned), manslaughter (unintentional).
- 12.4 — Defenses and Sentencing
  - Common defenses: self-defense (you were protecting yourself), alibi (you weren't there), insanity (you couldn't understand what you were doing), duress (you were forced). If convicted, sentencing considers the severity of the crime, the defendant's history, and other factors. Sentences can include fines, probation, community service, or prison.

**Test:** 4 questions on elements of a crime, mental states, degrees of offenses, and common defenses.

### Chapter 13: Property and Ownership

**Core concepts:** What property is, real vs. personal property, intellectual property, property disputes

- 13.1 — What Is Property?
  - Property is anything that can be owned. The law protects your right to use, enjoy, and control your property. Property rights are one of the foundations of a legal system — without them, there's no reliable way to know who owns what.
- 13.2 — Real Property and Personal Property
  - Real property is land and anything permanently attached to it (houses, buildings). Personal property is everything else you own (cars, clothes, phones, money, pets). Different rules apply to each. Buying a house involves deeds, titles, and recordings. Buying a sandwich doesn't.
- 13.3 — Intellectual Property
  - Intellectual property protects things you create with your mind. Patents protect inventions. Copyrights protect creative works (books, music, art, software). Trademarks protect brand names and logos. These exist because people need an incentive to create — if anyone could copy your invention immediately, why bother inventing?
- 13.4 — Property Disputes
  - Disputes arise over who owns something, where a property boundary is, whether someone can use your property, or whether someone damaged it. Easements (the right to use someone else's property for a specific purpose, like a shared driveway) and nuisance claims (your neighbor's activity is unreasonably interfering with your property) are common issues.

**Test:** 4 questions on what property is, real vs. personal, intellectual property types, and common disputes.

### Chapter 14: The Constitution and Government Power

**Core concepts:** Separation of powers, checks and balances, federalism, due process, equal protection

- 14.1 — Separation of Powers
  - The U.S. Constitution divides government into three branches. The legislative branch (Congress) makes laws. The executive branch (the President) enforces laws. The judicial branch (the courts) interprets laws. No single branch has all the power. This structure prevents any one group from becoming too powerful.
- 14.2 — Checks and Balances
  - Each branch can limit the others. The President can veto a law passed by Congress. Congress can override the veto with enough votes. The courts can strike down laws that violate the Constitution. This system of checks and balances keeps power in balance — no branch can act unchecked.
- 14.3 — Federalism
  - Power is divided between the federal government and the states. The federal government handles national issues (defense, immigration, interstate commerce). States handle local issues (education, traffic laws, most criminal law). Some issues overlap, and the Constitution is the tiebreaker.
- 14.4 — Due Process and Equal Protection
  - Due process means the government must follow fair procedures before taking away someone's life, liberty, or property. You can't be thrown in jail without a trial. Equal protection means the law must treat people in similar situations the same way. These two principles are the backbone of individual rights in the legal system.

**Test:** 4 questions on separation of powers, checks and balances, federalism, and due process/equal protection.

### Chapter 15: Law in Everyday Life

**Core concepts:** How law touches daily life, knowing your rights, resolving disputes, law as a system

- 15.1 — Law Is Everywhere
  - Speed limits, food safety, employment rules, rental agreements, product warranties, online privacy policies — law shapes daily life in ways most people don't notice. Every time you buy something, sign up for a service, or go to school, laws are involved. Understanding them helps you navigate the world.
- 15.2 — Knowing Your Rights
  - You don't need to be a lawyer to know your basic rights. The right to remain silent. The right to an attorney. The right to due process. The right to equal treatment. Knowing these rights means you can recognize when they're being respected — and when they're not.
- 15.3 — Resolving Disputes Without Court
  - Most disputes are resolved outside of court. Negotiation (talking it out), mediation (a neutral third party helps you reach agreement), and arbitration (a neutral third party makes a binding decision) are all alternatives. Courts are the last resort, not the first step.
- 15.4 — Law as a Living System
  - Law isn't a dusty set of rules in a book. It's a living system that evolves as society changes. New technologies create new legal questions. New social understanding changes old laws. Courts reinterpret the Constitution for modern times. The legal system is always in motion — and now you understand how it works.

**Test:** 4 questions on law in daily life, basic rights everyone should know, dispute resolution alternatives, and how law evolves.
