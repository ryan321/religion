# Logic for Kids — Book Proposal

## Why These Books

Logic is the second art of the Trivium: Grammar → Logic → Rhetoric. Grammar teaches the structure of meaning — how terms relate to each other in propositions. Logic teaches what knowledge can be validly extracted from those propositions. It's the art of correct reasoning.

Most people have never studied formal logic. They argue by instinct, accept or reject claims based on feeling, and can't identify why a bad argument is bad — they just know "something feels off." These books give kids the actual tools: the square of opposition, the rules of the syllogism, the formal and informal fallacies, the four causes, and the standards for genuine demonstration. A kid who finishes all four books will be able to evaluate any argument with precision.

This is Aristotelian/scholastic logic in the tradition of the Organon, taught to kids. It's a challenge for the age group, but the Grammar books have already laid the foundation — the four proposition types (A, E, I, O), predication, quantification, and compound propositions. Logic builds directly on that.

## Series Context

Four books. This is the Logic portion of the Trivium.

- **Book 1: Propositions and Immediate Inference** — The foundation: terms in logic, the four proposition types, distribution, the square of opposition, conversion, obversion, contraposition
- **Book 2: The Syllogism** — The categorical syllogism in full, compound syllogisms, extended forms
- **Book 3: Fallacies** — Formal and informal fallacies, comprehensive treatment
- **Book 4: Material Logic** — Definition, division, the four causes, judgment, truth, demonstration, induction, analogy, practical reasoning

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters per book, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300–500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7–12
- Real-world examples alongside formal examples (keep it concrete)

## Math expectations

- No math per se, but logical notation is used in simplified form
- Proposition types (A, E, I, O) used as labels
- Mood names (Barbara, Celarent, etc.) introduced as vocabulary
- Diagrams (square of opposition, Euler circles) used as visual tools
- The reasoning is rigorous but expressed in words, not symbols

## Prerequisites

- Grammar for Kids Books 1–3 (strongly recommended — provides the foundation of terms, propositions, and the four types)
- Or a solid understanding of what terms, propositions, subjects, predicates, and the A/E/I/O types are

## Book 1: Propositions and Immediate Inference

**Subtitle:** The Foundation of Correct Reasoning

### Chapter 1: From Grammar to Logic

**Core concepts:** What logic is, how it builds on Grammar, truth and validity, the scope of logic

- 1.1 — What Is Logic?
  - Grammar teaches how meaning is structured — terms, relationships, propositions. Logic takes the next step: given propositions, what can you correctly conclude from them? Logic is the art of correct reasoning. It's the set of rules that tells you when a conclusion actually follows from what came before.
- 1.2 — Truth vs. Validity
  - Truth is about whether a proposition matches reality. "Snow is white" is true. Validity is about whether the reasoning is correct — whether the conclusion follows from the premises. An argument can be valid but have a false premise. An argument can have true premises but be invalid. Logic cares about validity — the structure of reasoning. Truth comes from material logic (Book 4).
- 1.3 — Why Logic Matters
  - Without logic, you have no way to check whether a conclusion actually follows from the evidence. You're left with "it feels right" or "that sounds convincing." Logic gives you a standard: does this conclusion follow necessarily from these premises? If not, the argument fails, no matter how persuasive it sounds.
- 1.4 — The Three Acts of the Mind
  - The classical tradition identifies three acts of the mind that logic studies: simple apprehension (grasping a concept — covered in Grammar), judgment (affirming or denying something about a concept — propositions), and reasoning (drawing conclusions from propositions — the syllogism). This book covers judgment. Books 2–4 cover reasoning.

**Test:** 4 questions on what logic is, truth vs. validity, why logic matters, and the three acts of the mind.

### Chapter 2: Terms in Logic

**Core concepts:** Review of terms for logical purposes, distribution, categorematic vs. syncategorematic

- 2.1 — Terms as Building Blocks of Reasoning
  - In Grammar, you learned what terms are. In Logic, terms have a specific role: they're the parts of propositions that we reason about. The term "dog" in "All dogs are animals" functions differently from "dog" in "Some dogs are brown." Logic needs to be precise about how terms function within propositions.
- 2.2 — Categorematic and Syncategorematic Terms
  - Categorematic terms signify things by themselves — "dog," "animal," "large." Syncategorematic terms only have meaning in combination with other terms — "all," "some," "no," "not," "and," "if." In logic, syncategorematic terms are crucial because they determine the quantity, quality, and structure of propositions. "All" and "some" change everything.
- 2.3 — Distribution: A Key Concept
  - A term is distributed when it refers to every member of its class. In "All dogs are animals," "dogs" is distributed — we're talking about every dog. "Animals" is not distributed — we're not saying dogs are every animal. Distribution is one of the most important concepts in logic because the rules of valid reasoning depend on it.
- 2.4 — Why Distribution Matters
  - Many logical errors come from treating an undistributed term as if it were distributed. If you know "All dogs are animals," you cannot conclude "All animals are dogs" — because "animals" was not distributed in the original proposition. Understanding distribution prevents some of the most common reasoning mistakes.

**Test:** 4 questions on terms in logic, categorematic vs. syncategorematic, distribution, and why distribution matters.

### Chapter 3: The Four Categorical Propositions

**Core concepts:** A, E, I, O in full detail, quality, quantity, standard form

- 3.1 — Standard Form
  - Every categorical proposition can be put into standard form: [Quantifier] [Subject] [Copula] [Predicate]. "All dogs are animals." "No cats are dogs." "Some birds are blue." "Some birds are not blue." Standard form makes the logical structure visible and allows us to apply rules precisely.
- 3.2 — The A Proposition: Universal Affirmative
  - "All S is P." All dogs are animals. This claims that every member of the subject class is a member of the predicate class. The subject (S) is distributed — we're talking about all of them. The predicate (P) is not distributed — we're not saying they're all the animals there are.
- 3.3 — The E Proposition: Universal Negative
  - "No S is P." No dogs are cats. This claims that not a single member of the subject class is a member of the predicate class. Both terms are distributed — we're saying every dog is excluded from the entire cat class, and every cat is excluded from the entire dog class.
- 3.4 — The I and O Propositions
  - I: "Some S is P." Some birds are blue. At least one member of S is a member of P. Neither term is distributed — we're not talking about all birds or all blue things. O: "Some S is not P." Some birds are not blue. At least one member of S is not a member of P. The predicate is distributed (we're excluding from the entire predicate class), but the subject is not.

**Test:** 4 questions on standard form, the four types, distribution in each type, and putting propositions into standard form.

### Chapter 4: Distribution in Detail

**Core concepts:** Distribution rules for all four types, why the pattern exists, practice identifying

- 4.1 — The Distribution Pattern
  - A: Subject distributed, predicate not. E: Both distributed. I: Neither distributed. O: Subject not, predicate distributed. This pattern must be memorized because the rules of valid syllogisms depend entirely on it. The pattern isn't arbitrary — it follows from what each proposition claims.
- 4.2 — Why A Distributes Only the Subject
  - "All dogs are animals" talks about every dog (distributed) but doesn't claim dogs are all the animals (predicate not distributed). You're placing the entire subject class inside the predicate class, but the predicate class may be larger. Visualize it: the dog circle is entirely inside the animal circle, but the animal circle is bigger.
- 4.3 — Why E Distributes Both
  - "No dogs are cats" separates the entire subject class from the entire predicate class. Every dog is excluded from every cat, and every cat is excluded from every dog. Both circles are completely separate. Both terms are fully accounted for — hence both distributed.
- 4.4 — Why O Distributes Only the Predicate
  - "Some birds are not blue." We're saying at least one bird is excluded from the entire class of blue things. The subject isn't distributed (we're not talking about all birds). But the predicate is — we're saying this bird is outside the entire predicate class. This is the most counterintuitive distribution rule, and the most important to understand.

**Test:** 4 questions on the distribution pattern, explaining why each type distributes as it does, and identifying distribution in examples.

### Chapter 5: The Square of Opposition

**Core concepts:** Contradictory, contrary, subcontrary, subalternation, relationships between A/E/I/O

- 5.1 — What Is the Square of Opposition?
  - The square of opposition is a diagram showing how the four proposition types relate to each other when they share the same subject and predicate. "All dogs are animals" (A) and "Some dogs are not animals" (O) can't both be true — they're contradictories. The square maps all these relationships.
- 5.2 — Contradictories: A vs. O, E vs. I
  - Contradictories are diagonally opposite on the square. A and O: "All dogs are animals" vs. "Some dogs are not animals." E and I: "No dogs are animals" vs. "Some dogs are animals." Contradictories always have opposite truth values — if one is true, the other is false, and vice versa. Always. No exceptions.
- 5.3 — Contraries: A vs. E
  - Contraries are the two universals. "All dogs are animals" and "No dogs are animals." They cannot both be true (at least one must be false), but they can both be false. If some dogs are animals and some aren't, both the A and E are false. Contraries can't both be true, but they can both be false.
- 5.4 — Subcontraries and Subalternation
  - Subcontraries (I and O) can both be true but cannot both be false — at least one must be true. Subalternation: if the universal is true, the particular below it must also be true. If A is true ("All dogs are animals"), then I must be true ("Some dogs are animals"). But not the reverse — I being true doesn't prove A.

**Test:** 4 questions on contradictories, contraries, subcontraries, and subalternation.

### Chapter 6: Using the Square of Opposition

**Core concepts:** Drawing inferences from the square, practice with real propositions, existential import

- 6.1 — Drawing Inferences
  - If you know one proposition on the square is true or false, you can immediately determine the truth value of others. If A is true: E is false (contraries), I is true (subalternation), O is false (contradictory). If A is false: O is true (contradictory), but E and I are undetermined. Practice these chains.
- 6.2 — Starting from Each Corner
  - If E is true: A is false, O is true, I is false. If I is true: E is false, but A and O are undetermined. If O is true: A is false, but E and I are undetermined. Each starting point gives you a different set of inferences. Mastering all four starting points — for both true and false — gives you full command of the square.
- 6.3 — Practice with Real Propositions
  - "All students passed the test" is true. What follows? "No students passed" is false. "Some students passed" is true. "Some students did not pass" is false. Now: "Some students did not pass" is true. What follows? "All students passed" is false. That's all we can determine — the E and I are undetermined.
- 6.4 — Existential Import
  - Does "All unicorns are magical" assume unicorns exist? In traditional logic, universal propositions imply that the subject exists (existential import). In modern logic, they don't. This matters because subalternation only works with existential import. We follow the traditional interpretation but note the distinction.

**Test:** 4 questions on drawing inferences from the square, starting from different corners, real examples, and existential import.

### Chapter 7: Conversion

**Core concepts:** What conversion is, simple conversion, conversion per accidens, which types convert

- 7.1 — What Is Conversion?
  - Conversion is switching the subject and predicate of a proposition. "No dogs are cats" becomes "No cats are dogs." Conversion is an immediate inference — you derive a new proposition from a single given proposition. But not every conversion is valid. The rules depend on the proposition type.
- 7.2 — Simple Conversion: E and I
  - E converts simply: "No dogs are cats" → "No cats are dogs." Valid. I converts simply: "Some dogs are brown" → "Some brown things are dogs." Valid. In both cases, you can swap subject and predicate without changing the truth value. Both terms have the same distribution status before and after.
- 7.3 — Conversion Per Accidens: A
  - A converts by limitation (per accidens): "All dogs are animals" → "Some animals are dogs." You must change the quantity from universal to particular. Why? Because "animals" is not distributed in the original A proposition, so you can't make it a distributed universal subject. You can only say "some."
- 7.4 — O Does Not Convert
  - "Some animals are not dogs" does NOT validly become "Some dogs are not animals." The original is true, but the conversion is false. O propositions cannot be converted at all. This is important to memorize: E and I convert simply, A converts per accidens, O does not convert.

**Test:** 4 questions on what conversion is, simple conversion, conversion per accidens, and why O doesn't convert.

### Chapter 8: Obversion

**Core concepts:** What obversion is, changing quality and complementing the predicate, all four types

- 8.1 — What Is Obversion?
  - Obversion changes the quality of a proposition (affirmative to negative or vice versa) and replaces the predicate with its complement (its opposite). "All dogs are animals" becomes "No dogs are non-animals." The meaning is preserved — the same thing is said in a different way.
- 8.2 — The Complement
  - The complement of a term is everything outside that term's class. The complement of "animal" is "non-animal" (everything that isn't an animal). The complement of "blue" is "non-blue." Complements divide the universe into two: the class and everything outside it.
- 8.3 — Obversion of All Four Types
  - A → E: "All S is P" → "No S is non-P." E → A: "No S is P" → "All S is non-P." I → O: "Some S is P" → "Some S is not non-P." O → I: "Some S is not P" → "Some S is non-P." All four types can be validly obverted. This is the only immediate inference that works for all four.
- 8.4 — Practice: Obversion with Real Propositions
  - "All birds are animals" → "No birds are non-animals." True. "No fish are mammals" → "All fish are non-mammals." True. "Some students are athletes" → "Some students are not non-athletes." True. "Some students are not athletes" → "Some students are non-athletes." True. Obversion always works.

**Test:** 4 questions on obversion, complements, obverting each type, and practicing with real propositions.

### Chapter 9: Contraposition

**Core concepts:** What contraposition is, which types have valid contrapositives, combining conversion and obversion

- 9.1 — What Is Contraposition?
  - Contraposition switches the subject and predicate AND replaces both with their complements. "All dogs are animals" → "All non-animals are non-dogs." If something isn't an animal, it can't be a dog. Contraposition combines the moves of conversion and obversion.
- 9.2 — Contraposition of A and O
  - A contraposes validly: "All S is P" → "All non-P is non-S." "All dogs are animals" → "All non-animals are non-dogs." True. O contraposes validly: "Some S is not P" → "Some non-P is not non-S." "Some animals are not dogs" → "Some non-dogs are not non-animals." True.
- 9.3 — E and I Do Not Contrapose
  - E does not contrapose validly. "No dogs are cats" → "No non-cats are non-dogs" is false (plenty of non-cats are non-dogs). I does not contrapose validly either. This must be memorized: A and O contrapose, E and I do not.
- 9.4 — The Full Picture of Immediate Inferences
  - Conversion: E and I simply, A per accidens, O not at all. Obversion: all four types. Contraposition: A and O, not E and I. Each immediate inference has its own rules. Together they let you derive multiple new propositions from any single given proposition — expanding what you know from a single truth.

**Test:** 4 questions on contraposition, which types contrapose, why E and I fail, and the complete picture of all three inferences.

### Chapter 10: Combining Immediate Inferences

**Core concepts:** Chains of inferences, deriving multiple propositions from one, practice

- 10.1 — Chaining Inferences Together
  - You can chain immediate inferences. Start with an A proposition, obvert it to get an E, convert that E to get another E, obvert again to get an A — each step valid, each producing a new proposition you know is true. Chains extend your knowledge from a single starting point.
- 10.2 — All the Equivalences for A
  - Start: "All dogs are animals" (A). Obvert: "No dogs are non-animals" (E). Convert: "No non-animals are dogs" (E). Obvert: "All non-animals are non-dogs" (A). Contrapose the original: "All non-animals are non-dogs" (A). Multiple paths, same destination. All of these are logically equivalent.
- 10.3 — All the Equivalences for E
  - Start: "No dogs are cats" (E). Convert: "No cats are dogs" (E). Obvert original: "All dogs are non-cats" (A). Obvert the conversion: "All cats are non-dogs" (A). Each step gives you a new true proposition derived from the original.
- 10.4 — Practice: Deriving Everything You Can
  - Given "All squares are rectangles" — derive every valid immediate inference you can. Conversion per accidens: "Some rectangles are squares." Obversion: "No squares are non-rectangles." Contraposition: "All non-rectangles are non-squares." Then chain further. How many true propositions can you derive from one?

**Test:** 4 questions on chaining inferences, deriving equivalences, and practice generating all valid inferences from a given proposition.

### Chapter 11: Euler Diagrams

**Core concepts:** Visualizing propositions with circles, testing relationships, visual thinking about logic

- 11.1 — What Are Euler Diagrams?
  - Euler diagrams use circles to represent classes. "All dogs are animals" — draw the dog circle entirely inside the animal circle. "No dogs are cats" — draw two separate circles. "Some dogs are brown" — draw the circles overlapping. Euler diagrams make logical relationships visible.
- 11.2 — Diagramming Each Proposition Type
  - A: S circle inside P circle. E: S and P circles completely separate. I: S and P circles overlapping. O: Part of S circle outside P circle. Each diagram shows exactly what the proposition claims — no more, no less.
- 11.3 — Using Diagrams to Test Inferences
  - Does "All dogs are animals" mean "All animals are dogs"? Draw it: dog circle inside animal circle. Clearly animals extend beyond dogs. The inference fails — the diagram shows it instantly. Euler diagrams are a visual validity check.
- 11.4 — Limits of Diagrams
  - Euler diagrams are helpful for simple cases but get complicated with three or more terms. They're a thinking tool, not a replacement for the rules. Use them to build intuition, but rely on the formal rules for complex reasoning. The rules work where diagrams get messy.

**Test:** 4 questions on drawing Euler diagrams, diagramming each type, testing an inference visually, and recognizing limits.

### Chapter 12: Propositions in Ordinary Language

**Core concepts:** Translating everyday statements into standard form, hidden quantifiers, non-standard structures

- 12.1 — Everyday Language Is Messy
  - People don't speak in standard form. "Dogs are loyal" — is that A (all dogs) or I (some dogs)? "Only adults can vote" — what's the subject and predicate? Before you can apply logical rules, you need to translate ordinary language into standard form. This is a skill that takes practice.
- 12.2 — Hidden Quantifiers
  - "Fish swim" probably means "All fish swim" (A). "Students complained" probably means "Some students complained" (I). "Nobody came" means "No people came" (E). The quantifier is implied, and choosing the right one changes the logic entirely. Pay attention to what the speaker intends.
- 12.3 — "Only" and "None But"
  - "Only adults can vote" does NOT mean "All adults can vote." It means "All voters are adults" — the subject and predicate flip. "Only" and "none but" reverse the terms. This is one of the most common translation mistakes. "Only S is P" = "All P is S."
- 12.4 — Exclusive, Exceptive, and Other Tricky Forms
  - "All except seniors are required to attend" — this combines two propositions: "All non-seniors are required" AND "No seniors are required." "Not all birds fly" = "Some birds do not fly" (O, not E). Translating these correctly is essential. A mistranslation leads to a wrong logical form, which leads to wrong conclusions.

**Test:** 4 questions on translating everyday language into standard form, hidden quantifiers, "only" propositions, and tricky forms.

### Chapter 13: Logical Relationships Between Propositions

**Core concepts:** Equivalence, consistency, inconsistency, independence, logical relationships beyond the square

- 13.1 — Equivalence
  - Two propositions are logically equivalent if they always have the same truth value. "All dogs are animals" and "No dogs are non-animals" (its obverse) are equivalent. Equivalent propositions say the same thing in different ways. Every valid immediate inference produces an equivalent proposition.
- 13.2 — Consistency and Inconsistency
  - Propositions are consistent if they can all be true at the same time. "All dogs are animals" and "Some dogs are brown" are consistent. Propositions are inconsistent if they can't all be true at the same time. "All dogs are brown" and "Some dogs are not brown" are inconsistent (they're contradictories).
- 13.3 — Independence
  - Propositions are independent if the truth of one doesn't determine the truth of the other. "All dogs are animals" and "All cats are mammals" are independent — knowing one tells you nothing about the other. Most propositions in the world are independent of each other.
- 13.4 — Testing Relationships
  - Given a set of propositions, can you determine whether they're consistent? Equivalent? Independent? This is the skill of analyzing logical relationships — and it's the skill you'll use most often in evaluating arguments, contracts, laws, and scientific claims.

**Test:** 4 questions on equivalence, consistency, inconsistency, and independence.

### Chapter 14: Complex Propositions in Logic

**Core concepts:** Conjunctions, disjunctions, conditionals, and negation reviewed for logical rigor

- 14.1 — Compound Propositions Revisited
  - Grammar Book 3 introduced compound propositions. In Logic, we need to be more precise. "P and Q" (conjunction) is true only when both parts are true. "P or Q" (disjunction) is true when at least one part is true. These truth conditions are exact and must be applied strictly.
- 14.2 — The Conditional Proposition
  - "If P then Q." The conditional is true in every case except when P is true and Q is false. This means "If pigs fly, then the moon is cheese" is technically true — because the antecedent is false. This feels strange, but it's logically correct. The conditional only makes a claim about what happens when P is true.
- 14.3 — Negating Compound Propositions
  - The negation of "P and Q" is "not-P or not-Q" (at least one is false). The negation of "P or Q" is "not-P and not-Q" (both are false). The negation of "If P then Q" is "P and not-Q" (the antecedent is true but the consequent is false). These rules matter for evaluating complex arguments.
- 14.4 — Building Toward the Syllogism
  - You now have the complete foundation: terms, propositions, distribution, the square of opposition, immediate inferences, and compound propositions. Everything from here — the syllogism and beyond — builds on exactly these concepts. The foundation is in place.

**Test:** 4 questions on truth conditions for compound propositions, the conditional, negation of compounds, and connecting to the syllogism.

### Chapter 15: Review and Preparation

**Core concepts:** Full review, the logical toolkit so far, looking ahead to the syllogism

- 15.1 — The Toolkit So Far
  - Review: terms in logic, distribution, the four proposition types (A, E, I, O), quality and quantity, the square of opposition (contradictory, contrary, subcontrary, subalternation), conversion, obversion, contraposition, Euler diagrams, translating ordinary language, logical relationships, compound propositions.
- 15.2 — What You Can Do Now
  - You can take any proposition, put it in standard form, identify its type, determine which terms are distributed, find its contradictory/contrary/subcontrary/subaltern, derive its converse, obverse, and contrapositive (where valid), diagram it, and combine multiple propositions to test consistency. That's a powerful toolkit.
- 15.3 — From One Proposition to Two: The Syllogism
  - So far, you've worked with individual propositions or pairs on the square. The syllogism combines two propositions (premises) to derive a third (the conclusion). This is where logic gets its real power — deriving new knowledge from existing knowledge. That's Book 2.
- 15.4 — The Importance of What You've Learned
  - Every rule of the syllogism depends on distribution. Every test of validity uses the proposition types. Every fallacy exploits a violation of the principles you've just learned. This book isn't just preparation — it's the foundation that everything else stands on. Master this, and the rest follows.

**Test:** 4 questions reviewing key concepts, applying multiple tools to a proposition, and connecting to the syllogism.

## Book 2: The Syllogism

**Subtitle:** The Art of Deductive Reasoning

### Chapter 1: What Is a Syllogism?

**Core concepts:** Two premises and a conclusion, major/minor/middle terms, the structure

- 1.1 — The Basic Structure
  - A syllogism is an argument with exactly two premises and one conclusion. "All dogs are animals. All animals are living things. Therefore, all dogs are living things." Two premises, one conclusion. The conclusion follows necessarily from the premises — if the premises are true and the form is valid, the conclusion must be true.
- 1.2 — The Three Terms
  - Every categorical syllogism has exactly three terms, each appearing twice. The major term (P) is the predicate of the conclusion. The minor term (S) is the subject of the conclusion. The middle term (M) appears in both premises but not in the conclusion. The middle term is the link that connects the other two.
- 1.3 — Major and Minor Premises
  - The major premise contains the major term (P) and the middle term (M). The minor premise contains the minor term (S) and the middle term (M). The conclusion contains the minor term (S) and the major term (P). Always identify the conclusion first, then the terms, then label the premises.
- 1.4 — Why Syllogisms Matter
  - The syllogism is the fundamental unit of deductive reasoning. All complex arguments can be broken down into chains of syllogisms. If each syllogism in the chain is valid, the overall argument is valid. Understanding the syllogism means understanding the mechanics of all deductive reasoning.

**Test:** 4 questions on the structure, the three terms, major and minor premises, and why syllogisms matter.

### Chapter 2: The Four Figures

**Core concepts:** Position of the middle term defines the figure, all four figures, recognizing them

- 2.1 — What Determines the Figure?
  - The figure of a syllogism is determined by the position of the middle term in the two premises. Since the middle term can be subject or predicate in each premise, there are four possible arrangements — four figures.
- 2.2 — Figure 1: M-P, S-M
  - Middle term is subject of the major premise and predicate of the minor premise. "All animals (M) are living things (P). All dogs (S) are animals (M). Therefore, all dogs (S) are living things (P)." Figure 1 is considered the most natural and has the most valid moods.
- 2.3 — Figure 2: P-M, S-M
  - Middle term is predicate in both premises. "All cats (P) are animals (M). No rocks (S) are animals (M). Therefore, no rocks (S) are cats (P)." Figure 2 is useful for proving negative conclusions.
- 2.4 — Figures 3 and 4
  - Figure 3: middle term is subject in both premises (M-P, M-S). Only produces particular conclusions. Figure 4: middle term is predicate of the major and subject of the minor (P-M, M-S). The most unusual arrangement. Each figure has its own set of valid moods.

**Test:** 4 questions on what determines the figure, identifying the figure of a given syllogism, and the four arrangements.

### Chapter 3: Mood

**Core concepts:** What mood is, naming moods with A/E/I/O, mood + figure = specific form

- 3.1 — What Is Mood?
  - The mood of a syllogism is the combination of proposition types in order: major premise, minor premise, conclusion. A syllogism with an A major premise, an A minor premise, and an A conclusion has the mood AAA. A syllogism with E, A, E has the mood EAE.
- 3.2 — Naming Moods
  - Each mood gets a three-letter name from its proposition types. AAA, EAE, AII, EIO, and so on. There are 64 possible combinations of three proposition types (4 × 4 × 4), but most of them are invalid. Only certain moods produce valid syllogisms, and which ones depend on the figure.
- 3.3 — Mood + Figure = Complete Form
  - To fully identify a syllogism's form, you need both its mood and its figure. AAA-1 (mood AAA in figure 1) is valid. AAA-2 is not. The same mood can be valid in one figure and invalid in another. Mood alone doesn't determine validity — you need both.
- 3.4 — The Traditional Names
  - The valid moods have traditional Latin names: Barbara (AAA-1), Celarent (EAE-1), Darii (AII-1), Ferio (EIO-1), Cesare (EAE-2), Camestres (AEE-2), and so on. The vowels in each name tell you the mood. These names have been used for over 700 years.

**Test:** 4 questions on what mood is, naming moods, mood + figure, and the traditional Latin names.

### Chapter 4: Rules of Validity

**Core concepts:** The rules that separate valid from invalid syllogisms, distribution rules, quality rules, quantity rules

- 4.1 — Why Rules?
  - Instead of memorizing every valid mood, you can apply rules. If a syllogism follows all the rules, it's valid. If it breaks any rule, it's invalid. The rules are based on distribution and the relationships between proposition types. There are a small number of rules, and they cover every case.
- 4.2 — Distribution Rules
  - Rule 1: The middle term must be distributed at least once. If the middle term is never distributed, the premises don't fully connect — the argument commits the fallacy of the undistributed middle. Rule 2: Any term distributed in the conclusion must be distributed in its premise. Otherwise you're claiming more in the conclusion than the premises support.
- 4.3 — Quality Rules
  - Rule 3: If both premises are negative, no valid conclusion follows. Two negatives tell you S and P are each excluded from M, but not how S and P relate to each other. Rule 4: If one premise is negative, the conclusion must be negative. And if both premises are affirmative, the conclusion must be affirmative.
- 4.4 — Quantity Rules
  - Rule 5: If one premise is particular, the conclusion must be particular. You can't derive a universal conclusion from a particular premise. Rule 6: From two particular premises, no valid conclusion follows. You need at least one universal premise to make the middle term do its connecting work.

**Test:** 4 questions on the distribution rules, quality rules, quantity rules, and applying them to test a syllogism.

### Chapter 5: The Valid Moods of Figure 1

**Core concepts:** Barbara, Celarent, Darii, Ferio — the four valid moods of the first figure

- 5.1 — Barbara (AAA-1)
  - All M is P. All S is M. Therefore, all S is P. "All mammals are animals. All dogs are mammals. Therefore, all dogs are animals." The most straightforward and intuitive valid syllogism. Two universal affirmatives producing a universal affirmative.
- 5.2 — Celarent (EAE-1)
  - No M is P. All S is M. Therefore, no S is P. "No reptiles are mammals. All snakes are reptiles. Therefore, no snakes are mammals." A universal negative major and universal affirmative minor producing a universal negative conclusion.
- 5.3 — Darii (AII-1)
  - All M is P. Some S is M. Therefore, some S is P. "All mammals are animals. Some pets are mammals. Therefore, some pets are animals." A universal major and particular minor producing a particular conclusion.
- 5.4 — Ferio (EIO-1)
  - No M is P. Some S is M. Therefore, some S is not P. "No reptiles are mammals. Some pets are reptiles. Therefore, some pets are not mammals." Each of these four moods can be verified against the rules — all pass.

**Test:** 4 questions on each of the four moods, testing validity with the rules, and constructing examples.

### Chapter 6: The Valid Moods of Figures 2, 3, and 4

**Core concepts:** All remaining valid moods, organized by figure

- 6.1 — Figure 2: Cesare, Camestres, Festino, Baroco
  - Figure 2 requires at least one negative premise and always produces a negative conclusion. Cesare (EAE-2), Camestres (AEE-2), Festino (EIO-2), Baroco (AOO-2). These are useful for proving that something does NOT belong to a class.
- 6.2 — Figure 3: Darapti, Disamis, Datisi, Felapton, Bocardo, Ferison
  - Figure 3 always produces particular conclusions. Darapti (AAI-3), Disamis (IAI-3), Datisi (AII-3), Felapton (EAO-3), Bocardo (OAO-3), Ferison (EIO-3). These are useful when you can only prove "some."
- 6.3 — Figure 4: Bramantip, Camenes, Dimaris, Fesapo, Fresison
  - Figure 4 is the least intuitive. Bramantip (AAI-4), Camenes (AEE-4), Dimaris (IAI-4), Fesapo (EAO-4), Fresison (EIO-4). These are valid but rarely encountered in natural reasoning.
- 6.4 — The Complete List
  - Across all four figures, there are 15 valid moods (or more, depending on whether you count weakened forms). These 15 forms are the only valid categorical syllogisms that exist. Any categorical argument that doesn't match one of these forms is invalid.

**Test:** 4 questions on valid moods by figure, identifying the figure and mood of a given syllogism, and why only 15 are valid.

### Chapter 7: Testing Syllogisms for Validity

**Core concepts:** Step-by-step method for testing any syllogism, practice with valid and invalid examples

- 7.1 — The Testing Procedure
  - Step 1: Identify the conclusion (the claim being made). Step 2: Identify the three terms (major, minor, middle). Step 3: Identify the mood (proposition types in order). Step 4: Identify the figure (position of the middle term). Step 5: Check against the rules of validity. If any rule is broken, the syllogism is invalid.
- 7.2 — Testing a Valid Syllogism
  - "All mammals are warm-blooded. All dogs are mammals. Therefore, all dogs are warm-blooded." Conclusion: All dogs (S) are warm-blooded (P). Middle term: mammals. Mood: AAA. Figure: 1. This is Barbara. Check rules: middle term distributed in major premise (yes). No term distributed in conclusion that isn't distributed in its premise (check). Valid.
- 7.3 — Testing an Invalid Syllogism
  - "All dogs are animals. All cats are animals. Therefore, all cats are dogs." Conclusion: All cats (S) are dogs (P). Middle term: animals. Mood: AAA. Figure: 2. Check rules: is the middle term distributed? "Animals" is the predicate of both premises (A type — predicate not distributed in either). Undistributed middle. Invalid.
- 7.4 — Practice: Valid or Invalid?
  - Work through several syllogisms, applying the testing procedure step by step. Some will be valid, some will violate one or more rules. The goal is to make the testing procedure automatic — so you can evaluate any syllogism quickly and accurately.

**Test:** 4 questions requiring step-by-step testing of syllogisms for validity.

### Chapter 8: Reducing to the First Figure

**Core concepts:** Why reduction works, how to reduce syllogisms from other figures, the primacy of Figure 1

- 8.1 — The Primacy of Figure 1
  - Figure 1 is considered the most natural form of reasoning. The other figures can be converted into Figure 1 by applying immediate inferences (conversion, obversion, contraposition) to the premises. If the reduced form is a valid Figure 1 mood, the original was valid.
- 8.2 — The Hint in the Names
  - The traditional names encode reduction instructions. The initial letter tells you which Figure 1 mood it reduces to (B→Barbara, C→Celarent, D→Darii, F→Ferio). The letters 's', 'p', 'c' after a vowel tell you to convert simply, convert per accidens, or contrapose that premise. 'm' means switch the premises.
- 8.3 — Reducing Cesare (EAE-2) to Celarent
  - Cesare: No P is M. All S is M. Therefore no S is P. The name starts with C (→ Celarent). The 's' after E means convert the E premise simply: "No M is P." Now it's: No M is P. All S is M. → Celarent (EAE-1). Valid.
- 8.4 — Why Reduction Matters
  - Reduction proves that all valid syllogisms are ultimately grounded in the same basic logic — Figure 1. It also gives you a method for verifying any syllogism: reduce it and check. The entire system is unified.

**Test:** 4 questions on why Figure 1 is primary, decoding the traditional names, reducing a syllogism, and why reduction matters.

### Chapter 9: Modus Ponens and Modus Tollens

**Core concepts:** Hypothetical syllogisms, affirming the antecedent, denying the consequent

- 9.1 — The Hypothetical Syllogism
  - Not all arguments use categorical propositions. Many use conditionals. "If it rains, the ground gets wet. It is raining. Therefore, the ground is wet." This is a hypothetical syllogism — it uses an "if...then" proposition as a premise. Different structure from categorical syllogisms, different rules.
- 9.2 — Modus Ponens: Affirming the Antecedent
  - If P then Q. P is true. Therefore Q is true. This is always valid. "If you study, you'll pass. You studied. Therefore, you'll pass." The antecedent (the 'if' part) is affirmed, so the consequent (the 'then' part) must follow.
- 9.3 — Modus Tollens: Denying the Consequent
  - If P then Q. Q is false. Therefore P is false. This is always valid. "If you study, you'll pass. You didn't pass. Therefore, you didn't study." If the consequent is false, the antecedent must be false (otherwise the consequent would be true).
- 9.4 — The Power of These Two Forms
  - Modus ponens and modus tollens are the two valid forms of hypothetical reasoning. Together they account for a huge amount of everyday reasoning. "If the battery is dead, the car won't start. The car won't start. Therefore the battery is dead" — wait, is that valid? (It's not — that's affirming the consequent, a fallacy we'll cover in Book 3.)

**Test:** 4 questions on hypothetical syllogisms, modus ponens, modus tollens, and distinguishing valid from invalid forms.

### Chapter 10: Disjunctive and Conditional Syllogisms

**Core concepts:** Disjunctive syllogism, pure hypothetical syllogism, constructive/destructive dilemmas

- 10.1 — The Disjunctive Syllogism
  - P or Q. Not P. Therefore Q. If one option is eliminated, the other must be true. "It's either raining or snowing. It's not raining. Therefore it's snowing." Valid — as long as the disjunction is genuine (at least one must be true).
- 10.2 — The Pure Hypothetical Syllogism
  - If P then Q. If Q then R. Therefore, if P then R. This chains conditionals. "If it rains, the ground is wet. If the ground is wet, the game is cancelled. Therefore, if it rains, the game is cancelled." Each link in the chain must be valid for the whole chain to hold.
- 10.3 — The Constructive Dilemma
  - If P then Q, and if R then S. P or R. Therefore Q or S. "If you study math, you'll learn math. If you study science, you'll learn science. You're studying math or science. Therefore you'll learn math or science." Dilemmas combine conditionals with disjunctions.
- 10.4 — The Destructive Dilemma
  - If P then Q, and if R then S. Not Q or not S. Therefore not P or not R. The reverse of the constructive dilemma. Both forms are valid and commonly used in complex arguments.

**Test:** 4 questions on disjunctive syllogism, pure hypothetical syllogism, and constructive/destructive dilemmas.

### Chapter 11: Enthymemes

**Core concepts:** What an enthymeme is, finding the missing premise, why they're common, testing them

- 11.1 — What Is an Enthymeme?
  - An enthymeme is a syllogism with one premise missing — left unstated because the speaker assumes it's obvious. "Socrates is mortal because he's a man." The missing premise: "All men are mortal." Most real-world arguments are enthymemes. People rarely state all their premises explicitly.
- 11.2 — Finding the Missing Premise
  - To evaluate an enthymeme, supply the missing premise. Identify the conclusion, identify the stated premise, figure out what's missing. "You should bring an umbrella — it's going to rain." Missing: "If it's going to rain, you should bring an umbrella." Once supplied, you can test the full argument for validity.
- 11.3 — First, Second, and Third Order Enthymemes
  - First order: the major premise is missing. Second order: the minor premise is missing. Third order: the conclusion is missing (the speaker implies the conclusion without stating it). Each type requires you to identify what's been left out and whether, once supplied, the argument is valid.
- 11.4 — Why Enthymemes Matter
  - Almost every argument you encounter in daily life is an enthymeme. Editorials, conversations, advertisements, political speeches — they all leave premises unstated. The skill of identifying and evaluating the missing premise is one of the most practically useful skills in logic.

**Test:** 4 questions on identifying enthymemes, supplying missing premises, the three orders, and evaluating real-world enthymemes.

### Chapter 12: Sorites and Polysyllogisms

**Core concepts:** Chains of syllogisms, sorites, epicheirema, extended arguments

- 12.1 — What Is a Polysyllogism?
  - A polysyllogism is a chain of syllogisms where the conclusion of one becomes a premise of the next. "All A is B. All B is C. All C is D. Therefore all A is D." This is really two syllogisms linked together: A-B-C and A-C-D. Complex arguments are usually chains of simpler arguments.
- 12.2 — The Sorites
  - A sorites is a compressed chain where intermediate conclusions are left out. "All A is B. All B is C. All C is D. Therefore all A is D." You could break this into: "All A is B. All B is C. Therefore all A is C" (first syllogism), then "All A is C. All C is D. Therefore all A is D" (second). The sorites skips the intermediate conclusion.
- 12.3 — Testing a Sorites
  - To test a sorites, break it into individual syllogisms and test each one. If any link in the chain is invalid, the whole argument fails. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link.
- 12.4 — The Epicheirema
  - An epicheirema is a syllogism where one or both premises are themselves supported by reasons. "All mammals are warm-blooded (because they regulate their body temperature). All dogs are mammals. Therefore all dogs are warm-blooded." The parenthetical adds support for the premise. Complex arguments often have this nested structure.

**Test:** 4 questions on polysyllogisms, sorites, testing a chain, and the epicheirema.

### Chapter 13: Translating Real Arguments into Syllogistic Form

**Core concepts:** Finding the argument in natural language, identifying premises and conclusion, handling complexity

- 13.1 — Arguments Don't Come in Standard Form
  - Real arguments are messy. The conclusion might come first, last, or in the middle. Premises might be embedded in stories. Extra words, digressions, and rhetorical flourishes obscure the logical structure. Your job is to extract the skeleton: what's being claimed and what's offered as support.
- 13.2 — Indicator Words
  - Conclusion indicators: "therefore," "so," "thus," "consequently," "it follows that," "which means." Premise indicators: "because," "since," "given that," "for," "the reason is." These words signal the structure of the argument. Learn to spot them.
- 13.3 — Extracting the Syllogism
  - Read the passage. Find the conclusion (what's being argued for). Find the premises (what's offered as evidence). Identify the terms. Put everything in standard form. Check for missing premises (enthymemes). Now you can evaluate the argument using the rules.
- 13.4 — Practice: Real Arguments
  - Take real arguments from editorials, conversations, or debates. Extract the syllogisms. Test them. Are they valid? Are the premises true? This is where logic meets the real world — and where the skills you've learned become genuinely useful.

**Test:** 4 questions on extracting arguments from natural language, using indicator words, and evaluating real arguments.

### Chapter 14: The Limits of Deductive Logic

**Core concepts:** What deduction can and cannot do, validity vs. soundness, the need for true premises

- 14.1 — Valid but Unsound
  - A valid argument with a false premise can lead to a false conclusion. "All fish are mammals. All trout are fish. Therefore all trout are mammals." Perfectly valid. Completely wrong. Validity guarantees the conclusion follows from the premises. It doesn't guarantee the premises are true. A sound argument is valid AND has true premises.
- 14.2 — Deduction Doesn't Create New Information
  - Deduction only makes explicit what's already contained in the premises. "All dogs are animals" already contains the information that your specific dog is an animal. The syllogism makes this explicit, but it doesn't add new information from outside the premises. New information comes from observation, induction, and experience.
- 14.3 — Where Do Premises Come From?
  - Deductive logic tells you what follows from premises, but it doesn't tell you whether the premises are true. Where do premises come from? Experience, observation, induction, testimony, definition. Formal logic alone can't verify premises — that's the domain of material logic (Book 4).
- 14.4 — Looking Ahead
  - Formal logic gives you the tools to reason correctly from any set of premises. But correct reasoning from false premises gives you false conclusions. Material logic (Book 4) addresses the harder question: how do we know our premises are true? How do we move from observation to first principles? That's where logic connects to actual knowledge.

**Test:** 4 questions on validity vs. soundness, what deduction can't do, where premises come from, and connecting to material logic.

### Chapter 15: The Deductive Toolkit

**Core concepts:** Review of all deductive tools, the complete system, preparation for fallacies and material logic

- 15.1 — What You've Built
  - Review: the categorical syllogism (terms, figures, moods, validity rules), hypothetical syllogisms (modus ponens, modus tollens), disjunctive syllogisms, dilemmas, enthymemes, sorites, polysyllogisms, and translating real arguments. This is the complete toolkit of deductive reasoning.
- 15.2 — The System Is Unified
  - Every valid categorical syllogism reduces to Figure 1. Every hypothetical argument has exactly two valid forms (ponens and tollens). Every complex argument breaks down into a chain of simple ones. The system is elegant — a small number of rules covers infinite possible arguments.
- 15.3 — Recognizing Invalid Arguments
  - You now know what valid reasoning looks like. That means you can recognize when reasoning goes wrong — even when it sounds convincing. Book 3 catalogs the specific ways arguments fail (fallacies). But you already have the tools to test any argument. The fallacies are the names for the failures.
- 15.4 — From Form to Content
  - Books 1 and 2 covered formal logic — the structure of valid reasoning. Book 3 covers fallacies — the structure of invalid reasoning. Book 4 covers material logic — how we know premises are true and how we reason about the actual world. Form, failure, and content. The three faces of logic.

## Book 3: Fallacies

**Subtitle:** How Arguments Go Wrong

### Chapter 1: What Is a Fallacy?

**Core concepts:** Definition, formal vs. informal, why fallacies are persuasive, the goal of studying them

- 1.1 — What Is a Fallacy?
  - A fallacy is an error in reasoning that makes an argument invalid or misleading. Some fallacies break the formal rules of logic (formal fallacies). Others use psychologically persuasive tricks that bypass logic (informal fallacies). Both produce arguments that seem convincing but don't actually prove their conclusion.
- 1.2 — Formal vs. Informal Fallacies
  - A formal fallacy violates the structural rules of valid reasoning — like an undistributed middle term or affirming the consequent. The error is in the form. An informal fallacy has the right form but tricks you with the content — irrelevant evidence, ambiguous language, or hidden assumptions.
- 1.3 — Why Fallacies Are Persuasive
  - If fallacies were obviously wrong, nobody would fall for them. They work because they mimic good reasoning. They feel logical. They push emotional buttons. They exploit shortcuts in human thinking. The whole point of studying fallacies is to see through the illusion.
- 1.4 — Naming the Error
  - When you spot a bad argument, it's not enough to say "that's wrong." You should be able to say what's wrong — which specific error is being committed. Naming the fallacy gives you precision: "That's an ad hominem" or "That's equivocation" tells you and others exactly what went wrong.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a fallacy is, formal vs. informal, why they work, and why naming matters.

### Chapter 2: Formal Fallacies — Syllogistic

**Core concepts:** Undistributed middle, illicit major, illicit minor, four-term fallacy

- 2.1 — Undistributed Middle
  - "All dogs are animals. All cats are animals. Therefore all cats are dogs." The middle term (animals) is never distributed — it's the predicate of an A proposition in both premises. Without distributing the middle at least once, the premises don't connect. This is the most common formal fallacy.
- 2.2 — Illicit Major
  - "All dogs are animals. No cats are dogs. Therefore no cats are animals." The major term (animals) is distributed in the conclusion (E proposition — both terms distributed) but not in the major premise (A proposition — predicate not distributed). You can't distribute a term in the conclusion that wasn't distributed in its premise.
- 2.3 — Illicit Minor
  - "All dogs are animals. All dogs are pets. Therefore all pets are animals." The minor term (pets) is distributed in the conclusion (A — subject distributed) but not in the minor premise (A — predicate not distributed). Same rule, different term.
- 2.4 — The Four-Term Fallacy
  - A valid syllogism has exactly three terms. If a word is used with different meanings, it becomes two different terms — creating four terms. "All banks are financial institutions. All rivers have banks. Therefore all rivers have financial institutions." "Banks" means two different things. The middle term doesn't actually connect.

**Test:** 4 questions on undistributed middle, illicit major/minor, and the four-term fallacy with examples.

### Chapter 3: Formal Fallacies — Hypothetical

**Core concepts:** Affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, false conversion of conditionals

- 3.1 — Affirming the Consequent
  - "If it rains, the ground is wet. The ground is wet. Therefore it rained." Invalid. The ground could be wet for other reasons (sprinklers, spilled water). The consequent being true doesn't prove the antecedent. Only modus tollens (denying the consequent) gives a valid conclusion.
- 3.2 — Denying the Antecedent
  - "If it rains, the ground is wet. It didn't rain. Therefore the ground isn't wet." Invalid. The ground might be wet anyway. The antecedent being false doesn't prove the consequent is false. Only modus ponens (affirming the antecedent) gives a valid conclusion.
- 3.3 — Why These Are So Common
  - Both fallacies feel valid because of a natural confusion: people treat "If P then Q" as if it means "If Q then P" or "If not P then not Q." But a conditional only works in one direction. "If it rains, the ground is wet" does NOT mean "If the ground is wet, it rained." Mastering this distinction prevents a huge class of errors.
- 3.4 — Other Formal Errors
  - Exclusive premises (two negative premises in a syllogism), drawing an affirmative conclusion from a negative premise, drawing a universal conclusion from a particular premise. Each violates a specific rule from Book 2. Formal fallacies are systematic — they're violations of identified rules.

**Test:** 4 questions on affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, why they're common, and other formal errors.

### Chapter 4: Fallacies of Relevance — Part 1

**Core concepts:** Ad hominem, tu quoque, appeal to authority, appeal to force

- 4.1 — Ad Hominem: Attacking the Person
  - "You can't trust her argument about nutrition — she's overweight." Attacking the person making the argument instead of the argument itself. A person's character, appearance, or situation has nothing to do with whether their argument is logically valid. The argument stands or falls on its own merits.
- 4.2 — Tu Quoque: "You Do It Too"
  - "You say smoking is bad, but you smoke yourself!" This doesn't address whether smoking is bad — it just points out hypocrisy. Even a hypocrite can make a valid argument. Tu quoque is a special form of ad hominem that deflects by pointing the finger back.
- 4.3 — Appeal to Authority
  - "A famous actor says this vitamin works, so it must work." The actor isn't an expert in medicine. Appeal to authority cites someone's status instead of actual evidence. Authority is relevant only when the person is a genuine expert in the specific field being discussed — and even then, the argument should rest on evidence, not credentials.
- 4.4 — Appeal to Force (Ad Baculum)
  - "You should agree with me, or there will be consequences." This replaces evidence with threats. The conclusion doesn't follow from the threat — it's coercion disguised as argument. Power doesn't make an argument valid.

**Test:** 4 questions on ad hominem, tu quoque, appeal to authority, and appeal to force.

### Chapter 5: Fallacies of Relevance — Part 2

**Core concepts:** Appeal to pity, appeal to emotion, appeal to the people, red herring

- 5.1 — Appeal to Pity (Ad Misericordiam)
  - "I should get a good grade — I worked really hard and my dog just died." Hard work and sympathy are real, but they don't determine whether the answers are correct. Appeal to pity substitutes emotion for evidence. The question is whether the work meets the standard, not whether the person deserves sympathy.
- 5.2 — Appeal to Emotion
  - Using any strong emotion — fear, anger, excitement, disgust — to bypass logical evaluation. "Think of the children!" can be a legitimate concern or a way to shut down rational discussion. The question is whether the emotion is connected to actual evidence for the claim being made.
- 5.3 — Appeal to the People (Ad Populum)
  - "Everyone believes it, so it must be true." "Millions of people can't be wrong." Yes they can. Popularity is not evidence. The number of people who believe something has no bearing on whether it's logically valid. History is full of things "everyone" believed that turned out to be false.
- 5.4 — Red Herring
  - Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the actual argument. "We should discuss the school's budget problems." "But what about the sports program — the team had a great season!" The sports program is a red herring — it has nothing to do with the budget question. Red herrings change the subject without anyone noticing.

**Test:** 4 questions on appeal to pity, appeal to emotion, ad populum, and red herring.

### Chapter 6: Fallacies of Relevance — Part 3

**Core concepts:** Straw man, genetic fallacy, appeal to ignorance, irrelevant conclusion

- 6.1 — Straw Man
  - Misrepresenting someone's argument to make it easier to attack. "She thinks we should have bike lanes." "She wants to ban all cars!" The second statement misrepresents the first — it's attacking a weaker version (a "straw man") instead of the real argument. Always address the actual argument, not a distortion of it.
- 6.2 — Genetic Fallacy
  - Judging an argument by where it came from rather than by its logic. "That idea came from a political party I don't like, so it must be wrong." The origin of an idea doesn't determine its validity. Even a broken clock is right twice a day. Evaluate the argument, not its source.
- 6.3 — Appeal to Ignorance (Ad Ignorantiam)
  - "No one has proved it's false, so it must be true." Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — but it's also not evidence of presence. The fact that something hasn't been disproved doesn't mean it's been proved. The burden of proof lies with the person making the claim.
- 6.4 — Irrelevant Conclusion (Ignoratio Elenchi)
  - Proving something that's true but not actually the point. "I should get the job because I'm a good person." Being a good person may be true but is irrelevant to whether you're qualified. The conclusion doesn't address the actual question being asked.

**Test:** 4 questions on straw man, genetic fallacy, appeal to ignorance, and irrelevant conclusion.

### Chapter 7: Fallacies of Ambiguity

**Core concepts:** Equivocation, amphiboly, accent, composition, division

- 7.1 — Equivocation
  - Using a word with two different meanings as if it meant the same thing throughout. "A bank is by the river. Banks hold money. Therefore there's money by the river." "Bank" shifts meaning — this is the four-term fallacy dressed in informal clothing. Equivocation is one of the most common and most dangerous fallacies.
- 7.2 — Amphiboly
  - Ambiguity from sentence structure, not word meaning. "I saw the man with the telescope" — who has the telescope? "Visiting relatives can be boring" — visiting them or them visiting? When an amphibolous statement is used as a premise, the conclusion may follow from one reading but not the other.
- 7.3 — Accent and Emphasis
  - Changing the meaning by shifting emphasis. "We should not speak ill of our friends" (don't speak ill) vs. "We should not speak ILL of our friends" (speak well of them, or just not ill specifically). Quoting someone out of context is a form of accent fallacy — the emphasis changes when the surrounding words are removed.
- 7.4 — Composition and Division
  - Composition: assuming what's true of the parts is true of the whole. "Every player is excellent, so the team must be excellent" (not necessarily — they might not work well together). Division: assuming what's true of the whole is true of the parts. "The team is the best, so every player must be the best" (not necessarily).

**Test:** 4 questions on equivocation, amphiboly, accent, and composition/division.

### Chapter 8: Fallacies of Presumption — Part 1

**Core concepts:** Begging the question, complex question, false dilemma

- 8.1 — Begging the Question (Petitio Principii)
  - Using the conclusion as a premise — assuming what you're trying to prove. "Freedom of speech is important because people should be able to say what they want." The premise and conclusion say the same thing in different words. The argument goes in a circle instead of actually proving anything.
- 8.2 — Complex Question
  - Asking a question that presupposes something unproven. "Have you stopped cheating on tests?" Whether you answer yes or no, you've admitted to cheating. The question hides an assumption that hasn't been established. The correct response is to challenge the presupposition.
- 8.3 — False Dilemma (False Dichotomy)
  - Presenting only two options when more exist. "You're either with us or against us." "Either we cut the program or the school goes bankrupt." These force a choice between extremes when middle options exist. A genuine dilemma has only two options. A false dilemma hides the others.
- 8.4 — Loaded Language
  - Using emotionally charged words to smuggle in conclusions. "This reckless policy" presupposes the policy is reckless before arguing for it. "Common-sense reform" presupposes the reform is common sense. Loaded language is a form of begging the question through word choice.

**Test:** 4 questions on begging the question, complex question, false dilemma, and loaded language.

### Chapter 9: Fallacies of Presumption — Part 2

**Core concepts:** Hasty generalization, slippery slope, false cause, accident

- 9.1 — Hasty Generalization
  - Drawing a universal conclusion from too few examples. "I met two rude people from that city, so everyone there must be rude." Two instances can't support a universal claim. Hasty generalization is an induction error — jumping to "all" from "some" without enough evidence.
- 9.2 — Slippery Slope
  - Claiming that one step will inevitably lead to an extreme outcome without showing why. "If we allow students to retake one test, soon they'll want to retake everything, and eventually nobody will study at all." Each step in the chain needs to be shown, not just asserted. Without evidence linking the steps, the slope is imaginary.
- 9.3 — False Cause (Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc)
  - "After I wore my lucky socks, we won the game. Therefore my socks caused us to win." Just because B followed A doesn't mean A caused B. Sequence is not causation. Post hoc ergo propter hoc ("after this, therefore because of this") is one of the most deeply embedded human reasoning errors.
- 9.4 — Accident and Converse Accident
  - Accident: applying a general rule to a specific case where it doesn't fit. "Everyone should exercise daily" applied to someone with a broken leg. Converse accident: using a special case to create a general rule. "She was excused from the test because of an emergency, so everyone should be excused." Both misapply the relationship between general and particular.

**Test:** 4 questions on hasty generalization, slippery slope, false cause, and accident/converse accident.

### Chapter 10: Fallacies in Practice — Advertising and Media

**Core concepts:** Spotting fallacies in real contexts, advertising, news, social media

- 10.1 — Fallacies in Advertising
  - Advertising is built on persuasion, not logic. Bandwagon (ad populum): "Everyone's switching." Appeal to emotion: happy families using the product. False authority: a celebrity endorsing medicine. Recognizing these isn't about being cynical — it's about separating the sales pitch from the product's actual merits.
- 10.2 — Fallacies in the News
  - Headlines use loaded language. Stories present false dilemmas ("two sides" when there are many). Commentators use straw men. Statistics are presented without context (hasty generalization). Being a critical news consumer means noticing these patterns.
- 10.3 — Fallacies in Social Media
  - Social media amplifies fallacies. Ad populum (likes and shares = truth). Appeal to emotion (outrage drives engagement). Hasty generalization (one viral anecdote becomes "everyone"). Echo chambers reinforce confirmation bias. The speed of social media makes it harder to think carefully — which is exactly when you most need to.
- 10.4 — Fallacies in Everyday Arguments
  - Conversations, family disagreements, schoolyard debates — fallacies show up everywhere. "That's not fair because my sister got to do it" (appeal to precedent, possibly accident). "You wouldn't understand because you're too young" (ad hominem, dismissal). Recognizing fallacies in daily life is the most practical application of logic.

**Test:** 4 questions on spotting fallacies in ads, news, social media, and everyday arguments.

### Chapter 11: Fallacies in Longer Arguments

**Core concepts:** Fallacies in extended reasoning, multiple fallacies in one argument, fallacy stacking

- 11.1 — Arguments Often Contain Multiple Fallacies
  - A single paragraph in a speech or editorial might contain a straw man, an appeal to emotion, and a false dilemma all at once. Real arguments are messier than textbook examples. Identifying all the fallacies in an extended argument is a skill that requires practice.
- 11.2 — Fallacy Stacking
  - Sometimes fallacies are layered — one fallacy supports another. A hasty generalization provides a premise for a slippery slope argument. An appeal to authority provides the "evidence" for a false cause claim. Each fallacy hides behind the others. Untangle them one at a time.
- 11.3 — The Fallacy Fallacy
  - Just because an argument contains a fallacy doesn't mean the conclusion is false. The conclusion might be true — it's just not proven by that particular argument. "He committed an ad hominem, therefore his conclusion is wrong" is itself a fallacy (the fallacy fallacy). A bad argument for a true conclusion is still a bad argument, but the conclusion might still be right.
- 11.4 — Charity and Precision
  - The principle of charity: when evaluating an argument, interpret it in the strongest reasonable way before looking for fallacies. Don't look for the weakest reading just to "win." The goal of logic is truth, not victory. Be precise about what's wrong and charitable about what might be right.

**Test:** 4 questions on multiple fallacies, stacking, the fallacy fallacy, and the principle of charity.

### Chapter 12: Constructing Strong Arguments

**Core concepts:** How to avoid fallacies in your own reasoning, clear premises, relevant evidence

- 12.1 — Start with Clear Terms
  - Before arguing, define your terms. If your key terms are ambiguous, the argument will be ambiguous. "Freedom," "fairness," "success" — these mean different things to different people. State what you mean. This prevents equivocation and much of the confusion in debates.
- 12.2 — Use Relevant Premises
  - Every premise should be directly relevant to the conclusion. Ask: does this evidence actually support this specific conclusion? If not, it's a red herring. Relevance is the most basic test of a good argument, and the most frequently violated.
- 12.3 — Don't Assume What You're Trying to Prove
  - Check your premises: are any of them just restatements of your conclusion? Are you taking for granted what you should be proving? Begging the question is easy to commit and hard to spot in your own arguments.
- 12.4 — Anticipate Objections
  - A strong argument addresses the best counterarguments. If you only consider evidence that supports your view, you're cherry-picking. If you only refute the weakest objections, you're attacking straw men. The strongest arguments engage honestly with the strongest opposition.

**Test:** 4 questions on clear terms, relevant premises, avoiding circular reasoning, and anticipating objections.

### Chapter 13: Distinguishing Good Arguments from Bad

**Core concepts:** Evaluating arguments systematically, the checklist, practice with complex examples

- 13.1 — The Evaluation Checklist
  - Step 1: Identify the conclusion. Step 2: Identify the premises. Step 3: Are the premises relevant to the conclusion? Step 4: Is the logical form valid? Step 5: Are there any formal fallacies? Step 6: Are there any informal fallacies? Step 7: Are the premises actually true? This checklist works for any argument.
- 13.2 — Good Arguments
  - A good argument has: clearly defined terms, true premises, relevant evidence, valid logical form, no fallacies, and addresses counterarguments. This is a high bar — and most arguments in the real world don't meet it. But knowing the standard lets you evaluate how close any argument comes.
- 13.3 — Degrees of Quality
  - Arguments aren't just "good" or "bad" — they fall on a spectrum. A mostly valid argument with one weak premise is better than a fallacy-laden rant. An argument with a minor red herring is better than one built on begging the question. Evaluate proportionally.
- 13.4 — Practice: Complex Evaluations
  - Work through several multi-paragraph arguments. Identify the structure, check for fallacies, evaluate the premises, and render a judgment. This is the full application of everything in Books 1–3.

### Chapter 14: Fallacies and Rhetoric

**Core concepts:** How fallacies relate to persuasion, the ethics of argument, connecting to Rhetoric

- 14.1 — Fallacies Are Persuasive by Design
  - Fallacies work because they exploit how people think. Appeal to emotion bypasses logic. Ad hominem redirects attention. False dilemma simplifies decisions. Understanding fallacies means understanding the persuasive toolkit — both to defend against it and to recognize when you're tempted to use it.
- 14.2 — The Ethics of Argument
  - Using fallacies knowingly is dishonest. It's manipulating someone into a conclusion rather than reasoning them toward it. Logic isn't just a skill — it carries an ethical dimension. Good reasoning respects the other person's ability to think. Bad reasoning exploits their shortcuts.
- 14.3 — Rhetoric Is Not Fallacy
  - Rhetoric (the third art of the Trivium) is the art of persuasion. Persuasion can use valid logic presented compellingly — that's good Rhetoric. Or it can use fallacies dressed up to sound logical — that's manipulation. Logic helps you tell the difference. Rhetoric builds on logic, it doesn't replace it.
- 14.4 — From Logic to Rhetoric
  - You now have the complete formal toolkit (Books 1-2), the complete catalog of failures (Book 3), and material logic still to come (Book 4). After that: Rhetoric — using all of this to communicate persuasively and honestly. Logic tells you what's true. Rhetoric helps you share that truth with others.

**Core concepts:** Complete review, catalog of all fallacies covered, quick reference, application

- 15.1 — Formal Fallacies
  - Undistributed middle, illicit major, illicit minor, four-term fallacy, affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent, exclusive premises, illicit quality/quantity. These violate the structural rules of valid reasoning. Identify by checking the form against the rules.
- 15.2 — Fallacies of Relevance
  - Ad hominem, tu quoque, appeal to authority, appeal to force, appeal to pity, appeal to emotion, ad populum, red herring, straw man, genetic fallacy, appeal to ignorance, irrelevant conclusion. These substitute something other than evidence for the premise-conclusion link.

## Book 4: Material Logic

**Subtitle:** Reasoning About the Real World

### Chapter 1: From Form to Content

**Core concepts:** What material logic is, form vs. matter, why truth requires more than validity

- 1.1 — What Is Material Logic?
  - Formal logic (Books 1–3) asks: is the reasoning valid? Does the conclusion follow from the premises? Material logic asks the harder question: are the premises true? How do we know? Material logic is about the content of reasoning — not just the structure, but the actual knowledge.
- 1.2 — Form vs. Matter
  - The form of an argument is its logical structure. The matter is its content — what the terms actually refer to, whether the premises match reality. A perfectly formed argument with false premises is formally valid but materially unsound. Material logic bridges logic and the real world.
- 1.3 — Why Form Isn't Enough
  - "All fish are mammals. All trout are fish. Therefore all trout are mammals." Valid. False. The form is perfect; the content is wrong. Formal logic can't catch this error — it's not a structural problem. You need to check the premises against reality. That's material logic's job.
- 1.4 — The Questions of Material Logic
  - What makes a definition good? How do we divide categories correctly? What are the causes of things? What counts as a demonstration? How do we reason from particular observations to general principles? When is an analogy valid? These are the questions material logic answers.

**Test:** 4 questions on what material logic is, form vs. matter, why form isn't enough, and the questions material logic asks.

### Chapter 2: Definition as a Logical Act

**Core concepts:** Real vs. nominal definition, essential definition revisited, rules for good definitions

- 2.1 — Definition Revisited
  - Grammar covered definition as a way to explain what a term means. In logic, definition is more rigorous — it's a logical act that places a thing precisely within the structure of knowledge. A good logical definition doesn't just explain — it captures the essence of the thing.
- 2.2 — Real Definition vs. Nominal Definition
  - A nominal definition tells you what a word means — how it's used. A real definition tells you what a thing is — its nature. "A bachelor is an unmarried man" is nominal (defining a word). "A human is a rational animal" is real (defining a thing's essence). Material logic cares primarily about real definitions.
- 2.3 — Essential Definition: Genus + Differentia
  - The strictest definition: place the thing in its nearest genus and give the differentia that distinguishes it. "A triangle is a polygon (genus) with exactly three sides (differentia)." This form has been the gold standard for over 2,000 years because it tells you exactly what the thing is and what makes it different from everything else in its genus.
- 2.4 — Rules for Good Definitions
  - A definition must be convertible with the thing defined (not too broad, not too narrow). It should state the essence, not just properties. It shouldn't use the term being defined (no circularity). It should be positive rather than negative where possible. It should be clear. These rules prevent vague, circular, or useless definitions.

**Test:** 4 questions on real vs. nominal definition, essential definition, and applying the rules.

### Chapter 3: Division as a Logical Act

**Core concepts:** Logical division, physical division, metaphysical division, rules for good division

- 3.1 — Three Kinds of Division
  - Logical division: dividing a genus into its species (animal → mammal, bird, reptile, etc.). Physical division: dividing a whole into its parts (a car → engine, frame, wheels). Metaphysical division: dividing a being into its principles (a thing → substance + accidents, matter + form). Each serves a different purpose.
- 3.2 — Logical Division in Detail
  - Logical division splits a universal concept into its subordinate kinds. "Governance" → monarchy, democracy, oligarchy. "Triangle" → equilateral, isosceles, scalene. Each species must be a genuine kind of the genus, and the division must be based on a single, consistent principle.
- 3.3 — Rules for Good Division
  - The division must be exhaustive (nothing left out), exclusive (no overlap), based on a single consistent principle, and proceed by immediate steps (don't skip levels). "Animals divided into those with fur, those with feathers, and those that live in water" violates consistency — the first two use covering, the third uses habitat.
- 3.4 — Division and Classification
  - Division goes from general to specific (splitting). Classification goes from specific to general (grouping). They're inverse operations. Both are fundamental to organizing knowledge. Every science, every library, every system of categories uses division and classification.

**Test:** 4 questions on the three kinds of division, logical division, rules, and the relationship to classification.

### Chapter 4: The Categories Revisited

**Core concepts:** Aristotle's categories as tools for material logic, predication and reality, category mistakes

- 4.1 — Categories as Logical Tools
  - In Grammar, you learned the categories of being (substance, quantity, quality, relation, etc.). In material logic, the categories are tools for ensuring you reason correctly about reality. Predicating the wrong category leads to confusion. "What color is justice?" is a category mistake — justice doesn't have a color.
- 4.2 — Substance: The Primary Category
  - Substance is what a thing IS. Everything else (quantity, quality, relation, etc.) depends on substance — they're properties OF substances. You can have a red ball, but you can't have redness floating around without something to be red. Reasoning that confuses substances with their properties goes wrong at the foundation.
- 4.3 — The Accidental Categories
  - Quantity, quality, relation, place, time, position, state, action, passion — these are the ways substances can be described. Each category has its own logic. Qualities can be compared (more or less). Quantities can be measured. Relations require two things. Knowing which category you're reasoning about prevents applying the wrong rules.
- 4.4 — Category Mistakes
  - A category mistake attributes something to the wrong category. "What does the number 7 smell like?" "How much does happiness weigh?" These seem obviously absurd, but subtler category mistakes happen all the time: treating an opinion as a fact, treating a relation as a substance, treating a quality as a quantity. Categories keep reasoning disciplined.

**Test:** 4 questions on categories as logical tools, substance as primary, accidental categories, and spotting category mistakes.

### Chapter 5: The Predicables Revisited

**Core concepts:** Genus, species, differentia, property, accident — as tools for evaluating propositions

- 5.1 — The Five Predicables as Evaluation Tools
  - Grammar introduced the predicables (genus, species, differentia, property, accident). In material logic, they become tools for evaluating whether a proposition is necessarily true, always true, or only sometimes true. What you predicate about something determines the strength of the claim.
- 5.2 — Essential vs. Accidental Predication in Reasoning
  - "All humans are rational" (differentia — essential, necessarily true). "All humans are bipedal" (property — always true in practice but not part of the essence). "Some humans are tall" (accident — contingent). The type of predication determines how strong the proposition is and how it can be used in demonstration.
- 5.3 — Necessary vs. Contingent Propositions
  - A necessary proposition can't be false — it follows from the nature of the thing. "All triangles have three sides." A contingent proposition can be true or false depending on circumstances. "This triangle is red." Material logic distinguishes these because demonstrations require necessary premises.
- 5.4 — Per Se and Per Accidens
  - Per se: belongs to a thing by its nature. "Humans are rational" — rationality belongs to humans per se. Per accidens: belongs to a thing but not by its nature. "Humans are musical" — some are, some aren't; it's accidental. This distinction matters enormously for building sound arguments about the real world.

**Test:** 4 questions on the predicables as evaluation tools, essential vs. accidental, necessary vs. contingent, and per se vs. per accidens.

### Chapter 6: The Four Causes

**Core concepts:** Material, formal, efficient, final causes — Aristotle's framework for understanding things

- 6.1 — What Is a Cause?
  - In everyday language, "cause" usually means "what made it happen." In Aristotelian logic, cause is broader: it's any factor that explains why something is what it is. There are four types of cause, and understanding all four gives you a complete explanation of anything.
- 6.2 — Material Cause: What It's Made Of
  - The material cause is the stuff a thing is made from. A bronze statue's material cause is bronze. A cake's material cause is flour, eggs, sugar. The material constrains what the thing can be — you can't make a flexible statue from granite, and you can't make a cake from rocks.
- 6.3 — Formal Cause: What Makes It What It Is
  - The formal cause is the form, pattern, or structure that makes something the kind of thing it is. The same bronze can be a statue, a bell, or a doorknob — the material is the same, but the form is different. The formal cause of a triangle is its three-sided shape. Form is what distinguishes one kind of thing from another.
- 6.4 — Efficient Cause: What Brought It About
  - The efficient cause is the agent or event that produced the thing. The sculptor is the efficient cause of the statue. The baker is the efficient cause of the cake. Your parents are your efficient cause. This is closest to the everyday meaning of "cause" — the thing that made it happen.

**Test:** 4 questions on the four causes generally, material cause, formal cause, and efficient cause.

### Chapter 7: The Final Cause and Teleology

**Core concepts:** Final cause (purpose), teleology, natural and artificial purposes, why final cause matters

- 7.1 — Final Cause: What It's For
  - The final cause is the purpose or end (telos) of a thing. The final cause of a knife is cutting. The final cause of an eye is seeing. The final cause of a school is education. The final cause answers "why does this exist?" or "what is this for?" It's the most overlooked of the four causes in modern thinking.
- 7.2 — Teleology: Purpose in Nature
  - Teleology is the study of purpose. Aristotle believed everything has a natural end — acorns grow into oaks, eyes exist for seeing, hearts exist for pumping blood. Whether or not you accept teleology in nature, final causes are undeniable in human-made things. Every tool, every institution, every rule has a purpose.
- 7.3 — Understanding Through All Four Causes
  - A complete explanation uses all four causes. A house: material (wood, brick), formal (the architectural plan), efficient (the builders), final (shelter). A law: material (the specific words), formal (its structure as a statute), efficient (the legislature that passed it), final (the problem it solves). Four causes give a complete picture.
- 7.4 — Why Final Cause Matters for Reasoning
  - If you don't understand the purpose of something, you can't evaluate it properly. Is this a good knife? Depends on whether it cuts well (final cause). Is this a good law? Depends on whether it achieves its purpose. Is this a good argument? Depends on whether it proves its conclusion. Final cause is the standard of evaluation.

**Test:** 4 questions on final cause, teleology, complete four-cause explanations, and final cause as a standard of evaluation.

### Chapter 8: Judgment and Truth

**Core concepts:** What truth is, correspondence theory, self-evident propositions, degrees of assent

- 8.1 — What Is Truth?
  - Truth is the correspondence between what a proposition says and what is actually the case. "Snow is white" is true if and only if snow is in fact white. This is the correspondence theory of truth — a proposition is true when it matches reality. Simple in concept, complex in application.
- 8.2 — Judgment: Affirming or Denying Truth
  - Judgment is the act of the mind that affirms or denies a proposition. When you judge "Snow is white" to be true, you're making an intellectual commitment — you're saying reality is this way. Judgment is where the mind meets reality. Getting it right is what all of logic aims at.
- 8.3 — Self-Evident Propositions
  - Some propositions are self-evident — their truth is apparent once you understand the terms. "The whole is greater than any of its parts." "A thing cannot both be and not be at the same time and in the same respect." These don't need proof — they're the starting points from which proof proceeds.
- 8.4 — Degrees of Assent
  - Not all judgments are equally certain. Certainty: no reasonable doubt (mathematical proofs, self-evident truths). Opinion: probable but not certain (most everyday judgments). Doubt: insufficient evidence either way. Suspicion: slight evidence. Understanding where your judgment falls on this spectrum is intellectual honesty.

**Test:** 4 questions on truth as correspondence, judgment, self-evident propositions, and degrees of assent.

### Chapter 9: Knowledge, Opinion, and Belief

**Core concepts:** What knowledge is, how it differs from opinion and belief, the conditions for knowing

- 9.1 — What Is Knowledge?
  - Knowledge is justified true belief with certainty. You know something when: it's true, you believe it, you have adequate justification, and you're certain. "I know 2+2=4" meets all conditions. "I think it will rain tomorrow" doesn't — it's opinion, not knowledge.
- 9.2 — Opinion
  - Opinion is judgment with probability but not certainty. Most of what we "know" in daily life is actually opinion — well-informed, reasonable opinion, but still lacking the certainty that true knowledge requires. There's nothing wrong with opinion — most practical decisions are based on it. But knowing the difference matters.
- 9.3 — Belief and Faith
  - Belief is accepting something as true based on the testimony of others or on authority. You believe historical facts because historians report them. You believe scientific findings because scientists demonstrate them. Belief relies on the reliability of the source. Faith is belief where the evidence is not directly accessible to you.
- 9.4 — The Spectrum of Intellectual States
  - Ignorance (not knowing) → doubt (not sure either way) → suspicion (leaning slightly) → opinion (probable judgment) → belief (accepting testimony) → knowledge (certain, justified) → understanding (knowing the causes). Each state is appropriate in different contexts. Treating opinion as knowledge — or knowledge as mere opinion — creates problems.

**Test:** 4 questions on knowledge vs. opinion, belief, and the spectrum of intellectual states.

### Chapter 10: Demonstration

**Core concepts:** What demonstration is, premises of demonstration, scientific syllogism, first principles

- 10.1 — What Is Demonstration?
  - Demonstration is a syllogism that produces knowledge — not just a valid conclusion, but a conclusion known to be true because the premises are true, primary, and better known than the conclusion. Demonstration is the highest form of reasoning. It's what science aims at.
- 10.2 — Premises of Demonstration
  - A demonstrative premise must be: true (corresponding to reality), primary (not derived from something more basic), necessary (couldn't be otherwise), better known than the conclusion (more certain), and the cause of the conclusion (explaining why, not just that). These are strict requirements.
- 10.3 — "That" vs. "Why"
  - A demonstration "that" shows something is the case. A demonstration "why" shows why it's the case — it gives the cause. "All animals with lungs breathe. Whales have lungs. Therefore whales breathe" — demonstration that. "All animals that breathe have lungs. Whales breathe. Therefore whales have lungs" — demonstration why (breathing is the cause of needing lungs). The second is more powerful.
- 10.4 — First Principles
  - Demonstration can't go on forever — you can't prove every premise from a prior premise infinitely. Eventually you reach first principles — truths so basic they don't need proof. The law of non-contradiction ("A thing can't both be and not be"), basic definitions, and self-evident axioms. First principles are the foundation all demonstration rests on.

### Chapter 11: Induction

**Core concepts:** What induction is, how it differs from deduction, enumeration, observation, the problem of induction

- 11.1 — What Is Induction?
  - Induction reasons from particular observations to general principles. You observe that this swan is white, that swan is white, many swans are white — and conclude "swans are white." Induction goes the opposite direction from deduction: deduction goes from general to particular, induction goes from particular to general.
- 11.2 — How Induction Differs from Deduction
  - Deduction guarantees its conclusion (if the form is valid and premises true). Induction does not — the conclusion is probable, not certain. The next swan could be black. But induction is essential because it's how we discover the general truths that become premises for deduction.
- 11.3 — Simple Enumeration
  - The simplest induction: observe many instances, note a pattern, generalize. "Every crow I've seen is black. Therefore all crows are black." The more instances, the stronger the induction. But no number of instances gives certainty — it takes only one counter-example to refute a universal generalization.
- 11.4 — The Problem of Induction
  - How many observations are enough? You can never observe all cases (except in a finite, countable set). Induction gives probability, not certainty. This is the "problem of induction" — we depend on it for nearly all knowledge of the world, yet it can never give us the certainty of deduction. This isn't a flaw — it's a fundamental feature of reasoning about the real world.

### Chapter 12: Mill's Methods

**Core concepts:** Agreement, difference, concomitant variation, residues — systematic tools for finding causes

- 12.1 — The Method of Agreement
  - If several instances of an effect all share one common factor, that factor is probably the cause. Every sick person ate the potato salad. The potato salad is the common factor. The method of agreement looks for what's shared across instances.
- 12.2 — The Method of Difference
  - If two situations are identical except for one factor, and the effect occurs in one but not the other, that factor is probably the cause. Two groups of plants, same soil, same water — one group gets sunlight, one doesn't. The group with sunlight grows. Sunlight is the difference. This is the logic behind controlled experiments.
- 12.3 — The Method of Concomitant Variation
  - If the effect varies as the potential cause varies, they're probably connected. More fertilizer → more growth. Higher temperature → faster reaction. As one changes, the other changes proportionally. This method detects causal relationships that aren't all-or-nothing.
- 12.4 — The Method of Residues
  - If you can explain part of an effect by known causes, the remaining part must have another cause. You've accounted for wind, temperature, and humidity in predicting weather. There's still an unexplained pattern. Something else (maybe air pressure) accounts for the residue. Residues point you toward undiscovered causes.

**Test:** 4 questions on agreement, difference, concomitant variation, and residues.

### Chapter 13: Analogical Reasoning

**Core concepts:** Analogy in logic, proper proportionality, strength of analogies, limits

- 13.1 — What Is Analogical Reasoning?
  - Analogical reasoning concludes that because two things are similar in known respects, they're probably similar in an unknown respect. "Mars has an atmosphere, seasons, and polar ice caps like Earth. Earth has life. Therefore Mars might have life." The known similarities make the unknown similarity plausible — but not certain.
- 13.2 — The Structure of an Analogy
  - Every analogy has: the thing being compared (Mars), the thing it's compared to (Earth), the known similarities (atmosphere, seasons, ice), and the inferred similarity (life). The more relevant the known similarities, and the more of them there are, the stronger the analogy.
- 13.3 — Evaluating Analogies
  - Strong analogies: many relevant similarities, few relevant differences, the inferred property is related to the shared properties. Weak analogies: few similarities, many differences, the inferred property is unrelated to the shared ones. "A school is like a prison because both have buildings" is a very weak analogy.
- 13.4 — Analogy and Discovery
  - Analogy is not as certain as deduction or as systematic as induction, but it's one of the most powerful tools for discovery. Scientists use analogies to generate hypotheses. Lawyers use analogies to apply precedent. Inventors use analogies to adapt solutions from one domain to another. Analogy is how new ideas are born.

**Test:** 4 questions on analogical reasoning, structure, evaluating strength, and the role of analogy in discovery.

### Chapter 14: Practical Reasoning

**Core concepts:** Theoretical vs. practical reasoning, means-end reasoning, deliberation, prudential judgment

- 14.1 — Theoretical vs. Practical Reasoning
  - Theoretical reasoning seeks to know what is true. Practical reasoning seeks to know what to do. "All mammals are warm-blooded" is theoretical. "I should bring a coat because it's cold" is practical. Both use logic, but practical reasoning adds a dimension: action. The conclusion of practical reasoning is a decision, not just a proposition.
- 14.2 — Means-End Reasoning
  - Practical reasoning typically takes the form: I want to achieve X. Doing Y is a means to X. Therefore I should do Y. "I want to pass the test. Studying is a means to passing. Therefore I should study." The reasoning is valid, but it depends on both the goal being good and the means being effective.
- 14.3 — Deliberation
  - Deliberation is the process of weighing multiple means to an end. Should you study alone or with a group? Both are means to passing. Deliberation evaluates which means is better — more effective, more practical, less costly. Good deliberation considers multiple options and their consequences.
- 14.4 — Prudential Judgment
  - Prudence is the virtue of reasoning well about what to do. It combines knowledge of principles (from theoretical reasoning), awareness of circumstances (from experience), and good judgment about particulars (from deliberation). Prudence is where logic meets life — it's the application of everything you've learned to actual decisions.

**Core concepts:** Review of all four books, the unity of logic, preparing for Rhetoric

- 15.1 — The Complete Toolkit
  - Book 1: propositions, distribution, the square of opposition, immediate inferences. Book 2: the categorical syllogism, hypothetical and disjunctive syllogisms, enthymemes, sorites. Book 3: formal and informal fallacies. Book 4: definition, division, the four causes, demonstration, induction, analogy, practical reasoning. Together, these cover the full scope of logic.
- 15.2 — The Unity of Logic

  - You now know how to structure meaning (Grammar), reason validly (formal Logic), and evaluate content (material Logic). - 15.4 — The Power of Logic
  - You can evaluate any argument — in a conversation, a newspaper, a courtroom, a science paper. You can construct valid arguments, avoid fallacies, identify missing premises, and reason about causes. You can distinguish knowledge from opinion, demonstration from mere persuasion, and analogy from proof. That's the power of logic, and you now have it.
