# Probability and Statistics for Kids — Book Proposal

## Why These Books

Probability and statistics are everywhere — weather forecasts, sports, medicine, news, games, investing, science, elections. Yet most adults can't explain the difference between mean and median, confuse correlation with causation, get fooled by misleading graphs, and have no intuition for how probability actually works.

These two books teach both subjects from scratch using the same approach as the Economics for Kids series: plain language, real examples, and building one concept per lesson. A kid who finishes both books will understand chance and data better than most adults — and will be equipped to think clearly about the numbers they encounter for the rest of their life.

## Series Context

Two books. Probability first (the math of chance), Statistics second (what data tells us). Probability comes first because statistics uses probability concepts. Each stands alone, but they're stronger together.

The Risk for Kids book touches probability lightly (likelihood, expected value). These books go much deeper — probability as a full subject, and statistics as its own discipline.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters per book, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300–500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7–12
- Real-world examples from a kid's life (games, sports, weather, school, food)

## Math expectations

- Fractions, percentages, and decimals throughout — explained from scratch
- Basic multiplication and division for computing probabilities
- Addition of fractions for combined probabilities
- Averages (addition and division)
- No algebra, no calculus, no formal notation (no Σ, no P(A|B) notation — everything in words and simple math)
- Where possible, use concrete counts (dice, cards, coins) to make the math tangible

## Prerequisites

- Basic arithmetic (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division)
- Fractions and percentages helpful but introduced as needed
- No prior knowledge of probability or statistics required

## Book 1: Probability — The Math of Chance

**Subtitle:** How Likely Is It?

### Chapter 1: What Is Probability?

**Core concepts:** Chance, uncertainty, the probability scale, certain vs. impossible

- 1.1 — Things We Don't Know For Sure
  - Will it rain tomorrow? Will you win the game? Will the coin land on heads? You don't know for certain. Probability is the branch of math that deals with things we don't know for sure. It gives us tools to think clearly about uncertainty.
- 1.2 — The Probability Scale
  - Probability is a number between 0 and 1. Zero means impossible — it will not happen. One means certain — it will happen. Everything else is somewhere in between. You can also express it as a percentage: 0 = 0%, 1 = 100%, 0.5 = 50%.
- 1.3 — Equally Likely Outcomes
  - When you flip a fair coin, heads and tails are equally likely — each has a probability of 1/2 or 50%. When you roll a fair die, each face has a probability of 1/6. Equally likely outcomes are the simplest starting point for learning probability.
- 1.4 — Probability in Everyday Language
  - People say "probably," "likely," "unlikely," "no chance," "50-50," "almost certainly" all the time. These are informal probability statements. This book turns that informal language into precise thinking. Instead of "it'll probably rain," you'll understand what a "70% chance of rain" actually means.

**Test:** 4 questions on what probability is, the probability scale, equally likely outcomes, and interpreting everyday probability language.

### Chapter 2: Outcomes and Sample Spaces

**Core concepts:** Outcomes, sample space, events, listing all possibilities

- 2.1 — What Is an Outcome?
  - An outcome is one possible result. Flip a coin: heads is an outcome, tails is an outcome. Roll a die: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 are the six possible outcomes. An outcome is the most specific thing that can happen.
- 2.2 — The Sample Space
  - The sample space is the complete list of all possible outcomes. For a coin: {heads, tails}. For a die: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}. For two coins: {HH, HT, TH, TT}. Before you can calculate any probability, you need to know the sample space — all the things that could happen.
- 2.3 — What Is an Event?
  - An event is a specific outcome or group of outcomes you care about. "Rolling an even number" is an event that includes outcomes {2, 4, 6}. "Getting at least one head in two coin flips" includes {HH, HT, TH}. Events are what you're asking about when you say "what's the probability of...?"
- 2.4 — Listing All Possibilities
  - For simple situations, you can list every outcome by hand. Two coins: 4 outcomes. A coin and a die: 12 outcomes. As situations get more complex, listing gets harder — but starting with simple lists builds the foundation for everything else.

**Test:** 4 questions on outcomes, sample spaces, events, and listing possibilities.

### Chapter 3: Calculating Simple Probabilities

**Core concepts:** Favorable outcomes over total outcomes, fractions as probabilities, practice calculations

- 3.1 — The Basic Rule
  - When all outcomes are equally likely, the probability of an event = (number of favorable outcomes) ÷ (total number of outcomes). Rolling a 3 on a die: 1 favorable outcome out of 6 total = 1/6. Drawing a heart from a deck of cards: 13 hearts out of 52 cards = 13/52 = 1/4.
- 3.2 — Fractions, Decimals, and Percentages
  - 1/6 is the same as approximately 0.167, which is about 16.7%. All three are ways to express the same probability. Fractions are exact. Decimals and percentages are sometimes easier to compare. Get comfortable going between them.
- 3.3 — Probability of Something NOT Happening
  - The probability of something not happening = 1 minus the probability of it happening. If there's a 1/6 chance of rolling a 3, there's a 5/6 chance of NOT rolling a 3. This is called the complement. It's often easier to calculate the complement and subtract.
- 3.4 — Practice: Coins, Dice, and Cards
  - Work through several calculations. Probability of flipping heads: 1/2. Rolling a number greater than 4: 2/6 = 1/3. Drawing a face card from a deck: 12/52 = 3/13. Each one follows the same basic rule — count the favorable outcomes, divide by the total.

**Test:** 4 questions requiring simple probability calculations using the basic rule, complements, and converting between fractions and percentages.

### Chapter 4: "Or" Probabilities

**Core concepts:** Union of events, mutually exclusive, addition rule, overlapping events

- 4.1 — "What's the Probability of This OR That?"
  - Sometimes you want to know the probability of either of two things happening. What's the probability of rolling a 2 or a 5? What's the chance it rains today or tomorrow? "Or" questions combine events.
- 4.2 — Mutually Exclusive Events
  - Two events are mutually exclusive if they can't both happen at the same time. Rolling a 2 and rolling a 5 on the same roll are mutually exclusive — the die shows one number. For mutually exclusive events, just add the probabilities: P(2 or 5) = 1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3.
- 4.3 — Overlapping Events
  - What's the probability of drawing a heart or a queen from a deck? These overlap — the queen of hearts is both. If you just add P(heart) + P(queen) you'd count the queen of hearts twice. You need to subtract the overlap: 13/52 + 4/52 - 1/52 = 16/52.
- 4.4 — The Addition Rule
  - For mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B). For overlapping events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B). The subtraction prevents double-counting. This rule works for any "or" question.

**Test:** 4 questions on "or" probabilities, mutually exclusive events, overlapping events, and the addition rule.

### Chapter 5: "And" Probabilities

**Core concepts:** Intersection of events, independent events, the multiplication rule

- 5.1 — "What's the Probability of This AND That?"
  - Sometimes you want both things to happen. What's the probability of flipping heads and then heads again? What's the chance of drawing a heart and then another heart? "And" questions multiply.
- 5.2 — Independent Events
  - Two events are independent if one happening doesn't change the probability of the other. Coin flips are independent — the first flip doesn't affect the second. Each roll of a die is independent. For independent events, multiply the probabilities: P(heads then heads) = 1/2 × 1/2 = 1/4.
- 5.3 — Dependent Events
  - If one event changes the probability of the other, they're dependent. Drawing a card and then drawing another card without putting the first one back — the second draw depends on the first because there are fewer cards left. The probabilities change.
- 5.4 — The Multiplication Rule
  - For independent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B). For dependent events: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B given A already happened). The second version accounts for how the first event changes the situation.

**Test:** 4 questions on "and" probabilities, independent vs. dependent events, and the multiplication rule.

### Chapter 6: Conditional Probability

**Core concepts:** Probability given that something else happened, updating probabilities, real examples

- 6.1 — What Is Conditional Probability?
  - Conditional probability is the probability of something happening given that something else has already happened. What's the probability of drawing a king, given that you already know the card is a face card? The condition (it's a face card) narrows down the possibilities and changes the probability.
- 6.2 — How It Changes the Math
  - If you know a card is a face card, there are only 12 possibilities (not 52). Of those 12, 4 are kings. So the probability of a king given it's a face card = 4/12 = 1/3. Without the condition, it would be 4/52. The condition changed the sample space.
- 6.3 — Everyday Conditional Probability
  - What's the probability of the game being cancelled given that it's raining? What's the probability of getting an A given that you studied? What's the probability of a flight being delayed given that there's a storm? We think conditionally all the time — this formalizes it.
- 6.4 — The Difference It Makes
  - The probability of being tired is maybe 30%. The probability of being tired given that you slept 4 hours is much higher — maybe 90%. The condition makes a huge difference. Confusing unconditional and conditional probability is a common and serious error.

**Test:** 4 questions on conditional probability, how conditions change the sample space, everyday examples, and why the distinction matters.

### Chapter 7: Counting Methods

**Core concepts:** Counting principle, permutations, combinations, when order matters

- 7.1 — The Counting Principle
  - If you have 3 shirts and 4 pants, you have 3 × 4 = 12 outfits. If a code has 3 digits and each can be 0-9, there are 10 × 10 × 10 = 1,000 possible codes. The counting principle: multiply the number of choices at each step.
- 7.2 — When Order Matters: Permutations
  - If you're arranging 3 books on a shelf, order matters — ABC is different from BAC. The number of arrangements of 3 books is 3 × 2 × 1 = 6. For 4 books: 4 × 3 × 2 × 1 = 24. These are called permutations — arrangements where order matters.
- 7.3 — When Order Doesn't Matter: Combinations
  - If you're choosing 2 toppings from 5 options, order doesn't matter — pepperoni-and-mushroom is the same as mushroom-and-pepperoni. Combinations count groups, not arrangements. There are fewer combinations than permutations because you're not counting rearrangements of the same group.
- 7.4 — Using Counting to Find Probabilities
  - How many ways can you get exactly 2 heads in 4 coin flips? First count the total outcomes (16). Then count the favorable outcomes (6 ways to get exactly 2 heads). Probability = 6/16 = 3/8. Counting methods make these calculations possible.

**Test:** 4 questions on the counting principle, permutations, combinations, and using counting for probability.

### Chapter 8: Expected Value

**Core concepts:** What expected value is, how to calculate it, using it to make decisions, fair games

- 8.1 — What Is Expected Value?
  - Expected value is the average outcome you'd expect if you repeated something many times. Flip a coin — win $1 for heads, lose $1 for tails. Expected value: (1/2 × $1) + (1/2 × -$1) = $0. On average, you break even. Expected value isn't what happens once — it's the average over many repetitions.
- 8.2 — How to Calculate It
  - Multiply each possible outcome by its probability, then add them all up. Roll a die and win the amount shown: (1/6 × $1) + (1/6 × $2) + (1/6 × $3) + (1/6 × $4) + (1/6 × $5) + (1/6 × $6) = $3.50. On average, you'd win $3.50 per roll.
- 8.3 — Fair and Unfair Games
  - A fair game has an expected value of $0 — neither side has an advantage. Most casino games have a negative expected value for the player — the house always has an edge. Understanding expected value tells you whether a game or bet is in your favor, against you, or neutral.
- 8.4 — Expected Value in Real Decisions
  - Should you buy a $1 lottery ticket with a 1 in 1,000 chance of winning $500? Expected value: (1/1000 × $500) + (999/1000 × -$1) = $0.50 - $0.999 = -$0.50. On average, you lose 50 cents per ticket. Expected value helps you evaluate real choices, not just games.

**Test:** 4 questions on expected value, calculating it, fair vs. unfair games, and applying it to decisions.

### Chapter 9: The Law of Large Numbers

**Core concepts:** Short run vs. long run, why averages stabilize, gambler's fallacy, sample size

- 9.1 — Short Run vs. Long Run
  - Flip a coin 10 times and you might get 7 heads. That's not unusual — small samples are unpredictable. Flip it 10,000 times and you'll get very close to 50% heads. The more times you repeat something, the closer the results get to the expected probability. This is the law of large numbers.
- 9.2 — Why Averages Stabilize
  - In a small sample, unusual results can dominate. In a large sample, unusual results get diluted by all the normal results. A basketball player might miss 5 free throws in a row, but over a full season, their percentage will be close to their true skill level. Large samples smooth out the noise.
- 9.3 — The Gambler's Fallacy
  - "I've flipped tails 5 times in a row, so heads must be due." Wrong. The coin doesn't remember what happened before. Each flip is independent. Past results don't change future probabilities. This mistake is called the gambler's fallacy — it's one of the most common errors in probability thinking.
- 9.4 — Sample Size Matters
  - If a restaurant has 3 reviews and they're all 5 stars, is it really the best restaurant in town? Maybe — or maybe 3 people isn't enough to know. A restaurant with 500 reviews averaging 4.5 stars tells you much more. Sample size determines how much you should trust the result.

**Test:** 4 questions on short run vs. long run, why averages stabilize, the gambler's fallacy, and sample size.

### Chapter 10: Randomness

**Core concepts:** What random means, patterns in randomness, random vs. unpredictable, simulations

- 10.1 — What Does Random Mean?
  - Random means each outcome is determined by chance — no pattern, no prediction, no control. A fair coin flip is random. The result of a shuffled deck is random. Random doesn't mean "without cause" — it means the outcome isn't predictable in advance.
- 10.2 — Humans Are Bad at Recognizing Randomness
  - If you flip a coin 20 times, you'll probably see a streak of 4 or 5 heads in a row somewhere. That feels like a pattern, but it's completely normal in random sequences. Real randomness is clumpier than people expect. A truly random playlist will sometimes play 3 songs by the same artist in a row — that's randomness, not a glitch.
- 10.3 — Patterns That Aren't Real
  - After a basketball player makes 3 shots in a row, people say they have a "hot hand." Research shows the hot hand is mostly an illusion — the streaks you see are consistent with randomness. Humans are pattern-seekers. We see patterns even where none exist. Random data will always contain sequences that look meaningful.
- 10.4 — Simulations: Using Randomness on Purpose
  - Computers can generate random numbers to simulate probability experiments thousands of times. Want to know the probability of a complicated event? Simulate it 10,000 times and count how often it happens. This is called a Monte Carlo simulation, and it's one of the most powerful tools in probability.

**Test:** 4 questions on what random means, streaks in random data, false patterns, and simulations.

### Chapter 11: Probability Distributions

**Core concepts:** What a distribution is, uniform distributions, the idea of shape, common distributions

- 11.1 — What Is a Distribution?
  - A probability distribution shows all possible outcomes and how likely each one is. For a fair die, each face has a 1/6 probability — that's a uniform distribution (all outcomes equally likely). For two dice added together, 7 is the most likely sum and 2 and 12 are the least likely — that's a different shape.
- 11.2 — Uniform Distributions
  - When every outcome has the same probability, the distribution is uniform. A fair coin, a fair die, a random number between 1 and 10. Uniform distributions are the simplest kind — flat and even. They're the starting point, but most real situations aren't uniform.
- 11.3 — The Bell Curve
  - Many real things — heights, test scores, measurement errors — cluster around an average with fewer values far away. This creates a bell-shaped curve: tall in the middle, tapering on both sides. It's called the normal distribution, and it shows up everywhere in nature and in data.
- 11.4 — Skewed and Other Shapes
  - Not everything follows a bell curve. Incomes are skewed — most people earn moderate amounts, a few earn enormously more, pulling the shape to the right. Some distributions have two peaks. Some are flat. The shape of a distribution tells you a lot about the data.

**Test:** 4 questions on what a distribution is, uniform distributions, the bell curve, and skewed distributions.

### Chapter 12: Odds, Risk, and Probability in the Real World

**Core concepts:** Odds vs. probability, absolute vs. relative risk, medical testing, weather forecasts

- 12.1 — Odds vs. Probability
  - Probability says "1 in 4 chance" (1 out of 4 total). Odds say "1 to 3" (1 favorable to 3 unfavorable). Same situation, different format. Sports betting uses odds. Science uses probability. Being able to convert between them prevents confusion.
- 12.2 — Absolute vs. Relative Risk
  - "This doubles your risk" sounds scary. But if the base risk is 1 in a million, doubled is 2 in a million — still tiny. Relative risk (how much it changes) and absolute risk (the actual number) can paint completely different pictures. Always ask for the absolute number.
- 12.3 — Medical Testing and False Positives
  - A test is 99% accurate. You test positive. Do you definitely have the condition? Not necessarily. If the condition is rare (say 1 in 10,000), most positive results are false positives. This surprises almost everyone, but the math is straightforward once you work through it.
- 12.4 — Weather Forecasts and Probability
  - "70% chance of rain" means that in similar conditions, it rains about 70 out of 100 times. It doesn't mean 70% of the area will get rain, or that it will rain 70% of the day. Understanding what probability statements actually mean helps you interpret forecasts, polls, and predictions accurately.

**Test:** 4 questions on odds vs. probability, absolute vs. relative risk, false positives, and interpreting forecasts.

### Chapter 13: Common Probability Mistakes

**Core concepts:** Gambler's fallacy revisited, base rate neglect, conjunction fallacy, independence errors

- 13.1 — Ignoring Base Rates
  - If 1 in 1,000 people have a disease and a test is 95% accurate, a positive result doesn't mean there's a 95% chance you're sick. Most positives will be false positives because the disease is so rare. Ignoring the base rate — how common the thing is — leads to wildly wrong conclusions.
- 13.2 — The Conjunction Fallacy
  - Which is more likely: "Sarah is a bank teller" or "Sarah is a bank teller who plays chess"? The first is always more likely — a specific combination can never be more probable than one of its parts alone. But the added detail makes the second feel more likely because it paints a richer picture. More detail = lower probability, always.
- 13.3 — Assuming Independence
  - If events are not independent, you can't just multiply their probabilities. The probability of rain today and rain tomorrow aren't independent — weather patterns carry over. Treating dependent events as independent gives wrong answers. Always check whether one event affects the other.
- 13.4 — Confusing "Unlikely" with "Impossible"
  - A 1% chance is unlikely. It is not impossible. If something has a 1% chance and you do it 100 times, it's actually quite likely to happen at least once (about 63% likely). Unlikely events happen all the time when there are many opportunities for them to happen.

**Test:** 4 questions on base rate neglect, the conjunction fallacy, independence errors, and unlikely vs. impossible.

### Chapter 14: Games, Gambling, and Strategy

**Core concepts:** Probability in games, house edge, strategy games, risk vs. reward in games

- 14.1 — Probability in Games
  - Board games, card games, dice games — probability is built into them. Knowing the odds doesn't take the fun away; it helps you make better decisions. What's the probability of rolling what you need in a board game? What are the odds of drawing the card you want?
- 14.2 — The House Edge
  - In casino games, the math is designed so the house wins over time. Roulette, slots, lotteries — they all have negative expected value for the player. The house edge might be small per bet, but over thousands of bets, the house always wins. That's not luck — it's math.
- 14.3 — Strategy and Probability
  - In games with choices (poker, board games, even video games), good players use probability to make better decisions. "What's the chance my opponent has a better hand?" "Should I play it safe or take the risk?" Combining probability with strategy is how skilled players win more often.
- 14.4 — Risk vs. Reward in Games
  - Every game decision is a risk-reward calculation. Go for the big score and risk losing everything, or play it safe and take the small win? The right choice depends on the probabilities, the payoffs, and the situation. Games are a great place to practice thinking about risk — the stakes are low and the lessons are real.

**Test:** 4 questions on probability in games, the house edge, using probability for strategy, and risk vs. reward.

### Chapter 15: Probability as a Way of Thinking

**Core concepts:** Review, applying probability to everyday life, thinking in probabilities, what you've learned

- 15.1 — The Probability Toolkit
  - Review the core tools: sample spaces, basic probability, "and" and "or" rules, conditional probability, counting methods, expected value, the law of large numbers, distributions. These tools work together to help you think clearly about anything uncertain.
- 15.2 — Thinking in Probabilities
  - Instead of "will it rain?" think "what's the chance it rains?" Instead of "will this work?" think "how likely is this to work, and what's the payoff?" Thinking in probabilities doesn't require a calculator — it's a habit of mind that makes you a clearer thinker.
- 15.3 — Probability and Good Decisions
  - A good decision can have a bad outcome. A bad decision can have a good outcome. Probability helps you make the best decision given what you know — but it doesn't guarantee results. Judge your decisions by the process, not just the outcome. Over time, good process wins.
- 15.4 — What You Know Now
  - You can calculate probabilities, spot common mistakes, understand expected value, interpret real-world probability claims, and think clearly about chance and uncertainty. That's a set of skills most adults don't have. You'll use them in math class, in games, in life decisions, and everywhere else uncertainty shows up.

**Test:** 4 questions on the toolkit, thinking in probabilities, process vs. outcome, and applying probability to a scenario.

## Book 2: Statistics — What Data Tells Us

**Subtitle:** Collecting, Understanding, and Questioning Data

### Chapter 1: What Is Statistics?

**Core concepts:** What statistics is, data everywhere, why it matters, descriptive vs. inferential

- 1.1 — What Is Statistics?
  - Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, and interpreting data. Data is information — numbers, measurements, answers to questions. Statistics turns raw data into understanding. It's how we learn things about the world when we can't check every single case.
- 1.2 — Data Is Everywhere
  - How many students are in your school? What's the average temperature this month? Which flavor of ice cream is most popular? What percentage of free throws does a player make? All of these are data questions. Statistics gives you the tools to answer them — and to question the answers.
- 1.3 — Descriptive vs. Inferential Statistics
  - Descriptive statistics summarizes data you have. "The average score on the test was 82." Inferential statistics draws conclusions beyond the data you have. "Based on this sample, we estimate that most students in the state would score between 78 and 86." Describing what you see vs. predicting what you haven't seen.
- 1.4 — Why Statistics Matters
  - Doctors use statistics to decide which treatments work. Companies use statistics to understand their customers. Scientists use statistics to test theories. Voters see statistics in polls and claims. Understanding statistics helps you evaluate all of these — and spot when someone is using numbers to mislead.

**Test:** 4 questions on what statistics is, examples of data, descriptive vs. inferential, and why it matters.

### Chapter 2: Types of Data

**Core concepts:** Categorical vs. numerical, discrete vs. continuous, qualitative vs. quantitative

- 2.1 — Categorical Data
  - Categorical data sorts things into groups. Favorite color: red, blue, green. Type of pet: dog, cat, fish. Grade level: 3rd, 4th, 5th. You can count how many are in each category, but you can't average them. The average of "red" and "blue" doesn't make sense.
- 2.2 — Numerical Data
  - Numerical data is numbers you can do math with. Height, weight, temperature, test scores, number of siblings. You can add, subtract, average, and compare numerical data. It's the kind of data most statistical tools work with.
- 2.3 — Discrete vs. Continuous
  - Discrete data can only take specific values — number of pets (you can't have 2.7 pets), number of goals scored. Continuous data can take any value in a range — height (you could be 4 feet 7.3 inches), temperature, time. The distinction matters for choosing the right tools and graphs.
- 2.4 — Choosing the Right Type
  - When you collect data, knowing what type it is helps you decide how to organize and analyze it. Categorical data gets bar charts and counts. Numerical data gets averages and histograms. Mixing them up leads to nonsense — you can't calculate the average zip code.

**Test:** 4 questions on categorical vs. numerical, discrete vs. continuous, and matching data types to tools.

### Chapter 3: Collecting Data

**Core concepts:** Observation, surveys, experiments, censuses, why collection method matters

- 3.1 — Observation
  - Sometimes you collect data by watching and recording. Counting cars at an intersection, tracking how many birds visit a feeder, recording temperatures each day. Observation is simple and doesn't interfere with what you're studying.
- 3.2 — Surveys and Questionnaires
  - Surveys ask people questions. "What's your favorite lunch?" "How many hours do you sleep?" Surveys are powerful but tricky — the way you ask the question can change the answer. "Do you like broccoli?" and "Do you hate broccoli?" might get different results even though they're asking the same thing.
- 3.3 — Experiments
  - In an experiment, you change something on purpose and measure what happens. Give one group of plants extra water and another group normal water, then compare growth. Experiments are the best way to find cause and effect — not just that two things are related, but that one actually causes the other.
- 3.4 — Censuses and Why Collection Matters
  - A census tries to collect data from everyone in a group (like the U.S. Census counts every person in the country). Most of the time, we can't check everyone, so we use samples. How you collect data — who you ask, how you ask, how many you ask — affects whether the results are trustworthy.

**Test:** 4 questions on observation, survey design, experiments, and why collection method matters.

### Chapter 4: Sampling and Bias

**Core concepts:** Populations and samples, random sampling, bias, sample size

- 4.1 — Populations and Samples
  - The population is the entire group you want to learn about. The sample is the smaller group you actually study. You want to know what all 5th graders in the country think? You can't ask all of them — but you can ask a carefully chosen sample and draw conclusions about the whole group.
- 4.2 — Random Sampling
  - A random sample means every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected. This is the gold standard because it avoids systematic bias. If you only survey your friends, you get your friends' opinions, not everyone's. Random sampling gives a fair picture.
- 4.3 — Bias: When Samples Go Wrong
  - Bias means the sample doesn't fairly represent the population. Surveying only kids who eat in the cafeteria about school food ignores kids who bring lunch. Surveying only people who answer their phone misses people who don't. Bias can make results completely misleading.
- 4.4 — Sample Size
  - Bigger samples are generally more reliable. A survey of 10 people might give a quirky result. A survey of 1,000 people is much more stable. But there are diminishing returns — going from 1,000 to 10,000 helps less than going from 10 to 1,000. The right sample size depends on how precise you need to be.

**Test:** 4 questions on population vs. sample, random sampling, types of bias, and sample size.

### Chapter 5: Organizing Data

**Core concepts:** Frequency tables, sorting, grouping, tally marks, two-way tables

- 5.1 — Frequency Tables
  - A frequency table counts how many times each value or category appears. Favorite color: red (7), blue (12), green (5), yellow (3). It takes a messy pile of raw data and organizes it into something readable.
- 5.2 — Grouping Data
  - When data has many different values (like test scores from 45 to 100), you can group them into ranges: 40-49, 50-59, 60-69, and so on. Grouping makes patterns visible that are hard to see in raw numbers. The tradeoff: you lose some detail.
- 5.3 — Sorting and Rank Order
  - Sometimes just putting data in order from smallest to largest reveals a lot. You can see the range, spot outliers (values far from the rest), and find the middle value quickly. Sorting is one of the simplest and most useful things you can do with data.
- 5.4 — Two-Way Tables
  - A two-way table shows two categories at once. Rows might be grade level, columns might be favorite sport. Each cell shows the count for that combination. Two-way tables let you compare groups and spot relationships between categories.

**Test:** 4 questions on frequency tables, grouping, sorting, and reading two-way tables.

### Chapter 6: Graphs and Charts

**Core concepts:** Bar charts, line graphs, pie charts, histograms, choosing the right graph

- 6.1 — Bar Charts
  - Bar charts show categorical data — each category gets a bar, and the height (or length) shows the count or value. Easy to read, easy to compare. Best for: comparing categories (favorite colors, sales by product, goals by player).
- 6.2 — Line Graphs
  - Line graphs show how data changes over time. Each point is a value at a specific time, connected by lines. Best for: trends (temperature over a week, stock price over a year, your height each birthday). Lines make it easy to see direction — going up, going down, staying flat.
- 6.3 — Pie Charts and Histograms
  - Pie charts show parts of a whole — each slice is a percentage of the total. Best when you have a few categories that add up to 100%. Histograms look like bar charts but show numerical data grouped into ranges. They reveal the shape of the data — where values cluster and where they're sparse.
- 6.4 — Choosing the Right Graph
  - Categories → bar chart. Change over time → line graph. Parts of a whole → pie chart. Distribution of numerical data → histogram. Two numerical variables → scatter plot (coming later). The right graph makes data clear. The wrong graph makes it confusing — or misleading.

**Test:** 4 questions on when to use each graph type, reading a graph, and what different graphs reveal.

### Chapter 7: Center — Mean, Median, and Mode

**Core concepts:** Three measures of center, how to calculate each, when to use which, how they differ

- 7.1 — The Mean (Average)
  - Add up all the values and divide by how many there are. Five test scores: 80, 85, 90, 75, 90. Mean = 420 ÷ 5 = 84. The mean is the most common measure of center, but it can be pulled by extreme values. One very high or very low number changes the mean significantly.
- 7.2 — The Median (Middle Value)
  - Put the values in order and find the one in the middle. For {75, 80, 85, 90, 90}, the median is 85. If there's an even number of values, average the two middle ones. The median isn't affected by extreme values — it's a more stable measure of "typical" when data is skewed.
- 7.3 — The Mode (Most Frequent)
  - The mode is the value that appears most often. In {75, 80, 85, 90, 90}, the mode is 90. Data can have no mode (all different values), one mode, or multiple modes. The mode is most useful for categorical data — the most popular flavor, the most common answer.
- 7.4 — When They Disagree
  - If five people earn $40K, $45K, $50K, $55K, and $300K, the mean is $98K but the median is $50K. The mean is pulled up by the one high earner. The median better represents the "typical" person. When mean and median are far apart, extreme values are distorting the mean. Knowing when to use which prevents misleading conclusions.

### Chapter 8: Spread — Range, Variance, and Standard Deviation

**Core concepts:** Why center alone isn't enough, range, interquartile range, standard deviation (conceptual)

- 8.1 — Center Isn't the Whole Story
  - Two classes both average 80 on a test. In one class, scores range from 78 to 82. In the other, scores range from 40 to 100. The averages are the same, but the classes are very different. Spread tells you how much the data varies — and it matters as much as the center.
- 8.2 — Range
  - Range = highest value minus lowest value. Simple and quick. For {40, 75, 80, 85, 100}, the range is 60. Limitation: it only uses two values (the extremes), so one unusual value can make the range misleading.
- 8.3 — Interquartile Range (IQR)
  - The IQR is the range of the middle 50% of the data. Find the median, then find the median of the lower half (Q1) and the upper half (Q3). IQR = Q3 - Q1. It ignores the extremes and focuses on where most of the data lives. More stable than the range.
- 8.4 — Standard Deviation: The Average Distance from the Mean
  - Standard deviation measures how far, on average, each value is from the mean. Small standard deviation: data is clustered tightly around the mean. Large standard deviation: data is spread out. You don't need to memorize the formula — just understand the concept: it's the typical distance from average.

### Chapter 9: The Normal Distribution

**Core concepts:** The bell curve, 68-95-99.7 rule, z-scores (conceptual), why it matters

- 9.1 — The Bell Curve
  - Many things in nature and human behavior cluster around an average with fewer values far away. Heights, test scores, the weight of apples from a tree — plot them and you get a bell-shaped curve. Most values are near the middle, with fewer and fewer as you move toward the extremes.
- 9.2 — The 68-95-99.7 Rule
  - In a normal distribution: about 68% of values are within 1 standard deviation of the mean. About 95% are within 2. About 99.7% are within 3. This means values more than 2 standard deviations from the mean are unusual (5%), and more than 3 are very rare (0.3%).
- 9.3 — What "Normal" Really Means
  - "Normal" doesn't mean "good" or "expected" — it's a specific mathematical shape. Not everything is normally distributed. Income is skewed. Earthquake magnitudes follow a different pattern. Assuming data is normal when it isn't leads to wrong conclusions. Always check.
- 9.4 — Percentiles and the Bell Curve
  - A percentile tells you what percentage of values fall below yours. If your score is at the 90th percentile, you scored higher than 90% of people. In a normal distribution, percentiles map predictably to the bell curve — the 50th percentile is the mean, the 84th percentile is about 1 standard deviation above.

### Chapter 10: Correlation

**Core concepts:** Scatter plots, positive and negative correlation, strength, correlation is not causation

- 10.1 — Scatter Plots
  - A scatter plot shows two numerical variables — one on each axis. Each data point is a dot. Plot height vs. shoe size for your classmates and you'll see a pattern: taller people tend to have bigger feet. That pattern is a correlation.
- 10.2 — Positive and Negative Correlation
  - Positive correlation: as one variable goes up, the other tends to go up too (height and shoe size, study time and test scores). Negative correlation: as one goes up, the other tends to go down (hours of TV and test scores, price and quantity sold). No correlation: no pattern at all.
- 10.3 — Strength of Correlation
  - A strong correlation means the dots cluster tightly around a line. A weak correlation means the dots are loosely scattered with a vague trend. Strength tells you how reliable the relationship is. A strong correlation lets you make better predictions. A weak one doesn't tell you much.
- 10.4 — Correlation Is Not Causation
  - Ice cream sales and drowning deaths are positively correlated. Does ice cream cause drowning? No — hot weather causes both. Correlation means two things move together. Causation means one actually causes the other. This is one of the most important distinctions in all of statistics, and one of the most commonly confused.

### Chapter 11: Misleading Statistics

**Core concepts:** Misleading graphs, cherry-picking, missing context, loaded questions

- 11.1 — Misleading Graphs
  - A bar chart where the y-axis starts at 98 instead of 0 makes a tiny difference look enormous. A pie chart with slices that don't add to 100%. A graph with no labels. Manipulating the visual presentation of data can completely change how it feels, even when the numbers are technically correct.
- 11.2 — Cherry-Picking Data
  - Choosing only the data that supports your argument and ignoring the rest is cherry-picking. "Sales are up 50%!" (compared to the single worst month in company history). The number is true. The conclusion is misleading. Always ask: compared to what? Over what time period? What data was left out?
- 11.3 — Missing Context
  - "9 out of 10 dentists recommend this toothpaste." How many dentists were asked? What were the other options? Did they recommend it as the best, or just as acceptable? A statistic without context is almost meaningless. The context is where the truth lives.
- 11.4 — Loaded Survey Questions
  - "Don't you agree that school lunches should be healthier?" leads people toward "yes." "Should the government spend more taxpayer money on school lunches?" leads toward "no." Same topic, different framing, different results. How a question is worded can determine the answer. Good surveys use neutral language.

### Chapter 12: Experiments and Causation

**Core concepts:** Control groups, variables, random assignment, confounders, why experiments matter

- 12.1 — The Problem with Observation
  - Kids who eat breakfast get better grades. Does breakfast cause better grades? Maybe — or maybe families that serve breakfast also help with homework, have stable routines, and get more sleep. Observation shows correlation. To find causation, you need an experiment.
- 12.2 — Control Groups
  - An experiment compares a group that gets the treatment to a group that doesn't (the control group). If the treatment group does better, and everything else was the same, the treatment likely caused the difference. The control group is what makes the comparison valid.
- 12.3 — Variables and Random Assignment
  - The variable you change on purpose is the independent variable. The outcome you measure is the dependent variable. Everything else should be kept the same. Random assignment — randomly deciding who gets the treatment — prevents hidden differences between groups from contaminating the results.
- 12.4 — Confounders
  - A confounder is a hidden variable that affects both the thing you're studying and the outcome. Kids who read more have bigger vocabularies — but kids who read more also tend to have more books at home, which means higher income, which means access to more educational resources. The confounder (income) could be driving both. Experiments with random assignment help control for confounders.

### Chapter 13: Statistical Significance

**Core concepts:** Could this be random chance? Significance (conceptual), p-values (intuitive), replication

- 13.1 — Could It Just Be Chance?
  - You flip a coin 10 times and get 7 heads. Is the coin unfair, or was that just luck? Statistical significance is a way of asking: "Is this result unusual enough that it probably isn't just random chance?" It's the line between "interesting" and "probably real."
- 13.2 — What "Significant" Means in Statistics
  - In everyday language, "significant" means "important." In statistics, it means "unlikely to have happened by chance alone." A statistically significant result isn't necessarily a big or important result — it just means the pattern is probably real, not a fluke. Small effects can be statistically significant with a large enough sample.
- 13.3 — The P-Value (Intuitive Version)
  - The p-value asks: "If there were really no effect, how likely would I be to see a result this extreme?" A small p-value (like 0.01) means the result would be very unlikely by chance — so the effect is probably real. A large p-value (like 0.40) means the result could easily be random noise.
- 13.4 — Replication: The Real Test
  - One significant result could be a fluke. If other researchers repeat the experiment and get the same result, confidence grows. Replication is the real test of whether a finding is true. A result that can't be replicated — no matter how significant it looked initially — probably isn't real.

### Chapter 14: Statistics in the Real World

**Core concepts:** Polls and elections, sports statistics, medical studies, how statistics are reported

- 14.1 — Polls and Elections
  - "Candidate A leads with 52% support." That number comes from a sample with a margin of error — maybe ±3%. So the real support could be anywhere from 49% to 55%. Understanding margins of error helps you interpret polls accurately instead of treating them as exact predictions.
- 14.2 — Sports Statistics
  - Batting averages, free throw percentages, quarterback ratings, goals per game — sports are full of statistics. They measure performance, compare players, and help teams make decisions. Sports statistics are a great way to practice reading and interpreting numbers because the context is fun and familiar.
- 14.3 — Medical Studies
  - "A new study shows that eating blueberries reduces heart risk by 15%." Was it an experiment or an observation? How big was the sample? What was the control group? Was it replicated? Medical statistics affect real health decisions — understanding how to evaluate them is genuinely important.
- 14.4 — How Statistics Are Reported
  - Journalists, politicians, and advertisers use statistics to make points. They might highlight the most dramatic number, ignore the context, or confuse correlation with causation. The tools you've learned — asking about sample size, bias, significance, and context — are your defenses against being misled.

### Chapter 15: Thinking with Data

**Core concepts:** The statistical toolkit, asking good questions, healthy skepticism, what you've learned

- 15.1 — The Statistical Toolkit
  - Review the core tools: types of data, collection methods, sampling, organizing, graphing, center (mean, median, mode), spread (range, IQR, standard deviation), the normal distribution, correlation, experiments, significance. Together, these tools let you understand almost any dataset.
- 15.2 — Asking Good Questions
  - Good statistics starts with good questions. "What do I want to know?" "What data would answer that?" "How should I collect it?" "How should I analyze it?" "What could go wrong?" The tools are important, but the questions you ask matter even more.
- 15.3 — Healthy Skepticism
  - Not every statistic you see is trustworthy. Ask: Where did the data come from? How was it collected? What's the sample size? Is there bias? Was it an experiment or observation? Is the graph showing me the full picture? You don't need to distrust everything — just think before accepting.
- 15.4 — What You Know Now
  - You can collect data, organize it, summarize it, visualize it, and draw conclusions from it. You can spot misleading statistics, understand correlation and causation, interpret polls and studies, and evaluate claims other people make with numbers. That's a powerful set of skills — and one most adults don't have.
