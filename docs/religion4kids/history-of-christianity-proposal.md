# History of Christianity for Kids -- Book Proposal

## Why This Book

Latter-day Saint kids learn about the Great Apostasy in a few sentences: "The apostles were killed, the truth was lost, and the gospel had to be restored." But what actually happened during those 1,800 years? How did the simple church Jesus established become the medieval Catholic Church? Who were the people who tried to fix things along the way? What were the councils, the creeds, the crusades, and the Reformation? How did we get from twelve apostles to thousands of denominations?

This book gives young Latter-day Saints a continuous narrative of Christianity from the apostolic church through the conditions that led to the Restoration. It's the story of what happened to Christ's church after the apostles -- how doctrine changed, how authority was lost, how brave people tried to reform what they could, and how God prepared the world over centuries for a full restoration.

## Perspective

Written for families and children who are active members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. The Apostasy is treated as real -- priesthood authority was lost, doctrines were changed, and the fulness of the gospel was not on the earth. But this is not an anti-Catholic or anti-Protestant book. Many sincere, courageous people did their best with the light they had. The reformers are treated with respect. The medieval monks who preserved learning are acknowledged. The Pilgrims and founders who established religious freedom are honored. The tone is matter-of-fact: here's what happened, here's what changed, and here's why the Restoration was necessary.

## Series Context

One book covering the history of Christianity from the apostolic era through the Second Great Awakening (~30 AD - ~1820). Bridges the gap between The Ministry of Jesus Christ for Kids and The Restoration for Kids. Pairs naturally with History of Israel for Kids (which ends at 70 AD) and the Restoration book (which picks up with Joseph Smith).

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- Historical and scripture references included naturally
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7-12

## Prerequisites

- None required. Stands alone, but pairs well with the other religion books.

## History of Christianity for Kids

**Subtitle:** From the Apostles to the Restoration

### Chapter 1: The Church Jesus Built

**Core concepts:** What Jesus established, the apostles, Pentecost, the early Church's organization

- 1.1 -- What Jesus Left Behind
  - When Jesus ascended into heaven, He didn't leave behind a book of rules. He left behind a church -- a living organization led by twelve apostles who held priesthood authority, received revelation, and administered ordinances. The apostles had been trained by Jesus Himself for three years. They had the authority to preach, baptize, confirm, ordain, and receive revelation for the Church. This is the pattern Latter-day Saints recognize: a church led by living prophets and apostles.

- 1.2 -- The Day of Pentecost
  - Fifty days after the Resurrection, the apostles were gathered in Jerusalem when the Holy Ghost descended upon them with power (Acts 2). They spoke in tongues, and people from many nations understood them. Peter stood and preached, and about 3,000 people were baptized that day. The Church of Jesus Christ was launched. The apostles were no longer hiding -- they were preaching boldly, performing miracles, and growing the Church rapidly.

- 1.3 -- How the Early Church Worked
  - The early Church was organized simply. Apostles led and received revelation. Bishops, elders, deacons, and teachers served in local congregations (see Ephesians 4:11, 1 Timothy 3). Members met in homes, shared meals, took the sacrament (called the Lord's Supper), and studied the apostles' teachings. They practiced baptism by immersion for the remission of sins. They laid hands on people to confer the Holy Ghost. The organization was recognizable to any Latter-day Saint.

- 1.4 -- A Church Under Pressure
  - From the very beginning, the Church faced two kinds of pressure: external persecution and internal disagreement. The Jewish leaders who had crucified Jesus now persecuted His followers. Stephen was stoned to death -- the first Christian martyr (Acts 7). Meanwhile, within the Church, questions arose: Did Gentile converts need to follow the Law of Moses? The apostles held a council in Jerusalem and resolved it through revelation (Acts 15). As long as the apostles were alive, the Church had a way to settle disputes: ask God through His authorized servants.

**Test:** 4 questions on what Jesus established, Pentecost, early Church organization, and the pressures it faced.

### Chapter 2: The Apostles Spread the Gospel

**Core concepts:** Paul's missions, Peter's leadership, growth across the Roman Empire, the epistles

- 2.1 -- Paul: From Persecutor to Apostle
  - Saul of Tarsus was one of the most aggressive persecutors of the early Church. He was present at Stephen's stoning and actively hunted Christians. Then, on the road to Damascus, the resurrected Christ appeared to him in a blinding light: "Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me?" (Acts 9:4). Saul was converted, took the name Paul, and became the most prolific missionary in Christian history. His conversion shows that no one is beyond the reach of Christ.

- 2.2 -- Paul's Missionary Journeys
  - Paul made three major missionary journeys across the Roman Empire -- to Asia Minor (modern Turkey), Greece, Macedonia, and eventually Rome. He established churches in Corinth, Ephesus, Philippi, Thessalonica, and many other cities. He traveled by ship, on foot, and endured beatings, imprisonments, shipwrecks, and constant danger (2 Corinthians 11:23-28). Paul carried the gospel far beyond the Jewish world and into the heart of the Gentile Roman Empire.

- 2.3 -- Peter and the Other Apostles
  - Peter was the senior apostle -- the one Jesus had called the "rock" upon which He would build His Church (Matthew 16:18). Peter led the Church from Jerusalem, preached to Jews and Gentiles, and received the revelation that the gospel should go to all nations (Acts 10). The other apostles scattered across the known world -- tradition places them in India, Ethiopia, Persia, and beyond. The gospel was spreading, but the apostles were spreading thin.

- 2.4 -- The Epistles
  - Paul, Peter, James, John, and others wrote letters -- epistles -- to the churches they had established. These letters addressed doctrinal questions, corrected errors, encouraged the faithful, and warned against false teachers. The epistles became part of the New Testament. They show a Church that was already struggling with internal problems: false teachers, doctrinal confusion, and members drifting from the truth. The apostles were constantly correcting course -- which raises the question: what happened when they were no longer there to correct it?

**Test:** 4 questions on Paul's conversion, his missionary journeys, Peter and the other apostles, and the epistles.

### Chapter 3: Persecution and Martyrdom

**Core concepts:** Roman persecution, why Christians were targeted, the martyrs, faith under fire

- 3.1 -- Why Rome Persecuted Christians
  - The Roman Empire tolerated many religions, but Christianity was different. Christians refused to worship the emperor. They refused to participate in pagan festivals. They claimed there was only one God. They met in secret, which made Romans suspicious. And they were growing fast. Rome saw Christians as a threat to public order. Persecution ranged from local harassment to empire-wide campaigns of arrest, torture, and execution.

- 3.2 -- The Early Martyrs
  - The word "martyr" comes from the Greek word for "witness." Early Christians who died for their faith were considered the ultimate witnesses. Peter was reportedly crucified upside down in Rome. Paul was beheaded. James (the brother of Jesus, leader of the Jerusalem church) was killed. Tradition says most of the original apostles died as martyrs. The Church was being led by men who knew they might die for it -- and did.

- 3.3 -- The Great Persecutions
  - The worst Roman persecutions came in waves. Emperor Nero blamed Christians for the fire of Rome in 64 AD and had them killed in horrific ways. Emperor Diocletian launched the most systematic persecution around 303 AD -- destroying churches, burning scriptures, and demanding that Christians sacrifice to Roman gods or die. Thousands were killed. Yet the Church kept growing. One early writer said, "The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church."

- 3.4 -- Faith Under Fire
  - The early Christians' willingness to die rather than deny Christ is remarkable. They believed in the resurrection and in eternal life, and that belief made death less terrifying than denial. Their courage converted others -- Roman soldiers, jailers, and onlookers sometimes became Christians after watching believers die with peace and faith. Persecution was meant to destroy Christianity. Instead, it spread it.

**Test:** 4 questions on why Rome persecuted Christians, the early martyrs, the great persecutions, and how faith survived.

### Chapter 4: The Apostles Are Lost

**Core concepts:** Deaths of the apostles, loss of priesthood keys, no way to call new apostles, the beginning of the Apostasy

- 4.1 -- One by One
  - By the end of the first century, all the original apostles were dead except John (and Latter-day Saints believe John was translated -- he did not die). As each apostle was killed, the priesthood keys he held were lost. There was no mechanism in the Church to replace apostles without apostolic authority. When Peter died, no one had the authority to call a new Peter. The chain of authority that went back to Jesus Himself was breaking, link by link.

- 4.2 -- What Was Lost
  - When the apostles died, several things were lost: the authority to receive revelation for the whole Church, the authority to ordain and set apart, the authority to call new apostles, and the priesthood keys that governed temple ordinances. Local leaders remained -- bishops and elders -- but they didn't hold the keys the apostles had held. The Church still existed, but it was like a ship without a captain. The structure was there, but the authority to steer it was gone.

- 4.3 -- False Teachers
  - Even before the apostles died, false teachers were a problem. Paul warned Timothy about people who "will not endure sound doctrine" (2 Timothy 4:3). John warned about "antichrists" who had gone out from the Church (1 John 2:18-19). Peter warned about "false prophets" who would bring "damnable heresies" (2 Peter 2:1). With the apostles gone, there was no authoritative voice to correct these teachers. False doctrine spread unchecked.

- 4.4 -- The Beginning of the Apostasy
  - The Great Apostasy was not a single dramatic event. It was a gradual process -- a slow drift away from the truth over decades and centuries. Doctrines were debated and changed. Ordinances were modified. The simple organization Jesus established was replaced by an increasingly complex hierarchy. Latter-day Saints don't blame the people who lived through the Apostasy. Most were sincere. But without prophets and apostles, the fulness of the gospel could not be preserved. It had to be restored.

**Test:** 4 questions on the deaths of the apostles, what was lost, false teachers, and the beginning of the Apostasy.

### Chapter 5: The Early Church Fathers

**Core concepts:** Who the Church Fathers were, how doctrine began to change, Greek philosophy mixes with Christianity, the loss of simple truths

- 5.1 -- Who Were the Church Fathers?
  - After the apostles died, the Church was led by men historians call the "Church Fathers" -- bishops, theologians, and writers who shaped Christian teaching in the second, third, and fourth centuries. Men like Clement of Rome, Ignatius of Antioch, Origen, and Augustine were intelligent, devoted, and sincere. But they were not apostles. They did not have priesthood keys. They could not receive binding revelation for the Church. Their authority came from scholarship and position, not from God.

- 5.2 -- Greek Philosophy Enters
  - Many of the Church Fathers were educated in Greek philosophy -- the ideas of Plato, Aristotle, and the Stoics. As they tried to explain Christianity to a Greek-speaking world, they increasingly used philosophical concepts to describe God, the soul, and salvation. The simple teaching that God is our literal Father with a glorified body was replaced by abstract ideas about an immaterial, unknowable being. The God of the philosophers was not the God who appeared to Joseph Smith in the Sacred Grove.

- 5.3 -- Doctrines Begin to Change
  - Over the centuries, many original teachings were altered. Baptism by immersion was gradually replaced by sprinkling. Baptism of infants began, even though the original Church baptized only those old enough to believe and repent. The lay priesthood was replaced by a professional clergy. The idea that God, Jesus, and the Holy Ghost are three separate beings was gradually replaced by the doctrine of the Trinity -- one God in three persons. Each change moved the Church further from what Jesus had established.

- 5.4 -- The Loss of Simple Truths
  - The early Church taught that God has a body, that humans can become like God, that there is a premortal existence, and that baptism for the dead is real (Paul mentions it in 1 Corinthians 15:29). All of these teachings were gradually lost or rejected during the centuries after the apostles. Latter-day Saints don't believe these truths were invented by Joseph Smith. They were restored -- brought back from where they had always been in the original gospel of Jesus Christ.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Church Fathers, Greek philosophy's influence, specific doctrines that changed, and the loss of simple truths.

### Chapter 6: Constantine and the Empire

**Core concepts:** The Edict of Milan, Christianity becomes legal, the Council of Nicaea, the church and the state merge

- 6.1 -- Constantine
  - In 312 AD, the Roman Emperor Constantine reportedly saw a vision of the cross before a crucial battle. He won the battle and credited the Christian God. The next year, he issued the Edict of Milan (313 AD), which made Christianity legal throughout the Roman Empire. Almost overnight, Christianity went from a persecuted minority to a favored religion. Persecution ended. Churches could be built openly. Christians could worship freely. But there was a cost.

- 6.2 -- The Cost of Imperial Favor
  - When the emperor became the Church's protector, the Church became entangled with politics. Constantine saw religious unity as essential for political unity. He wanted the Church to agree on doctrine -- not for spiritual reasons, but for the stability of his empire. Church leaders who had been hiding in catacombs were suddenly dining with emperors. Power, wealth, and political influence entered the Church in ways that would shape it for centuries.

- 6.3 -- The Council of Nicaea (325 AD)
  - Constantine called the Council of Nicaea to settle a major theological dispute: Was Jesus the same substance as God the Father, or a separate, created being? About 300 bishops debated and voted. The result was the Nicene Creed, which declared the Father and the Son to be "of one substance." This creed defined mainstream Christian belief about God from that point forward. It was a decision by committee, influenced by an emperor, that contradicted what Joseph Smith would later see with his own eyes: two separate, distinct beings.

- 6.4 -- Christianity Becomes the State Religion
  - By 380 AD, Emperor Theodosius declared Christianity the official religion of the Roman Empire. Pagan temples were closed. Other religions were suppressed. Christianity went from being the persecuted to being the persecutors. The union of church and state meant that religious disagreements became political crimes. This was a long way from twelve apostles meeting in an upper room. The Church was powerful, wealthy, and influential -- but further than ever from what Jesus had established.

**Test:** 4 questions on Constantine, the cost of imperial favor, the Council of Nicaea, and Christianity becoming the state religion.

### Chapter 7: The Creeds and Councils

**Core concepts:** The Nicene Creed, the Trinity, later councils, how the nature of God was redefined

- 7.1 -- What Is a Creed?
  - A creed is a formal statement of belief. The early Church didn't have creeds -- the apostles received revelation directly from God. After the apostles were gone, church leaders needed a way to define what "true" belief was. They wrote creeds. The problem: without prophets and revelation, these creeds were decided by debate and majority vote. Sincere men disagreed, and the winners wrote the creeds. Dissenters were labeled heretics.

- 7.2 -- The Trinity
  - The most important creed was the Nicene Creed (325 AD, expanded at Constantinople in 381 AD), which defined the doctrine of the Trinity: God the Father, God the Son, and God the Holy Spirit are three persons in one God -- one substance, one being. This doctrine attempted to explain the relationship between the Father and the Son using Greek philosophical terms. Latter-day Saints do not accept the Trinity. We believe the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three separate beings, united in purpose -- as Joseph Smith saw in the First Vision.

- 7.3 -- More Councils, More Debates
  - The Council of Nicaea was not the last. The Council of Constantinople (381 AD), the Council of Ephesus (431 AD), and the Council of Chalcedon (451 AD) continued to debate the nature of Christ, the role of Mary, and other doctrines. Each council produced winners and losers. Bishops who disagreed were exiled. Entire communities were declared heretical. Christianity was being defined by politics as much as by theology.

- 7.4 -- What This Means
  - The creeds and councils show what happens when a church tries to function without revelation. Sincere, intelligent people debated endlessly about questions that could have been answered in moments by a prophet with access to God. The nature of God is not a question that should be decided by a vote. It should be revealed by God Himself. That is exactly what happened in the Sacred Grove -- and it contradicted centuries of creedal tradition.

**Test:** 4 questions on what creeds are, the Trinity doctrine, the later councils, and why this matters.

### Chapter 8: The Rise of the Catholic Church

**Core concepts:** The Pope, the hierarchy, monasticism, preserving learning, the medieval Church's power

- 8.1 -- The Bishop of Rome Becomes the Pope
  - As the Roman Empire weakened and eventually fell (476 AD in the West), the Bishop of Rome became increasingly powerful. He claimed authority over all other bishops, based on the idea that Peter had been the first Bishop of Rome. Over time, the Bishop of Rome became known as the Pope -- from the Latin word for "father." The Pope became the head of Western Christianity, with authority over doctrine, church appointments, and even political matters. It was a very different structure from twelve equal apostles led by revelation.

- 8.2 -- The Church Hierarchy
  - The Catholic Church developed an elaborate hierarchy: Pope, cardinals, archbishops, bishops, priests, monks, and nuns. The priesthood became a professional class -- only trained, celibate men could serve. Ordinary members could not read the Bible (it was in Latin, which most people didn't speak) and had to rely on priests to explain the faith. The distance between the people and God grew wider. Latter-day Saints believe that every worthy member can hold the priesthood and have direct access to God through prayer and personal revelation.

- 8.3 -- Monasteries: Preserving What Was Good
  - Not everything about the medieval Church was bad. Monks and nuns in monasteries preserved the Bible, copied manuscripts, maintained libraries, ran schools, and cared for the sick and poor. Without the monasteries, much of ancient learning -- including the scriptures -- would have been lost. God uses imperfect institutions and imperfect people to accomplish His purposes. The monks didn't have the fulness of the gospel, but they preserved what they had.

- 8.4 -- The Church's Power
  - By the Middle Ages, the Catholic Church was the most powerful institution in Europe. It owned vast lands, collected taxes (called tithes), crowned kings, and excommunicated rulers who defied it. The Pope could place entire countries under interdict -- banning church services until the ruler complied. This level of political power was a long way from "Render unto Caesar the things which are Caesar's" (Matthew 22:21). The Church had become an empire.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Pope, the hierarchy, monasteries, and the Church's political power.

### Chapter 9: The Great Schism

**Core concepts:** East vs. West, cultural and theological differences, the split of 1054, Orthodox and Catholic

- 9.1 -- Two Halves of Christianity
  - As the Roman Empire split into West (Rome) and East (Constantinople), Christianity split too. The Western Church, centered in Rome, spoke Latin. The Eastern Church, centered in Constantinople (modern Istanbul), spoke Greek. They had different traditions, different worship styles, and increasingly different ideas about authority. The Western Church followed the Pope. The Eastern Church was led by patriarchs who rejected the Pope's claim to supreme authority.

- 9.2 -- What They Disagreed About
  - The disagreements were both theological and practical. Did the Holy Spirit proceed from the Father alone (Eastern view) or from the Father and the Son (Western view)? Should priests be allowed to marry (Eastern yes, Western no)? Should leavened or unleavened bread be used in the sacrament? Should the Pope have authority over the Eastern churches? These seem like small questions, but centuries of cultural distance and political rivalry made them explosive.

- 9.3 -- The Split of 1054
  - In 1054, tensions boiled over. The Pope's representative traveled to Constantinople and placed a document on the altar of the Hagia Sophia, excommunicating the Eastern patriarch. The patriarch responded by excommunicating the Western delegation. Christianity was now formally divided into the Roman Catholic Church in the West and the Eastern Orthodox Church in the East. The split has never been fully healed.

- 9.4 -- What the Schism Shows
  - The Great Schism shows what happens when there is no prophet to unify the Church. Without revelation, disagreements become permanent divisions. Both sides believed they were right. Both sides had sincere leaders. But neither side had the authority to receive revelation for the whole Church. The Apostasy didn't just change doctrine -- it fractured the body of Christ into competing organizations, each claiming to be the true church.

**Test:** 4 questions on the two halves of Christianity, what they disagreed about, the split of 1054, and what the Schism shows.

### Chapter 10: The Medieval Church

**Core concepts:** The Crusades, cathedrals, indulgences, corruption, the Inquisition

- 10.1 -- The Crusades
  - From 1096 to about 1291, European Christians launched a series of military campaigns called the Crusades to recapture the Holy Land (especially Jerusalem) from Muslim control. Tens of thousands marched, fought, and died. Some crusaders were motivated by genuine faith. Others were motivated by land, wealth, and adventure. The Crusades were violent, often brutal, and left a legacy of bitterness between Christians and Muslims that persists to this day.

- 10.2 -- Cathedrals and Devotion
  - The medieval period also produced extraordinary devotion. Communities spent generations building massive cathedrals -- Notre-Dame, Chartres, Canterbury -- that still stand today. Ordinary people gave their labor, their money, and their lives to build houses of worship. Pilgrims traveled hundreds of miles to visit holy sites. Scholars like Thomas Aquinas wrote profound works of theology. Even during the Apostasy, many people sincerely loved God and sought to serve Him with the light they had.

- 10.3 -- Indulgences and Corruption
  - The medieval Church developed the practice of selling indulgences -- payments that supposedly reduced the punishment for sins. The idea was that the Pope had access to a "treasury of merit" built up by the saints, and could distribute it for a price. In practice, it meant the Church was selling forgiveness. Some church leaders lived in luxury while their people struggled. Corruption at the highest levels -- including Popes who were more politicians than spiritual leaders -- eroded trust.

- 10.4 -- The Inquisition
  - The Catholic Church established the Inquisition to investigate and punish heresy -- any belief that contradicted official church teaching. People could be arrested, tortured, and executed for disagreeing with the Church. The Inquisition operated for centuries in various forms across Europe. It was a tool of control, not of conversion. The idea that you could torture someone into believing the right thing about God was as far from the gospel of Jesus Christ as you could get.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Crusades, cathedrals and devotion, indulgences, and the Inquisition.

### Chapter 11: Early Voices of Reform

**Core concepts:** John Wycliffe, Jan Hus, the desire for change, the cost of speaking up

- 11.1 -- People Who Questioned
  - Throughout the medieval period, courageous individuals questioned the Church's practices. They didn't have priesthood authority to restore the gospel, but they recognized that something was wrong. They wanted the Bible in the people's language. They opposed indulgences. They challenged the Pope's absolute authority. These early reformers didn't succeed in their lifetimes, but they planted seeds that would grow into the Reformation.

- 11.2 -- John Wycliffe
  - John Wycliffe was an English scholar in the 1300s who believed the Bible should be available to everyone, not just the clergy. He organized the first complete translation of the Bible into English. He criticized indulgences, the wealth of the clergy, and the Pope's authority. The Church condemned his writings. After his death, his bones were dug up, burned, and thrown into a river. But his ideas survived -- and his followers, called the Lollards, continued to spread them.

- 11.3 -- Jan Hus
  - Jan Hus was a Czech priest in the early 1400s who was inspired by Wycliffe's ideas. He preached against corruption, called for reform, and said the Bible -- not the Pope -- should be the final authority. He was summoned to the Council of Constance, promised safe passage, then arrested, tried for heresy, and burned at the stake in 1415. His last words were reportedly a prayer. His followers, the Hussites, fought wars for decades to defend his teachings.

- 11.4 -- Why These People Matter
  - Wycliffe, Hus, and others like them didn't restore the gospel -- they didn't have the authority. But they prepared the way. They showed that people were hungry for truth, willing to die for it, and unwilling to accept corruption in God's name. Latter-day Saints believe God was working through these individuals as part of His long preparation for the Restoration. They opened doors that would eventually lead to the Sacred Grove.

**Test:** 4 questions on early reformers generally, Wycliffe, Hus, and why these people matter for the Restoration.

### Chapter 12: Martin Luther and the Reformation

**Core concepts:** The 95 Theses, Luther's stand, the printing press, Protestantism is born

- 12.1 -- Martin Luther
  - On October 31, 1517, a German monk named Martin Luther nailed a document called the 95 Theses to the door of a church in Wittenberg, Germany. The document challenged the sale of indulgences and other Church practices Luther believed were corrupt. Luther didn't intend to start a new church -- he wanted to reform the Catholic Church from within. But his ideas sparked a revolution that would split Western Christianity permanently.

- 12.2 -- Luther's Key Ideas
  - Luther taught that salvation comes through faith in Christ, not through buying indulgences or doing works prescribed by the Church. He said the Bible -- not the Pope -- is the ultimate authority. He insisted that every believer has direct access to God and doesn't need a priest as an intermediary. He translated the Bible into German so ordinary people could read it. These ideas were radical in 1517, but they were actually closer to what the original Church had taught.

- 12.3 -- The Printing Press Changes Everything
  - Luther's ideas spread faster than any previous reform movement because of the printing press, invented by Gutenberg around 1440. Within weeks, copies of the 95 Theses were circulating across Germany. Within months, they were all over Europe. For the first time, ideas could spread faster than the Church could suppress them. The printing press was the most important technology of the Reformation -- and Latter-day Saints believe God inspired its invention for this purpose.

- 12.4 -- Protestantism Is Born
  - The Catholic Church tried to silence Luther, but it was too late. Luther was protected by German princes who saw political advantage in breaking with Rome. His followers became known as Protestants -- those who "protested" against the Catholic Church. Within a generation, entire nations had left the Catholic Church. A movement that began with one monk's complaints about indulgences had shattered the religious unity of Western Europe.

**Test:** 4 questions on Luther and the 95 Theses, his key ideas, the printing press, and the birth of Protestantism.

### Chapter 13: The Reformation Spreads

**Core concepts:** Calvin, Zwingli, Henry VIII, the denominational explosion, what the Reformation did and didn't fix

- 13.1 -- John Calvin
  - In Geneva, Switzerland, a French theologian named John Calvin built on Luther's ideas and took them further. Calvin taught predestination -- the idea that God has already decided who will be saved and who will not. He established a strict religious community in Geneva where church and government worked together to enforce moral standards. Calvin's ideas influenced the Puritans, the Presbyterians, and many other groups. His theology spread across Europe and eventually to America.

- 13.2 -- Other Reformers
  - Ulrich Zwingli led the Reformation in Zurich, Switzerland. The Anabaptists insisted on adult baptism by immersion and separation of church and state -- ideas that got them persecuted by both Catholics and other Protestants. John Knox brought Calvinist ideas to Scotland and established Presbyterianism. Each reformer emphasized different doctrines, and each movement developed its own practices and structures.

- 13.3 -- Henry VIII and the Church of England
  - In England, the Reformation happened for political rather than theological reasons. King Henry VIII wanted to divorce his wife, and the Pope refused. Henry declared himself the head of the Church of England, breaking with Rome. The Church of England kept much of Catholic practice but rejected the Pope's authority. Later, under Henry's children, the English church moved further toward Protestantism. The Church of England shows that the Reformation was sometimes about power as much as principle.

- 13.4 -- What the Reformation Fixed and What It Didn't
  - The Reformation did important things: it put the Bible in people's hands, challenged corruption, and established the principle that individuals can have a relationship with God without a priestly intermediary. But it didn't restore priesthood authority. It didn't restore temple ordinances. It didn't restore the true nature of God. It created more churches, but none of them had apostles, prophets, or revelation. The Reformation was necessary preparation, but it was not the Restoration.

**Test:** 4 questions on Calvin, other reformers, Henry VIII, and what the Reformation did and didn't accomplish.

### Chapter 14: Religious Freedom and the New World

**Core concepts:** Persecution of dissenters, Pilgrims and Puritans, colonial America, the First Amendment

- 14.1 -- Persecution Continues
  - The Reformation didn't bring religious freedom. In most European countries, the official religion (whether Catholic or Protestant) was enforced by law. Dissenters were fined, imprisoned, or killed. Catholics persecuted Protestants. Protestants persecuted Catholics. And both persecuted smaller groups like the Anabaptists, the Quakers, and anyone else who disagreed. Religious freedom was not the norm -- it was the exception, and it was dangerous.

- 14.2 -- Seeking Freedom in the New World
  - Many people who wanted to worship freely left Europe for America. The Pilgrims sailed on the Mayflower in 1620 and established Plymouth Colony in Massachusetts. The Puritans followed and built the Massachusetts Bay Colony. Quakers settled in Pennsylvania. Catholics settled in Maryland. Each group came seeking religious freedom -- though some of them, ironically, denied that freedom to others once they were in charge.

- 14.3 -- Colonial America and Religious Diversity
  - By the 1700s, the American colonies had more religious diversity than anywhere in Europe. Baptists, Methodists, Presbyterians, Quakers, Catholics, Anglicans, and many others lived side by side. This diversity created both conflict and a growing conviction that government should not control religion. The idea of religious liberty -- the right to worship according to your conscience -- was taking root in American soil.

- 14.4 -- The First Amendment
  - When the United States was founded, the First Amendment to the Constitution guaranteed that "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof." This was revolutionary. For the first time in modern history, a major nation declared that government would not control religion. Latter-day Saints believe God inspired the founding of America and the establishment of religious freedom specifically so that the Restoration could take place (1 Nephi 13:12-19, D&C 101:77-80).

**Test:** 4 questions on persecution of dissenters, seeking freedom in America, colonial religious diversity, and the First Amendment.

### Chapter 15: The Stage Is Set

**Core concepts:** The Second Great Awakening, the Burned-over District, what was still missing, the need for Restoration

- 15.1 -- The Second Great Awakening
  - In the late 1700s and early 1800s, a wave of religious enthusiasm swept across America. Preachers held massive outdoor revivals and camp meetings. Thousands were converted, baptized, and joined various churches. This movement is called the Second Great Awakening. It produced genuine spiritual hunger -- people wanted to know God, wanted to be saved, and wanted to find the true church. The problem was that every denomination claimed to be right and said all the others were wrong.

- 15.2 -- The Burned-over District
  - Western New York was especially intense. So many revivals swept through the region that it was called the "Burned-over District." Baptists, Methodists, Presbyterians, and others competed for converts. Families were divided. Communities argued. A young boy named Joseph Smith lived in the middle of it all and was deeply confused by the conflicting claims. He wanted to know which church was right -- and he couldn't figure it out by listening to preachers.

- 15.3 -- What Was Still Missing
  - After 1,800 years, Christianity had the Bible, sincere believers, beautiful churches, and many good people trying to follow Christ. But it was still missing what had been lost when the apostles died: priesthood authority, living prophets, binding revelation, temple ordinances, the true nature of God, the plan of salvation, and the fulness of the gospel. No amount of reform could bring these back. They had to be restored by God Himself.

- 15.4 -- The Need for Restoration
  - This is where the story of Christianity connects to the story of the Restoration. God had been preparing for this moment for centuries: the printing press, the Reformation, the discovery of America, the establishment of religious freedom. Every step was necessary. And now, in a grove of trees near Palmyra, New York, a fourteen-year-old boy was about to read James 1:5 and do something no one had done in 1,800 years -- ask God directly which church was true. The answer would change the world.
