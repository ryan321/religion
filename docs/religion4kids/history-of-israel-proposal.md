# History of Israel for Kids -- Book Proposal

## Why This Book

Kids in the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints hear scripture stories every week -- in Primary, Come Follow Me, family scripture study, and seminary. But the stories often float in isolation, disconnected from the timeline, geography, and political context that shaped them. Who were the Philistines? Why did Israel split into two kingdoms? What was life like under Persian, Greek, and Roman rule? Why was the temple destroyed -- twice?

This book gives young Latter-day Saints a continuous historical narrative from Israel's arrival in the promised land through the destruction of the Second Temple in 70 AD. It covers roughly 1,400 years of history -- the periods, people, and events that form the backdrop of the Old Testament, the intertestamental period, and the New Testament. The goal is not to replace scripture study but to give kids the historical context that makes scripture study richer. When they read about Elijah confronting the priests of Baal, they'll know what was happening in the Northern Kingdom and why it mattered. When they read about the Savior's ministry, they'll understand the Pharisees, Sadducees, and Roman occupation that shaped the world He entered.

## Perspective

This book is written for families and children who are active members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. It treats the scriptural record as historical -- the events described in the Bible and Book of Mormon happened. Where the Book of Mormon intersects with this timeline (Lehi's departure from Jerusalem during the reign of Zedekiah, for example), those connections are noted naturally as part of the narrative. LDS terminology and scripture references (including the Book of Mormon, Doctrine and Covenants, and Pearl of Great Price) are used where appropriate. The tone is matter-of-fact, not apologetic or evangelical -- this is a history book for kids who already believe.

## Series Context

One book covering the history of Israel from the conquest of Canaan through the destruction of the temple in 70 AD. Future books in the religion series may cover the life of Christ, Church history, Book of Mormon narrative, or other topics -- but this book stands alone as a historical foundation.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7-12
- Real-world examples and comparisons that help kids grasp ancient contexts (comparing ancient Israel's size to their home state, for example)

## Prerequisites

- None required. No prior scripture knowledge assumed, though the book will feel most relevant to kids with some exposure to Bible and Book of Mormon stories.

## History of Israel for Kids

**Subtitle:** The Land, the People, and the Covenant

### Chapter 1: The Promised Land

**Core concepts:** The covenant with Abraham, why this land matters, the geography of Israel, entering Canaan

- 1.1 -- The Land God Promised
  - Hundreds of years before Israel entered Canaan, God made a covenant with Abraham -- a promise that his descendants would inherit a specific land. That promise passed to Isaac, then Jacob (whose name was changed to Israel), then to the twelve tribes. The whole story of the Old Testament is tied to this covenant and this land. Understanding the promise helps you understand everything that comes after.
- 1.2 -- What the Land Looks Like
  - Israel is small -- roughly the size of New Jersey. But the geography is dramatic: coastal plains along the Mediterranean, rolling hills in the center, the Jordan River valley, the Dead Sea (the lowest point on earth), and desert in the south (the Negev). Mountains, valleys, and trade routes crisscross this tiny land. Its location between Egypt, Mesopotamia, and the Mediterranean made it a crossroads of the ancient world -- and a target for every major empire.
- 1.3 -- Joshua and the Conquest
  - After Moses died, Joshua led Israel across the Jordan River into Canaan. The conquest began with Jericho, where the walls fell after Israel marched around the city for seven days. Over the next several years, Joshua led military campaigns across Canaan -- sometimes winning decisively, sometimes facing setbacks. The conquest was not instant or total. Many Canaanite groups remained in the land.
- 1.4 -- The Twelve Tribes Settle the Land
  - Joshua divided Canaan among the twelve tribes of Israel. Each tribe received its own territory. Judah settled in the south. Ephraim and Manasseh (the sons of Joseph) received large portions in the central hill country. Dan settled along the coast. Benjamin got a small but important strip between Judah and Ephraim. There was no central government -- each tribe governed itself. This would matter a great deal in the years ahead.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Abrahamic covenant, Israel's geography, Joshua's conquest, and the tribal allotments.

### Chapter 2: The Judges

**Core concepts:** The cycle of faithfulness and apostasy, key judges, the tribal period, no king in Israel

- 2.1 -- The Pattern
  - After Joshua died, Israel entered a period with no central leader. The book of Judges describes a repeating cycle: Israel would worship the Lord and prosper. Then they would turn to the gods of the surrounding nations. God would allow an enemy to oppress them. They would cry out for help. God would raise up a judge -- a leader, not a courtroom judge -- to deliver them. Peace would follow until the cycle started again. This pattern repeated for roughly 300 years.
- 2.2 -- Deborah, Gideon, and Samson
  - Some judges became famous. Deborah was a prophetess who led Israel to victory against the Canaanite general Sisera. Gideon defeated the Midianites with just 300 men -- God deliberately shrank his army so Israel would know the victory came from the Lord. Samson had extraordinary physical strength but made terrible personal choices, and his story is as much a warning as a triumph.
- 2.3 -- Life During the Judges
  - During this period, Israel had no king, no capital city, and no standing army. The tribes mostly governed themselves. Sometimes they cooperated; sometimes they fought each other. Worship was decentralized -- the tabernacle (the portable temple from the wilderness) was set up at Shiloh, but people often worshiped at local high places, sometimes mixing Israelite worship with Canaanite practices. The phrase that summarizes this era: "Every man did that which was right in his own eyes."
- 2.4 -- Samuel: The Last Judge
  - Samuel was the last and greatest of the judges. His mother, Hannah, dedicated him to the Lord before he was born, and he grew up serving in the tabernacle at Shiloh. Samuel served as judge, prophet, and priest. When he grew old, the people of Israel came to him with a demand that would change everything: "Give us a king."

**Test:** 4 questions on the cycle of judges, key judges, life during this period, and Samuel's role.

### Chapter 3: The United Kingdom -- Saul and David

**Core concepts:** Israel's first king, Saul's failure, David's rise, the covenant with David

- 3.1 -- Israel Wants a King
  - The elders of Israel told Samuel they wanted a king "like all the nations." Samuel warned them: a king would tax them, draft their sons into his army, and take their land. But the people insisted. God told Samuel to give them what they asked for -- and chose Saul, a tall, impressive man from the tribe of Benjamin, as Israel's first king.
- 3.2 -- Saul: A King Who Failed
  - Saul started well but gradually stopped following the Lord's commands. He offered sacrifices he wasn't authorized to offer. He disobeyed God's instructions in battle. He became jealous and paranoid, especially of a young shepherd named David. Over time, the Spirit of the Lord departed from Saul. His reign shows that looking like a king and being a good king are not the same thing.
- 3.3 -- David's Rise
  - David was the youngest son of Jesse, from the tribe of Judah. He was a shepherd, a musician, and -- famously -- the boy who killed Goliath with a sling and a stone. Saul brought David into his court, but Saul's jealousy turned violent, and David spent years as a fugitive. Through it all, David refused to harm Saul because Saul was the Lord's anointed. When Saul died in battle, David became king.
- 3.4 -- The Covenant with David
  - God made a covenant with David: his descendants would rule on the throne forever. This promise -- the Davidic covenant -- became one of the most important prophecies in all of scripture. Latter-day Saints recognize its fulfillment in Jesus Christ, who was born of the lineage of David and whose kingdom will have no end. David made Jerusalem his capital, captured the city from the Jebusites, and brought the Ark of the Covenant there, making Jerusalem the spiritual center of Israel.

**Test:** 4 questions on why Israel wanted a king, Saul's failure, David's rise, and the Davidic covenant.

### Chapter 4: The United Kingdom -- Solomon and the Temple

**Core concepts:** Solomon's wisdom and wealth, building the First Temple, the temple's significance, Solomon's decline

- 4.1 -- Solomon's Wisdom
  - David's son Solomon became king after him. God appeared to Solomon and offered him anything he wanted. Solomon asked for wisdom to govern the people. God was pleased and gave him wisdom, plus wealth and honor he hadn't asked for. Solomon became famous throughout the ancient world for his wisdom -- kings and queens (including the Queen of Sheba) traveled to hear him.
- 4.2 -- Building the Temple
  - Solomon's greatest achievement was building the temple in Jerusalem -- the permanent house of the Lord that David had wanted to build but was told his son would build instead. It took seven years to construct. The temple was built of stone and cedar, overlaid with gold. When it was finished, Solomon dedicated it in a prayer, and the glory of the Lord filled the temple so powerfully that the priests could not stand.
- 4.3 -- What the Temple Meant
  - The temple was not just a beautiful building. It was the place where God's presence dwelt among His people. Sacrifices were offered there daily. The high priest entered the Holy of Holies once a year on the Day of Atonement. For Latter-day Saints, the ancient temple is connected to the temples we have today -- sacred places where covenants are made and the Lord's presence is real. The temple was the center of Israel's spiritual life.
- 4.4 -- Solomon's Decline
  - Solomon's later years were troubled. He married many foreign wives who brought their gods with them, and Solomon built altars to those gods. He taxed the people heavily to fund his building projects. The northern tribes grew resentful. God told Solomon that because of his unfaithfulness, the kingdom would be torn apart after his death -- though for David's sake, one tribe would remain with Solomon's descendants.

**Test:** 4 questions on Solomon's wisdom, the building of the temple, the temple's significance, and Solomon's decline.

### Chapter 5: The Kingdom Divides

**Core concepts:** The split into Israel and Judah, Rehoboam and Jeroboam, two separate paths, the golden calves

- 5.1 -- The Split
  - When Solomon died, his son Rehoboam became king. The northern tribes came to him and asked for relief from Solomon's heavy taxes. Rehoboam's older advisors told him to lighten the load. His young friends told him to be even harsher. Rehoboam listened to the young friends. The northern tribes revolted. Ten tribes broke away and formed their own kingdom -- Israel -- under Jeroboam. Only Judah (and tiny Benjamin) stayed with Rehoboam. The united kingdom lasted about 120 years. It was over.
- 5.2 -- The Northern Kingdom: Israel
  - The Northern Kingdom kept the name Israel. It was larger, more fertile, and more populated. Jeroboam set up his capital at Shechem (later Samaria). But he faced a problem: the temple was in Jerusalem, in Judah. If his people kept going to Jerusalem to worship, their loyalty might shift back to Rehoboam. So Jeroboam set up two golden calves -- one at Dan in the north, one at Bethel in the south -- and told the people, "These are your gods who brought you out of Egypt." It was a political decision that became a spiritual disaster.
- 5.3 -- The Southern Kingdom: Judah
  - Judah was smaller and poorer but had two things Israel didn't: the temple and the Davidic dynasty. The kings of Judah were all descendants of David -- some good, some terrible. Judah's history is a mix of faithful kings who led reforms and wicked kings who introduced idol worship. The temple in Jerusalem remained the center of worship, which helped Judah maintain a connection to the covenant even during bad reigns.
- 5.4 -- Two Kingdoms, Two Paths
  - From this point on, the Bible tracks two parallel stories. Israel (the north) and Judah (the south) sometimes allied, sometimes fought each other, and sometimes intermarried their royal families. They faced different enemies and had different fates. The northern kingdom never had a single righteous dynasty. The southern kingdom had the covenant with David. Both kingdoms would eventually fall -- but in different ways, at different times, and with very different consequences.

**Test:** 4 questions on why the kingdom split, the Northern Kingdom's religious choices, the Southern Kingdom's advantages, and the parallel paths.

### Chapter 6: Prophets of Israel and Judah

**Core concepts:** What prophets did, Elijah and Elisha, Isaiah, Jeremiah, prophets as covenant enforcers

- 6.1 -- What Prophets Did
  - Prophets were God's messengers. Their job was not mainly to predict the future -- it was to call the people and their kings back to the covenant. They warned of consequences if the people continued in wickedness. They promised blessings if the people repented. Prophets spoke uncomfortable truths to powerful people. They were often unpopular, sometimes persecuted, and occasionally killed. Latter-day Saints understand the role of prophets as a continuing pattern -- God speaks to His people through living prophets in every dispensation.
- 6.2 -- Elijah and Elisha
  - Elijah served in the Northern Kingdom during the reign of King Ahab and Queen Jezebel, who promoted Baal worship. Elijah's most famous moment: he challenged 450 priests of Baal on Mount Carmel, and fire from heaven consumed his sacrifice while Baal's priests got nothing. Elijah was later taken to heaven in a chariot of fire. His successor, Elisha, continued the work -- healing, teaching, and performing miracles that demonstrated God's power over false gods.
- 6.3 -- Isaiah: The Prophet of the Messiah
  - Isaiah served in Judah during a time of political danger -- the Assyrian Empire was swallowing nations. Isaiah's prophecies cover both immediate events and the distant future. He prophesied about the coming Messiah in stunning detail -- the virgin birth, the suffering servant, the prince of peace. Latter-day Saints treasure Isaiah's writings, which appear extensively in the Book of Mormon. Nephi said, "My soul delighteth in the words of Isaiah."
- 6.4 -- Jeremiah: The Prophet Nobody Listened To
  - Jeremiah prophesied in Judah in the final decades before the Babylonian destruction. He warned that Jerusalem would fall if the people didn't repent. They didn't listen. Kings ignored him. Officials threw him in prison, then into a muddy cistern. He was right about everything, and nobody wanted to hear it. Jeremiah is also significant in Latter-day Saint scripture -- Lehi and his family left Jerusalem during Jeremiah's time, and the Book of Mormon confirms that other prophets were also warning the people.

**Test:** 4 questions on the role of prophets, Elijah's confrontation, Isaiah's messianic prophecies, and Jeremiah's warnings.

### Chapter 7: The Fall of the Northern Kingdom

**Core concepts:** Assyria's rise, the conquest of Israel, the lost ten tribes, Judah's narrow escape

- 7.1 -- The Assyrian Empire
  - Assyria was the superpower of the ancient Near East in the 8th and 7th centuries BC. Based in modern-day Iraq, Assyria built a massive military empire through conquest, deportation, and terror. They were deliberately cruel -- their own records boast of impaling, skinning, and deporting entire populations. When Assyria turned its attention to Israel, the small northern kingdom didn't stand a chance.
- 7.2 -- Israel Falls (722 BC)
  - After nearly 200 years of mostly wicked kings, the Northern Kingdom of Israel fell to Assyria in 722 BC. The Assyrians conquered the capital, Samaria, and deported much of the population to distant parts of the empire. Foreign peoples were brought in to settle the land. These new settlers mixed with the remaining Israelites, eventually becoming the Samaritans -- a people the Jews of Jesus' time looked down on. The ten tribes of the north were scattered and largely lost to history.
- 7.3 -- The Lost Ten Tribes
  - What happened to the ten tribes of Israel after the Assyrian deportation? The Bible doesn't say. They were absorbed into the populations of the Assyrian Empire and their descendants scattered. Latter-day Saints believe the lost tribes are still known to the Lord and that they will be gathered in the last days. The tenth Article of Faith speaks of "the literal gathering of Israel and in the restoration of the Ten Tribes." Their story is not over -- just hidden for now.
- 7.4 -- Judah Survives -- Barely
  - Assyria also attacked Judah. In 701 BC, the Assyrian king Sennacherib invaded and besieged Jerusalem. King Hezekiah, one of Judah's best kings, prayed for deliverance. The prophet Isaiah told him the Lord would defend the city. That night, the angel of the Lord struck the Assyrian camp, and 185,000 soldiers died. Sennacherib withdrew. Judah survived -- but its reprieve would not last forever.

**Test:** 4 questions on Assyria, the fall of the Northern Kingdom, the lost ten tribes, and Judah's survival under Hezekiah.

### Chapter 8: The Fall of Judah and the Babylonian Exile

**Core concepts:** Babylon's rise, Lehi's departure, the destruction of Jerusalem and the temple, exile in Babylon

- 8.1 -- Babylon Replaces Assyria
  - Empires rise and fall. In 612 BC, Babylon (also in modern-day Iraq) conquered Assyria's capital, Nineveh, and became the new dominant power. Babylon's most famous king, Nebuchadnezzar, built an empire that stretched across the region. Judah, caught between Babylon and Egypt, had to choose sides. Its kings made bad choices -- rebelling against Babylon, trusting Egypt for help that never came, and ignoring the prophets who warned them.
- 8.2 -- Lehi Leaves Jerusalem
  - Around 600 BC, during the reign of King Zedekiah, a prophet named Lehi received a vision warning him to take his family and leave Jerusalem. This is the beginning of the Book of Mormon. Lehi's departure happened during the same period when Jeremiah was prophesying the fall of Jerusalem. The Book of Mormon confirms that many prophets were warning the people, and that Jerusalem's destruction was not a surprise to those who listened. Lehi's family eventually traveled to the Americas.
- 8.3 -- Jerusalem Falls (586 BC)
  - Nebuchadnezzar besieged Jerusalem multiple times. The final siege lasted about two years. The city was starving. In 586 BC, the walls were breached. Nebuchadnezzar's army burned the temple Solomon had built, tore down the city walls, and carried the people of Judah into exile in Babylon. King Zedekiah was captured, forced to watch his sons killed, then blinded -- the last thing he ever saw. The temple, the center of Israel's worship for nearly 400 years, was gone.
- 8.4 -- Life in Exile
  - The Jews (the people of Judah -- this is where the word "Jew" comes from) lived in Babylon for about 70 years. Some, like Daniel, rose to positions of influence. The exiles maintained their identity, their scriptures, and their faith -- even without the temple. The synagogue system -- local houses of worship and scripture study -- likely developed during this period. The exile was devastating, but it also transformed Judaism. The people who returned would be different from the ones who left.

**Test:** 4 questions on Babylon's rise, Lehi's departure, the destruction of Jerusalem, and life in exile.

### Chapter 9: The Return and Rebuilding

**Core concepts:** Persia conquers Babylon, Cyrus's decree, rebuilding the temple, Ezra and Nehemiah

- 9.1 -- Cyrus the Great
  - In 539 BC, the Persian Empire conquered Babylon. The Persian king, Cyrus the Great, had a different approach than the Babylonians and Assyrians. Instead of deporting conquered peoples, he let them go home. Cyrus issued a decree allowing the Jews to return to Jerusalem and rebuild their temple. The prophet Isaiah had actually named Cyrus by name -- over a century before he was born -- calling him the Lord's "anointed" who would free Israel.
- 9.2 -- Rebuilding the Temple
  - The first group of Jews returned to Jerusalem around 538 BC under the leadership of Zerubbabel. They found the city in ruins. Rebuilding the temple was their first priority, but it was slow and discouraging. The older people who remembered Solomon's temple wept because the new one was so much smaller and less grand. But the prophets Haggai and Zechariah encouraged them, and the Second Temple was completed in 516 BC -- 70 years after Solomon's temple was destroyed.
- 9.3 -- Ezra and the Law
  - Ezra was a priest and scribe who led a second group of returnees to Jerusalem around 458 BC. He was shocked at what he found -- the people had intermarried with surrounding nations and drifted from the Law of Moses. Ezra led a major reform, publicly reading the Law to the assembled people. This moment is considered a turning point -- from this point on, the Jewish people became intensely focused on the written law as the center of their religious life.
- 9.4 -- Nehemiah and the Walls
  - Nehemiah was a Jewish official serving the Persian king. When he heard that Jerusalem's walls were still broken, he got permission to go and rebuild them. Despite opposition from neighboring leaders (Sanballat and Tobiah tried to stop the work through threats and politics), Nehemiah organized the people and rebuilt the walls in just 52 days. Walls meant security. A city without walls was a city that couldn't defend itself. Jerusalem was a real city again.

**Test:** 4 questions on Cyrus's decree, rebuilding the temple, Ezra's reforms, and Nehemiah's wall-building.

### Chapter 10: The Greek Period

**Core concepts:** Alexander the Great, Hellenism, the Ptolemies and Seleucids, the crisis under Antiochus

- 10.1 -- Alexander the Great
  - In 332 BC, a young Greek conqueror named Alexander the Great swept through the Near East and took control of the Persian Empire -- including Judea. Alexander didn't destroy Jerusalem; tradition says he was shown the prophecies of Daniel that predicted a Greek ruler, and he was impressed. Alexander died young (at 32), and his empire was divided among his generals. Judea found itself caught between two successor kingdoms: the Ptolemies in Egypt and the Seleucids in Syria.
- 10.2 -- Hellenism: Greek Culture Spreads
  - Alexander's lasting impact wasn't just military -- it was cultural. Greek language, philosophy, art, athletics, and religion spread across the entire region. This cultural influence is called Hellenism. Many Jews adopted Greek customs. They spoke Greek, attended Greek theaters, and some even tried to hide their circumcision at Greek gymnasia. Other Jews resisted fiercely, seeing Hellenism as a threat to their covenant identity. This tension -- how much to blend in with the surrounding culture vs. how much to stay separate -- became the defining question of the period.
- 10.3 -- Under the Ptolemies and Seleucids
  - For about 100 years, Judea was ruled by the Ptolemies (based in Egypt), who mostly left the Jews alone to practice their religion. Then the Seleucids (based in Syria) took over. At first, they were tolerant too. But when Antiochus IV came to power, everything changed. He needed money, he wanted cultural uniformity, and he had no patience for Jewish distinctiveness. The stage was set for one of the worst persecutions in Jewish history.
- 10.4 -- Antiochus and the Abomination
  - In 167 BC, Antiochus IV (who called himself "Epiphanes" -- "God Manifest") banned Jewish religious practices. He outlawed circumcision, Sabbath observance, and Torah study -- on penalty of death. Then he did the unthinkable: he set up a pagan altar in the temple and sacrificed a pig on it. The Jews called this the "abomination of desolation." It was the worst desecration the temple had ever suffered. But instead of crushing the Jews, it sparked a revolt.

**Test:** 4 questions on Alexander's conquest, Hellenism's influence, Ptolemaic and Seleucid rule, and the desecration under Antiochus.

### Chapter 11: The Maccabees and Independence

**Core concepts:** The Maccabean revolt, Hanukkah, the Hasmonean dynasty, independence and its problems

- 11.1 -- The Revolt Begins
  - In the village of Modiin, an elderly priest named Mattathias refused to offer a pagan sacrifice. When a Jewish man stepped forward to comply, Mattathias killed him -- and the Seleucid officer enforcing the order. Then he and his five sons fled to the hills and launched a guerrilla war against the most powerful army in the region. It was an impossible fight. But the Maccabees (the family's nickname, meaning "the hammer") had something the Seleucids didn't: a cause worth dying for.
- 11.2 -- Judah Maccabee and the Temple
  - Mattathias's son Judah became the military leader. Against overwhelming odds, Judah Maccabee won a series of stunning victories against Seleucid armies. In 164 BC, the Maccabees recaptured Jerusalem and cleansed the temple. They removed the pagan altar, built a new one, and rededicated the temple to the Lord. This rededication is celebrated as Hanukkah -- the Festival of Dedication. Jesus himself observed this holiday (see John 10:22).
- 11.3 -- The Hasmonean Dynasty
  - After the initial victories, the Maccabees' descendants (called the Hasmoneans) established an independent Jewish kingdom that lasted about 80 years (roughly 140-63 BC). For the first time since the Babylonian exile, Jews governed themselves. But the Hasmonean kings were not from the line of David, and they made themselves both king and high priest -- something many Jews found troubling. Over time, the dynasty became corrupt, with family members fighting and murdering each other for power.
- 11.4 -- The Problems with Independence
  - The Hasmonean period shows that political independence doesn't automatically mean spiritual faithfulness. The later Hasmonean kings were as worldly and violent as any pagan ruler. Internal divisions deepened. Different Jewish groups -- Pharisees, Sadducees, and Essenes -- emerged during this period, each with a different vision of what it meant to be faithful to God. When a civil war between two Hasmonean brothers erupted, both sides made the same mistake: they asked Rome to intervene.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Maccabean revolt, the rededication of the temple, the Hasmonean dynasty, and the problems that followed independence.

### Chapter 12: Rome Arrives

**Core concepts:** Pompey's conquest, Herod the Great, Roman rule, the world Jesus was born into

- 12.1 -- Pompey and the Roman Conquest (63 BC)
  - In 63 BC, the Roman general Pompey marched into Jerusalem to settle the Hasmonean civil war. He entered the Holy of Holies in the temple -- something only the high priest was allowed to do once a year. He was surprised to find it empty (no idol, no image -- just the presence of God). From this point on, Judea was under Roman control. Jewish independence was over. Rome would rule the land for the rest of our story -- and far beyond.
- 12.2 -- Herod the Great
  - Rome appointed Herod as "King of the Jews" in 37 BC. Herod was technically Jewish (his family had converted), but he was deeply unpopular. He was a brilliant builder -- constructing fortresses, cities, and a massive expansion of the Second Temple that made it one of the wonders of the ancient world. But he was also paranoid and cruel. He executed his own wife and sons when he suspected disloyalty. This is the same Herod who ordered the massacre of infant boys in Bethlehem when he heard a new "king" had been born.
- 12.3 -- Life Under Rome
  - Rome brought roads, aqueducts, law, and order. It also brought heavy taxes, pagan culture, and military occupation. Roman soldiers were everywhere. Jewish taxes went to a pagan emperor who claimed to be a god. The high priesthood became a political appointment controlled by Rome. Many Jews seethed with resentment. Some cooperated with Rome (like the tax collectors). Some retreated into religious purity (like the Essenes). Some plotted rebellion (like the Zealots). Everyone was waiting for something -- or someone.
- 12.4 -- The World Jesus Was Born Into
  - By the time Jesus was born in Bethlehem, the Jewish people had been living under foreign rule for centuries -- Persian, Greek, and now Roman. They longed for the Messiah -- the anointed one from the line of David who would deliver them. Most expected a military king who would overthrow Rome. The Pharisees focused on strict obedience to the law. The Sadducees controlled the temple and cooperated with Rome. The Essenes withdrew to the desert. Into this divided, occupied, expectant world, a baby was born in a stable.

**Test:** 4 questions on Rome's arrival, Herod the Great, life under Roman rule, and the conditions at the time of Jesus' birth.

### Chapter 13: The Setting of the Savior's Ministry

**Core concepts:** The religious groups Jesus encountered, the temple in Jesus' day, Roman governance of Judea, Galilee vs. Judea

- 13.1 -- Pharisees, Sadducees, and Others
  - By Jesus' time, several distinct groups shaped Jewish life. The Pharisees were the "strict observers" -- they believed in keeping every detail of the law and added extensive oral traditions on top of the written law. The Sadducees were the temple aristocracy -- wealthy, politically connected, and theologically conservative (they rejected the resurrection and angels). The Essenes had withdrawn from society entirely. The Zealots wanted armed revolt against Rome. Understanding these groups helps you understand why different people reacted to Jesus in different ways.
- 13.2 -- The Temple in Jesus' Day
  - Herod's temple was magnificent -- one of the largest religious complexes in the ancient world. It had multiple courts: the Court of the Gentiles (where anyone could enter), the Court of Women, the Court of Israel (Jewish men), the Court of the Priests, and the Holy of Holies. The money changers and animal sellers that Jesus drove out operated in the Court of the Gentiles. The temple was both a place of worship and a center of economic and political power. It was the beating heart of Jewish life.
- 13.3 -- Roman Governors and Local Rule
  - After Herod the Great died, his kingdom was divided among his sons, and eventually Rome sent its own governors (called prefects or procurators) to rule Judea directly. Pontius Pilate was the Roman governor during Jesus' ministry. The Sanhedrin -- a council of Jewish leaders, mostly Pharisees and Sadducees -- handled religious law and local disputes, but could not carry out the death penalty without Roman approval. This is why Jesus was tried before both the Sanhedrin and Pilate.
- 13.4 -- Galilee and Judea
  - Jesus grew up in Galilee, in the north -- a more rural, agricultural region that other Jews sometimes looked down on ("Can anything good come out of Nazareth?"). Jerusalem and Judea, in the south, were the religious and political center. Jesus' ministry moved between both regions. Many of His apostles were Galilean fishermen. Understanding the geography helps you follow His ministry: the Sea of Galilee, Capernaum, Nazareth, the Jordan River, Jericho, Bethany, and Jerusalem -- these are real places, and the distances between them shaped the story.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Jewish religious groups, the temple complex, Roman governance, and the geography of Jesus' ministry.

### Chapter 14: After the Savior -- The Road to Destruction

**Core concepts:** The early Church in Jerusalem, growing tensions with Rome, the Jewish revolt, factions and civil war

- 14.1 -- The Church in Jerusalem
  - After Jesus' death, resurrection, and ascension, His apostles remained in Jerusalem and led a growing community of believers. The early Church met in homes and at the temple. They were still Jewish -- they worshiped at the temple, kept the Law of Moses, and saw themselves as the true continuation of Israel's covenant. But tensions grew quickly. The Sanhedrin persecuted the apostles. Stephen was stoned. James, the brother of John, was executed by Herod Agrippa. Latter-day Saints understand that over time, the fulness of the gospel was lost -- a period called the Great Apostasy.
- 14.2 -- Growing Tensions with Rome
  - Through the 40s, 50s, and 60s AD, tensions between Jews and Romans escalated. Roman governors were often corrupt and insensitive to Jewish religious concerns. Protests and small revolts broke out regularly. Zealots assassinated Roman sympathizers. A new generation of Jews had grown up under occupation and had had enough. The moderates who counseled patience were losing influence to radicals who wanted war. Everyone could feel that something was coming.
- 14.3 -- The Jewish Revolt Begins (66 AD)
  - In 66 AD, a series of provocations by the Roman governor Florus triggered a full-scale revolt. Jewish rebels seized Jerusalem and wiped out a Roman garrison. It was an astonishing early success -- but Rome was the most powerful military force in the world. Emperor Nero sent his general Vespasian (and later Vespasian's son Titus) with a massive army to crush the rebellion. The Romans methodically reconquered Galilee first, then turned south toward Jerusalem.
- 14.4 -- Civil War Inside the Walls
  - While the Romans closed in, the Jews inside Jerusalem were fighting each other. Three rival factions controlled different parts of the city and spent as much time killing each other as fighting the Romans. One faction burned the city's food supply -- deliberately -- to force everyone to fight rather than negotiate. It was a catastrophe of division. The defenders of Jerusalem were brave, but they were not united, and their internal war weakened them fatally.

### Chapter 15: The Destruction of the Temple

**Core concepts:** The siege of Jerusalem, the temple's destruction, consequences for the Jewish people, looking forward

- 15.1 -- The Siege of Jerusalem (70 AD)
  - In 70 AD, the Roman general Titus surrounded Jerusalem with four legions. The siege was brutal. No food could enter the city. Starvation was widespread -- the historian Josephus (a Jewish eyewitness who had defected to Rome) described scenes too terrible to repeat. The Romans built siege works and battered the walls. The defenders fought with extraordinary courage. But the outcome was never really in doubt. Rome had the numbers, the engineering, and the patience.
- 15.2 -- The Temple Burns
  - In August of 70 AD, Roman soldiers breached the temple complex. According to Josephus, Titus wanted to preserve the temple, but a soldier threw a torch into a side chamber and the fire spread. The temple -- Herod's magnificent expansion of the Second Temple, the center of Jewish worship, the place where God's presence dwelt -- was consumed by flames. The date, the 9th of Av on the Jewish calendar, was the same date tradition held for the destruction of Solomon's temple in 586 BC.
- 15.3 -- What Was Lost
  - The destruction of the temple changed Judaism forever. Without the temple, there could be no more animal sacrifices. The priesthood lost its central function. The Sadducees, whose power was based in the temple, disappeared as a group. Judaism reorganized around the synagogue, the rabbis, and the study of Torah. The Pharisees' approach -- focused on the law and its interpretation -- became the foundation of modern Judaism. For Latter-day Saints, the destruction of the temple fulfilled the Savior's own prophecy: "There shall not be left here one stone upon another, that shall not be thrown down" (Matthew 24:2).
- 15.4 -- The Story Continues
  - The destruction of the temple in 70 AD is where this book ends, but the story of Israel is not over. The Jewish people survived exile, persecution, and dispersion across the world for nearly 2,000 years. Latter-day Saints believe that the gathering of Israel -- physical and spiritual -- is happening now, in our time. Every person who makes and keeps covenants with God is part of that gathering. The history you've learned in this book is your history too. The covenant God made with Abraham is the same covenant you participate in today.
