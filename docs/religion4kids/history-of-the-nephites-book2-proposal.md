# History of the Nephites for Kids -- Book 2 Proposal

## Book 2: The Reign of the Judges

**Subtitle:** Faith, War, and the Word of God

Covers Alma through 3 Nephi 1 (~92 BC - ~1 AD)

---

### Chapter 1: Alma the Chief Judge
**Core concepts:** Nehor, Amlici, the Church struggles, Alma gives up the judgment seat

- 1.1 -- The New Government: Alma the Younger as first chief judge and high priest. New system of judges chosen by voice of people. Immediately tested -- not everyone was happy about the change (Alma 1-2).
- 1.2 -- Nehor: Man who taught that priests should be popular/paid, that everyone would be saved regardless. Killed Gideon. Executed for murder. But his ideas spread and caused problems for years (Alma 1).
- 1.3 -- Amlici's Rebellion: Amlici wanted to be king. Lost the vote, started a war anyway. Allied with Lamanites. Alma led the Nephite army and killed Amlici personally in battle (Alma 2-3). Democracy under attack from day one.
- 1.4 -- Alma Gives Up the Judgment Seat: The Church was struggling -- members were proud, persecuting nonmembers. Alma realized the problem was spiritual, not political. He gave up the judgment seat to focus on preaching full-time. "The preaching of the word had more powerful effect upon the minds of the people than the sword" (Alma 4:18-19, 31:5).

### Chapter 2: Alma and Amulek in Ammonihah
**Core concepts:** The wicked city, Amulek called, the martyrs, delivered from prison

- 2.1 -- The Wicked City: Alma went to Ammonihah, a city that had rejected God. They threw him out. An angel told him to go back. On the way, he met Amulek, a wealthy man the Lord had prepared to help him (Alma 8).
- 2.2 -- Amulek's Story: Amulek was not a church leader -- he was a regular man who had been called by an angel. He gave up his comfortable life to stand with Alma. His testimony was powerful because he was an ordinary person testifying of extraordinary things (Alma 10).
- 2.3 -- The Believers Are Burned: Some people in Ammonihah believed Alma and Amulek. The wicked leaders gathered the believers -- women and children -- and burned them and their scriptures. Amulek wanted to stop it. Alma said the Lord would receive them in glory and would judge their persecutors (Alma 14:8-11). One of the hardest scenes in the Book of Mormon.
- 2.4 -- Delivered from Prison: Alma and Amulek were imprisoned, beaten, starved, and mocked. Finally, the power of God came upon them. The walls of the prison fell, killing their captors, and Alma and Amulek walked out unharmed (Alma 14:26-29). The city of Ammonihah was later destroyed by Lamanites -- in one day (Alma 16:2-3).

### Chapter 3: The Anti-Nephi-Lehies
**Core concepts:** Converted Lamanites, burying weapons, the people of Ammon, covenant of peace

- 3.1 -- The Sons of Mosiah's Success: The sons of Mosiah spent fourteen years preaching among the Lamanites. Thousands were converted -- including King Lamoni and his father, the king over all the Lamanites. These converts were deeply, genuinely changed (Alma 17-22).
- 3.2 -- Burying Their Weapons: The converted Lamanites made a covenant never to shed blood again. They buried their weapons deep in the ground as a testimony to God that they would never use them (Alma 24:15-18). When unconverted Lamanites attacked, the Anti-Nephi-Lehies lay down before them. Over a thousand were killed -- and more Lamanites were converted by their courage than were killed.
- 3.3 -- The People of Ammon: The converted Lamanites couldn't stay in Lamanite territory. The Nephites gave them the land of Jershon and promised to protect them (Alma 27). They became known as the people of Ammon -- one of the most faithful groups in the entire Book of Mormon. They never broke their covenant. Not once.
- 3.4 -- Why This Story Matters: The Anti-Nephi-Lehies show what true conversion looks like. They didn't just believe -- they changed completely. They were willing to die rather than break a covenant. Their example teaches that some covenants are so sacred that they're worth more than your life.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 4: Korihor and the Zoramites
**Core concepts:** Korihor the anti-Christ, the Rameumptom, mission to the Zoramites, Alma 32

- 4.1 -- Korihor the Anti-Christ: Korihor taught there was no God, no Christ, no sin, and no afterlife. He was persuasive and led many away. When brought before Alma, he demanded a sign. Alma warned him. Korihor insisted. He was struck dumb -- and later trampled to death (Alma 30).
- 4.2 -- The Zoramites and the Rameumptom: The Zoramites were a group of Nephite dissenters who had created their own religion. They built a high tower called the Rameumptom, where they would stand one at a time and recite the same prayer thanking God that they were better than everyone else (Alma 31:12-23). Then they went home and never thought about God until the next week.
- 4.3 -- Alma's Mission to the Zoramites: Alma led a mission to reclaim the Zoramites. The wealthy Zoramites rejected him. But the poor -- who were not allowed on the Rameumptom -- listened. Alma taught them that faith is like planting a seed: if the seed is good, it will grow (Alma 32:28-43). You don't need to know everything at once. Start with a desire to believe, plant the word, and see what grows.
- 4.4 -- Amulek on the Atonement: Amulek taught the Zoramite poor about the Atonement -- that Christ's sacrifice would be "infinite and eternal" (Alma 34:10). He taught that now is the time to prepare, not to procrastinate. "This life is the time for men to prepare to meet God" (Alma 34:32). The poor Zoramites believed. The wealthy did not.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 5: Alma's Counsel to His Sons
**Core concepts:** Alma to Helaman (Alma 36-37), to Shiblon (Alma 38), to Corianton (Alma 39-42)

- 5.1 -- Alma to Helaman: His Conversion Story: Alma told Helaman the full story of his conversion -- the angel, the three days of torment, crying out to Christ, and the joy that replaced the pain (Alma 36). He testified that he knew these things "not of the temporal but of the spiritual, not of the carnal mind but of God" (Alma 36:4). This is the most detailed conversion account in scripture.
- 5.2 -- Alma to Helaman: The Small and Simple Things: Alma entrusted Helaman with the sacred records and taught, "By small and simple things are great things brought to pass" (Alma 37:6). The Liahona, the scriptures, the daily habits of faithfulness -- none of them seem dramatic, but they lead to eternal outcomes. Don't underestimate the small things.
- 5.3 -- Alma to Shiblon: Alma's counsel to Shiblon was brief because Shiblon was faithful and steady (Alma 38). Alma told him to be diligent, temperate, and humble. Not every child needs a dramatic intervention. Some need quiet encouragement. Shiblon was that child.
- 5.4 -- Alma to Corianton: Corianton had committed serious sin during his mission to the Zoramites. Alma's counsel was direct -- he explained why sexual sin is so serious, taught about the resurrection and the spirit world, and explained the justice and mercy of God (Alma 39-42). Alma didn't sugarcoat the problem, but he also showed Corianton the way back through repentance and the Atonement.

### Chapter 6: Captain Moroni
**Core concepts:** The Title of Liberty, fortifying cities, Moroni's character, defending families and faith

- 6.1 -- Who Captain Moroni Was: Captain Moroni was appointed chief captain of the Nephite armies when he was only twenty-five years old (Alma 43:16-17). Mormon, who wrote the record, said that if all men had been like Moroni, "the very powers of hell would have been shaken forever" (Alma 48:17). He was fierce in battle but also a man of deep faith who hated bloodshed and fought only because his people were threatened.
- 6.2 -- The Title of Liberty: When a man named Amalickiah tried to make himself king, Moroni tore his coat and wrote on it: "In memory of our God, our religion, and freedom, and our peace, our wives, and our children" (Alma 46:12). He called it the Title of Liberty, fastened it to a pole, and rallied the people. Those who wouldn't defend their freedom were given a choice: support the cause or face consequences.
- 6.3 -- Fortifying the Cities: Moroni didn't just fight -- he prepared. He built walls, dug ditches, raised banks of earth, and constructed towers around Nephite cities (Alma 49-50). When the Lamanites attacked these fortified cities, they couldn't penetrate the defenses. Moroni understood that preparation is as important as courage. He made the Nephites' weak places strong.
- 6.4 -- Moroni's Faith: Moroni was a warrior, but his strength came from faith. He fasted and prayed for his people. He believed God would preserve the Nephites if they were faithful. He was passionate, sometimes angry, and always devoted to his people, his family, and his God. He was not a perfect man, but he was a great one.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 7: Amalickiah's Treachery
**Core concepts:** Amalickiah's rise to power, betrayal, becoming Lamanite king, war against the Nephites

- 7.1 -- Amalickiah the Traitor: Amalickiah was a Nephite dissenter who wanted power at any cost. When his attempt to become king failed, he defected to the Lamanites with his followers. His story is one of the most chilling in the Book of Mormon -- a brilliant, ruthless man who used deception to get everything he wanted (Alma 46-47).
- 7.2 -- How He Became Lamanite King: Amalickiah schemed his way to the top. He poisoned the Lamanite commander, took over the army, then murdered the Lamanite king and married the queen (Alma 47). Every step was calculated treachery. He didn't earn his position -- he stole it through murder and lies.
- 7.3 -- War Against the Nephites: Once in power, Amalickiah stirred up the Lamanites to attack the Nephites. He used anger, propaganda, and promises of power. The wars that followed were among the bloodiest in Nephite history (Alma 48-51). The Nephite dissenters who joined the Lamanites were often more vicious than the Lamanites themselves.
- 7.4 -- Lessons from Amalickiah: Amalickiah's story is a warning about ambition without integrity. He was talented, persuasive, and determined -- but he used every gift for selfish, destructive purposes. The Book of Mormon puts Amalickiah and Moroni side by side on purpose. Two men, two paths, two entirely different kinds of greatness.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 8: The War Chapters
**Core concepts:** Major battles, strategy, Nephite losses and victories, the cost of war

- 8.1 -- Why So Many War Chapters?: The war chapters in Alma (roughly chapters 43-63) are the longest sustained military narrative in the Book of Mormon. Mormon included them because they teach important lessons: about leadership, about faith during trials, about the cost of freedom, and about what happens when a nation is divided from within. War is terrible, but the principles revealed during war are timeless.
- 8.2 -- The Battles: The Nephites faced relentless attacks from Lamanite armies, often led by Nephite dissenters who knew the Nephites' weaknesses. Cities were taken and retaken. Supply lines were cut. Soldiers suffered hunger, exhaustion, and discouragement. Moroni sometimes had to fight enemies on multiple fronts while also dealing with political problems back home.
- 8.3 -- Teancum's Courage: Teancum was one of Moroni's best captains -- bold, decisive, and fearless. He single-handedly killed Amalickiah by sneaking into the Lamanite camp and putting a javelin through his heart (Alma 51:33-34). When Amalickiah's brother Ammoron took over, Teancum later killed him too -- but died in the attempt (Alma 62:36). Teancum gave everything for his people.
- 8.4 -- The Cost of War: War killed thousands of Nephites and Lamanites. Families were torn apart. Cities were destroyed. The Book of Mormon doesn't glorify war -- it shows its horror. Mormon, who compiled the record, was himself a military commander. He knew what war cost. The war chapters are a reminder that freedom has a price, and that the best way to avoid war is righteousness.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 9: Helaman and the Stripling Warriors
**Core concepts:** The people of Ammon's dilemma, 2,000 young warriors, faith of their mothers, not one lost

- 9.1 -- The People of Ammon's Dilemma: The people of Ammon (the converted Lamanites who had buried their weapons) saw the Nephites dying to protect them. They wanted to break their covenant and fight. Helaman begged them not to -- a covenant with God should not be broken. But there was another option (Alma 53:10-15).
- 9.2 -- The 2,000 Stripling Warriors: The sons of the people of Ammon had not made the covenant their parents had. These young men -- about 2,000 of them -- volunteered to fight. They chose Helaman as their leader. He called them his sons. They were young, inexperienced, and had never fought before. But they had something that made them extraordinary (Alma 53:16-22).
- 9.3 -- "We Do Not Doubt Our Mothers Knew It": These young warriors told Helaman, "We do not doubt our mothers knew it" (Alma 56:48). Their mothers had taught them that if they did not doubt, God would deliver them. They went into battle with perfect faith. They fought with astonishing courage. After the fiercest battles, Helaman counted them -- every single one was alive. Wounded, yes. Some fainted from blood loss. But not one died (Alma 56:56, 57:25).
- 9.4 -- What Their Story Teaches: The stripling warriors' story is about the power of what parents teach their children. These young men's faith didn't come from nowhere -- it was planted by their mothers. It's also about the power of covenant keeping. Their parents kept their covenant even when it was painful. Their sons honored that sacrifice by fighting with faith. The family's faithfulness across generations saved a nation.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 10: The End of the War and Moroni's Letters
**Core concepts:** Moroni's epistle to Pahoran, Pahoran's response, the war ends, Moroni retires

- 10.1 -- Moroni's Angry Letter: While fighting on the front lines, Moroni wasn't getting the supplies and reinforcements he needed. He wrote a furious letter to Pahoran, the chief judge, accusing him of sitting comfortably while soldiers died (Alma 60). He even threatened to march on the capital. Moroni was passionate -- sometimes too passionate. He assumed the worst about Pahoran without knowing the full situation.
- 10.2 -- Pahoran's Response: Pahoran wrote back one of the most gracious letters in scripture. He didn't get angry at Moroni's accusations. He explained that rebels had overthrown the government and driven him from the capital (Alma 61). He said, "I do not joy in your great afflictions...but behold, I do joy in the greatness of your heart" (Alma 61:9). Pahoran didn't take offense. He focused on the solution.
- 10.3 -- The War Ends: Moroni and Pahoran joined forces, defeated the rebels, and then turned to finish the war against the Lamanites. Peace was finally restored after years of brutal conflict. Helaman returned to rebuild the Church. Moroni retired from military service. The Nephite nation had survived -- barely.
- 10.4 -- Captain Moroni's Legacy: Moroni died around 56 BC. He had spent most of his adult life defending his people. He was not perfect -- his letter to Pahoran shows he could be hasty and unfair. But he was wholly devoted to God, family, and freedom. Mormon said, "He was a man who was firm in the faith of Christ" (Alma 48:13). His legacy is that faith and preparation can preserve a nation, even in the darkest times.

### Chapter 11: The Gadianton Robbers
**Core concepts:** Secret combinations, Gadianton, Kishkumen, corruption from within

- 11.1 -- A New Kind of Enemy: After the wars ended, a new threat emerged -- not from outside but from within. A man named Kishkumen assassinated the chief judge, and a man named Gadianton organized a secret band of criminals who used murder, oaths, and conspiracy to gain power (Helaman 1-2). These were the Gadianton robbers -- and they would plague the Nephites for the rest of their history.
- 11.2 -- How Secret Combinations Work: The Gadianton robbers operated through secret oaths, signs, and passwords. They protected each other from justice. They infiltrated the government. They murdered opponents and shared the spoils. Mormon called these "secret combinations" and warned that they are one of the greatest dangers any civilization can face (Ether 8:22-25).
- 11.3 -- The Threat Grows: Over the decades, the Gadianton robbers grew in power. Sometimes they were driven out; sometimes they controlled the government itself. They thrived when the people were wicked and shrank when the people were righteous. The cycle of faithfulness and apostasy -- familiar from the time of the judges in Israel -- was playing out again among the Nephites.
- 11.4 -- Why Mormon Included This Warning: Mormon, writing centuries later, included extensive warnings about secret combinations because he had seen their final effect. They destroyed the Nephite civilization. He wrote directly to us: "The Lord commandeth you, when ye shall see these things come among you, that ye shall awake to a sense of your awful situation" (Ether 8:24). This is not just ancient history. It's a warning for our time.

### Chapter 12: Nephi and Lehi in Prison
**Core concepts:** Nephi (son of Helaman) gives up judgment seat, Nephi and Lehi's mission, fire in prison

- 12.1 -- Nephi Gives Up the Judgment Seat: Like his ancestor Alma, Nephi (son of Helaman) gave up the chief judgeship to devote himself to preaching. The government was being corrupted by Gadianton influence, and Nephi believed the only real solution was spiritual. He and his brother Lehi went out to preach (Helaman 5:1-4).
- 12.2 -- Preaching with Power: Nephi and Lehi preached with such power that they baptized eight thousand Lamanites in the land of Zarahemla. Then they went to the land of Nephi -- Lamanite territory -- and continued preaching. They were captured and thrown into prison (Helaman 5:14-21).
- 12.3 -- Fire in Prison: While in prison, Nephi and Lehi were encircled by fire -- but the fire did not burn them. The earth shook. Darkness covered the Lamanite captors. A voice spoke from the darkness: "Repent ye, repent ye, and seek no more to destroy my servants" (Helaman 5:29-32). The Lamanites prayed, the darkness lifted, and they saw Nephi and Lehi's faces shining. Angels ministered. Three hundred people were converted.
- 12.4 -- The Ripple Effect: The mass conversion at the prison led to even more Lamanites giving up their hatred. Many returned Nephite lands they had conquered. For a time, the Lamanites were actually more righteous than the Nephites. The story shows that a single act of faithful courage -- going to prison rather than giving up -- can change the course of nations.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 13: Nephi's Power and Testimony
**Core concepts:** Prophecy from the garden tower, the sealing power, revealing a murder, Nephi's authority

- 13.1 -- Preaching from the Garden Tower: Nephi prayed on the tower in his garden near the highway. People gathered and he rebuked them for their wickedness and warned of destruction (Helaman 7-8). He prophesied that their chief judge had been murdered -- at that very moment.
- 13.2 -- The Murder Revealed: Messengers were sent and found the chief judge dead, exactly as Nephi said. Nephi was accused of the murder. He told them exactly who the murderer was and how to prove it. Everything happened as Nephi said (Helaman 8-9). His prophetic gift was undeniable.
- 13.3 -- The Sealing Power: The Lord gave Nephi the sealing power -- the authority that whatever he bound on earth would be bound in heaven (Helaman 10:4-7). God told Nephi, "I will make thee mighty in word and in deed." Nephi used this power to call a famine upon the land to humble the people, and later to end it when they repented (Helaman 11).
- 13.4 -- The Cycle Continues: Even after the famine brought repentance, the people quickly returned to wickedness. The Gadianton robbers returned. The cycle repeated again and again. Nephi preached tirelessly but couldn't permanently change the people's hearts. The lesson: prophets can warn and teach, but each person must choose for themselves.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 14: Samuel the Lamanite
**Core concepts:** A Lamanite prophet sent to the Nephites, signs of Christ's birth and death, rejected

- 14.1 -- A Lamanite Prophet: Around 6 BC, a Lamanite prophet named Samuel came to Zarahemla to preach to the Nephites (Helaman 13). The fact that God sent a Lamanite to call the Nephites to repentance shows just how far the Nephites had fallen. Samuel stood on the city wall to preach because the people wouldn't let him in.
- 14.2 -- Signs of Christ's Birth: Samuel prophesied specific signs that would accompany the Savior's birth: a day, a night, and a day with no darkness, and a new star (Helaman 14:3-6). He said the signs would come in five years. These prophecies would be fulfilled exactly as described in 3 Nephi 1.
- 14.3 -- Signs of Christ's Death: Samuel also prophesied signs of Christ's death: three days of darkness, earthquakes, cities destroyed and sunk and burned (Helaman 14:20-27). The destruction described in 3 Nephi 8 fulfilled these prophecies precisely. Samuel saw it all in advance.
- 14.4 -- Rejected by the Nephites: The people shot arrows and threw stones at Samuel on the wall, but they couldn't hit him. Some believed and went to be baptized by Nephi. Most rejected Samuel. He left and was never heard from again. Samuel is a reminder that God's messengers come in unexpected forms -- and that rejecting a prophet because of who he is rather than what he says is always a mistake.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 15: The Signs Are Fulfilled
**Core concepts:** Waiting for the signs, the night without darkness, the new star, transition to 3 Nephi

- 15.1 -- Believers and Doubters: As the five-year deadline approached, believers anxiously waited for Samuel's prophesied signs. Unbelievers mocked them: "The time is past, and the word of Samuel is not fulfilled; therefore, your joy and your faith have been in vain" (3 Nephi 1:6). The unbelievers set a day -- if the signs didn't come by then, the believers would be put to death.
- 15.2 -- Nephi's Prayer: Nephi (son of Nephi, grandson of Helaman) cried to the Lord all day on behalf of the believers who were about to die. The Lord's voice came: "On this night shall the sign be given, and on the morrow come I into the world" (3 Nephi 1:13). The Savior Himself told Nephi that His birth was imminent.
- 15.3 -- The Night Without Darkness: That evening, the sun set -- but it didn't get dark. The night was as bright as day. A new star appeared. The signs were fulfilled exactly as Samuel had prophesied (3 Nephi 1:15-21). The believers rejoiced. The unbelievers fell to the earth in terror. Everyone knew that Christ had been born.
- 15.4 -- Looking Ahead: The signs proved Samuel's words true, and for a time the people believed. But the book of 3 Nephi will show that belief faded quickly. Within a generation, the Nephites would face the greatest destruction in their history -- and then the greatest blessing: the visit of Jesus Christ Himself. The first 500 years of this book have set the stage. The climax of the entire Book of Mormon is about to unfold.

**Test:** 4 questions.
