# History of the Nephites for Kids -- Book 3 Proposal

## Book 3: The Coming of Christ and the End

**Subtitle:** From Destruction to Moroni's Promise

Covers 3 Nephi 2 through Moroni, plus the Jaredites (~1 - 421 AD)

---

### Chapter 1: Growing Wickedness
**Core concepts:** Signs forgotten, Gadianton robbers take over, society unravels

- 1.1 -- The Signs Are Forgotten: Just a few years after the night without darkness, many people began to explain away the signs. "Some lying wonder" they said (3 Nephi 1:22). Children grew up doubting. The Gadianton robbers grew strong again. Wickedness spread rapidly. The pattern: dramatic signs produce temporary faith, but lasting faith requires daily choices.
- 1.2 -- The Gadianton Threat: The Gadianton robbers became so powerful they controlled large portions of the land. They hid in the mountains and raided Nephite settlements. They sent a letter to the Nephite governor demanding surrender (3 Nephi 3:1-10). The Nephite response would determine whether the civilization survived.
- 1.3 -- The Gathering Strategy: The Nephite governor, Lachoneus, refused to surrender. Instead, he gathered the entire Nephite population into one place with seven years of supplies (3 Nephi 3:22-26). The Gadianton robbers couldn't sustain a siege because they had no food of their own -- they lived by plunder. The strategy worked.
- 1.4 -- Victory and Collapse: The Nephites defeated the Gadianton robbers in a great battle (3 Nephi 4). There was peace. But within just a few years, the people returned to wickedness. The government collapsed into tribes. The chief judge was murdered. By 30 AD, there was no central government at all (3 Nephi 7). The civilization was disintegrating just as the Savior's death approached.

### Chapter 2: Destruction at Christ's Death
**Core concepts:** Three hours of destruction, three days of darkness, the voice of Christ

- 2.1 -- The Destruction Begins: In the thirty-fourth year, a terrible storm arose (3 Nephi 8). Earthquakes, fires, whirlwinds, and floods struck the land for three hours. Cities were burned, sunk into the sea, or buried by mountains. Highways were broken up. The whole face of the land was changed. The destruction was sudden, total, and devastating.
- 2.2 -- The Darkness: After the destruction, thick darkness covered the land for three days. Not ordinary darkness -- no fire could be lit, no light could penetrate (3 Nephi 8:20-23). People stumbled in the blackness, mourning and howling. Entire cities had been destroyed. The survivors had no idea what had happened or who was still alive.
- 2.3 -- The Voice in the Darkness: In the darkness, a voice spoke -- the voice of Jesus Christ (3 Nephi 9). He told the survivors which cities had been destroyed and why. He called them to repent and come unto Him. He said the law of sacrifice by bloodshed was ended and He now required "a broken heart and a contrite spirit" (3 Nephi 9:19-20). The Savior was speaking from the spirit world -- He had just been crucified in Jerusalem.
- 2.4 -- Silence, Then Light: After the voice spoke, there was silence for many hours (3 Nephi 10:1-2). Then the voice came again, lamenting over the wicked and gathering the righteous: "How oft have I gathered you as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings" (3 Nephi 10:4-6). After three days, the darkness lifted and the people saw the devastation. Everything had changed. And they were about to experience something no one expected.

### Chapter 3: Jesus Appears at the Temple
**Core concepts:** The Father introduces the Son, 2,500 feel the wounds, Jesus teaches and prays

- 3.1 -- The Voice from Heaven: A multitude of about 2,500 people was gathered at the temple in the land Bountiful (3 Nephi 11). They heard a voice from heaven. They couldn't understand it at first. The second time they couldn't understand. The third time, they understood: "Behold my Beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased, in whom I have glorified my name -- hear ye him" (3 Nephi 11:7).
- 3.2 -- Jesus Descends: Then Jesus Christ descended out of heaven, dressed in a white robe (3 Nephi 11:8). He said, "Behold, I am Jesus Christ, whom the prophets testified shall come into the world" (3 Nephi 11:10). He invited the people to come forward and feel the prints of the nails in His hands and feet and the wound in His side. Every one of the 2,500 came forward, one by one.
- 3.3 -- "Arise and Come Forth": After the people knew for themselves that this was the Christ, He called Nephi forward and gave him authority to baptize (3 Nephi 11:21-28). He taught the people about baptism, about contention, and about His doctrine: "This is my doctrine, that the Father, and the Son, and the Holy Ghost are one; and I invite all to come unto me" (3 Nephi 11:27, 36).
- 3.4 -- The Savior Prays: Jesus prayed to the Father on behalf of the people, and His prayer was so sacred that it could not be written (3 Nephi 17:15-17). He wept. He said, "Blessed are ye because of your faith. And now behold, my joy is full" (3 Nephi 17:20). The people who had survived destruction, who had endured three days of darkness, were now in the presence of God.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 4: The Savior's Teachings Among the Nephites
**Core concepts:** Sermon at the temple (3 Nephi 12-14), the sacrament, twelve disciples called

- 4.1 -- The Sermon at the Temple: Jesus taught the Nephite version of the Sermon on the Mount (3 Nephi 12-14). The Beatitudes, the higher law, the Lord's Prayer, the wise and foolish builders -- all taught again in the promised land. Some differences reflect the Nephites' situation, but the core message is the same: Christ's standard is the condition of the heart.
- 4.2 -- The Twelve Disciples: Jesus chose twelve disciples to lead His Church among the Nephites (3 Nephi 12:1, 19:4). These were the Nephite equivalent of the Twelve Apostles in Jerusalem. He gave them authority to baptize and to administer the sacrament. The Church of Jesus Christ was now established on both sides of the world.
- 4.3 -- The Sacrament: Jesus instituted the sacrament among the Nephites -- bread and wine to remember His body and blood (3 Nephi 18:1-12). He gave the same commandment as at the Last Supper: do this in remembrance of me. Every time you take the sacrament on Sunday, you are participating in the same ordinance Jesus established among the Nephites.
- 4.4 -- "Come Unto Me": Jesus repeatedly invited the Nephites to come unto Him. "Come unto me and be saved" (3 Nephi 12:20). "Pray always" (3 Nephi 18:15-21). "Hold up your light that it may shine unto the world" (3 Nephi 18:24). His teachings were personal, direct, and urgent. He was not giving an abstract lecture. He was inviting real people to change their real lives.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 5: Jesus Heals and Blesses the Children
**Core concepts:** Healing every sick person, blessing the children one by one, angels descend

- 5.1 -- Healing the Sick: Jesus asked the people to bring their sick, their lame, their blind, their afflicted -- and He healed every single one (3 Nephi 17:7-10). The multitude bowed at His feet and worshipped Him, bathing His feet with their tears. This was not selective healing. Everyone who was brought was healed.
- 5.2 -- Blessing the Children: Jesus asked the people to bring their little children. He had them set the children on the ground around Him. He knelt and prayed for them with groans that could not be uttered (3 Nephi 17:11-15, 21). Then He took the children one by one and blessed them, weeping as He did. One by one.
- 5.3 -- Angels Descend: After blessing the children, angels descended from heaven and encircled the little ones in fire (3 Nephi 17:24). The 2,500 people witnessed it. This is one of the most sacred scenes in all of scripture -- the Savior of the world, kneeling with children, weeping over them, and angels surrounding them in fire. It tells you what Jesus cares about most.
- 5.4 -- Why This Matters: Jesus could have done anything with His time among the Nephites. He could have discussed politics, economics, or military strategy. Instead, He healed the sick and blessed children. He wept. He prayed. He served. The Nephite ministry shows us what the Savior prioritizes: individuals, families, and the most vulnerable.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 6: Final Teachings and the Three Nephites
**Core concepts:** Other sheep, scripture corrections, the Three Nephites, Jesus departs

- 6.1 -- Other Sheep: Jesus told the Nephites they were the "other sheep" He had spoken of in Jerusalem: "Other sheep I have which are not of this fold" (3 Nephi 15:21, John 10:16). He also said He would visit still other groups the Father had led away (3 Nephi 16:1-3). His ministry was not limited to one land.
- 6.2 -- Correcting the Record: Jesus reviewed the Nephite scriptures and told them things were missing. He commanded them to add Samuel the Lamanite's prophecies, which had been fulfilled but not recorded (3 Nephi 23:7-13). He also gave them additional scripture and prophecies. The Savior cared about accuracy in the record.
- 6.3 -- The Three Nephites: Before departing, Jesus asked each of the twelve disciples what they desired. Nine asked to come to Him speedily after their ministry. Three were too shy to say what they wanted. Jesus already knew -- they wanted to remain on the earth until His Second Coming (3 Nephi 28:1-9). They were changed so they wouldn't experience pain or death. These Three Nephites would minister among people for centuries.
- 6.4 -- Jesus Departs: After spending time teaching, healing, and establishing His Church, Jesus ascended into heaven (3 Nephi 18:38-39). The Nephites had experienced the most sacred event in their history. They had seen the resurrected Lord. They had felt His wounds. They had watched Him bless their children. Now they had to live what He had taught them.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 7: Two Hundred Years of Peace
**Core concepts:** 4 Nephi, no contention, all things in common, gradual decline

- 7.1 -- A Perfect Society: After Christ's visit, the Nephites lived in peace for about 200 years (4 Nephi). There were no contentions, no envyings, no strifes. "There were no robbers, nor murderers, neither were there Lamanites, nor any manner of -ites; but they were in one, the children of Christ" (4 Nephi 1:17). This is the only sustained period of true peace and righteousness in the entire Book of Mormon.
- 7.2 -- What Made It Work: The people lived the gospel completely. They had all things in common -- no rich and poor. They built cities. They multiplied. They kept the commandments. There was no contention "because of the love of God which did dwell in the hearts of the people" (4 Nephi 1:15). The source of peace was not a perfect government or economy -- it was the love of God in their hearts.
- 7.3 -- The Decline Begins: After about 200 years, cracks appeared. Some people became proud and stopped sharing. They divided into classes. Churches were built for profit. People denied Christ. The Gadianton robbers returned. The -ites came back: Nephites, Lamanites, and others. The distinctions that Christ had erased crept back in (4 Nephi 1:24-46).
- 7.4 -- How the Best Was Lost: The transition from 200 years of peace to complete wickedness took only a few generations. The people who had seen Christ died. Their grandchildren and great-grandchildren had only stories. Without personal conversion -- without the love of God in each heart -- even the most perfect society will crumble. The lesson: every generation must find Christ for itself.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 8: Mormon the Man
**Core concepts:** Young prophet in wicked times, chosen to keep the record, forbidden to preach

- 8.1 -- Who Mormon Was: Mormon was born around 311 AD into a Nephite civilization spiraling into wickedness. He was named after the Waters of Mormon where Alma had baptized. At age ten, the record keeper Ammaron told Mormon where the sacred records were buried and charged him to retrieve them when he was old enough (Mormon 1:1-5). Mormon was being prepared for something enormous.
- 8.2 -- A Boy Prophet: At fifteen, Mormon was "visited of the Lord, and tasted and knew of the goodness of Jesus" (Mormon 1:15). He tried to preach, but the Lord told him not to -- the people were too wicked. Imagine knowing the truth and being told you can't share it. Mormon obeyed. He also became the leader of the Nephite armies at age sixteen. A teenager leading armies in a dying civilization.
- 8.3 -- The Abridger: Mormon's greatest work was not military -- it was literary. He took the vast collection of Nephite records spanning a thousand years and abridged (condensed) them into the record we now call the Book of Mormon. He selected what to include, added his own commentary, and organized the narrative. The entire book is shaped by Mormon's choices about what mattered most.
- 8.4 -- Writing for Us: Mormon knew his people were doomed. He wrote for a future audience -- for us. He chose stories and teachings he believed would be most valuable to people living in the last days. When he interrupts the narrative to say "And thus we see..." he's speaking directly to you. The Book of Mormon is Mormon's message in a bottle, thrown across the centuries.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 9: The Final Wars
**Core concepts:** Mormon leads the army, refuses then returns, the gathering at Cumorah

- 9.1 -- Mormon Leads the Armies: Mormon led the Nephite armies for decades, fighting a losing war against the Lamanites. The Nephites were wicked -- they boasted, cursed God, and sought revenge rather than righteousness. Mormon fought for them anyway, hoping they would repent. They didn't (Mormon 2-3).
- 9.2 -- Mormon Refuses to Lead: Finally, Mormon could take no more. The Nephites had become so wicked that he refused to lead them. "I did utterly refuse from this time forth to be a commander" (Mormon 3:11). He stood as an idle witness of their destruction. But eventually, out of love for his people despite their wickedness, he agreed to lead them one final time.
- 9.3 -- The Gathering at Cumorah: Mormon knew the end was near. He wrote to the Lamanite king and asked for time to gather the Nephite nation at the Hill Cumorah for a final battle (Mormon 6:2). He hid the sacred records in the hill, giving only the abridged plates to his son Moroni. The Nephites gathered -- 230,000 warriors with their families. They knew this was the end.
- 9.4 -- The Last Battle: The Lamanites attacked. The slaughter was total. Of the 230,000 Nephite warriors, only twenty-four survived, including Mormon and Moroni (Mormon 6:10-15). Mormon looked over the battlefield and mourned: "O ye fair ones, how could ye have departed from the ways of the Lord!" (Mormon 6:17-20). A thousand years of civilization ended on that hill.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 10: Mormon's Final Words
**Core concepts:** Mormon's message to future readers, wounded, killed, Moroni takes the record

- 10.1 -- Mormon's Message to Future Readers: Even facing extinction, Mormon wrote for us. He told the future Lamanite descendants to "know ye that ye are of the house of Israel" (Mormon 7:2). He testified of Christ. He urged future readers to be baptized and follow Christ. His dying message was not about war or revenge -- it was about the gospel.
- 10.2 -- Mormon Is Killed: After the battle at Cumorah, Mormon was wounded. The Lamanites hunted down the survivors. Mormon was killed around 385 AD. He had spent his entire life serving a people who rejected God, leading armies he knew would lose, and writing a record he would never see read. His faith never wavered.
- 10.3 -- Moroni Takes Over: Mormon's son Moroni was left alone. He took the abridged record his father had given him and continued writing. He had no people, no home, no safety. The Lamanites hunted anyone with Nephite blood. Moroni wandered alone for decades, adding to the record when he could (Moroni 1:1-3).
- 10.4 -- Mormon and Moroni: Father and Son: Mormon and Moroni were both prophets in impossible circumstances. Mormon led a doomed army and wrote a book he'd never see published. Moroni wandered alone and sealed a record that wouldn't be read for 1,400 years. Their faith was not in outcomes they could see. It was in God's promise that the record would come forth in the last days.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 11: The Jaredites -- Rise
**Core concepts:** Tower of Babel, brother of Jared, crossing the ocean, a new civilization

- 11.1 -- The Tower of Babel: Long before Lehi, another group came to the Americas. At the Tower of Babel, when God confounded the languages, a man named Jared and his brother cried out to the Lord not to confound their language. The Lord heard them and told them to gather their families, friends, flocks, and seeds and travel to a land "choice above all the lands of the earth" (Ether 1:33-43).
- 11.2 -- The Brother of Jared: The brother of Jared (whose name, Mahonri Moriancumer, was revealed to Joseph Smith) was a man of extraordinary faith. When the Lord asked how to light their barges for the ocean crossing, the brother of Jared molten sixteen stones and asked the Lord to touch them (Ether 3:1-6). The Lord touched the stones with His finger. The brother of Jared saw the finger of God -- and then saw the Lord's entire spirit body. His faith was so great that the veil could not keep him out.
- 11.3 -- Crossing the Ocean: The Jaredites built eight barges -- tight like a dish, submersible, driven by the wind. They crossed the ocean in 344 days (Ether 6:11). They sang praises to God the entire journey. When they arrived, they wept for joy. It was a faithful, humble beginning to a civilization that would last for roughly 1,500 years.
- 11.4 -- A Warning About Kings: Jared's people wanted a king. The brother of Jared warned them: kings lead to captivity (Ether 6:22-23). They insisted. This choice -- the desire for a king despite the prophet's warning -- set the pattern for the entire Jaredite history. Just like Israel, just like the Nephites, the Jaredites would learn that rejecting prophetic counsel has consequences.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 12: The Jaredites -- Fall
**Core concepts:** Cycles of righteousness and wickedness, prophets rejected, complete destruction

- 12.1 -- The Jaredite Cycle: The Jaredites experienced the same cycle as the Nephites: righteousness brings prosperity, prosperity brings pride, pride brings wickedness, wickedness brings destruction, destruction brings humility, humility brings righteousness -- and the cycle repeats. Over roughly 1,500 years, the Jaredites went through this cycle many times.
- 12.2 -- Prophets Rejected: Throughout Jaredite history, God sent prophets to warn the people. The prophet Ether was the last. He prophesied about the New Jerusalem, about Christ, and about the destruction of the Jaredites if they didn't repent. They rejected him. He hid in a cave and watched the end from a distance (Ether 13:13-14).
- 12.3 -- The Final Destruction: The Jaredite civilization ended in a civil war between two factions led by Shiz and Coriantumr. The war consumed the entire nation. Millions died. In the end, only Coriantumr remained -- the last Jaredite, mortally wounded, alone among the dead (Ether 15:29-32). He was later found by the people of Zarahemla (the Mulekites).
- 12.4 -- Why the Jaredite Story Is in the Book of Mormon: Mormon (through Moroni) included the Jaredite record as a warning. A great civilization, blessed by God, destroyed itself through pride, secret combinations, and rejection of prophets. The parallels to the Nephites are deliberate. And the parallels to our time are the reason it's in the book.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 13: Moroni Alone
**Core concepts:** Wandering, preserving the record, Mormon's letters, Moroni's additions

- 13.1 -- The Last Nephite: After the battle at Cumorah, Moroni was alone. He wandered for at least 36 years (from about 385 to 421 AD), hiding from Lamanites who would kill him. He had no home, no people, no companion. He carried the sacred records and added to them when he could. He wrote, "I wander whithersoever I can for the safety of mine own life" (Moroni 1:3).
- 13.2 -- Mormon's Letters: Moroni included two letters from his father Mormon (Moroni 8-9). The first discusses infant baptism -- Mormon taught that little children are alive in Christ and do not need baptism (Moroni 8:8-12). The second describes the horrifying wickedness of the Nephites in their final days. These letters are precious -- they preserve a father's voice for his son across the centuries.
- 13.3 -- Moroni's Teachings: Moroni added important teachings to the record: the sacrament prayers (Moroni 4-5), instructions on conferring the Holy Ghost and ordaining priests and teachers (Moroni 2-3), and teachings on faith, hope, and charity (Moroni 7). He included practical information the future Church would need. He was building a handbook for a church that wouldn't exist for 1,400 years.
- 13.4 -- Preparing to Seal the Record: Moroni knew he would eventually bury the record. He had been told it would come forth in the last days. He was writing for people he would never meet, in a future he would never see. He had faith that God would bring the record to light -- and that it would change the world. He was right.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 14: Moroni's Promise
**Core concepts:** Moroni 10, the promise, spiritual gifts, come unto Christ, sealing the record

- 14.1 -- Moroni's Farewell: Moroni had expected to die long ago. He wrote several "final" entries, then kept living and kept writing. In Moroni 10, he wrote his true farewell -- his last message to the future readers who would hold this record in their hands. He wrote as someone who had nothing left to lose and everything to give.
- 14.2 -- The Promise: Moroni wrote the most quoted verse in all of Latter-day Saint missionary work: "When ye shall receive these things, I would exhort you that ye would ask God, the Eternal Father, in the name of Christ, if these things are not true; and if ye shall ask with a sincere heart, with real intent, having faith in Christ, he will manifest the truth of it unto you, by the power of the Holy Ghost" (Moroni 10:4). This is the test. Read, pray, and God will answer.
- 14.3 -- Spiritual Gifts: Moroni taught about spiritual gifts -- the gift of tongues, healing, faith, prophecy, visions, and more (Moroni 10:8-18). He insisted that these gifts have not ceased and that they exist wherever faith exists. "Deny not the gifts of God" (Moroni 10:8). God is still a God of miracles.
- 14.4 -- "Come Unto Christ": Moroni's final words: "Yea, come unto Christ, and be perfected in him, and deny yourselves of all ungodliness" (Moroni 10:32). He sealed the record. He buried it in the Hill Cumorah. And he waited -- for 1,400 years -- until a farm boy named Joseph Smith came to the hill and received the plates that Moroni had protected with his life. The Book of Mormon was coming forth.

**Test:** 4 questions.

### Chapter 15: The Book of Mormon -- Why It Matters
**Core concepts:** Review, the record's journey, what it teaches, your invitation

- 15.1 -- A Thousand Years in One Record: Over the course of three books, you've followed the Nephite civilization from Lehi's departure from Jerusalem to Moroni burying the plates. A thousand years of history -- prophets, kings, wars, covenants, destruction, and the visit of Jesus Christ. All of it preserved by faithful record keepers and compiled by one man, Mormon, for one purpose: to testify of Christ.
- 15.2 -- The Record's Journey: The plates were created by Nephi, passed through dozens of hands over a thousand years, abridged by Mormon, completed by Moroni, buried in the Hill Cumorah, delivered to Joseph Smith by the angel Moroni in 1827, translated by the gift and power of God, and published in 1830. The Book of Mormon traveled across centuries and continents to reach you.
- 15.3 -- What the Book of Mormon Teaches: The Book of Mormon teaches that Jesus is the Christ. It teaches that God keeps His covenants. It teaches that wickedness leads to destruction and righteousness leads to peace. It teaches that prophets are real and that rejecting them has consequences. It teaches that the Atonement can change anyone -- from Alma the Younger to the Anti-Nephi-Lehies. And it invites every reader to find out for themselves if it's true.
- 15.4 -- Your Invitation: Moroni's promise is still open. You've now read the story. You know the people, the events, the teachings. The question is the same one Moroni asked: Will you take it to God? Will you read the Book of Mormon, pray about it, and ask if it's true? The answer Moroni promised -- a witness through the Holy Ghost -- is available to you right now. The book is in your hands. The invitation is yours.

**Test:** 4 questions.
