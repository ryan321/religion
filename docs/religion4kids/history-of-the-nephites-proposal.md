# History of the Nephites for Kids -- Series Proposal

## Why This Series

Latter-day Saint kids read the Book of Mormon, but the narrative can be hard to follow -- especially the timeline, the geography, and the connections between different groups of people. Who are the Nephites, Lamanites, Mulekites, and people of Zarahemla? How do Zeniff's colony, Alma's people, and King Benjamin's kingdom fit together? Why do the war chapters matter?

This series gives young Latter-day Saints a continuous historical narrative of the Nephite civilization from Lehi's departure from Jerusalem to Moroni's final entry. It follows the people, the migrations, the wars, the prophets, and the covenants -- with scripture references throughout so kids can read the original accounts.

## Series Plan

**Book 1: From Jerusalem to Zarahemla** (~600 - ~92 BC)
1 Nephi through Mosiah. Lehi's departure, the journey, the promised land, the Nephite-Lamanite split, the small books, King Benjamin, Zeniff's colony, Abinadi, Alma the Elder, and Alma the Younger's conversion. Ends with the transition from kings to judges.

**Book 2: The Reign of the Judges** (~92 BC - ~1 AD)
Alma through Helaman. Alma the Younger as chief judge and high priest, Amulek, the Zoramites, Captain Moroni and the war chapters, Helaman and the stripling warriors, the Gadianton robbers, Nephi (son of Helaman), and Samuel the Lamanite. Ends with the signs of Christ's birth.

**Book 3: The Coming of Christ and the End** (~1 - 421 AD)
3 Nephi through Moroni, plus the Jaredites (Ether). Signs of the birth, destruction at Christ's death, Christ's appearance at the temple in Bountiful, 200 years of peace, the decline, Mormon's abridgment, the final battles, Moroni alone, and the Jaredite record. Ends with Moroni's promise.

## Perspective

Written for families and children who are active members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. The Book of Mormon is a true historical record. The events happened. The people were real. The tone is matter-of-fact, engaging, and never apologetic.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters per book, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- Scripture references included naturally
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7-12

## Book 1: From Jerusalem to Zarahemla

**Subtitle:** The First 500 Years

### Chapter 1: Lehi's Warning

**Core concepts:** Jerusalem in danger, Lehi called as prophet, his vision, preaching to the people, rejected

- 1.1 -- Jerusalem in Trouble
  - Around 600 BC, the kingdom of Judah was in serious danger. The Babylonian Empire was the dominant power in the region, and Judah's kings kept making bad decisions -- rebelling against Babylon, trusting Egypt for help that never came, and ignoring the prophets. Jeremiah was warning that Jerusalem would be destroyed. Other prophets were saying the same thing. Most people didn't want to hear it. The city was about to fall, and almost nobody was listening.

- 1.2 -- Lehi Is Called
  - In this dangerous time, God called a man named Lehi to be a prophet. Lehi lived in Jerusalem, was likely wealthy, and had a family -- his wife Sariah and at least six children. After praying on behalf of his people, Lehi saw a vision: God on His throne, angels, and the coming destruction of Jerusalem (1 Nephi 1:8-13). Like Jeremiah, Lehi was told to warn the people.

- 1.3 -- The People Reject Him
  - Lehi preached to the people of Jerusalem, telling them to repent or the city would be destroyed. The reaction was fierce. People mocked him. They were angry. They wanted to kill him -- just as they wanted to kill Jeremiah and the other prophets who were saying the same thing (1 Nephi 1:19-20). Jerusalem did not want to hear the truth.

- 1.4 -- The Command to Leave
  - The Lord warned Lehi in a dream to take his family and leave Jerusalem (1 Nephi 2:1-2). Lehi obeyed. He left behind his home, his wealth, his land -- everything except his family and provisions. They departed into the wilderness near the Red Sea. It was a dramatic, permanent departure. Lehi would never return. Neither would his family. And the city they left behind would be destroyed by Babylon just a few years later, exactly as the prophets had warned.

**Test:** 4 questions on Jerusalem's danger, Lehi's calling, the people's rejection, and the command to leave.

### Chapter 2: The Brass Plates

**Core concepts:** Laban and the plates, four attempts, Nephi's faith, why the scriptures mattered

- 2.1 -- Why They Needed the Plates
  - After the family had traveled into the wilderness, the Lord told Lehi to send his sons back to Jerusalem to get the brass plates from a man named Laban (1 Nephi 3:2-4). The brass plates were a record of the scriptures -- the five books of Moses, the history of the Jews, and the prophecies of the prophets. Without them, the family would lose their language, their history, and their knowledge of God's commandments. The scriptures were not optional. They were essential for survival as a covenant people.

- 2.2 -- Laman and Lemuel's Attempts
  - Lehi's oldest sons, Laman and Lemuel, were not happy about going back. They thought it was a "hard thing" (1 Nephi 3:5). Laman went to Laban first and simply asked for the plates. Laban refused and threatened to kill him. Then the brothers offered to buy the plates with the family's gold and silver. Laban took the treasure and sent his servants to kill them. They fled and hid. Laman and Lemuel were furious and beat Nephi and Sam with a rod.

- 2.3 -- Nephi's Faith
  - Nephi refused to give up. He said, "I will go and do the things which the Lord hath commanded, for I know that the Lord giveth no commandments unto the children of men, save he shall prepare a way for them that they may accomplish the thing which he commandeth them" (1 Nephi 3:7). Nephi went to Laban's house alone, at night, not knowing what he would do. He found Laban drunk and unconscious. The Spirit told Nephi to kill Laban -- a shocking command that Nephi struggled with before obeying (1 Nephi 4:10-18).

- 2.4 -- The Plates Secured
  - Nephi took Laban's clothes, disguised himself, and commanded Laban's servant Zoram to bring the plates. Zoram followed, thinking Nephi was Laban. When Zoram discovered the truth, Nephi convinced him to come with them, promising him freedom and a place among the family. They returned to Lehi's camp with the brass plates. The scriptures were secured, and the journey could continue. Sariah, who had feared her sons were dead, rejoiced (1 Nephi 5:1-8).

**Test:** 4 questions on why the plates were needed, the failed attempts, Nephi's faith (1 Nephi 3:7), and how they obtained the plates.

### Chapter 3: Ishmael's Family and the Wilderness

**Core concepts:** Ishmael's family joins, Laman and Lemuel's rebellion, the Liahona, traveling in the wilderness

- 3.1 -- Ishmael's Family Joins
  - The Lord told Lehi to send his sons back to Jerusalem again -- this time to bring the family of Ishmael so there would be families in the new land (1 Nephi 7:1-2). Ishmael agreed to come. His family included daughters who would marry Lehi's sons. But during the journey back, some of Ishmael's family sided with Laman and Lemuel, who wanted to go back to Jerusalem. They tied Nephi up. Nephi prayed, was freed, and the group continued to Lehi's camp.

- 3.2 -- The Liahona
  - One morning, Lehi found something outside his tent door -- a round ball of curious workmanship, made of fine brass, with two spindles (1 Nephi 16:10). This was the Liahona. It pointed the direction they should travel, and words appeared on it giving instructions from the Lord. But it only worked according to their faith and diligence. When they were faithful, it guided them. When they murmured and rebelled, it stopped working. The Liahona was a physical reminder that God's guidance depends on our faithfulness.

- 3.3 -- Hardship in the Wilderness
  - The journey through the Arabian wilderness was long and difficult. The family traveled for eight years (1 Nephi 17:4). They lived on raw meat when Nephi's steel bow broke. Ishmael died and was buried. Laman and Lemuel constantly complained and threatened Nephi. Sariah had children during the journey. The family suffered, argued, and endured. This was not a comfortable trip -- it was a grueling test of faith and endurance.

- 3.4 -- Nephi's Broken Bow
  - When Nephi's fine steel bow broke and the others' bows lost their spring, the family faced starvation (1 Nephi 16:18-32). Even Lehi began to murmur against the Lord. But Nephi made a new bow out of wood, made an arrow, and asked his father -- not Laman, not Lemuel, but his father the prophet -- where he should go to find food. Lehi was humbled, inquired of the Lord, and was told to look at the Liahona. It directed Nephi to food. The pattern: when things get hard, don't complain. Act, have faith, and follow the prophet.

**Test:** 4 questions on Ishmael's family joining, the Liahona and how it worked, hardships in the wilderness, and the broken bow story.

### Chapter 4: The Vision of the Tree of Life

**Core concepts:** Lehi's dream, the tree and the rod, Nephi's expanded vision, Laman and Lemuel don't understand

- 4.1 -- Lehi's Dream
  - Lehi had a powerful vision -- one of the most important in all of scripture (1 Nephi 8). He saw a tree whose fruit was "desirable to make one happy" and "most sweet, above all" (1 Nephi 8:10-11). He ate the fruit and wanted his family to come eat too. He saw a rod of iron leading to the tree along a strait and narrow path. He saw a great and spacious building filled with people mocking those who ate the fruit. Some people held the rod and reached the tree. Others let go and were lost in mists of darkness.

- 4.2 -- What the Symbols Mean
  - Nephi asked to understand his father's vision and was shown its meaning (1 Nephi 11-14). The tree represents the love of God. The fruit is the blessings of the Atonement and eternal life. The rod of iron is the word of God -- the scriptures. The mist of darkness is the temptations of the devil. The great and spacious building is the pride of the world. The strait and narrow path is the covenant path. Holding to the rod -- reading and following the scriptures -- is how you reach the tree.

- 4.3 -- Nephi Sees the Future
  - Nephi's vision went far beyond Lehi's dream. He saw the birth, ministry, and crucifixion of Jesus Christ. He saw the Twelve Apostles. He saw the Great Apostasy. He saw Columbus, the Pilgrims, the American Revolution, the Bible going to the nations, and the coming forth of the Book of Mormon (1 Nephi 13). He saw the last days and the work of the Lord among all nations. Nephi saw the entire sweep of history -- past, present, and future -- in one vision.

- 4.4 -- Laman and Lemuel Don't Understand
  - When Nephi returned from his vision, he found Laman and Lemuel arguing about their father's words. They said they couldn't understand them. Nephi asked, "Have ye inquired of the Lord?" They said no -- "the Lord maketh no such thing known unto us" (1 Nephi 15:8-9). That was the problem. They hadn't asked. They hadn't tried. They expected understanding to come without effort. Nephi taught them what the symbols meant, but the deeper lesson is that revelation requires action -- you have to ask.

**Test:** 4 questions on Lehi's dream, the meaning of the symbols, Nephi's expanded vision, and Laman and Lemuel's failure to ask.

### Chapter 5: Building the Ship

**Core concepts:** The command to build, Nephi's faith vs. brothers' doubt, crossing the ocean, arriving in the promised land

- 5.1 -- The Command to Build a Ship
  - After eight years in the wilderness, the family reached a place they called Bountiful -- a fertile coastal area. There, the Lord told Nephi to build a ship to carry them across the ocean (1 Nephi 17:8). Laman and Lemuel laughed. Build a ship? In the wilderness? Nephi had no shipbuilding experience. But God didn't ask Nephi to build it alone -- He showed him how, step by step. Nephi said, "If God had commanded me to do all things I could do them" (1 Nephi 17:50).

- 5.2 -- Nephi and His Brothers
  - Laman and Lemuel refused to help build the ship. They said Nephi was a fool. Nephi rebuked them -- powerfully -- reminding them of everything God had done for the children of Israel and for their own family. He reminded them of their own experiences: the angel who had stopped them from beating Nephi, the Liahona, the food in the wilderness. But they had short memories. When they tried to throw Nephi into the sea, the Lord filled Nephi with power and they were afraid (1 Nephi 17:48-55).

- 5.3 -- Crossing the Ocean
  - The ship was finished and the family sailed for the promised land. During the voyage, Laman, Lemuel, and some of Ishmael's sons began partying and behaving wildly. When Nephi told them to stop, they tied him up (1 Nephi 18:11). The Liahona stopped working. A terrible storm arose. For four days, the ship was driven back. Finally, Laman and Lemuel were frightened enough to untie Nephi. He prayed, the storm stopped, and the Liahona worked again. They continued to the promised land.

- 5.4 -- The Promised Land
  - The family arrived in the promised land -- somewhere in the Americas. They planted seeds, found animals, and discovered gold, silver, and ore (1 Nephi 18:24-25). It was a land of abundance. But the conflicts between Nephi and his older brothers were about to reach a breaking point. The promised land was beautiful, but the family that arrived in it was deeply divided. The seeds of the Nephite-Lamanite split had been growing for years. They were about to bear fruit.

**Test:** 4 questions on the command to build, Nephi vs. his brothers, the ocean crossing, and arrival in the promised land.

### Chapter 6: The Nephite-Lamanite Split

**Core concepts:** Lehi's death, the division, Nephites and Lamanites, two civilizations begin

- 6.1 -- Lehi's Final Words
  - Before he died, Lehi gathered his family and gave them blessings and counsel (2 Nephi 1-4). He testified of the promised land and warned that if the people became wicked, they would be cut off from God's presence. He pled with Laman and Lemuel to repent. He blessed his grandchildren. Then Lehi died. He had led his family out of Jerusalem, through the wilderness, across the ocean, and into the promised land. His mission was complete.

- 6.2 -- The Split
  - After Lehi's death, Laman and Lemuel were furious that Nephi continued to lead and teach. They wanted to kill him (2 Nephi 5:1-4). The Lord warned Nephi to take everyone who would follow him and leave. Nephi, Sam, Jacob, Joseph, Zoram, and their families departed and settled in a place they called the land of Nephi. Those who followed Nephi were called Nephites. Those who stayed with Laman were called Lamanites. The family was now permanently divided into two civilizations.

- 6.3 -- The Nephites
  - Nephi's people built a civilization. They constructed buildings, worked metals, and farmed the land. Nephi built a temple modeled after Solomon's temple. He taught his people from the brass plates and his own writings. He made swords for defense. He consecrated Jacob and Joseph as priests and teachers. The Nephites were a covenant people who tried to follow the Lord -- though, as the record shows, they often struggled.

- 6.4 -- The Lamanites
  - The Lamanites developed differently. The Book of Mormon describes them as a hunting people who lived in tents and harbored deep anger toward the Nephites. Laman and Lemuel taught their children that Nephi had wronged them and stolen their birthright. This grievance was passed from generation to generation for hundreds of years. It became the foundation of centuries of conflict between the two peoples. The family disagreement had become a civilizational divide.

**Test:** 4 questions on Lehi's final words, why the split happened, the Nephite civilization, and the Lamanite civilization.

### Chapter 7: Nephi's Teachings and Legacy

**Core concepts:** Nephi's prophecies about Christ, the Isaiah chapters, Nephi's psalm, passing the record

- 7.1 -- Nephi Teaches About Christ
  - Nephi's greatest contribution to the Book of Mormon may be his testimony of Jesus Christ. He saw the Savior's birth, ministry, and crucifixion in vision. He taught that all the prophets testified of Christ and that salvation comes through His name (2 Nephi 25:19-20). He wrote, "We talk of Christ, we rejoice in Christ, we preach of Christ, we prophesy of Christ...that our children may know to what source they may look for a remission of their sins" (2 Nephi 25:26). That verse tells you everything about what Nephi cared about most.

- 7.2 -- Isaiah in the Book of Mormon
  - Nephi included large portions of Isaiah's writings in his record (2 Nephi 12-24). He explained that Isaiah's words applied to the Nephites and to us. Isaiah is challenging to read, but Nephi gave us keys to understanding him: "I did liken all scriptures unto us, that it might be for our profit and learning" (1 Nephi 19:23). The Isaiah chapters in the Book of Mormon are not filler -- they contain prophecies about the scattering and gathering of Israel, the coming of the Messiah, and the last days.

- 7.3 -- Nephi's Psalm
  - Near the end of his record, Nephi wrote a deeply personal passage known as "Nephi's psalm" (2 Nephi 4:15-35). He rejoiced in the scriptures but also admitted his own weakness: "O wretched man that I am!" He was troubled by his sins and his temper. He asked God to help him. This is one of the most honest, vulnerable passages in all of scripture. Even Nephi -- the most faithful man in the Book of Mormon -- struggled with his imperfections and needed the Savior's grace.

- 7.4 -- Passing the Record
  - Nephi grew old and knew he would die. He passed the sacred records -- the brass plates and his own writings on the small plates -- to his brother Jacob, with instructions to continue the record (Jacob 1:1-4). Nephi had been the leader, prophet, teacher, and record keeper of his people for decades. He had led them out of danger, built their civilization, and testified of Christ. The record would now pass from hand to hand for the next thousand years.

**Test:** 4 questions on Nephi's testimony of Christ (2 Nephi 25:26), the Isaiah chapters, Nephi's psalm, and passing the record.

### Chapter 8: Jacob and His Successors

**Core concepts:** Jacob's ministry, Sherem the anti-Christ, Enos's prayer, the small books, passage of centuries

- 8.1 -- Jacob's Ministry
  - Jacob, Nephi's younger brother, was a powerful teacher. He condemned pride and the love of riches, and he defended the worth of every person. He taught about the Atonement using the allegory of the olive tree (Jacob 5) -- a long parable about God's patient work to save Israel. Jacob was direct, bold, and compassionate. He served as both priest and prophet to the Nephites after Nephi's death.

- 8.2 -- Sherem the Anti-Christ
  - During Jacob's ministry, a man named Sherem came among the people, denying that Christ would come (Jacob 7). Sherem was educated, persuasive, and skilled at arguing. He demanded a sign from God. Jacob testified of Christ and warned Sherem. The Lord struck Sherem down. Before dying, Sherem confessed he had been deceived by the devil and that Christ was real. The people's faith was restored. The pattern -- a charismatic person leading people away from Christ -- would repeat throughout Nephite history.

- 8.3 -- Enos's Prayer
  - Jacob's son Enos went hunting and found himself wrestling with God in prayer (Enos 1:1-8). He prayed all day and into the night. First he prayed for himself and received forgiveness. Then he prayed for the Nephites. Then he prayed for the Lamanites. His concern expanded outward -- from self to people to enemies. God promised Enos that the record would be preserved and someday come to the Lamanites. Enos's prayer is a model for how prayer can deepen and expand when we are truly seeking God.

- 8.4 -- The Small Books and the Passage of Time
  - After Enos, the record passes quickly through several keepers: Jarom, Omni, Amaron, Chemish, Abinadom, and Amaleki (Jarom, Omni). These books are short -- sometimes just a few verses. Centuries pass. The Nephites fight wars with the Lamanites, keep the commandments sometimes and break them other times, and gradually lose the spiritual intensity of Nephi's generation. By the time we reach Amaleki, the Nephites have left the land of Nephi and traveled to the land of Zarahemla, where they discover another people -- the Mulekites -- who had also come from Jerusalem.

**Test:** 4 questions on Jacob's ministry, Sherem, Enos's prayer, and the passage of time through the small books.

### Chapter 9: King Benjamin

**Core concepts:** Righteous king, his farewell address, service, the natural man, taking Christ's name

- 9.1 -- A Righteous King
  - King Benjamin ruled the Nephites in the land of Zarahemla. He was a warrior who defended his people against the Lamanites, but he was also a humble, righteous man who worked with his own hands so he wouldn't be a burden to his people (Mosiah 2:14). He taught alongside holy prophets to establish peace. When he grew old, he wanted to give his people one final message before his son Mosiah became king.

- 9.2 -- The Great Address
  - Benjamin built a tower so the people could hear him and gathered the entire nation (Mosiah 2:1-8). His address is one of the greatest sermons in scripture. He taught that when we serve others, we serve God: "When ye are in the service of your fellow beings ye are only in the service of your God" (Mosiah 2:17). He taught that we are all beggars before God -- none of us can repay what He has given us. We should not judge the poor because we are all poor before heaven.

- 9.3 -- The Natural Man
  - Benjamin taught about the Atonement of Jesus Christ -- by name, centuries before Christ's birth. An angel had appeared to Benjamin and told him what to teach. Benjamin declared that the natural man is an enemy to God and has been since the fall of Adam -- "unless he yields to the enticings of the Holy Spirit, and putteth off the natural man and becometh a saint through the atonement of Christ the Lord" (Mosiah 3:19). Becoming a saint isn't something we do alone. It's something Christ's Atonement makes possible.

- 9.4 -- Taking Christ's Name
  - The people were so moved by Benjamin's words that they fell to the earth and cried out for mercy, asking to receive the Atonement in their lives (Mosiah 4:1-2). The Spirit filled them and they were forgiven. Benjamin then invited them to take upon themselves the name of Christ -- to make a covenant. They did, all of them. This is what Latter-day Saints do at baptism and every time they take the sacrament: take upon themselves the name of Christ. King Benjamin made this covenant real and personal for an entire nation.

**Test:** 4 questions on King Benjamin as a leader, his teaching about service, the natural man, and taking Christ's name.

### Chapter 10: Zeniff's Colony

**Core concepts:** Zeniff goes to the land of Nephi, King Noah, Abinadi's warning, a wicked king

- 10.1 -- Zeniff's Expedition
  - While the Nephites were living in Zarahemla, a man named Zeniff led a group back to the original land of Nephi -- the land where Nephi had first settled after the split (Mosiah 9). Zeniff was "over-zealous" to reclaim the land. He made a deal with the Lamanite king to settle there. But the Lamanite king had a plan -- he let the Nephites build up the land so the Lamanites could eventually take it from them. Zeniff didn't realize he was walking into a trap.

- 10.2 -- Life in the Land of Nephi
  - Zeniff's people worked hard and prospered, but they lived surrounded by Lamanites who periodically attacked them. Zeniff defended his people successfully, trusting in the Lord. But after Zeniff grew old, his son Noah became king -- and Noah was nothing like his father. The story of Zeniff's colony shows how quickly things can change when leadership fails. One generation's faith can become the next generation's corruption.

- 10.3 -- King Noah
  - Noah was one of the most wicked kings in the Book of Mormon (Mosiah 11). He taxed the people heavily to fund his luxurious lifestyle -- fine buildings, thrones, expensive wine, and a court of wicked priests who lived off the people's labor. He replaced the righteous priests his father had appointed with men who were proud and lazy. The people followed Noah into wickedness. When prophets came to warn them, Noah's response was to try to kill them.

- 10.4 -- Abinadi Arrives
  - Into this corrupt kingdom came a prophet named Abinadi (Mosiah 11:20). Abinadi told the people that if they didn't repent, they would be conquered and enslaved. King Noah was furious and tried to arrest him. Abinadi escaped for two years, then returned disguised. He was captured and brought before King Noah and his priests. What happened next -- Abinadi's trial before the king -- is one of the most dramatic scenes in the entire Book of Mormon.

**Test:** 4 questions on Zeniff's expedition, life in the land of Nephi, King Noah's wickedness, and Abinadi's arrival.

### Chapter 11: Abinadi's Testimony

**Core concepts:** Abinadi before King Noah, teaching the Ten Commandments and Christ, his death, Alma believes

- 11.1 -- Abinadi Before the King
  - Abinadi stood before King Noah and his wicked priests, bound and on trial for his life (Mosiah 12-16). The priests tried to trap him with questions about Isaiah. Abinadi turned their questions back on them -- they were supposed to be teachers of the law, but they didn't even understand what they taught. He asked them, "Are you priests, and pretend to teach this people, and to understand the spirit of prophesying, and yet desire to know of me what these things mean?" (Mosiah 12:25). Then he taught them.

- 11.2 -- The Ten Commandments and the Law
  - Abinadi quoted the Ten Commandments and asked the priests if they kept them. They didn't -- and they knew it (Mosiah 13). Abinadi taught that the Law of Moses pointed to Christ. Every sacrifice, every ritual, every commandment was a type and shadow of Jesus Christ who would come. The law was not the end -- it was a teacher pointing to something greater. The priests should have known this. They didn't.

- 11.3 -- Abinadi Testifies of Christ
  - Abinadi delivered a powerful testimony of Jesus Christ -- His coming, His suffering, His Atonement, and His resurrection (Mosiah 15). He quoted Isaiah's prophecy of the suffering servant and explained that Christ would take upon Himself the sins of the world. "He shall be led, crucified, and slain, the flesh becoming subject even unto death" (Mosiah 15:7). Abinadi taught that salvation comes through Christ alone. When Noah ordered him to take back his words, Abinadi refused.

- 11.4 -- Death by Fire
  - King Noah ordered Abinadi to be killed. Abinadi warned that what they did to him would be a type of what would happen to them (Mosiah 17:15). He was burned to death, but as the flames consumed him, he prophesied that Noah would die the same way. And he did -- later, Noah's own people turned on him and burned him to death (Mosiah 19:20). Abinadi's courage cost him his life. But one man in the audience -- a priest named Alma -- had been listening. And Alma believed.

**Test:** 4 questions on Abinadi's trial, his teaching about the law and Christ, his testimony, and his death and the one who believed.

### Chapter 12: Alma at the Waters of Mormon

**Core concepts:** Alma's conversion, teaching in secret, baptism at the Waters of Mormon, fleeing from Noah

- 12.1 -- Alma Believes
  - Alma was one of King Noah's priests -- one of the wicked men who lived off the people's taxes. But when he heard Abinadi's testimony, something changed. He believed Abinadi's words and pleaded with Noah to let the prophet go. Noah expelled Alma and sent servants to kill him. Alma hid and wrote down Abinadi's teachings (Mosiah 17:2-4). One man's testimony, delivered at the cost of his life, converted one listener. That one listener would change everything.

- 12.2 -- Teaching in Secret
  - Alma went into hiding and began teaching Abinadi's words to anyone who would listen. He taught "privately" to avoid Noah's spies (Mosiah 18:1). People came to hear him in secret. He taught them about repentance, faith in Christ, and the covenant they could make through baptism. Alma was no longer a corrupt priest -- he was a repentant man teaching the truth he had heard from a martyr. His transformation is one of the most powerful conversion stories in scripture.

- 12.3 -- The Waters of Mormon
  - Alma led his followers to a place called the Waters of Mormon -- a spring surrounded by trees where they could meet without being discovered (Mosiah 18:5). There, Alma baptized his followers. The covenant he described is beautiful: "Willing to bear one another's burdens, that they may be light...willing to mourn with those that mourn; yea, and comfort those that stand in need of comfort" (Mosiah 18:8-9). This is what baptism means -- not just being dipped in water, but promising to carry each other.

- 12.4 -- Fleeing from King Noah
  - Noah discovered Alma's group and sent his army to destroy them. Alma and about 450 people fled into the wilderness (Mosiah 18:34). They established a small community called Helam, where they lived in peace for a time. But they would eventually be discovered by the Lamanites and brought into bondage. Even in bondage, the Lord was with them -- He lightened their burdens so they could bear them, and eventually He led them to freedom and to Zarahemla (Mosiah 24).

**Test:** 4 questions on Alma's conversion, teaching in secret, the baptismal covenant at the Waters of Mormon, and fleeing from Noah.

### Chapter 13: The People of Limhi

**Core concepts:** Noah's fall, Limhi's people in bondage, Ammon finds them, escape to Zarahemla

- 13.1 -- King Noah Falls
  - After Alma fled, things fell apart for King Noah. The Lamanites invaded. Noah tried to flee, ordering the men to leave their wives and children behind. Some refused and stayed. Noah was eventually caught by his own people and burned to death -- exactly as Abinadi had prophesied (Mosiah 19:20). Noah's son Limhi became king over the people who remained in the land of Nephi, now under Lamanite control.

- 13.2 -- Limhi's People in Bondage
  - Limhi's people were in a terrible situation. They were subject to the Lamanites and had to pay half of everything they produced as tribute (Mosiah 19:22). They tried to fight their way out three times and failed each time. Limhi was a decent man but his people were suffering the consequences of his father's wickedness. They cried out to the Lord, but the Lord was slow to answer -- they had been slow to turn to Him.

- 13.3 -- Ammon Finds Them
  - Back in Zarahemla, King Mosiah sent a search party of sixteen men led by Ammon to find out what had happened to Zeniff's colony (Mosiah 7:1-2). After wandering in the wilderness, they found Limhi's people. Limhi was overjoyed. Ammon told them about King Benjamin and the people in Zarahemla. Limhi told Ammon about Abinadi, Alma, and the 24 gold plates they had found -- plates that contained the record of the Jaredites, a much earlier civilization.

- 13.4 -- Escape to Zarahemla
  - With Ammon's help, Limhi's people devised an escape plan. They got the Lamanite guards drunk and slipped away in the night with their flocks, herds, and belongings (Mosiah 22:6-11). They traveled through the wilderness and eventually reached Zarahemla, where King Mosiah welcomed them. Meanwhile, Alma's people also made their way to Zarahemla after being freed from Lamanite bondage. Both groups -- Limhi's people and Alma's people -- were reunited with the main body of Nephites.

**Test:** 4 questions on Noah's fall, Limhi's bondage, Ammon's mission, and the escape to Zarahemla.

### Chapter 14: Alma the Younger

**Core concepts:** Alma's son rebels, the angel's visit, conversion, becoming a mighty preacher

- 14.1 -- The Sons Who Went Wrong
  - Alma (now called Alma the Elder) became the high priest over the Church in Zarahemla. But his son, also named Alma (Alma the Younger), rejected everything his father taught. Alma the Younger and the four sons of King Mosiah went about trying to destroy the Church, leading people away from the faith (Mosiah 27:8-10). Imagine being Alma the Elder -- you gave up everything to follow Christ, and your own son is actively working against you.

- 14.2 -- The Angel Appears
  - While Alma the Younger and the sons of Mosiah were on their way to cause more damage, an angel appeared to them with a voice like thunder (Mosiah 27:11-18). The angel said, "Alma, arise and stand forth, for why persecutest thou the church of God?" Alma fell to the earth, unable to move or speak. He was carried to his father. For three days and nights, Alma was "racked with eternal torment" -- he experienced the full weight of his sins. He later said he felt the pains of the damned.

- 14.3 -- Born Again
  - In his agony, Alma remembered his father's teachings about Jesus Christ. He cried out in his heart: "O Jesus, thou Son of God, have mercy on me" (Alma 36:18). Immediately, the pain was replaced by joy -- "as exceeding as was my pain" (Alma 36:20). He could see God on His throne and wanted to be there. Alma was born again. His conversion was dramatic, sudden, and complete. He went from the Church's worst enemy to one of its greatest champions.

- 14.4 -- A New Life
  - Alma the Younger spent the rest of his life preaching the gospel with extraordinary power. He eventually gave up the chief judgeship to focus on missionary work full-time (Alma 4:18-19). He said the only tool he needed was the word of God -- "the preaching of the word had a great tendency to lead the people to do that which was just -- yea, it had had more powerful effect upon the minds of the people than the sword" (Alma 31:5). The man who had tried to destroy the Church became the man who saved it.

**Test:** 4 questions on Alma the Younger's rebellion, the angel's visit, his conversion experience, and his new life as a preacher.

### Chapter 15: The Sons of Mosiah and the Transition to Judges

**Core concepts:** The sons of Mosiah go to the Lamanites, King Mosiah ends the monarchy, the reign of the judges begins

- 15.1 -- The Sons of Mosiah Choose Missions
  - After their conversion, the four sons of Mosiah -- Ammon, Aaron, Omner, and Himni -- wanted to make up for the damage they had done. They asked their father for permission to go preach the gospel to the Lamanites (Mosiah 28:1-7). This was dangerous -- the Lamanites were the Nephites' enemies. King Mosiah was afraid for his sons but inquired of the Lord, who promised they would be protected. They left for the land of Nephi and would spend fourteen years among the Lamanites.

- 15.2 -- Ammon and King Lamoni
  - Ammon went to the land of Ishmael and was captured and brought before King Lamoni (Alma 17-19). Ammon offered to be Lamoni's servant. While guarding the king's flocks, Ammon defended them against robbers, cutting off their arms with his sword. Lamoni was astonished and asked who Ammon was. Ammon taught him the gospel. The Spirit was so powerful that Lamoni, his wife, and many of his people fell to the earth and were converted. Ammon's mission is one of the great missionary stories in all of scripture.

- 15.3 -- The End of Kings
  - Back in Zarahemla, King Mosiah faced a problem -- none of his sons wanted to be king. They had chosen missionary work instead. Mosiah decided this was an opportunity to change the government entirely. He wrote to the people and argued that kings are dangerous: when you get a good king, things are great, but when you get a wicked king (like Noah), the people suffer terribly (Mosiah 29:16-17). Mosiah proposed replacing the monarchy with a system of judges chosen by the voice of the people.

- 15.4 -- The Reign of the Judges Begins
  - The people agreed. They chose Alma the Younger as the first chief judge and high priest (Mosiah 29:42). King Mosiah died shortly after. The era of kings was over. The reign of the judges had begun. This was a pivotal moment in Nephite history -- the shift from monarchy to a system of elected judges would shape everything that followed in the books of Alma and Helaman. The first 500 years of Nephite history -- from Lehi to the judges -- were complete.

**Test:** 4 questions on the sons of Mosiah's mission, Ammon and King Lamoni, why Mosiah ended the monarchy, and the beginning of the reign of the judges.
