# The Life of Joseph Smith for Kids -- Book Proposal

## Why This Book

The Restoration book covers the events of the Restoration with Joseph Smith as the central figure. But Joseph the person -- his family life, his personality, his friendships, his struggles, his growth -- gets compressed into the larger narrative. This book is different. It's a biography. It follows Joseph Smith from the prophecies about his coming, through his birth, childhood, young manhood, and every major season of his life, ending with his martyrdom at Carthage.

Kids in the Church know Joseph Smith as "the prophet of the Restoration." This book helps them know him as a person -- a real human being with a family, a sense of humor, physical courage, deep love for his friends, and an extraordinary burden placed on him by God at a very young age. The more they know Joseph as a person, the more they'll understand what it cost him to do what he did.

## Perspective

Written for families and children who are active members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Joseph Smith was a prophet of God -- the head of the last dispensation. That is the starting point. The book is honest about Joseph's humanity, his mistakes, and his growth without being critical or apologetic. He was a real person, and real people are more interesting than plaster saints.

## Relationship to The Restoration for Kids

The Restoration book focuses on events and doctrines -- what happened, what was restored, and why it matters. This book focuses on the man -- who Joseph was, what his life was like, how he handled the extraordinary things that happened to him, and what kind of person he was day to day. There is overlap in the events covered, but the lens is biographical rather than doctrinal.

## Series Context

One book covering the life of Joseph Smith from prophecies of his coming through his martyrdom on June 27, 1844.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- Scripture and historical references included naturally
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7-12

## Prerequisites

- None required. Can be read before or after The Restoration for Kids.

## The Life of Joseph Smith for Kids

**Subtitle:** The Prophet of the Restoration

### Chapter 1: Prophesied Before He Was Born

**Core concepts:** Prophecies about Joseph Smith in scripture, the name Joseph, the latter-day seer, why prophecy matters

- 1.1 -- Joseph of Egypt's Prophecy
  - Over 3,000 years before Joseph Smith was born, another Joseph -- Joseph who was sold into Egypt -- prophesied about him. In the Book of Mormon, we have Joseph of Egypt's words: a "choice seer" would be raised up in the last days from the loins of Joseph. "His name shall be called after me; and it shall be after the name of his father" (2 Nephi 3:15). Joseph Smith Jr., son of Joseph Smith Sr. -- both named Joseph, just as the ancient prophet said.

- 1.2 -- Isaiah and the Marvelous Work
  - Isaiah prophesied that the Lord would do "a marvellous work and a wonder" (Isaiah 29:14). He also spoke of a sealed book that would come forth -- a book that the learned could not read but the unlearned would bring to the world (Isaiah 29:11-12). Latter-day Saints recognize this as the Book of Mormon, brought forth through Joseph Smith, a young man without formal education. Isaiah saw it coming over 2,500 years before it happened.

- 1.3 -- Other Prophecies
  - The Bible contains other prophecies that Latter-day Saints connect to Joseph Smith and the Restoration. Ezekiel spoke of two sticks (records) becoming one -- the Bible and the Book of Mormon (Ezekiel 37:16-17). Malachi prophesied that Elijah would return before the great day of the Lord (Malachi 4:5-6) -- fulfilled when Elijah appeared in the Kirtland Temple. The Restoration was not an accident or an afterthought. It was planned from the beginning.

- 1.4 -- Why Prophecy Matters
  - God doesn't just react to history -- He plans it. The fact that Joseph Smith was prophesied about by name thousands of years before his birth tells you something important: God knew this young man before he was born, prepared the way for him, and had a specific mission for him. When Joseph walked into the Sacred Grove, he wasn't stumbling into something random. He was walking into something God had been preparing for thousands of years.

**Test:** 4 questions on Joseph of Egypt's prophecy, Isaiah's prophecy, other scriptural prophecies, and why prophecy matters.

### Chapter 2: The Smith Family

**Core concepts:** Joseph Sr. and Lucy Mack Smith, the family's character, financial struggles, their faith, siblings

- 2.1 -- Joseph Smith Sr.
  - Joseph's father was a tall, hardworking farmer and sometime merchant. He had little formal education but was deeply thoughtful about religion. He had several remarkable dreams and visions of his own -- Lucy Mack Smith recorded them in her history of the family. Joseph Sr. was not a member of any church, though he believed in God and studied the Bible. He was honest, kind, and struggled his whole life to provide for a large family in difficult economic times.

- 2.2 -- Lucy Mack Smith
  - Joseph's mother was a woman of tremendous faith and energy. She came from the Mack family of New England -- resourceful, independent-minded people. Lucy was deeply religious and searched for the true church of Christ throughout her life. She was the emotional and spiritual anchor of the family. She later wrote a detailed history of the Smith family and the early Church -- one of our most important primary sources for Joseph's early life.

- 2.3 -- Brothers and Sisters
  - Joseph grew up in a large family. His older brother Alvin was the natural leader of the family -- strong, capable, and encouraging of Joseph's spiritual experiences. Alvin's sudden death in 1823 was a devastating blow. Hyrum, Joseph's closest brother, became his lifelong companion and supporter. Samuel, William, Don Carlos, and several sisters rounded out the family. The Smiths were tight-knit. They worked together, prayed together, and supported each other through constant hardship.

- 2.4 -- A Family That Struggled
  - The Smiths were not wealthy. They moved multiple times -- from Vermont to New Hampshire to New York -- chasing opportunities that often didn't work out. A failed ginseng investment. Crop failures. A crooked merchant who cheated them out of their money. They eventually settled near Palmyra, New York, where they worked to buy a farm. The family knew poverty, hard labor, and disappointment. But they also knew faith, prayer, and loyalty to each other.

**Test:** 4 questions on Joseph Sr., Lucy Mack Smith, the siblings (especially Alvin and Hyrum), and the family's hardships.

### Chapter 3: Young Joseph

**Core concepts:** Childhood in Vermont and New York, the leg surgery, Joseph's personality, limited education

- 3.1 -- Born in Sharon, Vermont
  - Joseph Smith Jr. was born on December 23, 1805, in a small farmhouse in Sharon, Vermont. He was the fifth of eleven children. The family was poor but loving. Vermont in the early 1800s was a hard place to make a living -- rocky soil, short growing seasons, and constant financial pressure. The Smiths moved several times during Joseph's early childhood, always looking for better land and better opportunities.

- 3.2 -- The Leg Surgery
  - When Joseph was about seven years old, he developed a serious infection in his leg -- likely from typhoid fever. The doctors wanted to amputate. Joseph's mother refused to allow it. Instead, a surgeon performed an experimental operation, cutting into the bone to remove the infected parts -- without anesthesia. Joseph's father held him during the procedure. Joseph refused alcohol to dull the pain and asked only that his mother leave the room so she wouldn't have to watch. He walked on crutches for years afterward. It tells you something about this boy's courage even at seven.

- 3.3 -- Joseph's Personality
  - People who knew Joseph described him as cheerful, energetic, and fun to be around. He was physically strong and loved to wrestle, play games, and joke with friends. He was also deeply feeling -- he cried easily when moved by the Spirit or by the suffering of others. He was not a quiet, bookish child. He was active, sociable, and curious. He was the kind of person people naturally wanted to be around.

- 3.4 -- Limited Education
  - Joseph had very little formal schooling -- probably no more than a few years total. His family couldn't afford to keep him in school when there was work to be done. He could read and write, but his early letters show rough spelling and grammar. This matters because of what he would later accomplish. The Book of Mormon -- a complex, 500-page record of history, doctrine, and prophecy -- was produced by a young man who could barely compose a letter. His lack of education is evidence, not weakness.

**Test:** 4 questions on Joseph's birth and early moves, the leg surgery, his personality, and his limited education.

### Chapter 4: The Religious Excitement and the First Vision

**Core concepts:** The Burned-over District, James 1:5, the Sacred Grove, the vision

- 4.1 -- The Burned-over District
  - When the Smiths settled near Palmyra, New York, the region was in the middle of intense religious excitement. Preachers from many denominations held revivals, camp meetings, and outdoor preaching events. Baptists, Methodists, Presbyterians, and others all competed for converts. The region was called the "Burned-over District" because it had been swept by so many waves of religious fervor. Joseph's family was divided -- some leaned Presbyterian, others were unsure. Joseph was drawn to Methodism but confused by the conflicting claims.

- 4.2 -- James 1:5
  - In the midst of this confusion, Joseph read the Bible and came across James 1:5: "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God, that giveth to all men liberally, and upbraideth not; and it shall be given him." The verse struck him with extraordinary force. "Never did any passage of scripture come with more power to the heart of man than this did at this time to mine" (Joseph Smith--History 1:12). The answer was simple: if he wanted to know the truth, he should ask God directly.

- 4.3 -- Into the Grove
  - On a spring morning in 1820, fourteen-year-old Joseph walked into a grove of trees near his family's farm to pray. He had never prayed vocally before. He knelt and began to speak. Almost immediately, a dark force seized him -- he couldn't speak, he felt as if he were being destroyed. At the moment he was about to give up, he called upon God with everything he had. A pillar of light descended and rested upon him, and the darkness fled (Joseph Smith--History 1:15-17).

- 4.4 -- "This Is My Beloved Son"
  - In the light, Joseph saw two personages standing above him. One of them called Joseph by name, pointed to the other, and said, "This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!" (Joseph Smith--History 1:17). God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to a farm boy in a grove of trees. Joseph asked which church to join. He was told to join none of them. The fulness of the gospel would be made known to him in the future. A fourteen-year-old boy had just received the most important revelation since the Resurrection.

**Test:** 4 questions on the religious excitement, James 1:5, what happened in the grove, and who appeared and what they said.

### Chapter 5: Persecution and Preparation

**Core concepts:** Telling others, ridicule and opposition, Moroni's visits, the four years of waiting

- 5.1 -- Nobody Believed Him
  - Joseph told a local Methodist minister about his vision. The minister dismissed it, saying visions had ceased and that the experience was from the devil. Other people in the community mocked him. Joseph was astonished -- he was just a boy telling the truth about what had happened. But the pattern was set early: Joseph would tell the truth, and people would attack him for it. He later wrote that he felt like Paul -- persecuted for telling what he had seen, but unable to deny it (Joseph Smith--History 1:24-25).

- 5.2 -- Three Years Between
  - From 1820 to 1823, Joseph received no further major revelations. He was a teenager working on the farm, helping his family, and living a normal life. He later admitted he made mistakes during this period -- he was young and sometimes got caught up in foolish things. But he never forgot what he had seen in the grove. He prayed on the night of September 21, 1823, to know his standing before God.

- 5.3 -- Moroni's Visits
  - That night, the angel Moroni appeared in Joseph's room. Moroni was a resurrected being who had been the last prophet to write in the gold plates -- the same plates that would become the Book of Mormon. Moroni told Joseph about the plates, where they were buried, and the work God had for him. Moroni visited three times that night, each time repeating the message and adding details. The next day, Joseph went to the Hill Cumorah and saw the plates.

- 5.4 -- Four Years of Waiting
  - Moroni told Joseph he couldn't take the plates yet. He would need to return to the hill every September for four years, meeting with Moroni, receiving instruction, and being prepared. During these years, Joseph's beloved brother Alvin died. Joseph worked, grew up, and eventually married Emma Hale. The waiting was hard, but it served a purpose -- Joseph was being shaped into the person who could handle the responsibility of the plates.

**Test:** 4 questions on the persecution after the First Vision, the three-year gap, Moroni's visits, and the four years of preparation.

### Chapter 6: Emma and the Early Years of Marriage

**Core concepts:** Meeting Emma Hale, courtship, marriage, partnership, Emma's sacrifices

- 6.1 -- Meeting Emma
  - Joseph met Emma Hale while working for Josiah Stowell near Harmony, Pennsylvania, in 1825. Emma was tall, dark-haired, intelligent, and well-educated -- more educated than Joseph. Her father, Isaac Hale, did not approve of Joseph. He saw Joseph as a poor young man with strange religious ideas and no prospects. But Emma saw something in Joseph that her father didn't.

- 6.2 -- Marriage
  - Joseph and Emma eloped and were married on January 18, 1827, in South Bainbridge, New York. They moved in with Joseph's family near Palmyra. The marriage was a genuine partnership -- Emma supported Joseph's mission from the beginning, even when it brought hardship, danger, and heartbreak. She would become one of the most important women in Church history, though her life was marked by extraordinary sacrifice.

- 6.3 -- Emma as Scribe and Partner
  - Emma was Joseph's first scribe during the translation of the Book of Mormon. She sat on the other side of a curtain while Joseph dictated. She later testified that Joseph could not have written the Book of Mormon on his own -- he couldn't even compose a coherent letter at the time. She saw the process firsthand and knew it was beyond Joseph's natural ability. Emma was not just a supportive wife. She was a witness.

- 6.4 -- The Cost of Being a Prophet's Wife
  - Emma's life was extraordinarily difficult. She lost multiple children in infancy. She was forced to flee from mobs. She lived in poverty for much of her marriage. She packed up and moved repeatedly -- from Palmyra to Harmony to Kirtland to Far West to Nauvoo. She gave up comfort, stability, and sometimes safety to stand by Joseph. Not every moment was easy between them, but Emma's sacrifices were real and they were immense.

**Test:** 4 questions on how Joseph and Emma met, their marriage, Emma as scribe/witness, and the cost of being a prophet's wife.

### Chapter 7: The Book of Mormon Comes Forth

**Core concepts:** The 116 pages, Oliver Cowdery, the translation, the witnesses, publication

- 7.1 -- The 116 Pages
  - Joseph received the gold plates on September 22, 1827. Martin Harris, a wealthy farmer, served as scribe. Martin's wife Lucy pressured him to prove the plates were real. Martin begged Joseph to let him take the first 116 pages of manuscript to show her. Joseph asked the Lord and was told no. Martin kept asking. Eventually the Lord permitted it -- and Martin lost the pages. Joseph was devastated. The Lord rebuked him sharply (D&C 3). The gift of translation was taken away for a time. Joseph learned a painful lesson about yielding to pressure.

- 7.2 -- Oliver Cowdery
  - In April 1829, a young schoolteacher named Oliver Cowdery arrived and became Joseph's primary scribe. The two worked together with remarkable speed and intensity. Oliver later described the experience as "days never to be forgotten -- to sit under the sound of a voice dictated by the inspiration of heaven." Oliver became one of the Three Witnesses who saw the gold plates and the angel Moroni. He and Joseph were baptized together after John the Baptist restored the Aaronic Priesthood.

- 7.3 -- The Translation
  - Joseph translated the Book of Mormon by the gift and power of God. The bulk of the text was translated in approximately 65 working days -- a pace that astonishes scholars. Joseph, with his limited education, produced a 500-page record containing complex narrative, detailed geography, distinct writing styles, extensive doctrinal teaching, and hundreds of internal consistencies. He dictated without notes, without going back to review, and without revision. The book itself is evidence of divine assistance.

- 7.4 -- Witnesses and Publication
  - Before publication, the Lord provided witnesses. The Three Witnesses -- Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris -- were shown the plates by an angel and heard the voice of God. The Eight Witnesses handled the plates physically. Their testimonies are printed in every copy of the Book of Mormon. The book was published in March 1830 by E.B. Grandin in Palmyra. Martin Harris mortgaged his farm to pay for 5,000 copies. The Book of Mormon was now in the world.

**Test:** 4 questions on the 116 pages, Oliver Cowdery, the translation process, and the witnesses.

### Chapter 8: Prophet, Seer, and Revelator

**Core concepts:** The Church organized, Joseph's leadership style, receiving revelations, daily life as a prophet

- 8.1 -- April 6, 1830
  - The Church of Christ was organized on April 6, 1830, in a small log home in Fayette, New York, with six members. Joseph was sustained as the first elder and prophet. It was a humble beginning for what would become a worldwide church. From this day forward, Joseph was not just a translator or a visionary -- he was the leader of a growing religious community. He was twenty-four years old.

- 8.2 -- How Joseph Led
  - Joseph was not a distant, formal leader. He worked alongside the Saints, helped build their houses, played with their children, and shared their hardships. He led by example and by affection. People followed him because they loved him, not because he demanded obedience. He was also willing to be corrected -- when the Lord rebuked him (as with the 116 pages), he accepted it and changed. He wasn't perfect, and he didn't pretend to be.

- 8.3 -- Receiving Revelations
  - Joseph received revelations frequently -- instructions from the Lord on everything from Church organization to individual callings to doctrinal questions. These revelations became the Doctrine and Covenants. Sometimes revelations came in response to specific questions. Sometimes they came unexpectedly. Joseph treated revelation as a normal part of his relationship with God -- not a dramatic, rare event, but an ongoing conversation. "A prophet is a prophet only when he is acting as such," he taught.

- 8.4 -- Daily Life
  - Being a prophet didn't remove Joseph from ordinary life. He chopped wood, farmed, ran a store, studied languages, practiced law, served as mayor, and led a militia. He dealt with financial problems, family illnesses, and the deaths of children. He was not lifted above the difficulties of mortality. He experienced them fully -- and that experience made him more compassionate toward others who suffered.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Church's organization, Joseph's leadership style, how he received revelations, and his daily life.

### Chapter 9: Kirtland Years

**Core concepts:** The gathering to Kirtland, the School of the Prophets, the Kirtland Temple, triumphs and failures

- 9.1 -- Gathering to Kirtland
  - In early 1831, Joseph and Emma moved to Kirtland, Ohio, where a large group of converts awaited them. Kirtland became the Church's first headquarters. It was a period of rapid growth, intense revelation, and constant challenge. Joseph was learning how to lead a growing church, manage finances, oversee building projects, and handle disagreements -- all while continuing to receive revelation from God.

- 9.2 -- The School of the Prophets
  - Joseph organized the School of the Prophets in 1833 -- a place for Church leaders to study the gospel, learn Hebrew, and prepare for their callings. Joseph himself studied constantly. He studied Hebrew with a Jewish professor. He studied history, law, and language. He believed deeply in education: "The glory of God is intelligence" (D&C 93:36). Joseph was not content to be an uneducated farm boy. He was constantly growing, learning, and improving.

- 9.3 -- The Kirtland Temple
  - The Lord commanded the Saints to build a temple in Kirtland (D&C 95). The Saints were desperately poor, but they gave everything. Women crushed their china for the plaster. Men quarried stone in winter. Joseph worked alongside them. The temple took three years to build. At its dedication on March 27, 1836, the Spirit was poured out in power. One week later, the Savior appeared to Joseph and Oliver and accepted the temple (D&C 110). Moses, Elias, and Elijah restored priesthood keys.

- 9.4 -- The Kirtland Crisis
  - Not everything in Kirtland went well. Joseph helped establish a financial institution -- the Kirtland Safety Society -- that failed during the national financial panic of 1837. Many members lost money and blamed Joseph. Some of his closest friends turned against him. Apostles left the Church. Joseph faced lawsuits, threats, and an atmosphere of betrayal. By early 1838, Joseph had to flee Kirtland for his safety. Success and failure, revelation and betrayal -- Kirtland had it all.

**Test:** 4 questions on the gathering to Kirtland, the School of the Prophets, the Kirtland Temple, and the Kirtland crisis.

### Chapter 10: Missouri -- Suffering and Endurance

**Core concepts:** Zion in Missouri, mob violence, the Extermination Order, Haun's Mill, Liberty Jail

- 10.1 -- The Idea of Zion
  - The Lord revealed that the land of Zion -- where the Saints would build the New Jerusalem -- was in Jackson County, Missouri (D&C 57:1-3). Joseph visited Missouri in 1831 and dedicated the land. Saints began settling there. But the existing Missouri settlers deeply distrusted the Saints -- their religion was unfamiliar, they arrived in large numbers, many were Northerners opposed to slavery, and they voted together. Conflict was almost immediate.

- 10.2 -- Driven from County to County
  - In 1833, mobs drove the Saints from Jackson County -- burning homes, destroying the Church's printing press, tarring and feathering men. The Saints relocated to Clay County, then to Caldwell County (created specifically for the Mormons), and finally to Far West. At each stop, the pattern repeated: the Saints built, neighbors grew hostile, and violence followed. Joseph organized Zion's Camp in 1834 -- a march to Missouri to help the Saints -- but the Lord disbanded it before fighting occurred.

- 10.3 -- The Extermination Order and Haun's Mill
  - In October 1838, Governor Lilburn Boggs issued the Extermination Order: "The Mormons must be treated as enemies, and must be exterminated or driven from the State." Days later, a mob attacked the small settlement of Haun's Mill, killing seventeen men and boys. Joseph and other Church leaders surrendered to the militia to prevent further bloodshed. They were court-martialed and sentenced to be shot, but General Alexander Doniphan refused to carry out the order, calling it "cold-blooded murder."

- 10.4 -- Liberty Jail
  - Joseph spent the winter of 1838-1839 in Liberty Jail -- a cold, cramped dungeon with a low ceiling and no proper sanitation. He and his companions were fed rotten food and taunted by guards. In his darkest hours, Joseph cried out: "O God, where art thou?" The Lord answered with some of the most profound words in all of scripture: "My son, peace be unto thy soul; thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment; and then, if thou endure it well, God shall exalt thee on high" (D&C 121:7-8). Joseph later called Liberty Jail a "temple" -- a place where God taught him things he could not have learned in comfort.

**Test:** 4 questions on the idea of Zion, being driven from county to county, the Extermination Order/Haun's Mill, and Liberty Jail.

### Chapter 11: Nauvoo -- Joseph the City Builder

**Core concepts:** Building Nauvoo, Joseph as mayor and leader, the Nauvoo Legion, daily life in Nauvoo

- 11.1 -- From Swamp to City
  - After escaping Missouri in 1839, Joseph led the Saints to a swampy bend of the Mississippi River in Illinois. They purchased the land, drained the swamps (losing many to malaria in the process), and built a city they named Nauvoo. Within a few years, Nauvoo was one of the largest cities in Illinois -- larger than Chicago at the time. Joseph was at the center of it all, directing construction, planning city layout, and welcoming thousands of new arrivals.

- 11.2 -- Joseph as Mayor and Military Leader
  - Joseph served as mayor of Nauvoo and lieutenant general of the Nauvoo Legion -- a state-authorized militia of several thousand men. He was simultaneously the religious leader, political leader, and military leader of the community. These overlapping roles made him powerful but also made enemies. Critics -- both inside and outside the Church -- worried about the concentration of power. Joseph saw it as necessity; his enemies saw it as ambition.

- 11.3 -- Joseph the Man in Nauvoo
  - Nauvoo is the period from which we have the most descriptions of Joseph's personality. He wrestled with young men, played ball, pulled sticks (a frontier strength contest), and raced horses. He visited the sick, blessed children, and comforted the grieving. He hosted dinners, told stories, and laughed easily. He was physically imposing -- over six feet tall, broad-shouldered, and strong. He was also tender. He wept openly when friends died or when speaking of sacred things.

- 11.4 -- Family Life in Nauvoo
  - Joseph and Emma had several children, but they buried many in infancy. The grief was constant and real. Despite the demands on his time, Joseph was a devoted father. He played with his children, told them stories, and involved them in his life. Emma ran the household, managed finances, hosted visitors, and served as the first president of the Relief Society. Their home was almost never private -- visitors, refugees, and Church leaders came and went constantly. The prophet's house was the community's house.

**Test:** 4 questions on building Nauvoo, Joseph's public roles, his personality, and his family life.

### Chapter 12: Joseph the Teacher

**Core concepts:** Major doctrines Joseph taught, the King Follett discourse, the plan of salvation, Joseph's vision of God

- 12.1 -- The Plan of Salvation
  - Joseph taught more about the plan of salvation than any previous prophet. Through revelation, he explained where we came from (premortal life), why we're here (to gain a body and be tested), and where we're going (resurrection and degrees of glory). The vision of the three degrees of glory (D&C 76) was revolutionary -- instead of a simple heaven and hell, Joseph taught that God's plan includes a place for everyone, with rewards matched to how people lived. These teachings came through revelation, not philosophy.

- 12.2 -- Temples and Eternal Families
  - Joseph's greatest doctrinal contribution may be the teaching that families can be sealed together for eternity. In the Nauvoo period, Joseph introduced the temple endowment and the sealing ordinance. He taught that the same sociality that exists among us here will exist in eternity (D&C 130:2). Marriage and family are not just for this life -- they are eternal. This doctrine is unique to the Restoration and it changes everything about how Latter-day Saints see death, family, and the purpose of temples.

- 12.3 -- The King Follett Discourse
  - On April 7, 1844 -- just weeks before his death -- Joseph preached what may be his greatest sermon. At the funeral of a member named King Follett, Joseph taught about the nature of God, the potential of human beings, and the eternal nature of the soul. "God himself was once as we are now, and is an exalted man," he taught. He declared that human beings have the potential to become like God -- a doctrine that astonished some and offended others, but which Joseph taught with absolute confidence because he had seen the Father and the Son.

- 12.4 -- Joseph's View of God
  - Joseph taught that God is a real being with a body of flesh and bones, glorified and perfected (D&C 130:22). He taught that the Father, Son, and Holy Ghost are three separate beings, united in purpose. He taught that God is our literal Father and that we are His children. These teachings contradicted centuries of Christian tradition -- but they came from a man who had stood in God's presence and spoke from firsthand experience. Joseph didn't speculate about God. He knew Him.

**Test:** 4 questions on the plan of salvation, eternal families, the King Follett discourse, and Joseph's teachings about God.

### Chapter 13: Gathering Storms

**Core concepts:** Internal dissent, political tensions, the Nauvoo Expositor, the road to Carthage

- 13.1 -- Enemies from Within
  - Not all opposition came from outside. Some of Joseph's closest associates turned against him. William Law, a member of the First Presidency, broke with Joseph and became one of his fiercest critics. Other former leaders joined the opposition. Dissent within the Church was particularly painful for Joseph -- these were people he had trusted, served with, and loved. Betrayal by friends cut deeper than attacks by strangers.

- 13.2 -- Political Tensions
  - Joseph's political influence in Nauvoo worried both Democrats and Whigs. He briefly ran for president of the United States in 1844 -- not because he expected to win, but to raise awareness of the Saints' mistreatment and to advocate for religious liberty. His candidacy, combined with the Nauvoo city charter's broad powers, made him a target. Politicians who had courted the Mormon vote now saw Joseph as a threat.

- 13.3 -- The Nauvoo Expositor
  - In June 1844, dissenters published a newspaper called the Nauvoo Expositor, attacking Joseph and the Church. The Nauvoo city council declared the paper a public nuisance and ordered the press destroyed. This act -- legal under the standards of the time but controversial -- gave Joseph's enemies the pretext they needed. Joseph was charged with riot and ordered to stand trial in Carthage, the county seat. He knew the danger.

- 13.4 -- "Like a Lamb to the Slaughter"
  - Joseph considered fleeing west. Some urged him to go. But others accused him of abandoning his people. Joseph decided to go to Carthage. He said, "If my life is of no value to my friends, it is of none to myself." On the way, he reportedly said, "I am going like a lamb to the slaughter; but I am calm as a summer's morning; I have a conscience void of offense towards God, and towards all men" (D&C 135:4). He crossed the Mississippi toward Carthage, knowing he might not come back.

**Test:** 4 questions on internal dissent, political tensions, the Nauvoo Expositor, and Joseph's decision to go to Carthage.

### Chapter 14: Carthage

**Core concepts:** The jail, the last days, the attack, the deaths of Joseph and Hyrum

- 14.1 -- Carthage Jail
  - Joseph, Hyrum, John Taylor, and Willard Richards were held in Carthage Jail. The governor of Illinois, Thomas Ford, promised them protection. But Ford left Carthage on June 27, taking the reliable troops with him and leaving the jail guarded by a unit known to be hostile to the Saints. Joseph sensed what was coming. The four men spent their last hours together talking, singing, and reading scripture. Hyrum read from the Book of Mormon -- from Ether 12, about faith, hope, and charity.

- 14.2 -- The Last Hours
  - On the afternoon of June 27, 1844, Joseph asked John Taylor to sing "A Poor Wayfaring Man of Grief" -- a hymn about serving Christ by serving others. Taylor sang it twice. The mood was somber but peaceful. Joseph bore his testimony to the guards. He wrote a letter to Emma. He was not panicking. He was preparing.

- 14.3 -- The Attack
  - Late in the afternoon, a mob of about 150 men with painted faces stormed the jail. They rushed up the stairs and fired through the door. Hyrum was hit first. He staggered and fell, saying, "I am a dead man." Joseph rushed to his brother. The mob kept firing. John Taylor was hit multiple times -- a pocket watch in his vest absorbed a bullet that would have killed him. Willard Richards escaped with only a scratch. Joseph went to the window and was struck by bullets from inside and outside the building. He fell from the second-story window.

- 14.4 -- The Prophet Is Dead
  - Joseph Smith died at the age of thirty-eight. Hyrum Smith was forty-four. The Prophet and the Patriarch of the Church were gone. "In life they were not divided, and in death they were not separated" (D&C 135:3). When word reached Nauvoo, the grief was indescribable. Thousands viewed the bodies. The question everyone asked: Is it over? Joseph's enemies expected the Church to collapse without him. They underestimated what Joseph had built -- and what God had planned.

### Chapter 15: His Legacy

**Core concepts:** What Joseph left behind, the keys passed to the Twelve, the testimony that endures, Joseph's place in history

- 15.1 -- What He Left Behind
  - Joseph Smith died at thirty-eight, but look at what he accomplished in that short life. He translated the Book of Mormon. He received the Doctrine and Covenants. He restored the priesthood. He organized the Church. He built two cities and two temples. He sent missionaries across the world. He introduced temple ordinances, eternal marriage, and the plan of salvation. He established the Relief Society. He trained the Twelve Apostles. He did more in his short life than most people could do in ten lifetimes.

- 15.2 -- The Keys Were Safe
  - Before his death, Joseph had conferred every priesthood key, every power, and every authority upon the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. He told them, "I have lived until I have seen this burden, which has rested on my shoulders, rolled on to the shoulders of other men." He knew he might not survive. He made sure the Church would. Brigham Young, as the senior apostle, led the Church forward. The Restoration did not depend on one man staying alive -- Joseph had made sure of that.

- 15.3 -- The Testimony That Endures
  - Joseph's testimony has been tested by nearly two hundred years of scrutiny. Critics have attacked his character, his claims, and his history from every angle. But the Book of Mormon is still here. The Church is still here. The temples are still here. Millions of people in every nation have read the Book of Mormon, prayed about it, and received their own witness that it is true. Joseph's testimony doesn't depend on anyone defending it -- it depends on people testing it for themselves, just as Joseph tested James 1:5.

- 15.4 -- A Prophet for Our Time
  - Joseph Smith was the prophet of the last dispensation -- the dispensation of the fulness of times. Through him, God restored everything that had been lost since the Apostasy. The priesthood Joseph received is the same priesthood used in your ward today. The Book of Mormon he translated is the same book you study at home. The temple ordinances he introduced are the same ordinances performed in temples around the world right now. Joseph's work is not history. It is your present. And understanding his life helps you understand your own.
