# The Ministry of Jesus Christ for Kids -- Book Proposal

## Why This Book

Latter-day Saint kids hear about Jesus every week -- in Primary, sacrament meeting, Come Follow Me, and family scripture study. But the individual stories often float without a clear sense of sequence. What happened first? How did opposition build over time? Why did the leaders want Him dead? What happened between the Crucifixion and the Resurrection? And what did He do after He rose?

This book gives young Latter-day Saints a continuous narrative of the Savior's life and ministry -- from His birth in Bethlehem through His post-resurrection visits to the Nephites and "other sheep." It follows the chronological arc: birth, preparation, baptism, calling the Twelve, teachings, miracles, parables, growing opposition, the final week, the Atonement, Crucifixion, visit to the spirit world, Resurrection, and appearances to multiple groups including the Nephites. Scripture references are included throughout so kids can look up the accounts for themselves.

## Perspective

This book is written for families and children who are active members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Jesus Christ is the Son of God, the Only Begotten of the Father, the Savior and Redeemer of the world. That is not argued or defended -- it is the starting point. The Book of Mormon, Doctrine and Covenants, and Pearl of Great Price are used alongside the New Testament as complementary witnesses. The tone is matter-of-fact, reverent where appropriate, and never apologetic.

## Series Context

One book covering the ministry of Jesus Christ from birth through post-resurrection appearances. Pairs naturally with History of Israel for Kids (which provides the historical context for the world Jesus was born into) but stands alone.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- Scripture references included naturally (e.g., Matthew 5:14, 3 Nephi 11:10)
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7-12
- Real-world examples and comparisons to help kids connect

## Prerequisites

- None required. No prior scripture knowledge assumed. Pairs well with History of Israel for Kids but does not depend on it.

## The Ministry of Jesus Christ for Kids

**Subtitle:** The Life, Teachings, and Atonement of the Savior

### Chapter 1: Prophecies and the Birth of Jesus

**Core concepts:** Old Testament and Book of Mormon prophecies, the Annunciation, born in Bethlehem, shepherds, wise men

- 1.1 -- The World Was Waiting
  - For centuries, prophets had told Israel that a Messiah would come. Isaiah described a virgin who would bear a son called Immanuel (Isaiah 7:14). Micah said He would be born in Bethlehem (Micah 5:2). Book of Mormon prophets were equally clear -- King Benjamin said He would be called Jesus Christ and would come down among the children of men (Mosiah 3:8). Nephi saw His birth in a vision over 500 years before it happened (1 Nephi 11:18-21). The entire scriptural record points to this event.

- 1.2 -- The Announcements
  - The angel Gabriel appeared to Mary in Nazareth and told her she would bear the Son of God (Luke 1:26-35). Mary's cousin Elisabeth was already expecting John, who would prepare the way. An angel also appeared to Joseph, telling him not to be afraid to take Mary as his wife (Matthew 1:20-21). In the Americas, the prophet Samuel the Lamanite prophesied the signs of the Savior's birth -- a day, a night, and a day with no darkness, and a new star (Helaman 14:3-6).

- 1.3 -- Born in Bethlehem
  - A Roman census required Joseph and Mary to travel to Bethlehem, the city of David's lineage (Luke 2:1-5). There was no room at the inn, so Jesus was born and laid in a manger. Angels appeared to shepherds in the fields, announcing "good tidings of great joy" (Luke 2:10). The shepherds came and found the baby. In the Book of Mormon, the signs were fulfilled -- a night with no darkness and a new star appeared (3 Nephi 1:15-21).

- 1.4 -- The Wise Men and the Flight to Egypt
  - Wise men from the east followed a star to find the new king (Matthew 2:1-2). They came to Herod first, who asked them to report back. They found Jesus, presented gifts of gold, frankincense, and myrrh, and were warned in a dream not to return to Herod. Joseph was warned to flee to Egypt. Herod ordered the massacre of infant boys in Bethlehem (Matthew 2:16). The family stayed in Egypt until Herod died, then returned to Nazareth.

**Test:** 4 questions on the prophecies, the announcements, the birth in Bethlehem, and the wise men.

### Chapter 2: Childhood and Preparation

**Core concepts:** Presentation at the temple, growing up in Nazareth, the boy Jesus at the temple, John the Baptist's mission

- 2.1 -- Presented at the Temple
  - When Jesus was forty days old, Mary and Joseph brought Him to the temple in Jerusalem, following the Law of Moses (Luke 2:22-24). There, an old man named Simeon took the baby in his arms and said he could now die in peace -- he had seen the Lord's salvation (Luke 2:29-32). A prophetess named Anna also recognized who the child was and spoke of Him to everyone waiting for Israel's redemption.

- 2.2 -- Growing Up in Nazareth
  - Jesus grew up in the small town of Nazareth in Galilee. Joseph was a carpenter, and Jesus likely learned the trade. The scriptures say He "increased in wisdom and stature, and in favour with God and man" (Luke 2:52). He grew up like any other child -- learning, working, attending synagogue. But He was different in a way no one fully understood yet. He was the literal Son of God, living a mortal life.

- 2.3 -- The Boy at the Temple
  - When Jesus was twelve, His family traveled to Jerusalem for the Feast of Passover (Luke 2:41-42). On the way home, Mary and Joseph realized He was missing. After three days of searching, they found Him in the temple, sitting with the teachers, listening and asking questions. Everyone was amazed at His understanding. When Mary asked why He had stayed behind, Jesus said, "Wist ye not that I must be about my Father's business?" (Luke 2:49). Even at twelve, He knew who He was.

- 2.4 -- John the Baptist Prepares the Way
  - Before Jesus began His ministry, a prophet named John -- the son of Zacharias and Elisabeth -- began preaching in the wilderness of Judea. John called people to repent and baptized them in the Jordan River (Matthew 3:1-6). He told them someone greater was coming: "I indeed baptize you with water unto repentance: but he that cometh after me is mightier than I" (Matthew 3:11). John was preparing the way, just as Isaiah had prophesied (Isaiah 40:3).

**Test:** 4 questions on the temple presentation, Jesus' childhood, the boy at the temple, and John the Baptist's role.

### Chapter 3: Baptism and the Beginning of Ministry

**Core concepts:** Jesus' baptism, the temptations, the first disciples, turning water to wine

- 3.1 -- The Baptism of Jesus
  - Jesus came to John at the Jordan River to be baptized (Matthew 3:13-17). John hesitated -- he knew Jesus was sinless and didn't need to repent. But Jesus said, "Suffer it to be so now: for thus it becometh us to fulfil all righteousness." Jesus was baptized by immersion. When He came up out of the water, the heavens opened, the Holy Ghost descended like a dove, and the Father's voice said, "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased." All three members of the Godhead were present at this moment.

- 3.2 -- The Temptations in the Wilderness
  - After His baptism, Jesus fasted for forty days in the wilderness (Matthew 4:1-11). Satan came to tempt Him three times: turn stones to bread, throw Himself from the temple, and worship Satan in exchange for all the kingdoms of the world. Each time, Jesus answered with scripture. He showed that even the Son of God faced temptation -- and He showed exactly how to resist it.

- 3.3 -- The First Disciples
  - Jesus began calling followers. Andrew and John (the son of Zebedee) were among the first. Andrew brought his brother Simon, whom Jesus renamed Peter (John 1:35-42). Philip and Nathanael followed soon after. These were ordinary men -- fishermen, working people. Jesus didn't choose scholars or priests. He chose people who were willing to follow, learn, and eventually lead.

- 3.4 -- The Wedding at Cana
  - Jesus' first recorded miracle happened at a wedding feast in Cana of Galilee (John 2:1-11). The hosts ran out of wine. Mary told Jesus, and He instructed the servants to fill six stone water pots with water. When they drew from the pots, the water had become wine. The master of the feast was amazed at its quality. This miracle revealed Jesus' power over the physical world and "manifested forth his glory; and his disciples believed on him" (John 2:11).

**Test:** 4 questions on Jesus' baptism, the temptations, calling the first disciples, and the miracle at Cana.

### Chapter 4: Calling the Twelve and Early Ministry

**Core concepts:** Choosing the Twelve Apostles, early healings, teaching in synagogues, growing crowds

- 4.1 -- The Twelve Apostles
  - From among His many followers, Jesus chose twelve men to be His apostles -- His closest companions and the future leaders of His Church (Luke 6:12-16). Before choosing them, He spent the entire night in prayer. The Twelve were: Peter, Andrew, James, John, Philip, Bartholomew (Nathanael), Matthew, Thomas, James (son of Alphaeus), Thaddaeus, Simon the Zealot, and Judas Iscariot. The word "apostle" means "one who is sent." Jesus gave them authority to preach, heal, and cast out devils.

- 4.2 -- Teaching in the Synagogues
  - Jesus traveled throughout Galilee teaching in the synagogues (Matthew 4:23). In His hometown of Nazareth, He read from Isaiah in the synagogue: "The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, because he hath anointed me to preach the gospel to the poor" (Luke 4:18). Then He said, "This day is this scripture fulfilled in your ears." The people were amazed at first -- then angry. They tried to throw Him off a cliff. A prophet is often not accepted in his own country.

- 4.3 -- Healing the Sick
  - News of Jesus spread quickly because of His miracles. He healed a man with leprosy (Mark 1:40-42). He healed a paralyzed man lowered through a roof by his friends (Mark 2:1-12). He healed Peter's mother-in-law of a fever. Great crowds brought their sick to Him, and He healed them all (Matthew 4:24). These weren't magic tricks -- they were acts of compassion, and each one testified that He had power from God.

- 4.4 -- Growing Crowds, Growing Questions
  - As Jesus healed and taught, crowds grew larger. But so did the questions. He forgave sins -- something only God could do. He ate with tax collectors and sinners. He healed on the Sabbath. The religious leaders, especially the Pharisees, started watching Him closely. They weren't asking, "Is this the Messiah?" They were asking, "Who does He think He is?" The stage was being set for a conflict that would define the rest of His ministry.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Twelve Apostles, teaching in synagogues, early healings, and the beginning of opposition.

### Chapter 5: The Sermon on the Mount

**Core concepts:** The Beatitudes, higher law, prayer, the light of the world, building on a rock

- 5.1 -- The Beatitudes
  - Jesus went up on a mountainside and taught what may be His most famous sermon (Matthew 5-7). He began with the Beatitudes -- a series of blessings that describe the kind of people God values. Blessed are the poor in spirit, those who mourn, the meek, those who hunger for righteousness, the merciful, the pure in heart, the peacemakers, and those who are persecuted for righteousness' sake (Matthew 5:3-10). These weren't the qualities the world valued. Jesus was describing a different kind of greatness.

- 5.2 -- The Higher Law
  - The Law of Moses said "Thou shalt not kill." Jesus said don't even be angry without cause (Matthew 5:21-22). The law said "Thou shalt not commit adultery." Jesus said don't even look with lust (Matthew 5:27-28). The law said "An eye for an eye." Jesus said turn the other cheek (Matthew 5:38-39). He wasn't abolishing the law -- He was fulfilling it, raising the standard from outward behavior to the condition of the heart. "Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect" (Matthew 5:48).

- 5.3 -- How to Pray
  - Jesus taught His disciples how to pray, giving them a model known as the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:9-13). He told them not to pray to be seen by others, but to pray sincerely in private: "Thy kingdom come. Thy will be done in earth, as it is in heaven." He taught them to ask for daily bread, forgiveness, and deliverance from evil. Prayer is a real conversation with Heavenly Father -- not a performance.

- 5.4 -- Wise and Foolish Builders
  - Jesus taught, "Ye are the light of the world. A city that is set on an hill cannot be hid" (Matthew 5:14). He warned against judging others: "Why beholdest thou the mote that is in thy brother's eye, but considerest not the beam that is in thine own eye?" (Matthew 7:3). He closed the sermon with a parable: a wise man builds his house on a rock, and it stands through storms. A foolish man builds on sand, and it falls. "Whosoever heareth these sayings of mine, and doeth them" is the wise builder (Matthew 7:24).

**Test:** 4 questions on the Beatitudes, the higher law, prayer, and the wise and foolish builders.

### Chapter 6: The Parables

**Core concepts:** What parables are, the Sower, the Good Samaritan, the Prodigal Son, the Lost Sheep

- 6.1 -- Why Jesus Taught in Parables
  - A parable is a short story that teaches a spiritual truth using everyday situations. Jesus used parables constantly -- stories about farmers, fishermen, shepherds, merchants, and families. Why stories instead of straight lectures? Because stories stick. They meet people where they are. And they have layers of meaning -- the more you think about them, the more you find. Jesus said He spoke in parables so that those who truly sought truth would find it (Matthew 13:10-16).

- 6.2 -- The Sower and the Seeds
  - A sower scatters seed on four kinds of ground (Matthew 13:3-23). Some falls on the path and birds eat it -- people who hear the word but don't understand. Some falls on rocky ground and sprouts fast but dies -- people who receive it with joy but give up when it gets hard. Some falls among thorns and gets choked -- people distracted by the world. Some falls on good ground and produces fruit -- people who hear, understand, and act. The question isn't just whether you hear the gospel. It's what kind of ground you are.

- 6.3 -- The Good Samaritan and the Prodigal Son
  - A lawyer asked Jesus, "Who is my neighbour?" (Luke 10:25-37). Jesus told a story: a man was robbed and left for dead. A priest and a Levite walked by. A Samaritan -- someone the Jews looked down on -- stopped, cared for him, and paid for his recovery. Your neighbor is anyone who needs you. The Prodigal Son (Luke 15:11-32) is about a young man who wasted his inheritance, hit bottom, and came home. His father ran to meet him. The Father's love doesn't give up -- no matter how far you've wandered.

- 6.4 -- The Lost Sheep
  - A shepherd has a hundred sheep and one wanders off (Luke 15:3-7). Does he stay with the ninety-nine? No. He goes after the one until he finds it. Then he carries it home on his shoulders and calls his friends to celebrate. "I say unto you, that likewise joy shall be in heaven over one sinner that repenteth, more than over ninety and nine just persons, which need no repentance." Every single person matters to the Savior. You are never too lost to be found.

**Test:** 4 questions on why Jesus used parables, the Sower, the Good Samaritan/Prodigal Son, and the Lost Sheep.

### Chapter 7: The Miracles

**Core concepts:** Power over nature, healing, raising the dead, feeding the multitude, walking on water

- 7.1 -- Why Miracles?
  - Jesus' miracles weren't magic shows. Every miracle was an act of love, a response to faith, or a revelation of who He truly was. He healed because He had compassion. He calmed a storm because His disciples were afraid. He fed thousands because they were hungry. And every miracle pointed to the same truth: this is the Son of God. He has power over sickness, nature, and death itself. As Nephi taught, "He doeth not anything save it be for the benefit of the world" (2 Nephi 26:24).

- 7.2 -- Healing the Blind, the Lame, and the Lepers
  - Jesus healed people who had suffered for years. He gave sight to a man born blind (John 9:1-7). He healed ten lepers at once -- though only one came back to thank Him (Luke 17:12-19). He healed a woman who had been sick for twelve years just by her touching His garment (Mark 5:25-34). These healings show the Savior's power, but they also show His tenderness. He didn't heal from a distance and move on. He saw people. He touched them. He spoke to them.

- 7.3 -- Feeding the Five Thousand and Walking on Water
  - A huge crowd had followed Jesus into a remote area with no food (Matthew 14:13-21). A boy had five loaves of bread and two fish. Jesus blessed the food, and the disciples distributed it to over five thousand people -- with twelve baskets left over. That night, the disciples were in a boat on the Sea of Galilee in a storm. Jesus came to them walking on the water (Matthew 14:25). Peter got out of the boat and walked toward Him but began to sink when he looked at the waves. Jesus caught him: "O thou of little faith, wherefore didst thou doubt?"

- 7.4 -- Raising the Dead
  - Jesus raised three people from the dead during His ministry. He raised the daughter of Jairus, a synagogue leader (Mark 5:35-42). He raised the son of a widow in the town of Nain (Luke 7:11-15). And He raised His friend Lazarus, who had been dead four days (John 11:1-44). At Lazarus' tomb, Jesus wept -- showing His love even though He knew what He was about to do. Then He called out, "Lazarus, come forth." These miracles foreshadowed the greatest miracle of all -- His own resurrection.

**Test:** 4 questions on the purpose of miracles, healings, feeding the 5000/walking on water, and raising the dead.

### Chapter 8: Growing Opposition

**Core concepts:** Pharisees and Sabbath conflicts, cleansing the temple, plotting against Jesus, rejection at various cities

- 8.1 -- The Sabbath Conflicts
  - The Pharisees had built hundreds of rules around the Sabbath -- rules about how far you could walk, what you could carry, and what counted as "work." Jesus healed on the Sabbath repeatedly, and every time the Pharisees were furious (Mark 3:1-6). Jesus responded: "The sabbath was made for man, and not man for the sabbath" (Mark 2:27). The Sabbath was supposed to bless people, not burden them. The Pharisees cared more about their rules than about the suffering person standing right in front of them.

- 8.2 -- Cleansing the Temple
  - Jesus entered the temple in Jerusalem and found the Court of the Gentiles full of merchants selling animals and money changers charging high fees (John 2:13-16). He made a whip of cords and drove them out: "Make not my Father's house an house of merchandise." The temple was supposed to be a house of prayer. It had become a marketplace. Jesus' anger was not random -- it was the righteous anger of the Son of God seeing His Father's house desecrated. The religious leaders were not pleased.

- 8.3 -- Why the Leaders Opposed Him
  - The Pharisees, Sadducees, and other leaders had reasons to oppose Jesus. He challenged their authority. He called them hypocrites publicly (Matthew 23). He claimed to forgive sins. He drew massive crowds. He didn't follow their traditions. And most threatening of all -- people were starting to believe He might be the Messiah. If the people followed Jesus, the leaders would lose their influence and their arrangement with Rome. Their opposition was about power as much as theology.

- 8.4 -- The Plot Begins
  - After Jesus raised Lazarus from the dead, the chief priests and Pharisees called a council (John 11:47-53). They were afraid: "If we let him thus alone, all men will believe on him." The high priest Caiaphas said it was better for one man to die than for the whole nation to be destroyed by Rome. "From that day forth they took counsel together for to put him to death." They were no longer just watching. They were planning.

**Test:** 4 questions on Sabbath conflicts, cleansing the temple, why leaders opposed Jesus, and the plot against Him.

### Chapter 9: Who Is Jesus?

**Core concepts:** Peter's testimony, the Transfiguration, the "I Am" statements, the Good Shepherd

- 9.1 -- "Whom Say Ye That I Am?"
  - Near Caesarea Philippi, Jesus asked His disciples a direct question: "Whom do men say that I the Son of man am?" (Matthew 16:13-19). Some said John the Baptist. Some said Elijah or Jeremiah. Then Jesus asked, "But whom say ye that I am?" Peter answered: "Thou art the Christ, the Son of the living God." Jesus said this knowledge came not from man but from the Father. This is the most important question anyone can answer -- and Peter got it right.

- 9.2 -- The Transfiguration
  - Jesus took Peter, James, and John up a high mountain (Matthew 17:1-9). There, He was transfigured -- His face shone like the sun and His clothes became white as light. Moses and Elijah appeared and spoke with Him. A bright cloud overshadowed them, and the Father's voice said, "This is my beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased; hear ye him." The apostles saw Jesus in His glory. Latter-day Saints understand that Moses and Elijah brought priesthood keys -- a pattern repeated when they appeared in the Kirtland Temple (D&C 110).

- 9.3 -- "I Am"
  - Throughout His ministry, Jesus made bold declarations about who He was. "I am the bread of life" (John 6:35). "I am the light of the world" (John 8:12). "I am the door" (John 10:9). "I am the good shepherd" (John 10:11). "I am the resurrection, and the life" (John 11:25). "I am the way, the truth, and the life" (John 14:6). "Before Abraham was, I am" (John 8:58). That last one made the Jewish leaders furious -- "I AM" was the name God gave Himself to Moses (Exodus 3:14). Jesus was claiming to be God. And He was.

- 9.4 -- The Good Shepherd
  - Jesus described Himself as the Good Shepherd (John 10:1-18). A hired worker runs when wolves come. But the good shepherd "giveth his life for the sheep." Jesus knew every one of His sheep by name. And He said something the Jewish leaders couldn't fully understand yet: "Other sheep I have, which are not of this fold: them also I must bring" (John 10:16). Latter-day Saints know who those other sheep were -- the Nephites and others to whom Jesus would appear after His resurrection (3 Nephi 15:21).

**Test:** 4 questions on Peter's testimony, the Transfiguration, the "I Am" statements, and the Good Shepherd.

### Chapter 10: The Final Journey to Jerusalem

**Core concepts:** Teaching along the way, the rich young ruler, Triumphal Entry, Olivet Discourse

- 10.1 -- Teaching Along the Way
  - As Jesus traveled toward Jerusalem for the last time, He taught with increasing urgency. He told the parable of the ten virgins -- five were prepared with oil and five were not (Matthew 25:1-13). He told the parable of the talents -- three servants were given resources, and the one who buried his was condemned (Matthew 25:14-30). The message was clear: be prepared, use what God has given you, and don't waste the time you have.

- 10.2 -- The Rich Young Ruler
  - A wealthy young man came to Jesus and asked what he must do to have eternal life (Matthew 19:16-22). Jesus told him to keep the commandments. "All these things have I kept from my youth up," the young man said. Then Jesus said, "If thou wilt be perfect, go and sell that thou hast, and give to the poor, and come and follow me." The young man walked away sad because he had great possessions. Jesus wasn't saying wealth is always wrong. He was saying nothing can come before God -- not money, not comfort, not anything.

- 10.3 -- The Triumphal Entry
  - Jesus entered Jerusalem riding on a donkey, fulfilling the prophecy of Zechariah 9:9. Crowds spread palm branches and their garments on the road, shouting "Hosanna! Blessed is he that cometh in the name of the Lord!" (Matthew 21:9). The whole city was stirred. The Pharisees told Jesus to silence His followers. He said if they were silent, the very stones would cry out (Luke 19:40). This was His public declaration as the Messiah -- the King of Israel -- and it was the beginning of His final week.

- 10.4 -- The Olivet Discourse
  - Sitting on the Mount of Olives, Jesus' disciples asked Him about the future -- the destruction of the temple and the signs of His second coming (Matthew 24). Jesus prophesied that the temple would be destroyed ("not one stone upon another"), that there would be wars, famines, earthquakes, false prophets, and great tribulation. He told them to watch and be ready, for "of that day and hour knoweth no man" (Matthew 24:36). This prophecy was fulfilled in part with the Roman destruction in 70 AD, and will be fulfilled again before His return.

**Test:** 4 questions on the final parables, the rich young ruler, the Triumphal Entry, and the Olivet Discourse.

### Chapter 11: The Last Supper and Gethsemane

**Core concepts:** The Passover meal, the sacrament, washing feet, the Atonement begins in Gethsemane

- 11.1 -- The Last Supper
  - On the night before His death, Jesus gathered with His Twelve Apostles for the Feast of Passover (Luke 22:7-20). During the meal, He took bread, blessed it, broke it, and gave it to them: "This is my body which is given for you: this do in remembrance of me." Then He took the cup: "This cup is the new testament in my blood, which is shed for you." This was the first sacrament -- the ordinance that Latter-day Saints participate in every Sunday to remember His sacrifice.

- 11.2 -- Washing the Disciples' Feet
  - During the supper, Jesus rose, took a towel and a basin of water, and began washing His disciples' feet (John 13:1-17). Peter objected -- the Master shouldn't wash a servant's feet. Jesus said, "If I wash thee not, thou hast no part with me." Then He taught: "If I then, your Lord and Master, have washed your feet; ye also ought to wash one another's feet." The Son of God knelt and served. True leadership is service.

- 11.3 -- Gethsemane
  - After the supper, Jesus went to the Garden of Gethsemane on the Mount of Olives (Matthew 26:36-46). He asked Peter, James, and John to watch with Him. Then He went a little further and fell on His face and prayed: "O my Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me: nevertheless not as I will, but as thou wilt." In Gethsemane, Jesus took upon Himself the sins, pains, sicknesses, and sorrows of every person who has ever lived or ever will live (Alma 7:11-12). He bled from every pore (D&C 19:18). The Atonement had begun.

- 11.4 -- The Betrayal
  - While Jesus was still in the garden, Judas Iscariot arrived with a crowd carrying swords and clubs, sent by the chief priests (Matthew 26:47-56). Judas identified Jesus with a kiss -- the prearranged signal. Peter drew a sword and cut off the ear of the high priest's servant. Jesus healed the man's ear and told Peter to put the sword away: "All they that take the sword shall perish with the sword." Then all the disciples fled. Jesus was alone.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Last Supper/sacrament, washing feet, Gethsemane and the Atonement, and the betrayal.

### Chapter 12: The Trial and the Crucifixion

**Core concepts:** The trials before the Sanhedrin and Pilate, Peter's denial, the Crucifixion, the death of Jesus

- 12.1 -- The Trials
  - Jesus was taken first to Annas, then to Caiaphas the high priest, where the Sanhedrin had gathered (Matthew 26:57-68). They brought false witnesses. Finally, Caiaphas asked directly: "Art thou the Christ, the Son of the Blessed?" Jesus said, "I am" (Mark 14:61-62). The high priest tore his robes and called it blasphemy. They condemned Him to death -- but they needed Roman authority to carry it out, so they brought Him to Pontius Pilate.

- 12.2 -- Peter's Denial and Pilate's Decision
  - While Jesus was on trial, Peter waited outside in the courtyard. Three times people recognized him as a follower of Jesus. Three times Peter denied it (Luke 22:54-62). When the rooster crowed, Peter remembered Jesus' prediction and "went out, and wept bitterly." Meanwhile, Pilate could find no fault in Jesus. He tried to release Him, but the crowd demanded crucifixion. Pilate washed his hands and handed Jesus over (Matthew 27:24-26).

- 12.3 -- The Crucifixion
  - Jesus was scourged, mocked, and forced to carry His cross to Golgotha -- "the place of a skull" (Matthew 27:33). Roman soldiers nailed Him to the cross. He was crucified between two thieves. From the cross, He said, "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" (Luke 23:34). He promised one of the thieves, "To day shalt thou be with me in paradise" (Luke 23:43). He saw His mother and asked the apostle John to care for her (John 19:26-27).

- 12.4 -- "It Is Finished"
  - Darkness covered the land for three hours (Matthew 27:45). Jesus cried out, "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" (Matthew 27:46). For a moment, the Father withdrew His presence so that Jesus would experience the full weight of mortality alone. Then Jesus said, "It is finished" and "Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit" (John 19:30, Luke 23:46). He died. The veil of the temple was torn from top to bottom (Matthew 27:51). In the Americas, great destruction struck the land, and three days of darkness fell (3 Nephi 8).

**Test:** 4 questions on the trials, Peter's denial, the Crucifixion, and Jesus' death and the signs that accompanied it.

### Chapter 13: The Spirit World

**Core concepts:** Where Jesus went after death, preaching to the spirits, the spirit world in LDS theology, D&C 138

- 13.1 -- Where Did Jesus Go?
  - Between His death on Friday and His resurrection on Sunday morning, Jesus' body lay in the tomb. But He was not inactive. Peter wrote that Jesus "went and preached unto the spirits in prison" (1 Peter 3:19). Jesus told the thief on the cross, "To day shalt thou be with me in paradise" (Luke 23:43). Where is paradise? Latter-day Saints understand that the spirit world is where all people go after death to await the resurrection. Jesus visited that world during the three days His body was in the tomb.

- 13.2 -- President Joseph F. Smith's Vision
  - In October 1918, President Joseph F. Smith received a remarkable vision about what happened in the spirit world when Jesus visited (D&C 138). He saw a vast gathering of righteous spirits -- people who had lived faithfully from the days of Adam through John the Baptist. They were assembled, waiting with joy for the Savior's arrival. These spirits had been faithful in mortality and were now in paradise, awaiting their resurrection.

- 13.3 -- How the Gospel Reaches Everyone
  - President Smith saw that Jesus did not go personally to the wicked or disobedient spirits. Instead, He organized the righteous spirits and gave them authority to go and teach (D&C 138:29-30). This is how the gospel reaches people who never had a chance to hear it in mortality. The work of preaching continues in the spirit world. And this is why Latter-day Saints perform ordinances -- like baptism -- in temples on behalf of the dead. Everyone gets a chance to accept or reject the gospel.

- 13.4 -- Why This Matters
  - The visit to the spirit world shows that God's plan is fair. Billions of people have lived and died without ever hearing the gospel. Are they lost? No. The Savior organized missionary work in the spirit world so that every person who has ever lived can hear the truth and choose for themselves. Temple work and the spirit world work together -- ordinances are performed on earth, and teaching happens on the other side. No one is forgotten.

**Test:** 4 questions on where Jesus went after death, Joseph F. Smith's vision, how the gospel reaches everyone, and why temple work matters.

### Chapter 14: The Resurrection

**Core concepts:** The empty tomb, appearances to disciples, the road to Emmaus, the meaning of resurrection

- 14.1 -- The Empty Tomb
  - On Sunday morning, women came to the tomb to anoint Jesus' body (Mark 16:1-6). They found the stone rolled away and the tomb empty. Angels told them, "He is not here: for he is risen" (Matthew 28:6). Mary Magdalene stayed near the tomb, weeping. A man she thought was the gardener spoke her name -- "Mary" -- and she recognized the risen Lord (John 20:16). She was the first mortal to see the resurrected Christ. He told her, "Touch me not; for I am not yet ascended to my Father" (John 20:17).

- 14.2 -- Appearances to the Disciples
  - That same day, Jesus appeared to two disciples on the road to Emmaus (Luke 24:13-35). They didn't recognize Him at first, but when He broke bread with them, their eyes were opened. That evening, Jesus appeared to the apostles in a locked room (John 20:19-20). He showed them His hands and His side. Thomas was absent and refused to believe until he saw for himself. A week later, Jesus appeared again and invited Thomas to touch His wounds. Thomas said, "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28). Jesus replied, "Blessed are they that have not seen, and yet have believed."

- 14.3 -- The Forty Days
  - Jesus remained on the earth for forty days after His resurrection (Acts 1:3). He appeared to the apostles multiple times, teaching them about the kingdom of God. He ate fish with them on the shore of the Sea of Galilee (John 21). He gave Peter a chance to reaffirm his love three times -- once for each denial (John 21:15-17). Paul later wrote that Jesus appeared to over five hundred people at once (1 Corinthians 15:6). The resurrection was not a secret. It was witnessed by many.

- 14.4 -- The Ascension
  - After forty days, Jesus led His apostles to the Mount of Olives (Acts 1:9-11). He gave them final instructions: "Ye shall be witnesses unto me both in Jerusalem, and in all Judaea, and in Samaria, and unto the uttermost part of the earth" (Acts 1:8). Then He was taken up into heaven, and a cloud received Him out of their sight. Two angels appeared and said, "This same Jesus, which is taken up from you into heaven, shall so come in like manner as ye have seen him go." He will return.

**Test:** 4 questions on the empty tomb, appearances to disciples, the forty days, and the Ascension.

### Chapter 15: The Nephites and the Other Folds

**Core concepts:** Destruction in the Americas, Jesus appears at the temple in Bountiful, His teachings and ministry among the Nephites, other sheep

- 15.1 -- Destruction in the Americas
  - When Jesus died on the cross, great destruction struck the land described in the Book of Mormon (3 Nephi 8). Cities were burned, sunk, and buried. Earthquakes, storms, and fires raged for three hours. Then thick darkness covered the land for three days -- so dark that no light could be seen at all. In the darkness, the voice of Jesus Christ spoke to the survivors, calling them to repent and come unto Him (3 Nephi 9:13-14). He told them the law of sacrifice by the shedding of blood was ended, and He now asked for "a broken heart and a contrite spirit."

- 15.2 -- Jesus Appears at the Temple in Bountiful
  - After the destruction, a multitude gathered at the temple in the land Bountiful (3 Nephi 11). They heard a voice from heaven three times before they understood it. The Father said, "Behold my Beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased, in whom I have glorified my name -- hear ye him." Then Jesus descended out of heaven, dressed in a white robe. He said, "I am Jesus Christ, whom the prophets testified shall come into the world" (3 Nephi 11:10). He invited every person -- all 2,500 of them -- to come forward and feel the prints of the nails in His hands and feet.

- 15.3 -- His Ministry Among the Nephites
  - Jesus spent time among the Nephites teaching, healing, and establishing His Church. He taught the Sermon on the Mount again (3 Nephi 12-14). He called twelve disciples. He instituted the sacrament. He healed every sick and afflicted person they brought to Him (3 Nephi 17:9). He blessed the little children one by one, and angels descended and encircled them in fire (3 Nephi 17:24). He gave them the scriptures they were missing and commanded them to write what He taught. It was the most sacred experience in Nephite history.

- 15.4 -- Other Sheep
  - Before He left, Jesus told the Nephites, "Ye are they of whom I said: Other sheep I have which are not of this fold" (3 Nephi 15:21). He also said He would visit still other groups -- the lost tribes of Israel and others the Father had led away (3 Nephi 16:1-3). The Savior's ministry was not limited to one land or one people. He is the Lord of the whole earth, and He personally visited those the Father had scattered. His love reaches every nation, kindred, tongue, and people.

**Test:** 4 questions on the destruction in the Americas, Jesus' appearance at Bountiful, His ministry among the Nephites, and the "other sheep."
