# The Priesthood for Kids -- Book Proposal

## Why This Book

The priesthood is one of the most important and most misunderstood topics in the Church. Kids hear about it every week -- deacons pass the sacrament, elders give blessings, the bishop holds keys -- but most can't explain what the priesthood actually is, how it works, who has which keys, what the offices mean, or why D&C 121 is one of the most important sections in all of scripture.

This book covers everything: what the priesthood is, how Heavenly Father holds it, how it was given to Adam and passed through the prophets, the difference between Aaronic and Levitical, the Melchizedek Priesthood, what keys are, what dispensations are, how the priesthood was restored (and which keys were restored when), every office from deacon to apostle, every quorum from the deacons quorum to the First Presidency, the proper use of priesthood (D&C 121 gets two full chapters), the oath and covenant, how priesthood is used in ordinances, who holds keys at every level of the Church, and what the future holds -- the sons of Levi and the great meeting at Adam-ondi-Ahman.

## Perspective

Written for LDS kids ages 7-12 who are already members. Young men who are about to receive or have recently received the Aaronic Priesthood will find this especially relevant, but the book is for all kids -- young women benefit from and participate in priesthood power and need to understand it too.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms, scripture references
- 3 questions per lesson, 4 per test
- Target age: 7-12

## The Priesthood for Kids

**Subtitle:** The Power and Authority of God

### Chapter 1: What the Priesthood Is

- 1.1 -- The Power of God
  - The priesthood is the power and authority of God. It's not a title or a position -- it's the actual power by which God created the world, governs the universe, and blesses His children. When someone holds the priesthood, they hold a portion of God's own authority -- delegated to them, not owned by them. It's like being given the keys to a car: the car still belongs to the owner, but you've been authorized to drive it. The priesthood belongs to God. He lets His children use it.

- 1.2 -- Authority vs. Power
  - There's a difference between priesthood authority and priesthood power. Authority is the right to act -- you receive it through ordination. Power is the ability to act effectively -- you receive it through righteous living. A person can hold the authority of the priesthood and still lack the power if they are not living worthily. D&C 121:36 teaches that "the rights of the priesthood are inseparably connected with the powers of heaven" and that those powers can only be used "upon the principles of righteousness." Authority comes from ordination. Power comes from how you live.

- 1.3 -- Who Can Hold the Priesthood?
  - In the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, all worthy male members can be ordained to the priesthood. Young men receive the Aaronic Priesthood at age 12. Adult men receive the Melchizedek Priesthood, typically at age 18 or when preparing for a mission or temple marriage. The priesthood is not earned by intelligence, wealth, or talent -- it is given to those who are willing to serve. Women do not hold priesthood offices but do participate in priesthood power, especially through temple ordinances and covenants.

- 1.4 -- Why the Priesthood Matters
  - Without the priesthood, ordinances like baptism, confirmation, the sacrament, and temple sealings would have no authority. They would be sincere gestures but not binding in God's eyes. The priesthood is what makes ordinances valid. It's what makes covenants real. It's what connects the Church on earth to God in heaven. When a deacon passes the sacrament, he is exercising the same authority that comes from God Himself. That's not a small thing. That's an enormous trust.

### Chapter 2: The Priesthood from the Beginning

- 2.1 -- Heavenly Father and the Priesthood
  - The priesthood is not something that was invented or created. It is eternal -- it has always existed because God has always existed. Heavenly Father holds the fulness of the priesthood. Everything He does -- creating, governing, blessing, saving -- is done through priesthood power. The priesthood is not separate from God. It is the way God operates. When He delegates that power to His children, He is sharing the very means by which He does His work.

- 2.2 -- Adam and the Priesthood
  - Adam was the first man on earth to hold the priesthood. God ordained Adam personally (D&C 84:16). Adam held the Melchizedek Priesthood -- the higher priesthood -- and used it to teach, baptize, confer the Holy Ghost, and lead his family. Adam passed the priesthood to his righteous descendants. The priesthood didn't start with Moses or Aaron. It started at the very beginning, with the first man, given by God Himself.

- 2.3 -- The Patriarchs
  - The priesthood was passed from Adam through the patriarchs: Seth, Enos, Cainan, Mahalaleel, Jared, Enoch, Methuselah, Lamech, and Noah (D&C 107:40-52). Each one received the priesthood from his father or predecessor. The chain was unbroken. After Noah, the priesthood continued through Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob. These men used the priesthood to bless their families, make covenants with God, and administer ordinances. The priesthood was always a family affair -- fathers passing it to sons.

- 2.4 -- Why This History Matters
  - Understanding that the priesthood goes back to Adam helps you see something important: the priesthood you see in the Church today is the same priesthood Adam held. It's not a modern invention. It's not Joseph Smith's creation. It is God's power, delegated from the beginning of the world, passed through prophets, lost during the Apostasy, and restored through heavenly messengers. When a young man is ordained to the priesthood today, he is receiving authority that traces back through an unbroken chain to God Himself.

### Chapter 3: The Melchizedek Priesthood

- 3.1 -- Why It's Called the Melchizedek Priesthood
  - The higher priesthood is officially called "the Holy Priesthood, after the Order of the Son of God" (D&C 107:3). But the early Saints called it the Melchizedek Priesthood to avoid using God's name too frequently. Melchizedek was a king and high priest in Abraham's time who was so righteous that Abraham paid tithes to him (Genesis 14:18-20, Hebrews 7). The priesthood is named after him as a mark of honor -- he was one of the greatest priesthood holders in history.

- 3.2 -- What It Includes
  - The Melchizedek Priesthood is the higher priesthood. It includes the authority to lead the Church, give the gift of the Holy Ghost through confirmation, ordain others to the priesthood, dedicate temples, and administer all the ordinances of the gospel. It contains the authority to receive revelation for the Church (when held by those with appropriate keys). It is the priesthood that the apostles hold, that the prophet holds, and that every elder, high priest, patriarch, seventy, and apostle in the Church holds.

- 3.3 -- Offices in the Melchizedek Priesthood
  - The Melchizedek Priesthood has several offices: elder, high priest, patriarch, seventy, and apostle (D&C 107). Each office has specific duties, but they all hold the same priesthood. An elder and an apostle hold the same Melchizedek Priesthood -- but the apostle holds additional keys and responsibilities. The office determines your specific calling and duties. The priesthood determines your authority.

- 3.4 -- The Melchizedek Priesthood and You
  - Young men typically receive the Melchizedek Priesthood at age 18, when they are ordained as elders. This is a significant step -- you go from the preparatory priesthood (Aaronic) to the fulness. As a Melchizedek Priesthood holder, you can give blessings of healing, confirm members, participate in ordinations, and eventually officiate in the temple. It is a sacred trust and an enormous responsibility. But remember: the authority comes through ordination. The power comes through how you live.

### Chapter 4: The Aaronic and Levitical Priesthood

- 4.1 -- The Aaronic Priesthood
  - The Aaronic Priesthood is the lesser or preparatory priesthood. It is named after Aaron, the brother of Moses (D&C 107:13). Aaron was the first high priest of Israel under the Law of Moses. The Aaronic Priesthood includes the authority to baptize, administer the sacrament, and perform other outward ordinances. It does not include the authority to confirm, give the Holy Ghost, or ordain to the Melchizedek Priesthood. It is called "preparatory" because it prepares you for the higher priesthood.

- 4.2 -- The Levitical Priesthood
  - You'll sometimes hear the terms "Aaronic" and "Levitical" used interchangeably, but there is a distinction. The Levitical Priesthood refers specifically to the priesthood as it functioned among the tribe of Levi under the Law of Moses. The Levites were the tribe set apart to serve in the tabernacle and temple -- they handled the sacrifices, maintained the sacred spaces, and assisted the priests. Aaron and his descendants were the priests who officiated in the highest ordinances. The Levitical Priesthood is a subset of the Aaronic Priesthood -- it's the Aaronic Priesthood as it operated in ancient Israel.

- 4.3 -- Offices in the Aaronic Priesthood
  - Today, the Aaronic Priesthood has three offices: deacon, teacher, and priest (D&C 20:46-60). Deacons (age 12) pass the sacrament, collect fast offerings, and assist the bishop. Teachers (age 14) prepare the sacrament, serve as home ministers, and watch over the Church. Priests (age 16) can baptize, bless the sacrament, and ordain other deacons, teachers, and priests. Each office builds on the one before -- you grow in responsibility as you grow in maturity.

- 4.4 -- The Bishop and the Aaronic Priesthood
  - The bishop is the president of the Aaronic Priesthood in the ward (D&C 107:15). He holds the keys to direct all Aaronic Priesthood work, including the sacrament, baptisms, and the welfare of young men. The bishop is also a judge in Israel -- he conducts interviews, handles repentance matters, and oversees temporal welfare. The Presiding Bishop of the whole Church presides over the Aaronic Priesthood for the entire Church. The bishop's role connects the Aaronic Priesthood to the daily life of every ward.

### Chapter 5: Priesthood Keys

- 5.1 -- What Keys Are
  - Priesthood keys are the authority to direct the use of the priesthood. Having the priesthood is like having a car engine. Having keys is like having the ignition key -- without it, the engine can't start. A man may hold the Melchizedek Priesthood, but he can only use it in specific ways within his stewardship. A home teacher can give a blessing in a home, but he can't run a ward. A bishop can run a ward, but he can't run a stake. Keys determine who can authorize what, and where.

- 5.2 -- Who Holds Keys
  - Only certain priesthood holders have keys. The prophet and President of the Church holds all the keys on earth. Apostles hold all keys but only exercise them under the direction of the President. Stake presidents hold keys for their stakes. Bishops hold keys for their wards. Mission presidents hold keys for their missions. Quorum presidents hold keys for their quorums. Keys are not general -- they are specific to a stewardship. The deacons quorum president holds the keys to direct the work of his quorum, and no one else holds those specific keys.

- 5.3 -- Keys vs. Authority vs. Power
  - This is a critical distinction. Authority is the right to act in God's name -- received through ordination. Keys are the right to direct how that authority is used -- received through setting apart. Power is the spiritual capacity to act effectively -- received through righteous living. You need all three. A deacon has authority (he's ordained). His quorum president has keys (he directs the quorum's work). And both need power (they must live worthily) for the priesthood to function as God intended.

- 5.4 -- Why Keys Matter
  - Without keys, the priesthood would be chaos. Anyone could claim authority to do anything. Keys create order. They ensure that ordinances are performed properly, that the Church is organized correctly, and that priesthood power is used within appropriate boundaries. When the prophet speaks for the whole Church, he does so because he holds the keys. No one else on earth holds the same keys at the same time. This is how God maintains order in His Church.

### Chapter 6: Dispensations

- 6.1 -- What a Dispensation Is
  - A dispensation is a period of time when God gives priesthood keys and gospel knowledge to a prophet on earth. God calls a prophet, gives him authority and keys, and that prophet teaches and administers ordinances. Over time, the people fall away (apostasy), the keys are lost, and God calls a new prophet to begin a new dispensation. The pattern repeats throughout history. Each dispensation is a fresh start -- God restoring what was lost.

- 6.2 -- The Major Dispensations
  - The scriptures identify several dispensations: Adam, Enoch, Noah, Abraham, Moses, Jesus Christ, and Joseph Smith (D&C 112:30). Each prophet received priesthood keys appropriate to his time. Adam received the fulness. Moses received keys to gather Israel. Elijah received sealing keys. Jesus gave the apostles all keys necessary to lead His Church. When those keys were lost through apostasy, the final dispensation -- through Joseph Smith -- restored everything.

- 6.3 -- Which Keys Were Held When
  - Not every dispensation had the same keys. Adam held the fulness. Moses held keys of the gathering of Israel. Elijah held the sealing keys. The apostles in Christ's time held all necessary keys. During the Apostasy, all keys were lost from the earth. In the Restoration, specific messengers returned specific keys: John the Baptist brought the Aaronic Priesthood. Peter, James, and John brought the Melchizedek Priesthood. Moses, Elias, and Elijah brought additional keys at the Kirtland Temple.

- 6.4 -- The Dispensation of the Fulness of Times
  - We live in the final dispensation -- the "dispensation of the fulness of times" (D&C 112:30, Ephesians 1:10). It is called "fulness" because every key, every ordinance, and every truth from every previous dispensation has been restored in this one. Nothing is missing. And unlike previous dispensations, this one will not end in apostasy. The keys will remain on the earth until Christ comes again. This is it -- the final chapter before the Lord returns.

### Chapter 7: The Restoration of the Priesthood

- 7.1 -- John the Baptist Restores the Aaronic Priesthood
  - On May 15, 1829, John the Baptist appeared as a resurrected being to Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery near the Susquehanna River. He laid his hands on their heads and said: "Upon you my fellow servants, in the name of Messiah I confer the Priesthood of Aaron, which holds the keys of the ministering of angels, and of the gospel of repentance, and of baptism by immersion for the remission of sins" (D&C 13). Joseph and Oliver then baptized each other in the river. The Aaronic Priesthood was back on earth.

- 7.2 -- Peter, James, and John Restore the Melchizedek Priesthood
  - Shortly after, Peter, James, and John -- the senior apostles from Christ's time -- appeared and conferred the Melchizedek Priesthood on Joseph and Oliver. The exact date is not recorded, but it happened in late May or early June 1829. With this ordination, Joseph and Oliver had the authority to confirm, bestow the Holy Ghost, ordain others, and organize the Church. The fulness of priesthood authority was now on earth for the first time since the Apostasy.

- 7.3 -- Keys Restored at the Kirtland Temple
  - On April 3, 1836, in the Kirtland Temple, three additional heavenly messengers appeared (D&C 110). Moses committed the keys of the gathering of Israel. Elias committed the dispensation of the gospel of Abraham. Elijah committed the keys of the sealing power -- the authority to bind on earth and in heaven. Each messenger brought specific keys that had been held in previous dispensations. Now all of them were gathered into one dispensation.

- 7.4 -- Are Any Keys Still Missing?
  - The Lord has said that in the dispensation of the fulness of times, all things will be gathered together (Ephesians 1:10). Church leaders have taught that all the keys necessary for the salvation and exaltation of God's children have been restored. However, the scriptures suggest that there may be future events involving priesthood keys -- such as the meeting at Adam-ondi-Ahman and the return of the sons of Levi. Whether these involve keys not yet given or the culmination of keys already held is a matter the Lord will reveal in His time.

### Chapter 8: The Proper Use of Priesthood -- Part 1

- 8.1 -- Why This Matters Most
  - Of everything in this book, this chapter and the next may be the most important. Having the priesthood means nothing if you don't use it properly. D&C 121 -- written by Joseph Smith in Liberty Jail during some of his darkest hours -- contains the Lord's definitive teaching on how priesthood should and should not be used. If you learn nothing else about the priesthood, learn this: power in the priesthood comes not from the office you hold, but from the kind of person you are.

- 8.2 -- "Many Are Called, But Few Are Chosen"
  - D&C 121:34-36 begins with a warning: "Many are called, but few are chosen." Many men receive the priesthood, but few use it the way God intends. Why? Because they try to use it through unrighteous dominion -- through control, compulsion, and pride. The moment a man tries to "exercise control or dominion or compulsion upon the souls of the children of men, in any degree of unrighteousness," the Spirit withdraws, and his priesthood authority is effectively gone (D&C 121:37).

- 8.3 -- What Unrighteous Dominion Looks Like
  - Unrighteous dominion means using your authority to control, manipulate, or force people. A father who uses the priesthood to demand obedience through fear is using it unrighteously. A leader who bullies, shames, or pressures people is using it unrighteously. A young man who thinks holding the priesthood makes him better than others is on the wrong path. The priesthood is not about power over people. It is about power to serve people. The moment it becomes about you, it stops working.

- 8.4 -- "Amen to the Priesthood"
  - D&C 121:37 contains one of the most sobering warnings in all of scripture: when a man uses the priesthood unrighteously, "the heavens withdraw themselves; the Spirit of the Lord is grieved; and when it is withdrawn, Amen to the priesthood or the authority of that man." The man may still hold the title, but the actual power is gone. God doesn't take the ordination away -- He takes the power away. You can have the authority on paper and have zero power in practice. The priesthood only works through righteousness.

### Chapter 9: The Proper Use of Priesthood -- Part 2

- 9.1 -- How the Priesthood Should Be Used
  - After explaining how not to use the priesthood, D&C 121:41-42 describes how it should be used: "by persuasion, by long-suffering, by gentleness and meekness, and by love unfeigned; by kindness, and pure knowledge." This is the opposite of unrighteous dominion. Instead of forcing, you persuade. Instead of impatience, you endure. Instead of harshness, you are gentle. Instead of pretending to care, you actually care. The priesthood operates through Christlike character, not through rank or position.

- 9.2 -- Persuasion, Long-suffering, and Gentleness
  - Persuasion means influencing people through reason, testimony, and example -- not through threats or pressure. Long-suffering means being patient when people are slow, difficult, or frustrating. Gentleness means being kind and tender, even when you have the authority to be harsh. These are not signs of weakness. They are signs of strength -- the strength of a person who doesn't need to dominate because they have the quiet confidence that comes from living right.

- 9.3 -- Love Unfeigned and Pure Knowledge
  - "Love unfeigned" means genuine love -- not fake, not performative, not conditional. You love people because you actually love them, not because it's your calling. "Pure knowledge" means truth without manipulation -- teaching people honestly, not twisting information to get what you want. The combination of real love and real truth is the most powerful leadership tool in the universe. It's how the Savior led. And it's how every priesthood holder should lead.

- 9.4 -- "Let Thy Bowels Be Full of Charity"
  - D&C 121:45-46 gives the promise: "Let thy bowels also be full of charity towards all men, and to the household of faith, and let virtue garnish thy thoughts unceasingly; then shall thy confidence wax strong in the presence of God; and the doctrine of the priesthood shall distil upon thy soul as the dews from heaven." If you live this way -- with charity, virtue, and pure motives -- the priesthood will flow through you naturally. The Holy Ghost will be your constant companion. And you will lead the way Christ leads: with love.

### Chapter 10: The Oath and Covenant of the Priesthood

- 10.1 -- What the Oath and Covenant Is
  - D&C 84:33-44 describes the oath and covenant of the priesthood -- the most sweeping promise God makes to any person. Those who receive the Melchizedek Priesthood enter into a covenant: they promise to magnify their callings and live by every word of God. In return, God promises by oath -- the strongest kind of promise -- to give them "all that my Father hath" (D&C 84:38). Everything. All of God's power, glory, and blessings. It is the most remarkable promise in all of scripture.

- 10.2 -- Your Part: Magnify Your Calling
  - To magnify your calling means to take it seriously, serve faithfully, and grow into the responsibility. It doesn't mean being perfect -- it means being diligent. A deacon who passes the sacrament reverently and prepares carefully is magnifying his calling. An elder who gives a blessing with real faith is magnifying his calling. "Magnify" means to make larger -- to make the calling bigger through your effort and faithfulness, not smaller through neglect.

- 10.3 -- God's Part: All That the Father Hath
  - God's promise is staggering: "All that my Father hath shall be given unto him" (D&C 84:38). This means exaltation -- becoming like God, receiving His power, His glory, His eternal life. This is not a metaphor. It is a literal promise, guaranteed by God's own oath. No other promise in scripture is this vast. And it is available to every man who faithfully receives and honors the Melchizedek Priesthood.

- 10.4 -- The Warning
  - The oath and covenant also comes with a warning: "Whoso breaketh this covenant after he hath received it, and altogether turneth therefrom, shall not have forgiveness of sins in this world nor in the world to come" (D&C 84:41). This is serious -- the greater the promise, the greater the responsibility. God doesn't make this covenant lightly, and neither should you. But this warning is not meant to scare you. It's meant to show you how sacred this covenant is. Treat it accordingly.

### Chapter 11: Priesthood and Ordinances

- 11.1 -- Why Ordinances Require the Priesthood
  - An ordinance is a sacred physical act that carries spiritual meaning -- baptism, confirmation, the sacrament, blessings, temple ordinances. For an ordinance to be valid in God's eyes, it must be performed by someone holding the proper priesthood authority. A baptism performed by someone without the priesthood is sincere but not binding. It's like a contract signed by someone who doesn't have the authority to sign it -- it may look official, but it doesn't count. The priesthood is what makes ordinances real.

- 11.2 -- Which Priesthood for Which Ordinance
  - The Aaronic Priesthood administers outward ordinances: baptism and the sacrament (D&C 20:46, 107:20). A priest or Melchizedek Priesthood holder can baptize. Priests and teachers prepare the sacrament; priests bless it; deacons pass it. The Melchizedek Priesthood administers the higher ordinances: confirmation, giving the Holy Ghost, blessings of healing, ordinations, setting apart, dedicating graves, and all temple ordinances including the endowment and sealings.

- 11.3 -- How Ordinances Work
  - In most ordinances, the priesthood holder lays his hands on the person's head -- a physical act that symbolizes the transfer of God's power and authority. The words spoken are either prescribed (like the sacrament prayers and baptismal prayer) or given by the Spirit (like in blessings of healing or comfort). The person performing the ordinance acts in the name of Jesus Christ and by the authority of the priesthood he holds. He is not acting on his own -- he is acting as God's authorized representative.

- 11.4 -- What the Priesthood Cannot Do
  - The priesthood is powerful, but it is not unlimited. A priesthood holder cannot force God to act. A blessing of healing does not guarantee the person will be healed -- it is subject to God's will. The priesthood cannot override agency -- no one can be forced to accept a blessing, a covenant, or an ordinance. And the priesthood cannot be used for personal gain, pride, or unrighteous purposes (D&C 121:36-37). The priesthood is God's power, used on God's terms, for God's purposes.

### Chapter 12: Offices of the Priesthood

- 12.1 -- Aaronic Priesthood Offices
  - Deacon (age 12): Passes the sacrament, collects fast offerings, cares for the meetinghouse, assists the bishop. Teacher (age 14): Prepares the sacrament, serves as a ministering brother, watches over the Church and strengthens members. Priest (age 16): Can baptize, bless the sacrament, ordain deacons/teachers/priests, and teach. Each office builds on the previous one. The duties of a deacon are included in a teacher's responsibilities, and a teacher's duties are included in a priest's.

- 12.2 -- Elder and High Priest
  - Elder: The foundational office of the Melchizedek Priesthood. Elders can confirm, bestow the Holy Ghost, bless the sick, ordain other elders, and conduct meetings. Most adult men in the Church are elders. High Priest: A higher office, typically held by those called to preside -- bishops, stake presidents, and mission presidents are high priests. High priests hold the same priesthood as elders but are set apart for presiding functions.

- 12.3 -- Patriarch, Seventy, and Apostle
  - Patriarch: Called to give patriarchal blessings -- a personal, prophetic blessing declaring a person's lineage in the house of Israel and giving promises and guidance. Each stake has a patriarch. Seventy: Called to be special witnesses of Christ and to assist the Twelve Apostles in directing the Church worldwide. The First and Second Quorums of the Seventy are General Authorities. Apostle: A special witness of Jesus Christ, holding all priesthood keys. The Twelve Apostles, with the First Presidency, lead the entire Church.

- 12.4 -- The Prophet
  - The President of the Church is the prophet, seer, and revelator -- the only person on earth authorized to exercise all priesthood keys for the whole Church. He is the senior apostle. He holds every key ever restored. He receives revelation for the entire Church. When he speaks as the prophet, he speaks for God. The office of the President is the highest priesthood calling on earth, and it has been held by an unbroken succession of prophets from Joseph Smith to the present day.

### Chapter 13: Quorums and Organization

- 13.1 -- What a Quorum Is
  - A quorum is an organized group of priesthood holders. It's not just a class -- it's a brotherhood with a president, specific duties, and a shared responsibility to serve. Quorums exist at every level of the Church. The word "quorum" means the minimum number needed to act -- it implies that every member matters. A quorum is incomplete when someone is missing. The purpose of a quorum is mutual support: to teach, serve, lift, and strengthen each other.

- 13.2 -- Ward-Level Quorums
  - In a typical ward, you'll find: a deacons quorum (up to 12 members, presided over by a quorum president from among the deacons), a teachers quorum (up to 24 members), a priests quorum (up to 48 members, presided over by the bishop), and an elders quorum (all elders and high priests in the ward, presided over by an elders quorum president). Each quorum has a president (called from within the quorum) who holds keys to direct the quorum's work.

- 13.3 -- Stake and General Church Quorums
  - At the stake level, the stake presidency presides over all priesthood work. The stake patriarch gives blessings. At the general Church level, the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles directs the worldwide Church under the First Presidency. The Quorums of the Seventy assist the Twelve. The Presiding Bishopric oversees temporal affairs and the Aaronic Priesthood worldwide. The First Presidency -- the prophet and his two counselors -- presides over everything.

- 13.4 -- The First Presidency and Quorum of the Twelve
  - The First Presidency is the highest governing body of the Church. The Quorum of the Twelve Apostles is the second-highest. Together, they hold all priesthood keys and direct the Church under revelation from God. When the prophet dies, the First Presidency is dissolved and the Quorum of the Twelve -- led by the senior apostle -- becomes the presiding authority until a new First Presidency is organized. This is how succession has worked since Brigham Young, and it ensures the keys are never lost.

### Chapter 14: Keys in the Church Today

- 14.1 -- The Prophet Holds All Keys
  - The President of the Church is the only person on earth who holds all priesthood keys. He can authorize any ordinance, call any leader, and direct any aspect of the Church. No one else holds the same set of keys at the same time. The Twelve Apostles hold all keys but only exercise them under the prophet's direction (or when the First Presidency is dissolved). This ensures unity -- one person at the top, directed by God, holding all the keys.

- 14.2 -- Keys at Every Level
  - Keys are delegated throughout the Church. The mission president holds keys for his mission. The stake president holds keys for his stake. The bishop holds keys for his ward. The elders quorum president holds keys for his quorum. The deacons quorum president holds keys for his quorum. Each president directs the work within his stewardship -- and only within his stewardship. A bishop can authorize baptisms in his ward but not in another ward. Keys are specific, not general.

- 14.3 -- What Keys Allow
  - Keys allow a priesthood leader to authorize ordinances, call and release members to serve, receive revelation for his stewardship, and direct the work of the priesthood within his area. A bishop can authorize a baptism, call a Sunday School teacher, receive revelation about who needs help in the ward, and direct the sacrament ordinance. He cannot do these things in another bishop's ward. Keys create both authority and boundaries.

- 14.4 -- Keys and Revelation
  - One of the most important functions of keys is the right to receive revelation for a specific stewardship. The prophet receives revelation for the whole Church. A bishop receives revelation for his ward. A father receives revelation for his family. Keys and revelation go together -- if you hold keys, you have the right to ask God for guidance about the work those keys cover. This is how the Church functions: authorized leaders seeking and receiving revelation within their stewardship.

### Chapter 15: The Future of the Priesthood

- 15.1 -- The Sons of Levi
  - Malachi 3:3 and D&C 13 both mention a future event: the sons of Levi will offer "an offering unto the Lord in righteousness." Joseph Smith taught that this prophecy will be fulfilled -- that descendants of the tribe of Levi will once again make an offering to the Lord. This does not mean a return to animal sacrifice as the regular law of worship. It appears to be a one-time offering connected to the final establishment of God's kingdom. The details are not fully revealed, but the promise is in the scriptures.

- 15.2 -- Adam-ondi-Ahman
  - D&C 116 identifies a place in Missouri called Adam-ondi-Ahman -- where Adam blessed his posterity before he died (D&C 107:53-56). The prophet Daniel saw a vision of a great meeting where the "Ancient of Days" (Adam) would sit and the Son of Man (Christ) would come to him (Daniel 7:9-14). Joseph Smith and subsequent prophets have taught that before the Second Coming, a grand council will be held at Adam-ondi-Ahman where Adam will return all priesthood keys to Jesus Christ.

- 15.3 -- Returning the Keys to Christ
  - At the meeting at Adam-ondi-Ahman, every prophet who ever held priesthood keys in any dispensation will give an accounting of his stewardship. Adam will then deliver all the keys back to Jesus Christ -- the rightful holder of all priesthood power. This meeting will prepare the way for the Second Coming. Christ will then come in glory to reign on the earth. The priesthood that was given to Adam in the beginning will return to the One who gave it.

- 15.4 -- The Priesthood Is Eternal
  - The priesthood will not end when Christ returns. It is eternal -- it existed before the world was created and it will exist forever. In the celestial kingdom, those who have been faithful will continue to hold and exercise priesthood power as part of their eternal lives. The priesthood is not just for this life. It is the power by which God operates, and those who are exalted will share in that power forever. Understanding the priesthood is not just understanding a Church program. It is understanding how God works -- and how you can work with Him.
