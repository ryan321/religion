# The Restoration for Kids -- Book Proposal

## Why This Book

Latter-day Saint kids hear about the Restoration in Primary, Come Follow Me, and general conference. They know about the First Vision, the Book of Mormon, and the pioneers. But the individual stories often lack the connecting thread -- the sequence of events that explains how a fourteen-year-old boy reading James in a farmhouse led to a worldwide church. Why was the Restoration necessary? What had been lost? How did each event build on the ones before it?

This book gives young Latter-day Saints a continuous narrative of the Restoration from the Great Apostasy through Joseph Smith's martyrdom and Brigham Young becoming president of the Church. It follows the historical arc: the loss of priesthood authority, the centuries of preparation, Joseph Smith's early life, the First Vision, the coming forth of the Book of Mormon, the restoration of the priesthood, the organization of the Church, the temples, the persecutions, Nauvoo, Carthage, and the transition of leadership to Brigham Young.

## Perspective

Written for families and children who are active members of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. Joseph Smith was a prophet of God. The First Vision happened. The Book of Mormon is true. The priesthood was restored. These are not argued or defended -- they are the starting point. The tone is matter-of-fact, reverent where appropriate, and never apologetic. Historical difficulties (persecution, the loss of the 116 pages, Liberty Jail) are presented honestly as part of the story.

## Series Context

One book covering the Restoration from the Great Apostasy through Brigham Young becoming president. Pairs naturally with History of Israel for Kids and The Ministry of Jesus Christ for Kids but stands alone.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300-500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- Scripture and historical references included naturally
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7-12

## Prerequisites

- None required. Pairs well with the other religion books but does not depend on them.

## The Restoration for Kids

**Subtitle:** How the Gospel Was Brought Back

### Chapter 1: The Great Apostasy

**Core concepts:** What was lost after the Savior's death, the apostles are killed, priesthood authority lost, doctrine changes

- 1.1 -- What Jesus Established
  - When Jesus was on the earth, He established His Church. He called twelve apostles, gave them priesthood authority, taught doctrine, and organized ordinances like baptism and the sacrament. The Church was led by living apostles and prophets who received revelation from God. After the Savior's resurrection and ascension, the apostles continued to lead. But opposition was fierce from the very beginning.

- 1.2 -- The Apostles Are Killed
  - One by one, the original apostles were killed. James was executed by Herod Agrippa (Acts 12:2). Tradition holds that Peter was crucified upside down in Rome and Paul was beheaded. As the apostles died, the priesthood keys they held -- the authority to lead the Church and receive revelation for it -- were lost. Without apostles, there was no one authorized to call new apostles. The chain of authority was broken.

- 1.3 -- Doctrine Changes
  - Without prophets and apostles to receive revelation, people began to interpret the gospel on their own. Councils of scholars debated what to believe. Greek philosophy was mixed with Christian teaching. Simple truths -- like the nature of God, the purpose of baptism, and the plan of salvation -- were changed, reinterpreted, or lost entirely. This gradual process is called the Great Apostasy. It wasn't one dramatic moment. It was a slow drift away from the truth.

- 1.4 -- Why the Apostasy Matters
  - The Great Apostasy means that the gospel couldn't simply be reformed or corrected from within. It had to be restored -- brought back by God Himself, with new revelation, new prophets, and new priesthood authority. Every part of the Restoration -- the First Vision, the Book of Mormon, the priesthood, the temples -- exists because something was lost. Understanding the Apostasy helps you understand why the Restoration was necessary.

**Test:** 4 questions on what Jesus established, how the apostles were lost, how doctrine changed, and why the Apostasy matters.

### Chapter 2: Centuries of Preparation

**Core concepts:** The Dark Ages, the Reformation, the printing press, religious freedom, America

- 2.1 -- The Dark Ages
  - For over a thousand years after the Apostasy, most people in Europe had limited access to the Bible. The Catholic Church was the dominant religious authority. Services were conducted in Latin, which most people didn't speak. Those who questioned church teachings were often persecuted. Ordinary people had no way to read the scriptures for themselves and no authority to receive revelation. The fulness of the gospel was not on the earth.

- 2.2 -- The Reformation
  - In the 1500s, brave men began to challenge the established church. Martin Luther in Germany nailed his 95 Theses to a church door, protesting practices he believed were wrong. John Calvin, William Tyndale, and others taught that people should be able to read the Bible in their own language. Tyndale translated the Bible into English and was executed for it. These reformers didn't restore the gospel -- they didn't have priesthood authority -- but they opened the door for religious thinking and freedom.

- 2.3 -- The Printing Press and the Bible
  - Around 1440, Johannes Gutenberg invented the printing press. Before this, books were copied by hand and incredibly expensive. The printing press made it possible to produce Bibles and other books quickly and cheaply. For the first time, ordinary people could own and read the scriptures. This was a key step in God's preparation -- people needed access to the Bible so they could hunger for the fulness of the gospel.

- 2.4 -- Religious Freedom in America
  - Many people who wanted to worship freely left Europe and came to America. The Pilgrims, the Puritans, and others crossed the ocean seeking a place where they could follow their conscience. Over time, America developed a tradition of religious freedom, eventually protected by the First Amendment to the Constitution. Latter-day Saints believe God prepared America as a land of liberty specifically so the Restoration could take place (1 Nephi 13:12-19). The stage was set.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Dark Ages, the Reformation, the printing press, and religious freedom in America.

### Chapter 3: Joseph Smith's Early Life

**Core concepts:** The Smith family, Palmyra, the religious excitement, a young boy's question

- 3.1 -- The Smith Family
  - Joseph Smith Jr. was born on December 23, 1805, in Sharon, Vermont. His parents, Joseph Sr. and Lucy Mack Smith, were hardworking, God-fearing people who struggled financially. The family moved several times before settling near Palmyra, New York, around 1816. There were many children in the family, and they worked the land together. Joseph's family took religion seriously -- they read the Bible, prayed together, and discussed matters of faith.

- 3.2 -- Life in Palmyra
  - Palmyra and the surrounding area in western New York were part of what historians call the "Burned-over District" -- a region so full of religious revivals and excitement that it seemed spiritually "burned over." Preachers from many denominations held camp meetings, competed for converts, and argued about which church was right. Baptists, Methodists, Presbyterians, and others all claimed to have the truth.

- 3.3 -- The Religious Confusion
  - Young Joseph was deeply affected by the religious excitement. Some of his family leaned toward Presbyterianism. Joseph felt drawn to Methodism at times. But the competing claims confused him. Each church said the others were wrong. Joseph later wrote: "So great were the confusion and strife among the different denominations, that it was impossible for a person young as I was...to come to any certain conclusion who was right and who was wrong" (Joseph Smith--History 1:8).

- 3.4 -- A Question Forms
  - Joseph wanted to know which church was right -- and he wanted to know for himself, not just based on what a preacher said. He was fourteen years old. He didn't have a theological education. He didn't have a position of authority. He was a farm boy with a sincere question. And that question -- which church is true? -- would lead to the most important event since the Resurrection of Jesus Christ.

**Test:** 4 questions on the Smith family, the Burned-over District, the religious confusion, and the question forming in Joseph's mind.

### Chapter 4: The First Vision

**Core concepts:** James 1:5, the Sacred Grove, God the Father and Jesus Christ appear, the aftermath

- 4.1 -- James 1:5
  - While studying the Bible, Joseph came across a verse in the Epistle of James: "If any of you lack wisdom, let him ask of God, that giveth to all men liberally, and upbraideth not; and it shall be given him" (James 1:5). Joseph later said this verse struck him with tremendous power: "Never did any passage of scripture come with more power to the heart of man than this did at this time to mine" (Joseph Smith--History 1:12). If God would give wisdom to anyone who asked, then Joseph would ask.

- 4.2 -- The Sacred Grove
  - On a spring morning in 1820, Joseph went into a grove of trees near his family's farm to pray. He knelt down and began to ask God which church he should join. Almost immediately, he was seized by a dark power that nearly overcame him. He couldn't speak. Just when he was about to give up, he called upon God with all his strength -- and a pillar of light appeared above him and descended upon him (Joseph Smith--History 1:16-17).

- 4.3 -- The Father and the Son
  - In the light, Joseph saw two personages standing above him in the air. One of them called Joseph by name, pointed to the other, and said, "This is My Beloved Son. Hear Him!" (Joseph Smith--History 1:17). God the Father and Jesus Christ appeared to a fourteen-year-old boy in a grove of trees in New York. Joseph asked which church he should join. The Savior told him to join none of them -- the fulness of the gospel was not on the earth. It would be restored.

- 4.4 -- The Aftermath
  - Joseph told a local minister about his experience. The minister dismissed it, saying visions had ceased. Others mocked and persecuted him. Joseph was surprised -- he was just a boy telling the truth about what had happened. But he never denied it. He later wrote: "I had actually seen a light, and in the midst of that light I saw two Personages, and they did in reality speak to me; and though I was hated and persecuted for saying that I had seen a vision, yet it was true" (Joseph Smith--History 1:25).

**Test:** 4 questions on James 1:5, what happened in the Sacred Grove, who appeared and what they said, and the aftermath.

### Chapter 5: Moroni and the Gold Plates

**Core concepts:** The angel Moroni's visits, the Hill Cumorah, four years of preparation, receiving the plates

- 5.1 -- The Angel Moroni Appears
  - Three years after the First Vision, on the night of September 21, 1823, Joseph prayed to know his standing before God. An angel appeared in his room -- a resurrected being named Moroni. Moroni told Joseph that God had a work for him to do. There was a book, written on gold plates, buried in a nearby hill. The book contained the fulness of the gospel as delivered by the Savior to the ancient inhabitants of the Americas. Moroni visited Joseph three times that night, repeating the message each time.

- 5.2 -- The Hill Cumorah
  - The next day, Joseph went to the Hill Cumorah, about three miles from his family's farm. He found the stone box Moroni had described and saw the gold plates inside, along with the Urim and Thummim -- an instrument prepared for translating the record. But Moroni told Joseph he could not take the plates yet. He was not ready. Joseph would need to return to the hill every year for four years, meeting with Moroni each time, being taught and prepared.

- 5.3 -- Four Years of Preparation
  - From 1823 to 1827, Joseph returned to the Hill Cumorah each September. During these years, he grew, matured, and was taught by Moroni. He also faced challenges -- his family continued to struggle financially, and his older brother Alvin, who had encouraged him, died unexpectedly. Joseph worked, married Emma Hale in January 1827, and waited for the time when he would be trusted with the sacred record.

- 5.4 -- Receiving the Plates
  - On September 22, 1827, Joseph finally received the gold plates from Moroni at the Hill Cumorah. He also received the Urim and Thummim and the breastplate. Moroni charged Joseph with their safekeeping -- he was personally responsible for them. Almost immediately, people tried to steal the plates. Joseph had to hide them repeatedly -- in a log, under the hearth, in a barrel of beans. The opposition was intense, but the work had begun.

**Test:** 4 questions on Moroni's visit, the Hill Cumorah, the four years of preparation, and receiving the plates.

### Chapter 6: Translating the Book of Mormon

**Core concepts:** Martin Harris and the 116 pages, Oliver Cowdery, the translation process, publication

- 6.1 -- Martin Harris and the 116 Pages
  - Joseph began translating the plates with the help of Martin Harris, a prosperous farmer who believed in the work and served as scribe. Martin's wife, Lucy, was skeptical and pressured Martin to prove the plates were real. Martin begged Joseph to let him take the first 116 pages of the manuscript to show her. Joseph asked the Lord twice and was told no. Martin kept asking. The third time, the Lord permitted it -- and Martin lost the pages. The Lord rebuked Joseph severely, and the gift of translation was taken away for a time (D&C 3, 10).

- 6.2 -- Oliver Cowdery Arrives
  - In April 1829, a young schoolteacher named Oliver Cowdery came to Joseph's home. Oliver had heard about the plates and felt prompted to help. He became Joseph's primary scribe, and the two worked together intensely. Joseph translated and Oliver wrote. Oliver later testified that these were "days never to be forgotten -- to sit under the sound of a voice dictated by the inspiration of heaven" (footnote in Joseph Smith--History 1:71).

- 6.3 -- The Translation
  - Joseph translated the Book of Mormon by the gift and power of God. He used the Urim and Thummim and later a seer stone to receive the translation. The process was remarkably fast -- the bulk of the text we have today was translated in about 65 working days. Joseph, who had limited formal education, produced a complex record of over 500 pages containing history, doctrine, prophecy, and poetry. The translation itself is evidence of divine assistance.

- 6.4 -- Publication
  - The Book of Mormon was published in March 1830 by E.B. Grandin in Palmyra, New York. Martin Harris mortgaged his farm to pay for the printing -- 5,000 copies. Before publication, three witnesses (Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, Martin Harris) and eight additional witnesses saw and handled the gold plates. Their testimonies are printed in every copy of the Book of Mormon to this day. The book was now in the world.

**Test:** 4 questions on the 116 pages, Oliver Cowdery's role, the translation process, and the publication and witnesses.

### Chapter 7: The Restoration of the Priesthood

**Core concepts:** The Aaronic Priesthood restored by John the Baptist, the Melchizedek Priesthood restored by Peter, James, and John, what priesthood authority means

- 7.1 -- Why Priesthood Matters
  - Priesthood is the authority to act in God's name. Without it, ordinances like baptism and confirmation are not recognized by God -- even if they are performed sincerely. During the Great Apostasy, priesthood authority was lost from the earth. It couldn't be claimed or invented. It had to be given by someone who already held it. That's why the Restoration required heavenly messengers -- only resurrected beings who held the priesthood could pass it on.

- 7.2 -- John the Baptist and the Aaronic Priesthood
  - On May 15, 1829, while translating the Book of Mormon, Joseph and Oliver came across passages about baptism and wondered about their own need to be baptized. They went into the woods near the Susquehanna River to pray. John the Baptist appeared as a resurrected being, laid his hands on their heads, and said: "Upon you my fellow servants, in the name of Messiah I confer the Priesthood of Aaron" (D&C 13). Joseph and Oliver then baptized each other in the river.

- 7.3 -- Peter, James, and John and the Melchizedek Priesthood
  - Shortly after receiving the Aaronic Priesthood, Joseph and Oliver received the Melchizedek Priesthood from the apostles Peter, James, and John -- the same three who had been with Jesus at the Transfiguration and in Gethsemane. The Melchizedek Priesthood includes the authority to confirm members, bestow the gift of the Holy Ghost, ordain others, and lead the Church. With both priesthoods restored, the authority to perform all gospel ordinances was back on the earth.

- 7.4 -- What This Means for You
  - The restoration of the priesthood is not just a historical event. It affects your life directly. When you are baptized, the person performing the ordinance holds the same Aaronic Priesthood that John the Baptist restored. When you are confirmed, it is by the authority of the Melchizedek Priesthood restored by Peter, James, and John. Every priesthood blessing, every ordinance, every setting apart in the Church traces back to these moments on the banks of the Susquehanna River.

**Test:** 4 questions on why priesthood matters, the Aaronic Priesthood restoration, the Melchizedek Priesthood restoration, and how it connects to your life today.

### Chapter 8: The Church Is Organized

**Core concepts:** April 6, 1830, six members, early missionaries, the gathering begins

- 8.1 -- April 6, 1830
  - On April 6, 1830, Joseph Smith organized the Church of Christ (later renamed the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints) in a small log home in Fayette, New York. Six members were present -- the minimum required by New York law. Joseph was sustained as the first elder and prophet of the Church. The sacrament was administered. Members were confirmed and received the gift of the Holy Ghost. The church that Christ had established in the meridian of time was back on the earth.

- 8.2 -- Early Converts
  - The Church grew quickly in its first months. Joseph's family joined. Friends and neighbors were baptized. Parley P. Pratt, a former preacher, read the Book of Mormon and was converted almost immediately -- he became one of the Church's greatest missionaries. Sidney Rigdon, a popular minister in Ohio, was converted and brought much of his congregation with him. Within a year, hundreds of people had joined the Church.

- 8.3 -- Early Missionaries
  - From the very beginning, the Lord called missionaries to preach the restored gospel. Oliver Cowdery was sent west to teach the Native Americans. Along the way, the missionaries stopped in Kirtland, Ohio, where they baptized over a hundred people in just a few weeks. Missionary work was not easy -- missionaries traveled on foot, often without money, and faced rejection and hostility. But the converts kept coming.

- 8.4 -- The Gathering Begins
  - The Lord revealed that the Saints should gather -- come together in one place to build Zion. The first gathering place was Kirtland, Ohio, where Sidney Rigdon's converts provided a ready-made community. Joseph and Emma moved to Kirtland in early 1831. The gathering was a pattern that would repeat throughout the Restoration -- the Saints would gather, build, face persecution, and move again. But the principle remained: God's people were to gather, strengthen each other, and build temples.

**Test:** 4 questions on the organization of the Church, early converts, missionary work, and the gathering.

### Chapter 9: Kirtland -- The First Gathering Place

**Core concepts:** Revelations received, the School of the Prophets, building the Kirtland Temple, the Doctrine and Covenants

- 9.1 -- Revelations in Kirtland
  - Kirtland was a season of learning and revelation. Joseph received many of the revelations now found in the Doctrine and Covenants during the Kirtland period. The Lord gave instructions on Church organization, missionary work, the law of consecration, the Word of Wisdom (D&C 89), and dozens of other topics. Joseph was learning how to lead the Church -- and the Lord was teaching him step by step.

- 9.2 -- The School of the Prophets
  - In 1833, the Lord directed Joseph to organize the School of the Prophets -- a place for Church leaders to study the gospel, learn languages, and prepare for their callings. The Word of Wisdom was given as a result of concerns about tobacco smoke in the school. The school was part of a larger pattern: the Lord expected His people to learn. "The glory of God is intelligence" (D&C 93:36). Education -- spiritual and secular -- was central to the Restoration from the beginning.

- 9.3 -- Building the Kirtland Temple
  - The Lord commanded the Saints to build a temple in Kirtland (D&C 95). The Saints were poor, but they gave everything they had. Women donated their china to be crushed and mixed into the plaster so the walls would sparkle. Men quarried stone and cut timber. It took three years of sacrifice. The Kirtland Temple was not like modern temples -- it was a place for instruction, worship, and the outpouring of the Spirit. It was the first temple of the Restoration.

- 9.4 -- The Doctrine and Covenants
  - The revelations Joseph received were compiled into a book -- first called the Book of Commandments (1833) and later expanded and published as the Doctrine and Covenants (1835). Unlike the Bible or Book of Mormon, the Doctrine and Covenants is not an ancient record. It is modern revelation -- God speaking directly to His people in their own time. It contains instructions, prophecies, doctrinal explanations, and personal counsel that guided the early Church and continues to guide it today.

**Test:** 4 questions on revelations in Kirtland, the School of the Prophets, building the Kirtland Temple, and the Doctrine and Covenants.

### Chapter 10: The Kirtland Temple Dedication

**Core concepts:** The dedicatory prayer, spiritual outpouring, the Savior appears, Moses, Elias, and Elijah restore keys

- 10.1 -- The Dedicatory Prayer
  - On March 27, 1836, the Kirtland Temple was dedicated. Over a thousand people gathered inside and outside the building. Joseph Smith offered the dedicatory prayer, which had been given to him by revelation (D&C 109). He asked the Lord to accept the temple, to pour out His Spirit on the Saints, and to bless all who would worship there. Many in attendance reported seeing angels, hearing heavenly choirs, and feeling the Spirit with unusual power.

- 10.2 -- The Spiritual Outpouring
  - The weeks surrounding the dedication were filled with remarkable spiritual experiences. Some members reported visions. Others spoke in tongues or prophesied. Some saw angels in the temple. Joseph compared it to the Day of Pentecost described in Acts 2, when the Holy Ghost was poured out upon the early apostles. The Saints had sacrificed everything to build the temple, and the Lord poured out blessings in return.

- 10.3 -- The Savior Appears
  - On April 3, 1836 -- one week after the dedication -- Joseph Smith and Oliver Cowdery were praying behind the veil in the temple. The Savior appeared to them and accepted the temple: "I have accepted this house, and my name shall be here; and I will manifest myself to my people in mercy in this house" (D&C 110:7). Jesus Christ Himself stood in the Kirtland Temple. The God of Israel had a house on the earth again.

- 10.4 -- Moses, Elias, and Elijah
  - After the Savior departed, three more heavenly messengers appeared. Moses committed the keys of the gathering of Israel. Elias committed the dispensation of the gospel of Abraham. And Elijah committed the keys of the sealing power -- the authority to bind families together for eternity (D&C 110:11-16). Malachi had prophesied that Elijah would return "before the coming of the great and dreadful day of the Lord" (Malachi 4:5-6). That prophecy was fulfilled in the Kirtland Temple.

**Test:** 4 questions on the dedicatory prayer, the spiritual outpouring, the Savior's appearance, and the keys restored by Moses, Elias, and Elijah.

### Chapter 11: Missouri and Trouble

**Core concepts:** Zion in Missouri, conflict with settlers, the Extermination Order, Haun's Mill, Liberty Jail

- 11.1 -- Zion in Missouri
  - While the Saints gathered in Kirtland, the Lord also revealed that the land of Zion -- the place where the Saints would ultimately gather and build the New Jerusalem -- was in Jackson County, Missouri (D&C 57:1-3). Missionaries and settlers began moving to Missouri. But problems arose almost immediately. The existing settlers distrusted the Saints -- their religion was strange, they voted as a bloc, many were from the North and opposed slavery, and they kept coming in large numbers.

- 11.2 -- Driven Out
  - In 1833, mobs in Jackson County attacked the Saints, destroyed their printing press, tarred and feathered Church leaders, and drove them from their homes in the dead of winter. The Saints relocated to other counties in Missouri, but the pattern repeated. In 1838, tensions exploded again. Governor Lilburn Boggs issued what became known as the Extermination Order, declaring that "the Mormons must be treated as enemies, and must be exterminated or driven from the State."

- 11.3 -- Haun's Mill
  - On October 30, 1838, a mob attacked a small Latter-day Saint settlement at Haun's Mill. Seventeen men and boys were killed, including a ten-year-old boy. Women and children hid in the woods. The attack was brutal and unprovoked. Three days later, Joseph Smith and other Church leaders surrendered to the Missouri militia to prevent further bloodshed. They were imprisoned without a proper trial.

- 11.4 -- Liberty Jail
  - Joseph Smith spent the winter of 1838-1839 in Liberty Jail -- a cold, dark, miserable dungeon. He and his companions were given rotten food, cramped conditions, and no hope of release. In his anguish, Joseph cried out to God: "O God, where art thou?" The Lord answered: "My son, peace be unto thy soul; thine adversity and thine afflictions shall be but a small moment" (D&C 121:1, 7). Some of the most powerful scripture in the Doctrine and Covenants was revealed in that jail. Joseph called it a "temple of God" -- he learned things there he could not have learned anywhere else.

**Test:** 4 questions on Zion in Missouri, being driven out, Haun's Mill, and Liberty Jail.

### Chapter 12: Nauvoo the Beautiful

**Core concepts:** Building Nauvoo, the Nauvoo Temple, the Relief Society, growth and prosperity

- 12.1 -- A New Beginning
  - After escaping from Missouri in 1839, Joseph and the Saints settled on a swampy bend of the Mississippi River in Illinois. They drained the swamps, built homes, and named the city Nauvoo -- a Hebrew word meaning "beautiful." The city grew rapidly. Within a few years, Nauvoo was one of the largest cities in Illinois, rivaling Chicago in population. The Saints built homes, shops, schools, and a university. It seemed like they had finally found a place to stay.

- 12.2 -- The Nauvoo Temple
  - The Lord commanded the Saints to build another temple -- this time in Nauvoo (D&C 124:27-28). The Nauvoo Temple would be different from Kirtland. It would include ordinances like baptism for the dead, endowments, and sealings. The Saints worked on the temple constantly, even as troubles grew around them. The temple was the priority -- the Lord made clear that the Saints were to build it or face rejection as a people.

- 12.3 -- The Relief Society
  - On March 17, 1842, Joseph Smith organized the Female Relief Society of Nauvoo with Emma Smith as its first president. Joseph said the Church was not fully organized until the women were organized. The Relief Society was a formal organization for the women of the Church -- for charity, service, and spiritual growth. It remains one of the largest women's organizations in the world today.

- 12.4 -- Growth and Gathering
  - Missionaries were sent to England, where thousands were converted and gathered to Nauvoo. The Book of Mormon was printed in multiple languages. Joseph continued to receive revelations. He began teaching about the temple endowment, the sealing of families for eternity, and the plan of salvation in greater detail. Nauvoo became a center of learning, faith, and covenant-making. But opposition was building again -- both from outside and, this time, from within.

**Test:** 4 questions on building Nauvoo, the Nauvoo Temple, the Relief Society, and the growth of the Church.

### Chapter 13: Joseph Smith -- Prophet of the Restoration

**Core concepts:** Joseph's character, what he accomplished, his testimony, preparing the Church

- 13.1 -- Who Joseph Was
  - Joseph Smith was not a perfect man -- and he never claimed to be. He was warm, energetic, and loved people. He played with children, wrestled with friends, and laughed easily. He also made mistakes, learned from them, and grew. He was a farm boy with limited formal education who, by the power of God, translated an ancient record, received hundreds of revelations, built cities, organized a church, and established temples. No one in modern history did more to reveal the nature of God and the plan of salvation.

- 13.2 -- What He Restored
  - Through Joseph Smith, the Lord restored the priesthood, the Book of Mormon, the temple ordinances, the organization of the Church, the sealing power, the doctrine of eternal families, the plan of salvation, the Word of Wisdom, the law of tithing, missionary work, and much more. Joseph received more published revelation than any other prophet in history. The Doctrine and Covenants, the Pearl of Great Price, and the Book of Mormon all came through him. He was the instrument, and the Lord was the source.

- 13.3 -- His Testimony
  - Despite years of persecution, Joseph never denied what he had seen and experienced. He wrote: "I had seen a vision; I knew it, and I knew that God knew it, and I could not deny it, neither dared I do it" (Joseph Smith--History 1:25). He testified of the Father and the Son, of angels, of the Book of Mormon, of the priesthood, and of the Restoration. He sealed that testimony with his life.

- 13.4 -- Preparing the Church
  - Joseph seemed to sense that his time was limited. In the final months of his life, he conferred all the necessary priesthood keys upon the Twelve Apostles, ensuring that the Church could continue without him. He said to the Twelve: "I have lived until I have seen this burden, which has rested on my shoulders, rolled on to the shoulders of other men...the keys of the kingdom are planted on the earth to be taken away no more forever." He was preparing the Church to survive his death.

**Test:** 4 questions on Joseph's character, what he restored, his testimony, and how he prepared the Church to continue.

### Chapter 14: The Martyrdom

**Core concepts:** Growing threats, Carthage Jail, the deaths of Joseph and Hyrum, the Saints' grief

- 14.1 -- Growing Threats
  - By 1844, opposition to Joseph Smith was intense -- from outside Nauvoo and from within. Some former members started a newspaper, the Nauvoo Expositor, attacking Joseph. The Nauvoo city council ordered the press destroyed, which gave Joseph's enemies the legal pretext they needed. Joseph was charged with riot. He knew going to Carthage to face the charges was dangerous. He said, "I am going like a lamb to the slaughter; but I am calm as a summer's morning" (D&C 135:4).

- 14.2 -- Carthage Jail
  - Joseph and his brother Hyrum, along with John Taylor and Willard Richards, were held in Carthage Jail. The governor of Illinois promised them protection, but the protection was withdrawn. On the afternoon of June 27, 1844, a mob with painted faces stormed the jail. The four men were upstairs in the jailer's bedroom. The mob fired through the door.

- 14.3 -- The Deaths of Joseph and Hyrum
  - Hyrum was shot first and fell, saying "I am a dead man." Joseph rushed to him. The mob kept firing. John Taylor was hit multiple times but survived. Willard Richards was barely touched. Joseph went to the window, was struck by bullets from inside and outside the jail, and fell from the second story. He died at age 38. Hyrum was 44. The Prophet and the Patriarch of the Church were gone. "In life they were not divided, and in death they were not separated" (D&C 135:3).

- 14.4 -- The Saints' Grief
  - When word reached Nauvoo, the grief was overwhelming. Thousands lined the streets to view the bodies. The question on everyone's mind was: What happens now? Joseph was the prophet. He had led the Church from its first day. Without him, would the Restoration survive? Joseph's enemies assumed the Church would collapse. They were wrong.

### Chapter 15: Brigham Young and the Way Forward

**Core concepts:** The succession question, the mantle of the prophet, Brigham Young leads, finishing the temple, heading west

- 15.1 -- Who Will Lead?
  - After Joseph's death, several people claimed the right to lead the Church. Sidney Rigdon, a counselor in the First Presidency, argued he should be the "guardian" of the Church. Others put forward their own claims. The question was critical -- the wrong decision could split the Church permanently. But Joseph had already given the answer: he had conferred all the keys of the kingdom on the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles. The senior apostle was Brigham Young.

- 15.2 -- The Mantle of the Prophet
  - On August 8, 1844, Brigham Young spoke to the Saints in Nauvoo. Something remarkable happened. Many who were present testified that as Brigham spoke, his voice, his manner, and even his appearance seemed to change -- he sounded and looked like Joseph Smith. This experience, often called the "mantle of the prophet," confirmed to many Saints that the Twelve Apostles held the authority to lead the Church. Brigham Young was sustained as the president of the Twelve, and the Twelve led the Church.

- 15.3 -- Finishing the Nauvoo Temple
  - Even as persecution intensified and it became clear the Saints would have to leave Nauvoo, Brigham Young insisted on finishing the temple. The Saints worked day and night. Ordinances began in the temple in late 1845, even before the building was fully complete. Thousands of Saints received their endowments and sealings before leaving. The temple was the priority -- Brigham understood that the covenants made there would strengthen the Saints for everything ahead.

- 15.4 -- Looking West
  - By early 1846, it was clear the Saints could not stay in Nauvoo. Brigham Young organized the exodus. The first companies crossed the frozen Mississippi River in February 1846. Ahead of them lay over a thousand miles of plains, rivers, and mountains. They were heading to the Great Basin -- a place so remote that no one would drive them out again. Brigham Young would lead them there, becoming the second president of the Church. The Restoration had survived the death of its founding prophet. It would survive everything else that was coming.
