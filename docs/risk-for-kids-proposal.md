# Risk for Kids — Book Proposal

## Why This Book

Risk is one of the most useful concepts a person can understand, and one of the least taught. Kids already deal with risk every day — trying new things, spending or saving money, choosing who to trust, deciding what's worth the effort. But nobody teaches them the actual framework for thinking about it.

This book teaches risk the same way the econ series teaches economics: factually, clearly, with real examples and a vocabulary the student can use for the rest of their life. By the end, a kid should be able to identify a risk, estimate its likelihood and impact, compare it to alternatives, decide how to respond, and recognize when someone else is communicating risk poorly or with an agenda.

## Series Context

One book. Risk is a tight, self-contained framework. The econ series touches risk as an economics concept (Book 2: uncertainty, insurance, moral hazard). This book treats risk as its own subject — what it is, how to measure it, how to respond to it, and how to understand it when other people talk about it.

## Format (matching econ series)

- 15 chapters, 4 lessons + 1 test per chapter
- Each lesson: ~300–500 words, conversational second-person prose
- Bold key terms defined inline
- 3 questions per lesson: one recall, one list-based, one applied/scenario
- Tests: 4 questions pulling from the whole chapter
- Target age: 7–12
- Real-world examples from a kid's life

## Math expectations

- Probability as fractions and percentages (1 in 100, 1%, etc.) — explained from scratch
- Comparison of numbers (which is bigger, how many times bigger)
- Multiplication for likelihood × impact — simple, concrete numbers
- No algebra or complex formulas

## Prerequisites

- None required. This book stands alone.
- Pairs well with the Economics for Kids series but doesn't depend on it.

## Risk for Kids

**Subtitle:** Understanding Uncertainty

### Chapter 1: What Is Risk?

**Core concepts:** Risk as uncertainty about outcomes, risk is everywhere, risk can be positive or negative

- 1.1 — Risk Means Uncertainty
  - Risk means you don't know exactly what will happen. When you try something new, there's uncertainty about how it will turn out. That uncertainty is risk. Risk isn't the same as danger — it just means the outcome isn't guaranteed.
- 1.2 — Risk Can Be Good or Bad
  - Most people think risk means something bad might happen. But risk also means something good might happen. Trying out for the team is risky — you might not make it, or you might discover your favorite sport. Risk includes both sides.
- 1.3 — Risk Is Everywhere
  - Every decision involves some risk. What to eat, who to trust, how to spend your time, whether to try something new. Some risks are tiny (trying a new food). Some are big (moving to a new school). You're already dealing with risk constantly — this book gives you the tools to do it well.
- 1.4 — Risk vs. Certainty
  - Very few things are certain. The weather might change. A friend might cancel. A test might be harder than you expected. Certainty is rare. Most of life is about making decisions when you don't know exactly what will happen. That's not a problem — it's just how things work.

**Test:** 4 questions on what risk means, positive vs. negative outcomes, where risk shows up, and risk vs. certainty.

### Chapter 2: Likelihood — How Probable Is It?

**Core concepts:** Probability, fractions and percentages, base rates, common vs. rare

- 2.1 — What Is Probability?
  - Probability is a number that tells you how likely something is to happen. 0% means impossible. 100% means certain. A coin flip is 50%. A rolled dice landing on 6 is about 17%. Probability gives you a way to talk about likelihood precisely instead of just saying "maybe."
- 2.2 — Ways to Express Probability
  - "1 in 100," "1%," and "0.01" all mean the same thing. You'll see probability written different ways in different places. "1 in a million" means 0.0001%. Getting comfortable with these formats helps you compare risks accurately.
- 2.3 — Base Rates
  - A base rate is how often something actually happens. About 1 in 10,000 flights has a serious incident. About 1 in 100 car trips involves some kind of close call. Base rates are your starting point for understanding how likely a risk really is, instead of guessing.
- 2.4 — Common vs. Rare
  - Some risks happen all the time (tripping, catching a cold). Some almost never happen (lightning strikes, shark attacks). Knowing whether a risk is common or rare is the first step in deciding how much attention it deserves. Common risks add up. Rare risks usually don't.

**Test:** 4 questions on probability, converting between formats, base rates, and common vs. rare.

### Chapter 3: Impact — How Bad or Good Could It Be?

**Core concepts:** Severity, reversibility, scope, positive impact, not all outcomes are equal

- 3.1 — What Is Impact?
  - Impact is how much something matters if it happens. A scraped knee has low impact. A broken arm has higher impact. Losing $1 has low impact. Losing your life savings has very high impact. Likelihood tells you how often — impact tells you how much.
- 3.2 — A Scale of Impact
  - A useful way to think about it: negligible (barely notice), minor (inconvenient), moderate (significant but manageable), major (serious, hard to recover from), catastrophic (life-changing). Not every bad outcome is the same size, and treating them all as equally serious leads to poor decisions.
- 3.3 — Reversibility
  - Some impacts can be undone. A bad haircut grows out. A failed quiz can be retaken. A lost game happens again next week. Other impacts are permanent — a serious injury, a destroyed trust, money gone forever. Reversible outcomes are much easier to recover from, which changes how you should think about the risk.
- 3.4 — Positive Impact
  - Impact isn't only about bad outcomes. Trying something risky could lead to a new friendship, a new skill, a big win. When evaluating risk, the potential upside matters just as much as the potential downside. Ignoring the upside makes everything look scarier than it is.

**Test:** 4 questions on what impact is, the severity scale, reversibility, and considering positive impact.

### Chapter 4: Combining Likelihood and Impact

**Core concepts:** The two-question framework, risk matrices, expected value

- 4.1 — The Two Questions
  - For any risk, ask: "How likely is it?" and "How bad (or good) would it be?" These two questions together give you a much clearer picture than either one alone. A likely event with tiny impact is different from an unlikely event with massive impact.
- 4.2 — The Risk Matrix
  - A risk matrix is a grid with likelihood on one axis and impact on the other. Plot a risk on the grid and you can see at a glance whether it's something to worry about. High likelihood + high impact = top priority. Low likelihood + low impact = don't spend time on it.
- 4.3 — Expected Value
  - Expected value is likelihood multiplied by impact. A 10% chance of losing $100 has an expected value of $10. A 1% chance of losing $1,000 also has an expected value of $10. Different situations, same expected cost. Expected value lets you compare very different risks on the same scale.
- 4.4 — Using the Framework
  - Walk through a few examples: riding a bike without a helmet (moderate likelihood, potentially major impact), eating a new food you've never tried (moderate likelihood of not liking it, negligible impact), investing money (uncertain likelihood, variable impact both directions). The framework works for anything.

**Test:** 4 questions on the two-question approach, risk matrices, expected value, and applying the framework.

### Chapter 5: Comparing Risks

**Core concepts:** Relative risk, comparing fairly, the risk of inaction, surprising comparisons

- 5.1 — Every Choice Compares Risks
  - When you choose to do something, you're also choosing not to do the alternative. Both options have risks. Driving to school has risk. Walking has different risk. Staying home has its own risk. There's no option with zero risk — just different risks to compare.
- 5.2 — Comparing Fairly
  - To compare risks, use the same units and the same timeframe. Comparing a yearly risk to a daily risk without adjusting is misleading. Comparing a risk that affects one person to a risk that affects a city doesn't work without context. Fair comparisons need consistent measurement.
- 5.3 — The Risk of Doing Nothing
  - Doing nothing feels safe, but it has its own risks. Not investing means inflation shrinks your savings. Not exercising means health problems accumulate. Not speaking up means missed opportunities. Inaction isn't risk-free — it's just risk that's harder to see.
- 5.4 — Comparisons That Surprise People
  - Driving is more dangerous per trip than flying. Swimming pools cause more child deaths than many things parents worry about more. Falls cause more harm than many dramatic-sounding risks. When you compare actual numbers, the ranking often looks very different from what most people assume.

**Test:** 4 questions on comparing both sides of a choice, fair comparison, the risk of inaction, and real-world comparisons.

### Chapter 6: Responding to Risk

**Core concepts:** The four responses — avoid, reduce, transfer, accept

- 6.1 — Four Ways to Respond
  - Once you understand a risk, you have four options: avoid it entirely, reduce its likelihood or impact, transfer it to someone else, or accept it and move forward. Every risk response falls into one of these four categories.
- 6.2 — Avoid
  - Avoidance means not doing the thing at all. Don't swim in dangerous water. Don't invest money you need next week. Avoidance makes sense when the downside is severe and irreversible, or when the reward isn't worth it. But avoiding everything means missing everything too.
- 6.3 — Reduce
  - Reducing risk means doing the thing but making it safer. Wear a helmet. Diversify investments. Practice before the performance. Study before the test. Reduction is the most common response — you still get the reward, with less downside.
- 6.4 — Transfer and Accept
  - Transferring risk means paying someone else to carry it — insurance is the most common example. Accepting risk means acknowledging it and proceeding anyway because the reward is worth it or the risk is small enough. Trying a new restaurant, asking someone to play, raising your hand in class — you accept small risks all the time.

**Test:** 4 questions on the four responses, when to avoid, when to reduce, and when to transfer or accept.

### Chapter 7: Risk Tradeoffs

**Core concepts:** Safety has costs, reducing one risk can create another, acceptable risk, zero risk is impossible

- 7.1 — Safety Has a Cost
  - Every safety measure costs something — money, time, freedom, or opportunity. Helmets cost money. Extra studying takes time away from other things. Extreme caution means fewer new experiences. The question isn't "can I be safer?" — it's "is the extra safety worth what it costs?"
- 7.2 — Reducing One Risk Can Create Another
  - Avoiding all germs weakens your immune system. Never letting kids take physical risks means they don't develop coordination and judgment. Keeping all your money in cash avoids market risk but creates inflation risk. Risk doesn't disappear — it shifts. Make sure the new risk isn't worse than the original one.
- 7.3 — Acceptable Risk
  - Zero risk is impossible and would be miserable if you tried. The goal is to reach a level of risk you've thought about and decided is worth it. What's acceptable depends on the situation, the person, and the stakes. A surgeon and a skateboarder have different acceptable risk levels for their hands — and both are being rational.
- 7.4 — Diminishing Returns on Safety
  - The first safety measures usually reduce risk a lot. A seatbelt dramatically reduces injury risk. But after the big reductions, each additional measure does less. At some point, spending more time or money on safety produces very little additional benefit. Knowing when "safe enough" has been reached is an important skill.

**Test:** 4 questions on the cost of safety, risk shifting, acceptable risk, and diminishing returns.

### Chapter 8: Probability and Scale

**Core concepts:** Why small numbers are hard, orders of magnitude, relative vs. absolute risk, large populations

- 8.1 — Small Numbers Are Tricky
  - 0.01% and 0.1% both feel like "almost never." But 0.1% is ten times more likely than 0.01%. If a million people are affected, 0.01% is 100 people and 0.1% is 1,000 people. The difference between "small" numbers can be enormous when you pay attention.
- 8.2 — Orders of Magnitude
  - An order of magnitude is a factor of 10. The difference between 1 in 1,000 and 1 in 10,000 is one order of magnitude — one is ten times less likely. Between 1 in 100 and 1 in 1,000,000? Four orders of magnitude — 10,000 times less likely. These differences are huge, even though both sound "rare."
- 8.3 — Relative Risk vs. Absolute Risk
  - "This doubles your risk!" sounds terrifying. But doubling from 1 in a million to 2 in a million is still incredibly rare. Relative risk (how much it changes) and absolute risk (the actual probability) can tell very different stories. Always ask for the absolute number.
- 8.4 — Large Populations
  - A 1 in 10,000 risk affects 1 person in a town of 10,000. In a country of 300 million, that same risk affects 30,000 people. Small probabilities applied to large populations produce large numbers. This is important for public policy, health, and understanding news stories.

**Test:** 4 questions on why small numbers are tricky, orders of magnitude, relative vs. absolute risk, and large populations.

### Chapter 9: How Other People Talk About Risk

**Core concepts:** Risk communication from news, parents, friends, advertisers; framing, agenda, checking claims

- 9.1 — Everyone Has a Perspective
  - When someone tells you something is "dangerous" or "safe," they're giving you their perception, not a fact. Parents worry about different things than kids. News covers dramatic events, not common ones. Friends repeat what they've heard. Everyone communicates risk through their own lens.
- 9.2 — How the News Covers Risk
  - News reports on unusual, dramatic events because they're interesting — plane crashes, rare diseases, shark attacks. This makes rare events feel common. Meanwhile, the things that actually hurt the most people (car accidents, heart disease, falls) get very little coverage because they're not exciting stories. News is not a reliable source for how likely something is.
- 9.3 — Framing: Same Risk, Different Feeling
  - "This procedure has a 95% survival rate" and "This procedure has a 5% mortality rate" are the same fact. But they feel completely different. How a risk is framed — which number is highlighted, which words are chosen — changes how people react, even when the underlying facts are identical. Pay attention to framing.
- 9.4 — Checking Claims
  - When someone makes a risk claim — "that's dangerous," "this is perfectly safe," "you should be worried about X" — ask: what's the actual likelihood? What's the actual impact? Where did that information come from? Do they have a reason to make it sound bigger or smaller? Checking claims is how you form your own informed view instead of borrowing someone else's perception.

**Test:** 4 questions on perspective in risk communication, news coverage, framing effects, and checking claims.

### Chapter 10: Common Mistakes in Risk Thinking

**Core concepts:** Availability bias, confusing familiar with safe, anchoring, ignoring base rates

- 10.1 — Judging by What Comes to Mind
  - If you can easily think of an example of something happening, it feels more likely. A recent news story about a house fire makes house fires feel common. This is called availability bias — your brain judges likelihood by how easily it can recall an example, not by how often it actually happens.
- 10.2 — Confusing Familiar with Safe
  - Things you do every day feel safe because they're familiar. Things you've never done feel risky because they're unfamiliar. But familiarity and safety are not the same. Driving is very familiar and statistically risky. Flying is unfamiliar to many people and statistically very safe. Familiarity is about experience, not about actual risk levels.
- 10.3 — Anchoring
  - If someone says "there could be 10,000 cases," the number 10,000 sticks in your mind as an anchor, even if the actual number turns out to be 200. First numbers, first impressions, and first estimates tend to stick and influence your thinking even when better information arrives later.
- 10.4 — Ignoring Base Rates
  - A test is 99% accurate and you test positive. You probably have the condition, right? Not necessarily — if only 1 in 10,000 people have it, most positive results are false positives. Ignoring the base rate (how common the condition is) leads to wrong conclusions. The base rate is always part of the picture.

**Test:** 4 questions on availability bias, familiarity vs. safety, anchoring, and base rates.

### Chapter 11: Risk and Decisions

**Core concepts:** Decision-making under uncertainty, expected value in decisions, sunk costs, reversible vs. irreversible decisions

- 11.1 — Decisions Under Uncertainty
  - Most decisions are made without complete information. You don't know if the weather will change, if the new school will be better, if the investment will pay off. Making decisions under uncertainty is normal — the goal isn't to eliminate uncertainty but to make the best decision you can with what you know.
- 11.2 — Using Expected Value
  - When comparing options, expected value helps. Option A: 80% chance of earning $50 (expected value: $40). Option B: 20% chance of earning $300 (expected value: $60). Option B has higher expected value even though it's less likely. Expected value isn't the only factor, but it's a useful one.
- 11.3 — Sunk Costs
  - Money or time you've already spent is gone. It shouldn't affect your next decision. "I already spent $50 on this, so I should keep going" is a common trap. The question is: "Given where I am now, is continuing the best choice?" What you already spent doesn't change the answer.
- 11.4 — Reversible vs. Irreversible Decisions
  - For reversible decisions (trying a new food, signing up for a class), take the risk freely — you can always undo it. For irreversible decisions (major purchases, permanent changes), be more careful. The amount of caution should match how hard the decision is to undo.

**Test:** 4 questions on deciding with incomplete information, expected value, sunk costs, and reversible vs. irreversible decisions.

### Chapter 12: Types of Risk

**Core concepts:** Physical, financial, social, health, reputational, information/privacy

- 12.1 — Physical Risk
  - Risk of injury or harm to your body. Sports, outdoor activities, transportation, weather. Physical risks are the most intuitive — kids understand them naturally. The same framework applies: likelihood, impact, response.
- 12.2 — Financial Risk
  - Risk involving money. Spending, saving, investing, lending, starting a business. Financial risks range from small (buying something you end up not liking) to large (losing a significant amount of money). Time horizon matters — short-term financial risk is different from long-term.
- 12.3 — Social and Reputational Risk
  - Risk to relationships and how others see you. Speaking up in class, sharing an opinion, standing up for someone, trying something in front of others. The downside is usually embarrassment or social friction. The upside is often connection, respect, and confidence. These risks are frequently worth taking.
- 12.4 — Health and Information Risk
  - Health risk: long-term choices about diet, exercise, sleep, and habits that compound over time. The impact is often invisible day to day but significant over years. Information risk: sharing personal information — online, with companies, with strangers. Once information is out, it's hard to take back.

**Test:** 4 questions on physical risk, financial risk, social/reputational risk, and health/information risk.

### Chapter 13: Risk in Groups and Systems

**Core concepts:** Shared risk, systemic risk, cascading failures, diversification as risk management

- 13.1 — Shared Risk
  - When risk is shared among a group, each person bears less. Insurance pools risk across many people — everyone pays a little so no one pays everything. Teams share the risk of a project. Communities share the risk of natural disasters. Sharing risk is one of the oldest and most powerful risk management tools.
- 13.2 — Systemic Risk
  - Sometimes a risk doesn't just affect one person or one company — it affects the whole system. A bank failure can trigger other bank failures. A drought can affect an entire region's food supply. Systemic risks are harder to diversify away because everything is connected.
- 13.3 — Cascading Failures
  - Sometimes one risk event triggers another, which triggers another. A power outage causes traffic signals to fail, which causes accidents, which overwhelms hospitals. Cascading failures are why small problems can sometimes become big ones. Understanding chains of events helps you spot where things could spiral.
- 13.4 — Diversification
  - Diversification means spreading your exposure so no single event can wipe you out. Don't invest all your money in one stock. Don't rely on one friend for everything. Don't put all your effort into one activity. Diversification reduces the impact of any single thing going wrong.

**Test:** 4 questions on shared risk, systemic risk, cascading failures, and diversification.

### Chapter 14: Risk Over Time

**Core concepts:** Risk changes, monitoring, updating estimates, cumulative risk, compounding

- 14.1 — Risk Changes Over Time
  - A road that's safe on a sunny day is risky in an ice storm. An investment that was solid last year may be shaky now. Conditions change, information changes, and risks change with them. A risk assessment is a snapshot — it needs updating.
- 14.2 — Monitoring
  - Good risk management isn't a one-time exercise. It's ongoing. Check in: has the likelihood changed? Has the potential impact changed? Are the conditions different? Has new information arrived? Monitoring means staying aware rather than assuming your first assessment is still correct.
- 14.3 — Cumulative Risk
  - Small risks add up over time. One day of poor diet doesn't matter much. Years of it matter enormously. One car trip is low risk. Thousands of car trips over a lifetime add up to meaningful risk. Cumulative risk is why everyday habits and repeated exposures matter more than single events.
- 14.4 — Learning from Outcomes
  - After a risk plays out, compare what happened to what you expected. Were you right? Too cautious? Too optimistic? This feedback loop is how you get better at assessing risk over time. Keep track of your predictions and how they turned out.

**Test:** 4 questions on risk changing, monitoring, cumulative risk, and learning from outcomes.

### Chapter 15: Putting It All Together

**Core concepts:** The full framework, applying it, risk as a life skill, making informed choices

- 15.1 — The Risk Framework
  - Review the full process: identify the risk → estimate likelihood → estimate impact → combine them → compare to alternatives (including doing nothing) → choose a response (avoid, reduce, transfer, accept) → monitor and update. This works for physical risks, financial risks, social risks, health risks — anything.
- 15.2 — Applying the Framework
  - Walk through several real examples using the full framework: a kid deciding whether to try a new sport, a family deciding whether to buy a used car, a student deciding whether to speak up in class, someone deciding whether to invest money. Same framework, different domains, same clear thinking.
- 15.3 — When Others Communicate Risk to You
  - When a friend, parent, news story, or advertisement tells you something is risky or safe, run it through the framework. What's the actual likelihood? What's the actual impact? Where's the data? What's their perspective or incentive? You don't have to distrust everyone — just think for yourself.
- 15.4 — Risk as a Life Skill
  - Understanding risk doesn't mean eliminating it. It means seeing it clearly — the real likelihood, the real impact, and the real alternatives — so you can make informed choices instead of just reacting. That skill applies to every part of life, and it only gets more valuable as you get older.

**Test:** 4 questions on the full framework, applying it to a scenario, evaluating risk claims from others, and risk as a life skill.
